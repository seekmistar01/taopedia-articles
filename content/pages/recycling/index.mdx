# Recycling

Recycling describes protocol reuse or redistribution of tokens rather than simple transfer between
ordinary wallets. In Bittensor tokenomics, the term is useful because some token flows are better
understood as protocol-level accounting than as user-to-user payments.

References: [Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Tokenomics Context

Recycling is a supply-flow concept. It helps readers describe tokens that pass through a protocol
process and are reused or redistributed. That is different from burning, which emphasizes removing
tokens from spendable circulation.

References: [Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)

## Why the Distinction Matters

Bittensor articles often discuss emissions, staking, registration, burning, and recycling near each
other. Keeping recycling separate from burning prevents readers from treating every tokenomics sink
as destruction. Recycling is about what the protocol does with a token flow after it enters that
process.

Reference:
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Total Issuance Context

The
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
explains that recycled tokens are subtracted from the chain's record of token issuance, so the same
quantity can be emitted again. For readers, recycling names a supply-flow adjustment rather than a
permanent removal from spendable circulation.

References: [Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Registration Fee Recycling

When a hotkey registers on a subnet, part of the registration cost interacts with subnet pool
accounting rather than disappearing as a simple wallet-to-wallet payment. The official glossary
notes that alpha tokens worth the current swap value of the registration fee can be taken from the
subnet's alpha liquidity pool and recycled as part of that process.

For readers, recycling here names protocol reuse of that value inside tokenomics accounting. It is
one example of why Bittensor distinguishes recycling from ordinary transfers or from permanent
burning.

References: [Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)

## Reusable Supply Versus Destruction

Recycling and burning both move tokens through protocol processes, but they answer different supply
questions. The
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
describes recycled tokens as subtracted from the chain's record of token issuance so the same
quantity can be emitted again, while burning emphasizes removing tokens from spendable circulation.

Readers should not treat every token sink as destruction. Recycling marks flows the protocol can
return to issuance accounting, whereas burning marks flows meant to reduce spendable supply.

References:
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)

## Protocol Accounting Rather Than User Payment

Recycling language is most useful when a token move stays inside protocol accounting. Registration
fee handling, fee collection, and other documented reuse paths describe what the chain does with a
token quantity after it enters a protocol process, not a direct payment from one user wallet to
another for goods or services.

That accounting frame keeps recycling in tokenomics vocabulary. It names how Bittensor reuses or
redistributes value inside documented protocol flows rather than describing marketplace transfers.

References: [Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Relationship to Transaction Fees

Recycling and transaction fees address related but different parts of Bittensor token flow. The
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
describes collected transaction fees as recycled tokens deducted from the record of token issuance
for future emission, while
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) describes the costs the
chain attaches to state-changing actions.

For readers, transaction fees name the cost layer on submitted actions, while recycling describes
what can happen to collected fees after they enter issuance accounting.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Relationship to Burning

Recycling and burning address related but different parts of Bittensor tokenomics. The
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling) describes
protocol reuse of tokens through issuance accounting, while the
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning) describes removal
from spendable circulation.

For readers, recycling can return deducted tokens to future emission, while burning removes
transactable tokens without making that quantity available for emission again.

References: [Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Relationship to Issuance

Recycling and issuance address related but different parts of Bittensor supply accounting. The
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance) describes TAO
circulating in the network, while the
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
explains that recycled tokens are subtracted from the issuance record so the same quantity can be
emitted again.

For readers, issuance names the circulating supply record, while recycling describes a supply-flow
adjustment that can return deducted amounts to future emission.

References: [Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Recycling describes protocol reuse or redistribution of tokens. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that each
subnet has one or more incentive mechanisms.

For readers, recycling still names a network tokenomics supply flow. Subnet incentive mechanisms
distribute emissions among participants, but recycling describes issuance accounting rather than
per-mechanism miner evaluation.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)

## Relationship to Registration

Recycling and registration are related but different parts of Bittensor vocabulary. Registration
names the act of claiming a UID slot in a subnet, while recycling names what happens to the TAO cost
paid for that registration. The
[Glossary: Registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#registration) describes
registration as the process of obtaining a UID, and the
[Glossary: Recycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycle) describes recycling
as returning that paid cost to issuance rather than transferring it to another participant.

For readers, registration names the action that consumes TAO to enter a subnet, while recycling
names the issuance-accounting treatment of that consumed TAO. One is the entry event; the other is
where its cost goes.

References:
[Glossary: Registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#registration),
[Glossary: Recycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycle)

## Relationship to Conviction and Locked Stake

Recycling and conviction describe different token operations in Bittensor. Recycling is protocol
reuse or redistribution of tokens — accounting for removed tokens that can be re-emitted rather than
a user-to-user transfer — as the
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling) describes.
Conviction and locked stake, by the
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation, describe locking subnet alpha held as stake so that it builds a time-based commitment
score and the staked balance cannot be silently reduced below the locked amount.

For readers, recycling answers how the protocol accounts for and reuses removed tokens, while
conviction answers whether a participant's staked alpha has been locked to signal commitment. The
two are not interchangeable: recycling is a supply-accounting process at the protocol level, whereas
conviction is a per-position lock on staked alpha. One concerns how tokens re-enter the issuance
picture; the other concerns whether a staked position can be reduced while it remains locked.

References: [Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the term. It does not report live recycled amounts, predict token price, or give
investment advice. Readers should use current official sources when they need exact network values.

## Relationship to Coinbase

Recycling and coinbase are related but different parts of TAO supply flow. Recycling is the
protocol-level return of tokens to issuance so they can be emitted again rather than being paid
between wallets. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission,
accumulates pending emissions, and triggers Yuma Consensus rounds at epoch boundaries, while the
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling) describes the
supply-return concept.

The two sit on opposite ends of the emittable-supply cycle. Recycling returns tokens to the pool of
supply that can later be emitted; coinbase is the per-block engine that actually performs emission.
Recycled TAO replenishes what is available to emit over time, while coinbase is the recurring
process that emits. One describes tokens flowing back into the emittable pool, the other describes
the mechanism that pays new emission out of it — they are complementary parts of the same supply
cycle rather than the same operation.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For recycling,
that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about token supply flows and emission
re-availability.

In localnet, recycling mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet recycling events
and issuance changes reflect local chain history and do not represent production supply accounting.

On testnet, recycling occurs in a shared, non-production network. Testnet supply changes are
separate from mainnet token circulation.

On mainnet, recycling is a live Subtensor supply operation that subtracts tokens from the issuance
record so the same quantity can enter future emission. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes how recycled amounts re-enter the emission cycle on the production network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A recycling example from one environment should not be
read as representing supply accounting in another environment.
