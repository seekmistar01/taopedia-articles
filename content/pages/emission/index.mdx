# Emission

Emission is the process through which Bittensor generates and allocates currency to network
participants. The official
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes emission
as the flow in which TAO is injected into each subnet every block and, every tempo, is extracted by
miners, validators, stakers, and subnet creators.

## Injection and Allocation

Bittensor emission vocabulary separates currency entering subnet contexts from currency reaching
participant roles. The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)
documentation frames this as injection and distribution stages: injection describes how newly
created assets enter subnet contexts, while distribution describes how those assets reach miners,
validators, and stakers.

For readers, emission names the combined process rather than a single wallet balance or one subnet
ranking. A participant's emissions for a period describe what that role received through the
process, not a manual transfer label.

## Participant Role Context

Emission connects several role families in Bittensor vocabulary. Miners receive incentive-side
allocations inside subnets, validators receive validator-side outcomes tied to evaluation and bonds,
stakers receive staking-related allocations, and subnet creators participate in creator-side
emission context described in official tokenomics references.

This article does not rank subnets, calculate live rewards, or prescribe how any role should
participate. Those details belong in official emission documentation and live chain data.

References: [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)

## Contrast with Issuance and Halving

Emission describes ongoing generation and allocation, while issuance describes circulating TAO
supply and halving describes scheduled emission-rate reductions at supply thresholds. The
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance) entry names
circulating TAO, and halving documentation describes when network-level emission rates step down.

For readers, emission is the process term, issuance is the supply-state term, and halving is the
rate-change rule term. A statement about emissions reaching stakers is not the same as a statement
about total circulating TAO or the next halving milestone.

## EMA and Manipulation Context

The emission glossary also notes that emissions are protected from manipulation through exponential
moving average mechanisms that smooth validator-miner bond evolution and subnet price effects. That
context belongs to how emission outcomes are stabilized over time, not to a single block's raw
allocation figure.

This article does not describe EMA formulas or parameter values. It only marks that emission
outcomes are interpreted through smoothed protocol mechanisms in official documentation.

References: [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Emission reaches participants inside subnets that may run more than one incentive mechanism. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms) notes
that subnets can operate separate mechanism contexts with their own bond pools for Yuma Consensus
calculations.

For readers, emission still names the network-wide generation and allocation process. Mechanism
count belongs with subnet incentive design, not with the emission term itself.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)

## Relationship to Tempo

Emission and tempo are related but different parts of Bittensor tokenomics vocabulary. Emission
names the process that injects and allocates currency to participants, while tempo names the
block-based cadence that governs epoch transitions inside a subnet. The
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes TAO
injection every block and extraction every tempo, and the
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo) describes the
subnet-specific cadence for epochs.

For readers, emission names the currency generation and allocation process, while tempo names the
subnet periodic cadence that shapes part of that distribution rhythm.

References: [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)

## Relationship to Yuma Consensus

Emission and Yuma Consensus address related but different parts of Bittensor incentive flow.
Emission names the broader process that injects and allocates currency, while Yuma Consensus
aggregates validator weight signals into miner incentives and validator dividends inside subnets.
The [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes
network-wide generation and allocation, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes how validator
evaluations become subnet-level reward outcomes.

For readers, emission names the overall currency process, while Yuma Consensus names the subnet
aggregation step that turns validator evaluations into participant allocations.

References: [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Incentives

Emission and incentives are related but different parts of Bittensor reward vocabulary. Emission
names the full generation and allocation process, while incentives name the miner-side emission
portion received when miners provide valuable services inside a subnet. The
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes the
combined injection and distribution process, and the
[Glossary: Incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives) describes the
miner reward portion within that process.

For readers, emission is the process term for currency reaching participants, while incentives name
the miner-side share of that process inside subnet evaluation context.

References: [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Glossary: Incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives)

## Relationship to Dividends

Emission and dividends are related but different parts of Bittensor reward vocabulary. Emission
names the full generation and allocation process, while dividends name the validator-side reward
portion tied to evaluation performance and bonds inside a subnet. The
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes the
combined injection and distribution process, and the
[Glossary: Dividends](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#dividends) describes the
validator reward portion within that process.

For readers, emission is the process term for currency reaching participants, while dividends name
the validator-side share of that process inside subnet evaluation context.

References: [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Glossary: Dividends](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#dividends)

## Relationship to Validator Weights

Emission and validator weights address related but different parts of Bittensor reward vocabulary.
Emission names the full generation and allocation process, while validator weights are the
evaluation signals validators submit for miners inside a subnet before consensus resolves
participant allocations. The
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes the
combined injection and distribution process, and the
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights)
describes the per-validator evaluation inputs used by Yuma Consensus.

For readers, emission is the process term for currency reaching participants, while validator
weights name the evaluation submissions that help determine how within-subnet allocations are
resolved.

References: [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights)

## Relationship to Consensus Score

Emission and a consensus score refer to related but different parts of the Yuma Consensus path that
feeds subnet allocations. A consensus score is the stake-weighted median used to filter outlier
validator weight submissions, while emission names the broader generation and allocation process
that distributes currency to participants after consensus resolves outcomes. The
[Glossary: Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score)
describes the agreement threshold applied to validator inputs, and the
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes the
combined injection and distribution process.

For readers, a consensus score names input filtering inside consensus, while emission names the
process that distributes currency once those consensus outcomes are known.

References:
[Glossary: Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score),
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)

## Relationship to Commit Reveal

Emission and Commit Reveal address related but different parts of validator-weight processing.
Commit Reveal can conceal validator weight evaluations until a later reveal time before consensus
uses them publicly, while emission names the generation and allocation process that distributes
currency after those evaluations are processed inside subnet consensus. The
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal) documentation describes
concealed weight submission and delayed reveal, and the
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes the
combined injection and distribution process.

For readers, Commit Reveal names a visibility and timing layer around weight submission, while
emission names the allocation process that follows once consensus uses revealed validator signals.

References: [Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)

## Relationship to Validator-Miner Bonds

Emission and validator-miner bonds address related but different parts of Bittensor reward
vocabulary. Emission names the full generation and allocation process, while validator-miner bonds
describe the smoothed relationship between a validator and miners that helps shape validator-side
emission outcomes inside Yuma Consensus. The
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) describes the
combined injection and distribution process, and the
[Glossary: Validator-Miner Bonds](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-miner-bonds)
describes the bond values that connect validators to miners across epochs.

For readers, emission is the process term for currency reaching participants, while validator-miner
bonds name the smoothed relationship that helps connect evaluation to validator-side outcomes inside
consensus.

References: [Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Glossary: Validator-Miner Bonds](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-miner-bonds)

## Relationship to Coinbase

Emission and coinbase describe the same reward flow from different angles. Emission is the broad
process of generating and allocating TAO and subnet alpha to participant roles over time, while the
coinbase mechanism is the per-block engine that drives that flow. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as running every block to inject liquidity, accumulate pending
emissions, and trigger each subnet's Yuma Consensus epoch, while the
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) overview frames how those rewards
ultimately reach validators, miners, and stakers.

For readers, coinbase names the recurring block-level step that keeps value moving, while emission
names the overall outcome of that step distributed across participants. Coinbase is the upstream
engine; emission is the system-wide result it produces over many blocks.

References:
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Reader Boundary

Emission should not be read as a live reward table, a staking recommendation, or a synonym for TAO
price movement. It is a process term for how Bittensor generates and allocates currency to
participant roles over time.
