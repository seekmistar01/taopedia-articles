# Coldkey Swap

A coldkey swap migrates a Bittensor wallet's entire on-chain identity to a new coldkey key pair. The
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap) documentation
describes it as an operation that moves on-chain identity, including TAO balances and subnet
ownership, to a new cryptographic key pair, and recommends it when a coldkey may have been leaked or
compromised.

## Why a Coldkey Swap Exists

A coldkey secures a wallet's identity and assets, so a leaked coldkey threatens everything that
wallet controls. The
[Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)
guide frames the coldkey as the security-critical key behind TAO and subnet ownership, in contrast
to the operational hotkey used for day-to-day subnet work.

Coldkey swap exists for that boundary. It gives an owner a recovery path that re-homes the full
on-chain identity under a fresh coldkey instead of abandoning the wallet's registrations and
ownership.

## How a Swap Unfolds

A coldkey swap is deliberately staged rather than instant. The swap documentation describes an
initiation step in which the owner announces the swap on chain and supplies the destination address
as a published hash, which triggers a mandatory waiting period that locks the wallet against
transfers and staking.

A pending lock-out period then elapses before the swap can finalize. The documentation states this
window is currently 36,000 blocks, roughly five days, during which the swap can be disputed but not
completed. The staged sequence and its dispute path are implemented in the Subtensor
[coldkey swap source code](https://github.com/opentensor/subtensor/blob/main/pallets/subtensor/src/swap/swap_coldkey.rs).

## What Happens During the Lock Period

Once a coldkey swap is initiated, the wallet enters a restricted period before finalization. The
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap) documentation
explains that initiation publishes the destination as a hash and triggers a mandatory waiting period
during which the wallet is locked against transfers and staking.

For readers, that lock is part of the design rather than an incidental delay. The owner cannot keep
rearranging balances or stake while the migration is pending, which reduces the chance that a
compromised key continues moving assets during recovery. The lock period also gives observers time
to notice an unexpected swap announcement before the identity migration completes.

## Why the Dispute Window Exists

The documentation describes a pending lock-out period — currently about 36,000 blocks, roughly five
days — before the swap can finalize. During that window the swap can be disputed but not completed.
The Subtensor
[coldkey swap implementation](https://github.com/opentensor/subtensor/blob/main/pallets/subtensor/src/swap/swap_coldkey.rs)
implements that staged sequence and its dispute path on chain.

The dispute window matters when a swap was not authorized by the true coldkey owner. The official
coldkey swap documentation and SDK material describe disputing as an action the coldkey with an
active announcement can call, requiring the coldkey private key. If an attacker gained temporary
access and announced a migration to an attacker-controlled destination, the legitimate coldkey owner
has time to dispute the pending swap before the identity move becomes final. A coldkey swap is
therefore not an instant key handoff; it is a deliberate recovery process with a public waiting
period built in.

## Disputing Blocks the Coldkey Entirely, Pending Resolution

A dispute is not a quiet pause. The same documentation describes a disputed coldkey as completely
blocked from performing any operation while the dispute is open, and resolving that dispute requires
the [Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate)
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

That escalation path keeps the dispute mechanism from being a routine cancel button. Once a dispute
is filed, the coldkey stays frozen until the Triumvirate resolves the matter, rather than reverting
automatically to normal use.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
coldkey swap, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about on-chain identity
migration.

In local testing, a coldkey swap can be exercised inside an isolated environment to check whether
the staged migration process runs correctly. That is useful for verifying the swap flow, but it
remains isolated feedback from a local environment and does not involve real on-chain identity or
TAO balances.

On testchain, coldkey swap behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives stronger
evidence about how the staged dispute window, balance migration, and hotkey inheritance work than a
private local run, while still keeping the result separate from production mainchain identity state.

On mainchain, a coldkey swap migrates a participant's actual on-chain identity, TAO balances, and
subnet ownership to a new coldkey. The
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap) documentation
describes the swap as a staged on-chain process, so production coldkey swap context is tied to real
wallet identity on the active network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A coldkey swap observed in one environment should not be
read as identical evidence about identity migration in another environment.

For readers, this keeps coldkey swap examples from sounding stronger than their environment
supports. Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all describe the on-chain identity migration
process, but they do not carry the same interpretive weight.

## Distinction from Moving Stake

Moving stake is the atomic rearrangement of an existing delegated position from one hotkey or subnet
to another without first withdrawing to the coldkey, while a coldkey swap is the staged migration of
the entire wallet's on-chain identity — TAO balances, subnet ownership, and registrations — to a new
coldkey
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

Moving stake rearranges where a position sits; a coldkey swap changes which coldkey owns the whole
wallet without rearranging the positions themselves.

## Distinction from SS58 Encoded

An SS58 encoded value is the compact, public-key-derived address format used to identify wallets on
the Bittensor network, while a coldkey swap is the on-chain identity migration that moves a
coldkey's hotkeys and registrations to a new coldkey address
([Glossary: SS58 Encoded](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#ss58-encoded),
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

SS58 encoding names the address format itself; a coldkey swap changes which address holds the
identity, not how addresses are encoded.

## Distinction from Wallets, Coldkeys, and Hotkeys

The wallets, coldkeys, and hotkeys structure is the two-layer key model used for financial
operations and on-chain participation, while a coldkey swap is the mechanism that moves a
participant's on-chain identity — including associated hotkeys and their registrations — to a new
coldkey ([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

The key structure names the roles coldkeys and hotkeys play; a coldkey swap changes which coldkey
fills the owning role without changing the structure itself.

[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap) documentation
describes the swap as migrating the wallet's on-chain identity, including subnet ownership and the
hotkeys associated with that identity. Coldkey swap therefore moves control over existing hotkey
registrations rather than leaving them on the old coldkey.

Readers should treat hotkey inheritance as part of the identity migration concept. A finalized swap
re-homes the coldkey that governs those operational keys under the destination key pair
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

## Distinction from Hotkey Swap

Coldkey swap and hotkey swap address different security concerns and operate at different layers of
the Bittensor wallet model. A coldkey swap migrates the wallet's full on-chain identity — including
TAO balances, subnet ownerships, and registrations — to a new coldkey pair. A hotkey swap moves
subnet participation from an old hotkey to a new hotkey owned by the same coldkey
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).

Coldkey swap changes the controlling identity of the wallet — the security-critical key that owns
TAO balances and subnet ownership — while hotkey swap changes the operational key under the
unchanged controlling identity. Security documentation treats hotkey swap as the lighter path when
only the operational key may have leaked; coldkey swap is the heavier recovery path when the
security-critical coldkey itself may be compromised.

- **Coldkey swap** — full on-chain identity migration to a new coldkey pair.
- **Hotkey swap** — operational-key rotation under an unchanged coldkey owner.

Coldkey swap documentation describes migrating an existing on-chain identity to a new coldkey rather
than registering fresh subnets from zero. The destination key inherits the associations tied to the
old identity once the swap finalizes, including hotkeys and subnet ownership context
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

For readers, this is why swap vocabulary is heavier than ordinary key rotation language. It is a
deliberate identity migration with lock-out and finalization steps, not a routine hotkey update
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey)).

That migration framing also explains associated hotkeys. Documentation states that hotkeys
registered under the old coldkey move with the swapped identity, so operational keys keep their
subnet relationships under the new controlling coldkey
([Child Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators/child-hotkeys)).

A coldkey swap is not free to initiate. The documentation states the cost for a coldkey swap
transaction is 0.1 TAO, and that amount must already be available in the source coldkey at the
moment the swap is announced
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

That requirement matters for the exact scenario the feature protects against: a coldkey owner
preparing to migrate away from a key they suspect is compromised needs to confirm the source coldkey
still holds enough TAO to cover the announcement fee before counting on the swap path being
available.

## Distinction from Proxy Wallets

Coldkey swap is the on-chain identity migration that replaces a coldkey's controlling address,
moving all associated hotkeys and their registrations to a new coldkey, while a proxy wallet grants
a separate account limited, scoped authority to act on behalf of a protected wallet without changing
which coldkey controls it ([Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies),
[Coldkey Swap](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

Identity-migration vocabulary and delegated-permission vocabulary answer different security
questions. Swap vocabulary re-homes ownership; proxy vocabulary adds revocable scoped action under
unchanged ownership.

- **Coldkey swap** — permanent change of the controlling coldkey identity.
- **Proxy wallet** — revocable, scoped permission without changing ownership.

## Distinction from Transfer

A transfer moves TAO or stake value between accounts. Coldkey swap migrates the wallet's on-chain
identity, including associated hotkeys and registrations, to a new coldkey
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer)).

A transfer moves TAO between addresses. A coldkey swap re-homes the same on-chain identity under a
new coldkey the owner controls, including registrations and associated hotkeys that a plain transfer
cannot carry.

- **Transfer** — movement of TAO or stake value between accounts.
- **Coldkey swap** — staged migration of the wallet's on-chain identity to a new coldkey.

A swap is not always the right tool. The swap documentation notes that it is only necessary to
migrate a coldkey that holds subnet ownerships or registrations; a holder who only keeps and stakes
TAO does not need a swap and can instead move assets to a new coldkey through ordinary balance and
stake transfers ([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

## Distinction from Coldkey

A coldkey is the ownership key in the wallet model. Coldkey swap is the staged operation that moves
that ownership role, and the identity tied to it, to a new coldkey address
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Coldkey Swap](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

Key-role vocabulary names the owner; swap vocabulary names the migration process that replaces the
owner while preserving associated operational keys.

## Distinction from Hotkey-Coldkey Pair

Coldkey swap and the hotkey-coldkey pair address related parts of Bittensor key vocabulary. The
hotkey-coldkey pair is the ownership relationship between an operational hotkey and the coldkey that
controls it, while coldkey swap is the operation that moves that ownership to a new coldkey,
including all associated hotkeys and their subnet registrations
([Glossary: Hotkey-Coldkey Pair](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey-coldkey-pair),
[Coldkey Swap](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

A coldkey swap changes only which coldkey owns the pair. Before the swap the pair is the original
coldkey with its hotkey; after it the same hotkey keeps its registrations and on-chain positions
while the owning coldkey is the new one.

- **Hotkey-coldkey pair** — the standing ownership structure between operational and custody keys.
- **Coldkey swap** — the migration event that re-homes that ownership to a new coldkey.

## Distinction from Coinbase

A coldkey swap migrates a wallet's full on-chain identity — including TAO balances, subnet
ownerships, and all associated hotkeys — to a new coldkey, while coinbase is the per-block Subtensor
mechanism that accumulates pending emissions and triggers Yuma Consensus epoch distribution
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Coinbase documentation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)).

Identity-migration vocabulary and emission-engine vocabulary answer different questions. Coldkey
swap transfers control over hotkey-identified positions; coinbase credits emission outcomes to those
positions on its normal schedule.

- **Coldkey swap** — identity-ownership migration at the wallet layer.
- **Coinbase** — per-block emission accumulation and epoch distribution mechanism.

## Distinction from Transferring Stake

Coldkey swap and transferring stake are related but distinct parts of Bittensor stake-management
vocabulary. A coldkey swap migrates the wallet's full on-chain identity — including TAO balances,
subnet ownerships, and registrations — to a new coldkey pair. Transferring stake is the operation
that changes which coldkey owns a particular staked position while keeping that stake on the same
validator and subnet
([Staking and delegation overview: Transferring stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation#transferring-stake)).

A coldkey swap migrates the entire wallet's on-chain identity in one staged operation, while
transferring stake moves a single staked position to a different owning coldkey without changing the
validator.

- **Coldkey swap** — full wallet identity migration across balances, ownerships, and registrations.
- **Transferring stake** — targeted reassignment of one staked position's owning coldkey.

## Distinction from Seed Phrase

A coldkey swap migrates wallet identity to a new coldkey, often as a response after recovery
material is compromised, while a seed phrase is the recovery material that leak-response guidance
may require replacing
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)).

The swap is the on-chain migration action; the seed phrase is the off-chain secret whose exposure
can be the reason to perform that migration.

## Distinction from Toggle Transfer

Toggle transfer and coldkey swap both involve coldkeys but do different things. Toggle transfer is a
per-subnet permission that allows or blocks moving a staked position from one coldkey to another on
that subnet, while a coldkey swap is the identity migration that moves all of a coldkey's hotkeys to
a new coldkey address without removing their existing stake or registrations
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

One gates whether a single staked position may change coldkey on a subnet; the other migrates an
entire coldkey identity.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Coldkey swap names the staged on-chain process that migrates a wallet's identity to a new coldkey.
That custody-layer migration is separate from the tempo pass that converts validator weight
submissions into emission shares
([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Associated hotkeys and balances move with the coldkey swap workflow. Yuma still settles from weight
signals submitted under the active hotkeys on each netuid
([Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should not treat identity migration as a miner payout or validator dividend event. Credited
subnet outcomes still follow the documented weight-aggregation path after included weights are
recorded ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Coldkey swap** — staged migration of wallet identity to a new coldkey.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain mechanism that uses validator weights to determine emission shares
  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).

## Reader Boundary

Coldkey swap should not be read as a step-by-step command guide, a live parameter quote, or a
recommendation to swap any specific wallet. The lock-out length and other values can change on
chain, so current figures belong in references and live chain state. This article describes the
concept of migrating a wallet's on-chain identity to a new coldkey.

## Distinction from Coldkeys

A coldkey swap and a coldkey relate as a procedure and the key it acts on. A coldkey is the secure
key that owns a wallet's funds and stake, while a coldkey swap is the on-chain operation that
migrates everything controlled by one coldkey to a new coldkey — used to recover from exposure
without unstaking ([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Coldkey and hotkey workstation security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).

- **Coldkey** — the secure key owning a wallet's funds and stake.
- **Coldkey swap** — the on-chain operation migrating a coldkey to a new one.
