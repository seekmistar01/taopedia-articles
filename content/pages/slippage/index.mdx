# Slippage

Slippage is the gap between an expected amount and the amount received when a conversion moves
through a pool ([Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

In Bittensor, slippage can appear when staking or unstaking converts between TAO and subnet alpha
tokens through subnet pool reserves. The term describes the conversion outcome, not a separate fee
or token class.

In plain language, slippage answers what gap to expect between a pre-conversion estimate and the
amount the pool returns. It is not a fee, a penalty charge, or a signal about subnet quality
([Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Pool Conversion

Each Bittensor subnet has a TAO reserve and a subnet-specific alpha reserve. Those reserves form the
pool setting for converting between network-level TAO and subnet-level alpha tokens
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The reserve pair is part of the evidence for any slippage claim. TAO reserve and alpha reserve
describe the starting relationship, and the conversion changes that relationship as it moves through
the pool.

## Constant Product Mechanics

Slippage documentation describes subnet pools through constant-product mechanics. The reserve
relationship changes as TAO or alpha moves through the pool, so staking and unstaking pass through a
paired-reserve model rather than a fixed one-for-one conversion
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

That model separates an estimate from the resulting amount. A pre-conversion quote is tied to the
pool relationship before the action, while the received amount reflects the pool after the
conversion has moved the reserves.

## Conversion Size and Pool Liquidity

Slippage grows when the conversion is large relative to available pool liquidity. A larger movement
through thinner reserves changes the reserve relationship more, widening the difference between the
expected amount and the amount received
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The same amount can matter differently across pools. A movement that is small relative to one
reserve pair can be larger relative to another, so slippage belongs with the selected subnet, pool
liquidity, and conversion direction.

The practical question is whether the amount being converted is small or large relative to the
subnet pool being used. Smaller movements through larger reserves leave the reserve relationship
closer to its original state, narrowing the gap between expected and received.

That makes pool depth part of the reading. Slippage is not only about the requested amount; it is
about that amount relative to the reserves available in the selected subnet pool
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Staking and Unstaking

When TAO is staked into a subnet, the result is represented in that subnet's alpha-token setting.
Staking documentation describes subnet-local staking, and slippage documentation explains why the
received amount can differ from a static expectation
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The direction matters because staking moves TAO into a subnet pool and receives subnet-specific
alpha-denominated stake. The expected and received amounts are interpreted through that direction.

Unstaking uses the same pool in the other direction. Alpha-denominated stake is expressed back into
TAO terms through the subnet pool, so the received TAO amount can differ from a static expectation
for the same reserve-based reason
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Staking and unstaking therefore share one slippage concept even though the conversion direction is
opposite. Both compare an expected amount with the amount received after the pool relationship has
changed.

This keeps slippage separate from staking strategy. Staking and unstaking explain the action
direction, while slippage explains the difference between expected and received amounts after that
action moves through the pool.

## Reserve Terms

Reserve ratio describes the pool relationship between TAO reserves and alpha reserves, while
slippage describes the outcome difference when staking or unstaking moves through that relationship
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Alpha price sits beside the same reserve pair. It expresses the reserve relationship as a
TAO-per-alpha quote, while slippage explains why using the pool can produce a different received
amount after the reserve movement
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Reserve ratio, alpha price, and slippage all describe pool behavior from different angles. Reserve
ratio and alpha price describe the relationship being used; slippage describes the conversion
outcome after movement through that relationship.

The ordering matters for examples. Reserve ratio and alpha price can describe the pool before the
conversion, while slippage describes what happened to the received amount after the conversion
changed the reserve pair
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Price Protection

Price protection is adjacent to slippage because it controls how staking-related actions handle
adverse price movement. Slippage describes the received-amount difference; price protection
describes the response when movement exceeds tolerance
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The two concepts are useful together, but they answer different questions. Slippage identifies the
conversion effect. Price protection defines a response when the movement crosses a selected
tolerance.

That pairing is enough for reference prose. Slippage explains the gap; price protection explains the
tolerance response for staking and unstaking actions
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Development Stage Context

Mainnet, testnet, and localnet are separate environments for observed pool behavior
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

For slippage on a subnet such as netuid 1, that separation changes how readers should interpret
measured conversion gaps. The slippage concept can be explained across those environments, but an
observed example belongs to the network, subnet, and pool where it was measured. A local or testnet
example is not production pool evidence for netuid 1 mainnet reserves.

Specific numbers need the network, subnet, pool state, and source that produced them. The slippage
concept travels across environments; measured pool behavior belongs to one environment.

## Staking and Unstaking Move Reserves Through Pool Math

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) describes staking and
unstaking as pool conversions that shift TAO and alpha reserves through the same constant-product
relationship as swaps
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Slippage therefore applies to delegation moves that change reserve balances on a subnet such as
netuid 1, not only to explicit swap actions.

## Price Protection Applies to the Trader Who Set Limits

[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) documentation describes
limits a trader places on their own staking or unstaking order. When adverse pool movement exceeds
that tolerance, the protection response applies to the order the trader configured rather than
acting as a network-wide slippage cap
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Slippage names the conversion gap; price protection names the trader-side tolerance response on a
protected order.

## Distinction from Price Protection

Slippage names the conversion gap between expected and received amounts. Price protection names the
trader-side tolerance a caller sets on a staking or unstaking order
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Slippage explains what the pool did to the amount. Price protection explains what happens when that
movement crosses the caller's configured limit.

## Distinction from Protocol Alpha

Slippage names the outcome gap when TAO and subnet alpha move through a pool. Protocol alpha names
the subnet-specific alpha token in that conversion
([Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Use protocol alpha when the question is which subnet token is moving. Use slippage when the question
is how much the received amount differed from the estimate.

## Distinction from Staking and Unstaking

Staking and unstaking name the delegation actions that move value through subnet pools. Slippage
names the received-amount difference those moves can create
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The action direction and the pool outcome are separate questions. Staking vocabulary covers the
move; slippage vocabulary covers the conversion gap.

## Distinction from Sandwich Attack

Slippage is the ordinary price impact of trade size against pool liquidity. A sandwich attack is an
adversarial ordering pattern that wraps a visible pending trade to capture that movement
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

Pool slippage alone is not evidence of an attack. Sandwich vocabulary belongs to MEV ordering, not
to every wide conversion gap.

## Distinction from Reserve Ratio

Reserve ratio describes the TAO-to-alpha relationship in a subnet pool. Slippage describes the
received-amount gap after a conversion moves through that relationship
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Reserve ratio can describe the pool before a move. Slippage describes the outcome after the move.

When a conversion looks expensive, check whether the gap comes from pool depth (reserve ratio and
trade size) or from an added protocol charge (fees) before treating every shortfall as slippage
alone ([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Distinction from Fees

A fee is an explicit charge attached to a state-changing action. Slippage is the price impact a swap
creates as it moves the pool's TAO-to-alpha ratio
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Fees** — a posted TAO cost to complete an operation.
- **Slippage** — an execution effect that grows with trade size relative to pool depth.

A staking move can carry a posted fee and incur slippage in the same action without those two labels
naming the same cost.

## Distinction from Arbitrage

Slippage is the price impact a single conversion pays as it moves the pool's reserve balance.
Arbitrage is profit-seeking trading that acts on a price gap by buying the underpriced side and
selling the overpriced one
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Slippage names a cost any trade incurs, while arbitrage names an activity that itself pays slippage
while closing a gap. A pool showing slippage is therefore not evidence that arbitrage occurred; it
is the ordinary effect of trade size against pool liquidity
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Readers comparing costs on a subnet move should name slippage for pool impact, fees for posted
charges, and arbitrage only when the question is profit-seeking gap closure—not for every wide gap.

## Distinction from Alpha Distribution Ratio

The alpha distribution ratio and slippage both relate to a subnet pool but measure different things.
ADR is an emissions-side ratio comparing distributed alpha against pool-injected alpha, while
slippage is the difference between the expected and received amounts when a single trade moves
through the pool's reserves
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage)).

- **Alpha distribution ratio** — an emissions ratio of distributed alpha to pool-injected alpha.
- **Slippage** — the expected-versus-received gap on one trade through the pool.

## Distinction from Price Discovery

Price discovery and slippage describe the resulting price and the per-trade impact on it. Price
discovery is the emergent price from the pool's TAO-to-alpha ratio, while slippage is how far a
given stake, unstake, or swap moves that price, which is larger in a thinner pool
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Price discovery** — the emergent price from the pool's reserve ratio.
- **Slippage** — how far a single trade moves that price.

## Reader Boundary

Slippage is a pool-conversion outcome difference. It is not a staking recommendation, fixed amount,
subnet quality claim, or operations guide
([Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

The stable point is that slippage appears because Bittensor subnet activity uses pool reserves
rather than a fixed one-for-one conversion. The term belongs with conversion direction, pool
liquidity, and reserve movement.
