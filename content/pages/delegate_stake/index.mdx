# Delegate Stake

Delegate stake is the TAO a Bittensor delegate stakes themselves. It is the self-staked portion of
the delegate's staking context, not the full amount of TAO that may be associated with that delegate
after nomination
([Glossary: Delegate Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate-stake),
[Glossary: Delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate)).

The term is useful because delegate, nominator, stake, and effective stake all sit close together in
Bittensor staking vocabulary. Delegate stake keeps the focus on who supplied the TAO.

## Delegate and Nominator Boundary

A [delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate) is a validator that can
receive stake from nominators. A
[nominator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominator) stakes TAO to a validator
rather than becoming that validator
([Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Delegate stake belongs to the delegate side of that relationship. Nominated TAO can add to the
delegate's broader staking context, but it is not delegate stake because it did not come from the
delegate themselves.

## Effective Stake and Stake Weight

Delegate stake and
[effective stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#effective-stake) describe
different totals. Effective stake is the total staked TAO associated with a delegate after both
self-stake and nominated support are considered.

[Stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight) is another related
but distinct term. It is the computed stake value used for validator consensus power and emissions
inside a subnet
([Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)).

These boundaries keep delegate stake from being mistaken for every amount attached to a delegate or
for the consensus measure derived from stake context. Use delegate stake when the article needs the
validator-supplied amount; use effective stake when it needs the full backing total behind that
hotkey ([Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

## Staking Action Context

[Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) names the process of attaching
TAO support to a validator hotkey. Delegate stake names only the TAO a delegate stakes themselves
rather than nominated support from others
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

For readers, staking is action vocabulary and delegate stake is validator-supplied amount vocabulary
on the same hotkey. Effective stake and nominator vocabulary answer follow-up questions about full
backing totals and who supplied nominated support.

Delegate stake is therefore descriptive rather than advisory. It does not rank validators, quote
reward outcomes, or explain how to choose a validator. It identifies the self-supplied part of a
staking relationship.

## Yuma Stake-Weight Pipeline

Delegate stake sits upstream of subnet consensus influence. Validators submit
[validator weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights); Yuma
Consensus aggregates those signals into miner incentives and validator dividends
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

[Stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight) is the computed
stake value that shapes how strongly a validator's signals count inside that consensus pass. It is
not the same label as delegate stake: delegate stake names self-supplied TAO, while stake weight
names subnet consensus influence derived from the broader stake context behind the hotkey
([Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)).

A higher delegate-stake total does not automatically mean the same stake-weight reading, because
nominated support and subnet asset rules also feed the influence measure. Delegate stake helps
explain the validator-supplied portion of that pipeline; Yuma and stake weight explain how support
turns into consensus influence on a selected
[netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid).

## Root Subnet Self-Stake Stays in TAO

[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
documentation states that, except on the root subnet, stake held by a validator hotkey is recorded
in that subnet's [alpha tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-tokens)
units after TAO enters the subnet pool. Root subnet stake is recorded in
[TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau) instead
([Understanding Subnets: Root subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#root-subnet)).

That recording rule sits beside the delegate-stake label rather than replacing it. Delegate stake
still names TAO the delegate committed from their own balance, while the root subnet is the case
where that self-stake can remain TAO on the hotkey instead of converting into subnet alpha.

A delegate who self-stakes on both the root subnet and an alpha subnet therefore has separate
on-chain stake records with different unit labels, even though both portions count as the delegate's
own commitment in staking vocabulary.

## Each Subnet Hotkey Carries Its Own Self-Stake

The same delegation overview notes that staking is always local to a subnet and that support sent to
one validator hotkey on one subnet is independent from support sent to that validator's hotkeys on
other subnets ([Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid)).

Delegate stake follows that locality. A delegate's self-staked TAO on one subnet hotkey does not
automatically appear as delegate stake on another hotkey, even when the same coldkey owns both
positions. Each subnet relationship keeps its own self-stake total alongside its own nominated
support.

That separation matters when a delegate validates across multiple subnets. Effective stake and stake
weight may be discussed subnet by subnet, but delegate stake must also be read subnet by subnet
rather than as one network-wide self-stake figure copied across hotkeys.

## Validator Take Targets Delegated Emissions Only

[Glossary: Validator Take %](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-take-)
defines validator take as the share of emissions a validator keeps from the portion tied to
delegated stake, before the remainder returns to stakers. The glossary explicitly excludes emissions
the validator already receives in proportion to their own self-stake from that take calculation.

Delegation documentation walks through the same split in plain terms. A validator first keeps the
share of emissions matching their self-stake fraction of total stake, and take applies separately to
emissions earned on nominator support before the rest is credited back to those nominators
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Delegate stake therefore names a staking amount, not a fee schedule. Validator take describes how
delegated-emission rewards are divided after consensus, while delegate stake identifies how much of
the validator's own TAO sits in the self-stake portion that take does not re-label.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For delegate stake
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret self-stake totals,
nomination, and stake-weight context.

In localnet, delegate-stake examples can test staking wiring in an isolated environment. Local
self-stake totals reflect local chain configuration rather than production validator relationships
on the selected netuid.

On testnet, delegate stake can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet self-stake
and nomination on a selected netuid are separate from mainnet staking state
([Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

On mainnet, delegate stake concerns production validator self-stake on the active Bittensor network
for the connected subnet context.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A delegate-stake example from one environment should not
be read as representing production staking behavior on another network.

## Reader Boundary

Delegate stake names the TAO a delegate stakes themselves, not nominated support, effective stake,
stake weight, or validator-selection advice
([Glossary: Delegate Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate-stake),
[Glossary: Effective Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#effective-stake)).

Effective stake combines self-stake and nomination; stake weight is the subnet consensus influence
measure derived from stake context
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)).
