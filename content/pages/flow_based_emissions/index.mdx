# Flow-Based Emissions

Bittensor emissions include TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens. Flow-based emissions are the
subnet-level allocation model that compares net TAO movement before TAO reserve injection is
assigned across subnets. The term belongs to the injection side of emissions, before within-subnet
reward distribution
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

## Net Flow Signal

The signal compares TAO entering a subnet through staking with TAO leaving through unstaking. The
net direction of that movement is the demand signal used when subnets are compared
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The important idea is the direction of flow. Net inflow supports stronger injection-share context,
while net outflow weakens it.

That makes flow-based emissions different from a price-only model. The comparison is based on net
TAO movement into and out of subnet pools, not just a static reserve-ratio snapshot
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

This also separates flow-based emissions from alpha price and reserve ratio. Those terms describe
pool relationships; flow-based emissions describe how net TAO movement influences the injection
share assigned across subnet pools.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For flow-based
emissions on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret net flow,
reserve injection, and subnet pool state.

In localnet, flow signals and injection examples can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet
staking flow on netuid 1 reflects local configuration rather than production Dynamic TAO behavior.

On testnet, flow-based emissions can be observed in a shared non-production network. Testnet net
flow, subnet reserves, and injection outcomes on a selected netuid are separate from mainnet chain
state
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection),
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

On mainnet, flow-based emission interpretation concerns production Dynamic TAO behavior on the
active network. Observed smoothed flow and injection share depend on current staking activity and
pool state on the connected netuid.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A flow-based emission example from one environment should
not be read as representing production tokenomics behavior in another environment.

## Distinction from Dynamic TAO

Dynamic TAO is the subnet tokenomics model of per-subnet TAO and alpha reserves. Flow-based
emissions compare net TAO movement to allocate injection share across those subnets
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Dynamic-TAO vocabulary names reserve structure. Flow-based-emission vocabulary names cross-subnet
injection comparison inside that structure.

Put another way, flow-based emissions are the cross-subnet allocation logic that routes TAO
injection by subnet demand signals, while Dynamic TAO is the broader model defining the per-subnet
TAO and alpha reserve structure that logic operates inside
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Distinction from Emission

Emission names the full value-creation and distribution process. Flow-based emissions name only the
subnet-comparison step that informs TAO reserve injection before within-subnet distribution
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Emission: Injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

Emission vocabulary spans injection and distribution. Flow vocabulary stays on the injection-stage
subnet comparison.

Flow-based emissions sit upstream of the reward path subnet participants experience each tempo.
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation separates injection and
distribution: every block, TAO and subnet alpha enter each subnet's pools, and every tempo the
accumulated value is extracted through
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
([Emission: Injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

Within the injection stage, smoothed net TAO flow compares subnets before reserve injection is
assigned. That cross-subnet comparison decides injection share; it does not replace validator
evaluation or the consensus aggregation step that turns weight signals into miner incentives and
validator dividends
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

For readers on a subnet such as netuid 1, the pipeline order is: EMA-smoothed net flow ranks subnet
injection share, per-block injection adds liquidity to subnet pools, validators submit weight
signals from off-chain evaluation, and Yuma resolves those signals into tempo-bound reward outcomes.
Flow vocabulary names the cross-subnet injection comparison; Yuma vocabulary names within-subnet
allocation after injection is resolved.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Flow-based emissions compare net TAO movement across subnets to assign reserve injection share
before within-subnet reward distribution runs. Yuma Consensus is the separate tempo process on a
selected netuid that converts eligible validator weights into miner incentives and validator
dividends
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

The emission documentation separates injection from distribution: flow vocabulary ranks subnet-level
injection context, while Yuma vocabulary names consensus aggregation after validators submit weight
signals on the same netuid
([Emission: Injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

Smoothed net flow uses exponential moving average context so brief staking bursts do not dominate
subnet comparison. That smoothing step stays on the injection side and does not replace clipping,
bonding, or weight aggregation inside Yuma
([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema),
[Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

Dynamic TAO reserve structure defines per-subnet pools that receive injection. Flow-based emissions
decide how TAO injection is routed across those subnets; Yuma decides how injected value is
allocated to miners and validators on one netuid
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

When smoothed flow turns negative, injection share for a subnet can reach zero without describing
every later weight or dividend outcome inside that subnet. Yuma still names the within-subnet path
that credits roles from validator weights when distribution runs
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Flow-based emissions** — cross-subnet injection comparison from net TAO flow.
- **Yuma Consensus** — within-subnet settlement from validator weights into emission shares.

## Distinction from Incentives

Incentives name miner-side emission outcomes credited after consensus on the selected subnet.
Flow-based emissions inform how much TAO enters subnet pools before that crediting step
([Glossary: Incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives),
[Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

Incentive vocabulary belongs to credited miner rewards. Flow vocabulary belongs to upstream
injection-share comparison.

## Distinction from Reserve Ratio

Reserve ratio describes TAO and alpha balance inside a subnet pool. Flow-based emissions compare net
TAO movement across subnets to set injection share, not to replace pool-ratio vocabulary
([Glossary: Reserve Ratio](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#reserve-ratio),
[Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

Reserve-ratio vocabulary belongs to within-pool balance. Flow vocabulary belongs to cross-subnet
injection comparison.

## Distinction from Injection

Bittensor emissions can be read in two stages. Injection adds liquidity to subnet pools, while
distribution allocates value to subnet roles. Flow-based emissions belong to the injection stage,
where net TAO movement informs TAO reserve injection
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection),
[Emission: injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

That makes the term narrower than emissions as a whole. It does not rank one miner, calculate one
validator dividend, or describe an entire incentive mechanism. It sets a subnet-level
reserve-injection context.

The term therefore belongs near TAO reserve injection. Net TAO flow helps determine which subnet
contexts receive reserve injection, while later distribution logic decides how value is handled
inside each subnet.

## Distinction from Smoothing

Flow signals can be noisy, so smoothing helps prevent brief bursts from dominating subnet
comparison. Bittensor uses [exponential moving averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)
in several contexts for smoothing changing signals
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

Here, smoothing belongs to subnet comparison. It is not the final reward table inside a subnet and
not a replacement for the Yuma Consensus path that interprets validator signals.

Smoothing also keeps the term tied to subnet comparison rather than a single block's movement. The
flow signal is interpreted as part of an allocation model rather than as an isolated staking or
unstaking event.

## Distinction from Negative Flow

When smoothed net flow is negative, the emissions documentation connects that condition with a
zero-injection outcome for the affected subnet
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

Zero injection is an injection-stage result. It is not a separate penalty rule for an individual
miner, validator, or staker.

The condition is still scoped to subnet-level injection. A subnet can have a weak or zero injection
context without that sentence explaining every later scoring, weight, or distribution outcome inside
the subnet.

Zero injection is also separate from deregistration. Flow-based emission context can reduce or
remove injection for a subnet, while subnet-removal logic remains a different tokenomics and subnet
lifecycle topic
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)).

## Reader Boundary

Flow-based emissions are tokenomics vocabulary for subnet comparison. They are not a staking
tutorial, miner-ranking formula, return forecast, or complete explanation of all Bittensor
emissions. The stable point is that net TAO movement helps determine subnet-level reserve injection
before rewards are allocated inside the selected subnet
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Flow-based emissions compare subnets at the injection stage. Yuma Consensus and within-subnet
distribution explain later role-level allocation; zero injection is an injection-stage result, not a
separate penalty rule for an individual miner or validator
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)).
