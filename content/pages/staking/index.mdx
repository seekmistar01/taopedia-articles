# Staking

Staking is the process of attaching TAO support to a validator in Bittensor. The official
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) defines staking as attaching
TAO to a validator so the validator's total stake, consensus power, and dividend context can
increase.

## Validator Support

Staking is read through validator support. The
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
explains that staking, also known as delegation, supports validators because their total stake in a
subnet includes stake delegated by others. That support affects validator consensus power and
emission share.

This makes staking a participation process, not the validator role itself. The validator performs
validation; staking attaches support to that validator's subnet position.

## Relationship to Stake

Staking and stake are closely related but not identical. Staking names the process. Stake names the
associated token amount after support has been attached to a validator position. The
[Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake) entry defines stake as
the currency-token amount delegated to a validator UID in a subnet, including both self-stake and
stake delegated from others.

For readers, the distinction prevents a common collapse: staking is the action context, while stake
is the amount that becomes relevant for consensus and emissions.

## Relationship to Delegation

Official Bittensor documentation uses staking and delegation as closely related terms. The
[Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation) describes
delegation as staking TAO to a validator, and the
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
uses the same pairing.

In Taopedia, staking is the broad process name, while delegation emphasizes the support relationship
between a TAO holder and the validator receiving that support.

## Hotkey Attachment Context

The [Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) describes
staking as attaching TAO to a validator hotkey to increase the validator's total stake, consensus
power, and share of dividends. For readers, staking names the support action and its validator-side
target in subnet consensus vocabulary.

References: [Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Glossary: Nominate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominate)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Staking attaches TAO support to a validator inside a subnet. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms) notes
that each mechanism operates with its own bond pool for Yuma Consensus calculations.

For readers, staking still names validator support in one subnet market. When that subnet runs more
than one incentive mechanism, bond-pool context should be read per mechanism.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)

## Relationship to Yuma Consensus

Staking and Yuma Consensus are related but different parts of Bittensor subnet vocabulary. Staking
names the TAO that validators commit to back their weight signals in a subnet market, while Yuma
Consensus is the aggregation step that reads those validator weights and stake to compute miner
incentives and validator dividends. The
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
describes how stake gives validators influence, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes the clipping,
bonding, and emission calculation that weight those signals.

For readers, staking names the committed validator support, while Yuma Consensus names the on-chain
aggregation process that uses stake-weighted signals to distribute emissions.

References:
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Autostaking

Staking and autostaking describe the same underlying action with different triggers. Staking is the
general act of committing TAO to a validator to support subnet participation, while autostaking is a
standing instruction that performs that action automatically as rewards arrive. The
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking) documentation
describes autostaking as automatically staking a miner's emissions to a chosen validator, removing
the need for repeated manual staking, while the
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
frames the manual flow staking normally follows.

For readers, staking names the operation itself, while autostaking names an automated routing of new
emissions into that same operation. Autostaking does not create a new reward type; it simply feeds a
miner's ongoing earnings into ordinary staking without manual steps. The destination validator is
the miner's own choice, so autostaking inherits the same delegation decisions that manual staking
requires.

References: [Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)

## Relationship to Halving

Staking and halving are related but distinct parts of Bittensor tokenomics. Staking is the process
of attaching TAO to a validator to support subnet participation and earn emissions, while halving is
the supply-threshold mechanism that progressively reduces new token emission rates. The
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) describes staking as
attaching TAO to a validator hotkey, and the
[Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving) entry describes
halvings as supply-threshold events that cut emission rates at both the network scope (TAO halving)
and the subnet scope (alpha halving).

For readers, staking names the participant-side action that grows delegated alpha positions and
earns emissions, while halving names the long-run supply rule that shapes how many emissions the
network produces over time. The two are not interchangeable: staking is the act of attaching support
to a validator, halving is the policy that can slow the rate at which new alpha emissions are
produced. A halving can change the emission rate that staking interacts with; staking does not
change when halvings occur.

References: [Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving)

## Relationship to Child Hotkeys

Staking and child hotkeys both attach to a validator, but they do different things. Staking is the
act of attaching TAO support to a validator so its total stake and consensus context can increase,
as the [Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) defines it. A child
hotkey, by the [Child Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators/child-hotkeys)
documentation, is a separate key that a parent hotkey authorizes to validate on its behalf by
re-delegating a portion of the parent's stake to it, without transferring ownership of that stake.

For readers, staking answers how TAO support is added to a validator, while a child hotkey answers
which key carries out validation for a parent. The two are not interchangeable: staking increases
the TAO behind a validator, whereas a child hotkey adds no stake — it is a delegated key that
validates using stake-weight the parent re-delegates to it without giving up ownership. Staking
changes how much stake supports a validator; authorizing a child hotkey changes which key performs
the validation.

References: [Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Child Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators/child-hotkeys)

## Reader Boundary

Staking should not be read as a validator recommendation, a balance report, or a complete guide to
staking operations. Exact balances, fees, slippage, validator take, and subnet-specific behavior
depend on current official staking references.

## Relationship to Coinbase

Staking and coinbase are related but different parts of Bittensor's economics. Staking is the action
of attaching TAO to a validator so the validator's stake can increase. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission,
accumulates pending emissions, and triggers Yuma Consensus rounds at epoch boundaries, while the
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) describes the
staking action.

The connection is indirect. Staking increases the stake behind a validator, which contributes to its
stake weight and consensus influence; coinbase separately triggers the epoch and
emission-distribution process. Staking is a user action on the staking side, not something coinbase
reads directly — it shapes consensus only through the resulting stake-weight relationship. Coinbase
is the protocol process that runs the epoch cycle each block, while staking is one of the
participant actions that set up the standing stake that cycle eventually weighs.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For staking, that
sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about validator support, stake amounts, and
consensus outcomes.

In localnet, staking can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet stake amounts and validator
consensus outcomes reflect local chain configuration and do not represent production subnet state.

On testnet, staking can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet stake positions and
emission outcomes are separate from mainnet consensus state.

On mainnet, staking is a live Subtensor action that attaches real TAO to a validator hotkey and
affects production consensus weight and dividend distribution. The
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
describes the validator support mechanics that apply on the production network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A staking example from one environment should not be read
as representing stake amounts or consensus influence in another environment.
