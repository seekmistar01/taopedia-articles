# Wallets, Coldkeys, and Hotkeys

In Bittensor, a wallet represents identity and signing authority. The
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation
separates that authority across two key types:

- **Coldkey** — The security-critical key that controls TAO and stake. Use for financial
  transactions (stake/unstake/transfer). Keep offline or in a secure environment.
- **Hotkey** — The operational key used by network participants to run miners/validators, register,
  and submit weights on-chain.

## Why two keys?

- **Security separation:** Coldkey protects funds; hotkey performs day-to-day on-chain actions for
  miners/validators.
- **Operational safety:** If a hotkey is compromised on a node, cold funds remain protected.

## Signing Authority Boundary

Coldkeys and hotkeys divide signing authority inside one Bittensor wallet model. The
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation
describes the coldkey as the key that controls TAO and stake, while hotkeys handle operational roles
such as mining and validation. The distinction is about what kind of action a key can authorize, not
just where the key is stored.

A coldkey is the high-authority side of the wallet. It belongs with actions that can affect TAO,
stake, hotkey management, or ownership-sensitive wallet state. A hotkey is the operational side. It
belongs with subnet activity, validator or miner operation, registration context, and weight
submission. Both can be part of the same participant identity, but they carry different risk
profiles.

That boundary explains why a hotkey compromise and a coldkey compromise are not equivalent. A
compromised hotkey can damage operational participation or subnet reputation, but it is not the same
as exposing the coldkey that controls funds and stake. A compromised coldkey affects the ownership
layer of the wallet and can put the wallet's TAO and stake authority at risk.

The same separation keeps public identifiers and recovery material in their proper places. A wallet
address can identify a destination or account reference without granting signing authority. A seed
phrase or mnemonic belongs to recovery and custody context because it can recreate the key material
behind the wallet. Coldkey and hotkey roles sit between those ideas by explaining what the protected
key material is allowed to do.

For readers, the useful split is authority first and tooling second. BTCLI, SDKs, nodes, or wallet
applications may interact with either key role, but the role determines the kind of permission being
used. Coldkey and hotkey vocabulary therefore helps explain why some Bittensor actions belong on
secure custody paths while others belong on operational machines.

## Typical lifecycle

- Create/import wallet and generate a coldkey (store seed securely).
- Generate a hotkey for the role (mining/validating/subnet operations).
- Fund and manage stake with the coldkey; operate services using the hotkey.

## Best practices

- Store coldkey seed offline; use hardware-backed storage where possible.
- Never place coldkey on internet-exposed nodes.
- Validate you’re using official tools and documentation.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For wallets,
coldkeys, and hotkeys, that sequence matters when wallet examples are drawn from different network
environments during development or testing.

In localnet, coldkey and hotkey pairs can be generated and exercised in an isolated environment.
Local wallet material does not represent production custody or live subnet registration state.

On testnet, wallet operations can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet coldkeys,
hotkeys, and registration context are separate from mainnet wallet and stake state
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

On mainnet, coldkey and hotkey authority applies to live production wallets and subnet participation
on the connected network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A wallet example from one environment should not be read
as representing production key authority or balances in another environment.

## One Coldkey Can Own Several Hotkeys

A Bittensor wallet pairs one coldkey with one or more hotkeys. The
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation notes
that a coldkey can exist without a hotkey and can also control multiple hotkeys over time.

That many-to-one structure lets one ownership identity support separate operational keys for
different subnet roles. Each hotkey still acts under the same coldkey authority, but subnet
participation can be organized per hotkey rather than through one shared operational key.

## Coldkey Paths Without a Hotkey

Not every wallet holder needs a hotkey. Wallet guidance states that holding balance, delegating
stake, or creating a subnet can rely on a coldkey alone, while mining or validating in a subnet
requires a hotkey paired with that coldkey
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

That distinction keeps hotkey vocabulary attached to subnet operation rather than to every TAO
holder role. Staking and delegation questions can stay in coldkey custody language until a
participant also registers operational keys on subnets.

The same guidance notes that validators and miners are often identified by their hotkey public keys
even when the broader wallet identity centers on the coldkey.

## Registration Uses Hotkey Identity

Subnet registration attaches operational identity to a hotkey. The
[Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register) describes
registration as purchasing a UID slot on a subnet, while the
[Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot) states that the
UID is assigned to a hotkey when it registers as a miner or validator.

Registration therefore belongs to hotkey signing context rather than to coldkey fund custody.
Coldkey authority still matters for creating or managing hotkeys, but the registered neuron slot
itself is bound to the hotkey that entered the subnet.

## Existential Deposit Applies to Coldkey Balances

The [Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation
notes that an existential deposit is the minimum required TAO in a coldkey wallet. If the balance
falls below that deposit, the account is deactivated and the remaining TAO is destroyed, and the
documented value is 500 RAO for any Bittensor wallet.

That rule ties coldkey custody to a live balance floor rather than to hotkey subnet operation.
Readers checking whether a coldkey remains active can confirm the on-chain existential deposit
constant alongside ordinary balance checks
([Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain)).

## TAO Transfers Should Target the Coldkey Public Key

The same wallet documentation warns that TAO transfers should go to a coldkey public key, which
serves as the wallet's public address, rather than to a hotkey. Sending TAO to a hotkey is contrary
to the intended design and can result in irreversible loss of funds
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

For readers, that boundary keeps fund-receipt vocabulary on the coldkey side. Hotkeys remain
operational identities for subnet registration and weight work, and staking to a hotkey is distinct
from transferring a TAO balance to it.

This article and the coldkeys article describe the same wallet model at different granularity. This

article centers on the security-critical key that controls TAO and stake

[Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey)).

- **This article** — the combined coldkey and hotkey wallet model.
- **Coldkeys** — the high-authority custody key vocabulary.

## Distinction from Hotkeys

This article and the hotkeys article also differ in scope. This page keeps both keys in one wallet
picture, while the hotkeys article focuses on operational keys used for mining, validation, and
subnet actions ([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey)).

- **This article** — joint coldkey and hotkey roles in one wallet.
- **Hotkeys** — operational signing keys for subnet-facing work.

## Distinction from Seed Phrase

Coldkeys and hotkeys are the keys that divide signing authority, while a seed phrase is the recovery
material that can recreate them. The coldkey and hotkey authorize actions, whereas the seed phrase
belongs to recovery and custody context because it can regenerate the underlying key material
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)).

- **Coldkey / hotkey** — the keys that authorize actions in the wallet.
- **Seed phrase** — the recovery material that can recreate those keys.

## Distinction from Wallet Address

A coldkey and a wallet address name a signing key and a public identifier. The coldkey is the
high-authority key that controls TAO and stake, while the wallet address is the coldkey public key
used as the wallet's public destination for receiving TAO
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Glossary: Wallet Address](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#wallet-address)).

- **Coldkey** — the high-authority signing key controlling TAO and stake.
- **Wallet address** — the coldkey public key used to receive TAO.

## Distinction from Coldkey Swap

Coldkeys and hotkeys form the two-layer key structure used for financial operations and on-chain
participation, while coldkey swap moves a participant's on-chain identity — including associated
hotkeys and registrations — to a new coldkey without resetting those registrations
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Coldkey Swap](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

- **Coldkey / hotkey structure** — how signing authority is divided inside one wallet.
- **Coldkey swap** — migrating that on-chain identity to a new coldkey.

## Distinction from Nominator

A coldkey-hotkey wallet defines the two key roles a single participant holds, whereas a nominator
describes an account that stakes its TAO onto a validator's hotkey. The wallet model is about which
of your own keys authorizes which action, while the nominator role is about a staking relationship
pointed at another participant's hotkey rather than your own operational identity
([Glossary: Nominator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominator)).

- **Coldkey-hotkey wallet** — the paired keys that hold your own custody and operational authority.
- **Nominator** — an account that stakes its TAO onto a chosen validator's hotkey.

## Distinction from SS58 Encoding

The coldkey and hotkey are the key roles that carry signing authority, while SS58 encoding is the
compact representation format used to express the corresponding public keys as readable account
addresses. The key role describes what a key is allowed to do, whereas the SS58 form describes how
its public key is presented for use as an address
([Glossary: SS58 Encoded](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#ss58-encoded)).

- **Coldkey-hotkey wallet** — the key roles that authorize custody and operational actions.
- **SS58 encoding** — the compact format that represents those keys' public keys as addresses.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Wallets, coldkeys, and hotkeys describe how Bittensor separates custody authority from operational
signing. Coldkeys secure TAO and stake; hotkeys run subnet roles. Together they name key-role
vocabulary, not the on-chain step that turns validator weight submissions into emission shares
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Hotkeys submit operational extrinsics including weights; Yuma
Consensus aggregates those weight submissions into emission shares at epoch boundaries
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep key-role vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Coldkeys and hotkeys
answer who holds funds and who operates on subnets; Yuma Consensus answers how submitted validator
weights become emission shares in a subnet
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Wallets, coldkeys, and hotkeys** — custody and operational key roles in Bittensor.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

Coldkeys and hotkeys divide signing authority inside one wallet model. A coldkey is not a hotkey,
and a wallet address or seed phrase is not the same thing as either role
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey)).

Coldkeys authorize financial and custody-sensitive actions; hotkeys handle subnet operation such as
registration and weight submission. A hotkey compromise is not equivalent to exposing coldkey
authority over TAO and stake
([Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).
