# Collusion

Collusion is when participants coordinate to bend an outcome in their favor instead of acting
honestly. In a Bittensor subnet, for example netuid 1, the concern is colluding validators that
assign inflated weights to particular miners, or to each other, so that consensus rewards those
targets beyond what their actual work merits
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## What It Would Target

The aim of collusion is to capture more emissions than honest evaluation would allow. A group of
validators acting together might try to push the consensus result toward favored miners, steering a
larger share of rewards their way. The attack depends on the colluders being able to move the
consensus outcome by coordinating their weights
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## How Yuma Consensus Resists It

Yuma Consensus is built to blunt exactly this. The documentation describes consensus as a
stake-weighted agreement point, with weights that outlie that agreement being clipped. Weights
pushed beyond the consensus benchmark are not rewarded, so an inflated rating that departs from the
broader agreement is trimmed away rather than paid out. The clipping is meant to punish patterns
that look like collusion to favor certain miners
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Stake Behind Influence

Coordination alone is not enough, because influence over consensus is weighted by stake. The
agreement point that defines what gets clipped is itself stake-weighted, so a colluding group can
only move it if it holds enough stake to shift the weighted middle. A coalition of low-stake
validators cannot drag the consensus toward its targets, which raises the cost of mounting a
believable collusion ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For collusion on a
subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret stake-weighted consensus
and validator agreement examples.

In localnet, consensus resistance mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet stake
distributions do not represent production validator influence.

On testnet, validator weight and stake patterns can be exercised in a shared non-production network.
Testnet consensus outcomes are separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, collusion resistance concerns live production validator stake and Yuma Consensus on
subnets such as netuid 1. Observed stake distributions and consensus values depend on the selected
subnet's current chain state
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A collusion-resistance example from one environment should
not be read as representing production consensus behavior in another environment.

## Clipped Weights Lose Reward at Tempo Boundaries

[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)
states that Yuma Consensus calculates emissions within each incentive mechanism and finalizes those
results at the end of each [tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo).
Weights that exceed the stake-weighted consensus benchmark can be clipped before that distribution
step ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Collusion resistance therefore lands at payout time, not only at the moment weights are submitted.
An inflated coordinated rating must still survive clipping and tempo-bound distribution to produce
extra reward ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)).

## Clipping Trims Outlier Weight Before Rank and Emissions

Yuma Consensus documentation describes clipping excess weights that depart from the stake-weighted
agreement point before rank and incentive calculations complete. A colluding coalition may still
submit inflated scores, but weights that outlie consensus are trimmed rather than fully rewarded
([Yuma Consensus: Clipping excess weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#clipping-excess-weights),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

For readers, collusion names coordinated bias in what validators submit, while clipping names the
post-submission correction that limits how much outlier weight can influence emissions. The
coalition must therefore move enough stake-backed agreement to survive clipping, not merely agree
privately
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Glossary: Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score)).

Commit Reveal and other timing defenses add a separate layer by delaying visible weight signals,
which makes copied alignment less useful than independent evaluation
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Coalition Size Alone Does Not Equal Consensus Influence

Stake-weighted aggregation means each validator's submitted weights count in proportion to stake
behind that validator, not in proportion to headcount. Documentation therefore treats a low-stake
coalition as unable to drag the agreement point far enough to reward coordinated inflation
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

For readers, that is why collusion resistance is economic as well as algorithmic. Coordinators must
hold or influence enough stake-weighted signal for their inflated targets to survive clipping and
still matter at the tempo boundary where emissions resolve
([Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Similar-looking weight matrices therefore remain ambiguous until stake context is included
([Glossary: Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust)).

## Distinction from Sybil Attack

Collusion and a Sybil attack both try to capture more reward than honest participation would allow,
but they operate on different layers of the subnet. A Sybil actor multiplies identities at
registration, hoping that more UID slots translate into more influence, while a colluding group
coordinates validator weights toward favored miners inside an existing participant set. Identity
flooding pays per-registration burn and competes for slot ceilings; coordinated weighting must move
the stake-weighted agreement point and survive clipping
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

For readers tracing defenses, Sybil resistance is mostly economic at entry — burn cost, max allowed
UIDs, and low-stake headcount — whereas collusion resistance lands inside the weight-aggregation
pass where clipping trims inflated scores before tempo-bound emissions resolve. Similar bad outcomes
therefore require different evidence: registration history and slot pressure for Sybil reading,
stake-backed coalition size and outlier weight patterns for collusion reading
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot),
[Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight)).

- **Collusion** — multiple validators coordinating inflated weights toward chosen miners.
- **Sybil attack** — one actor registering many identities to seek leverage from numbers.

## Distinction from Weight Copying

Collusion and weight copying can both make separate validators look aligned in a weight matrix, but
the motive and mechanism differ. Collusion is validators acting together to push inflated ratings
toward chosen miners, while weight copying is validators reusing visible weight signals instead of
independently evaluating miners — alignment without a coordinating group
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Glossary: Weight Copying](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-copying)).

For readers investigating suspicious agreement, copied weights can mimic coalition behavior even
when no private coordination occurred. Collusion instead requires coordinated inflation that must
still clear stake-weighted clipping before extra reward lands at the tempo boundary. Timing defenses
such as commit reveal add a separate layer by delaying visible signals, which makes copied alignment
less useful than independent evaluation
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)).

- **Collusion** — coordinated inflation of weights toward shared targets.
- **Weight copying** — reusing another validator's visible weights without independent evaluation.

## Distinction from Validator Permit

Collusion and the validator permit sit on the same submission path but name different ideas.
Collusion is coordinated bias in how permitted validators score miners, while the validator permit
is the on-chain flag that records whether a UID may submit non-self weights that count in consensus
at all
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

For readers, holding a permit is necessary but not sufficient for a colluding coalition to matter. A
coordinated group still has to submit inflated weights, clear stake-weighted clipping, and survive
tempo-bound distribution before extra emissions arrive. A permitted validator acting alone faces the
same clipping path; collusion adds coalition stake, not a bypass around permit or clipping rules
([Yuma Consensus: Clipping excess weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#clipping-excess-weights),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Collusion** — coordinated validator bias in weight submissions.
- **Validator permit** — the on-chain right to submit non-self weights that count in consensus.

## Distinction from Kappa

Collusion and kappa meet at how much coordinated stake can move consensus, but they name different
layers. Collusion is coordinated inflation of weights toward chosen miners, while kappa is the
subnet hyperparameter that sets the stake-weighted majority ratio deciding which validator weights
count toward Yuma aggregation
([Subnet Hyperparameters: Kappa](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#kappa),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

For readers, kappa is a protocol threshold, not a coordination tactic. A colluding group must still
clear the stake-weighted majority kappa defines for its inflated weights to participate in the
consensus calculation; low-stake coordinators cannot replace that requirement simply by agreeing
privately
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## Distinction from Self-Weighting

Collusion and self-weighting both bias validator weights, but the coordination model differs.
Collusion requires multiple validators acting together toward shared targets, while self-weighting
is one validator inflating weights for entities it controls without a coordinating group
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus: Clipping excess weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#clipping-excess-weights)).

For readers, stake-backed coalition size matters for collusion resistance, whereas self-weighting
turns on whether a lone validator can move enough stake-weighted signal to survive clipping on its
own. The two patterns can look similar in a weight matrix until stake context and actor count are
included ([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Glossary: Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust)).

## Distinction from Stake Weight

Collusion and stake weight meet at how much coordinated cheating can move. Collusion is participants
coordinating to inflate weights toward chosen targets, while stake weight is the stake-derived value
that scales how much each colluder's weights count, so a colluding group sways consensus only in
proportion to its combined stake weight and stays weak without it
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Distinction from Consensus Score

Collusion and consensus score meet at the stake-weighted agreement point. Collusion is validators
coordinating to inflate weights toward chosen targets, while consensus score is the measure of how
closely a validator's weights align with that stake-weighted agreement
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Glossary: Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score)).

The two terms answer different questions. Collusion names the coordinated behavior a coalition might
attempt, while consensus score names the alignment value Yuma Consensus reads when weights that
depart from the agreement point are clipped, so an inflated colluding rating that outlies the
consensus is trimmed rather than rewarded
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Glossary: Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score)).

## Distinction from Incentive Compatibility

Collusion and incentive compatibility sit on opposite sides of the same design problem. Incentive
compatibility is the goal that honest, high-quality contribution is the most profitable choice,
while collusion is validators coordinating to inflate weights toward chosen miners so rewards flow
beyond what honest evaluation would allow
([Anatomy of an Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)).

Exploit vocabulary and design-goal vocabulary answer different questions. Collusion names
coordinated deviation that clipping and consensus alignment must make unprofitable; incentive
compatibility names the property a subnet mechanism aims for when honesty is the rational strategy
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Collusion names coordinated validator behavior intended to skew subnet consensus toward favored
miners or validators. It describes an attack pattern on weight signals, not the on-chain mechanism
that aggregates those signals into emission shares
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Yuma Consensus is the separate tempo process that reads eligible validator weights, applies stake
weighting, clipping against consensus scores, bonding, and dividend logic, then maps the result into
miner and validator emission shares
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep attack vocabulary separate from mechanism vocabulary. Collusion concerns how
validators might coordinate their submitted weights; Yuma concerns how the protocol processes those
submissions once they are on chain.

- **Collusion** — coordinated manipulation of validator weight signals.
- **Yuma Consensus** — mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.
- **Consensus score** — benchmark Yuma uses when clipping outlier weights.

## Why Collusion Targets Inputs, Not Settlement Math

Yuma Consensus documentation emphasizes clipping, bonding, and stake-weighted aggregation as
defenses against out-of-consensus weight patterns
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Collusion vocabulary belongs to the threat model around those inputs: validators attempting to move
the submitted matrix together rather than independently evaluating miners.

Settlement math still runs each tempo; collusion describes coordinated distortion of what enters
that path.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept and Bittensor's consensus-level resistance at a high level. It does
not claim collusion is impossible, quantify how much stake would be required, or describe how to
attempt it. The stake distribution and consensus values that determine how strong the resistance is
on any subnet are live chain state and change over time
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).
