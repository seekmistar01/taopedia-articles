# Autostaking

Autostaking is a Bittensor feature that automatically routes a miner's emissions into stake with a
validator selected by that miner
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

The concept belongs to reward handling. Mining produces emissions, staking puts value behind a
validator, and autostaking connects those two ideas automatically.

## Emission Routing Context

Autostaking acts on mining emissions after they are earned. The feature routes mining income into a
staking position, so the durable idea is emission routing rather than a new emissions category
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Emission names the reward flow. Autostaking names one treatment of that flow after the miner earns
it.

## Delegation Context

Autostaking uses Bittensor's existing staking and delegation vocabulary. Stake can be delegated to
validators, and a delegate is the validator-side recipient in that relationship
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate)).

Autostaking does not create a separate validator role or a separate reward type. It is an automatic
path into the same validator-staking context used elsewhere.

## Miner-Side Boundary

The feature is miner-side because miners are the role receiving the emissions being routed. A subnet
miner performs work for a subnet and can receive emissions through the subnet reward process
([Glossary: Subnet Miner](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-miner),
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)).

For readers, autostaking is not a generic name for every form of staking. It is automation for
handling newly earned miner emissions.

## Validator Destination Boundary

The destination in autostaking is a validator chosen by the miner, but the term itself is not a
validator endorsement. Autostaking names the routing mechanism; validator selection remains a
separate delegation decision
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

This distinction keeps the article from turning a concept into advice. The feature explains where
new miner emissions can go, not which validator should receive them.

## Relationship to Staking

Staking names the destination activity, while autostaking names the automation that routes new miner
emissions into that activity
([Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)).

The two terms are related but not interchangeable. Autostaking feeds staking; it is not a separate
staking category.

## Reader Boundary

Autostaking should be read as a concept for automatic emission routing. It is not an earnings
guarantee, validator recommendation, wallet guide, or instruction manual
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The stable article-level point is that miner emissions can be automatically sent into validator
staking once autostaking is in use.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Autostaking,
that sequence changes how readers should interpret miner-emission routing examples, validator
destination choices, and staking automation.

In localnet, Autostaking examples can be exercised in an isolated environment. Localnet observations
reflect local chain state and local configuration rather than production Bittensor behavior.

On testnet, Autostaking behavior can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet
examples are useful for checking interactions under shared chain conditions while staying separate
from mainnet state.

On mainnet, Autostaking describes live miner emission routing on the production Bittensor network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An example or outcome from one environment should not be
read as evidence of production behavior in another environment.

## Routing Scope

Autostaking should be read as a miner's automatic emission-routing choice, not as a manual stake
operation, a delegation, or a separate balance. The mining documentation places autostaking
alongside the rest of the miner workflow as an emissions destination setting, while the staking and
delegation overview keeps the manual staking vocabulary separate
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

For readers, the relevant boundary is what autostaking changes. It changes where miner emissions are
routed by default, while the surrounding validator, stake, and delegation vocabulary explains how
that destination behaves once the emissions arrive. A precise claim should keep the routing role
separate from the manual stake and delegation concepts that share the validator hotkey.

## Yuma Consensus and Emission Context

Mining emissions that autostaking can route are produced through the subnet's incentive and
consensus process. [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) converts
validator weight vectors into per-miner emission shares each tempo, and those shares are the source
material that autostaking then routes into a validator-staking position
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The distinction is useful because it keeps autostaking tied to earned emissions rather than to any
other form of balance movement. A miner earns an emission share because validators weighted its work
positively through consensus; autostaking acts on that earned share by moving it into a staking
position automatically.

In Bittensor's [Dynamic TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#dynamic-tao) model,
subnet emissions can involve both TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens. Autostaking routes miner
emissions into the selected validator's staking position; the exact asset type is determined by the
subnet's tokenomics context. Autostaking names the automatic routing step that follows the
consensus-derived emission
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).
