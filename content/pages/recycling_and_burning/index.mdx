# Recycling and Burning

Recycling and burning are Bittensor token-accounting terms for different kinds of removal from
ordinary circulation
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The distinction matters because both terms describe removal, but they do not describe the same
supply outcome.

## Recycling Context

The glossary describes recycled tokens as subtracted from issuance so they can be emitted again
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Transaction fees are one official example. The fee documentation describes fees as recycled rather
than permanently removed from the emission path
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

## Burning Context

The glossary describes burned tokens as remaining in issuance while unavailable for future emission
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Subnet stake burn is one context where the distinction is useful. The stake-burn documentation
describes TAO moving into a subnet reserve while corresponding alpha is removed from circulation and
burned ([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

## Issuance Boundary

Issuance gives recycling and burning their accounting meaning. The issuance glossary entry describes
TAO circulating in the network, including TAO held in subnet liquidity pools
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

Recycling affects the emission path by making removed tokens re-emittable. Burning removes tokens
without making them available for later emission.

## Mechanism Context

Removed-token claims should stay attached to the mechanism being described. Transaction fees, subnet
stake burn, and emission accounting can all involve removed tokens, but they do not imply the same
later-emission result ([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The useful question is not only whether tokens left ordinary circulation. It is whether the source
describes the removed amount as re-emittable or unavailable for future emission.

## Network Context

Bittensor documentation separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet contexts
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

A recycling or burning example should therefore keep the selected network attached when the claim
depends on observed supply accounting. A figure from one environment is not evidence for another
environment.

## Reader Boundary

The recycling-and-burning glossary supports conceptual claims about supply accounting
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

It does not by itself prove an exact amount, diagnose a specific transaction, or explain every
mechanism that can move TAO or alpha. Mechanism-specific claims need the relevant source for that
mechanism.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Recycling and
Burning, this sequence gives readers a boundary for interpreting token-flow examples and
supply-accounting notes.

Localnet examples are isolated and reflect local chain state, so they are useful for controlled
experiments rather than evidence of live Bittensor behavior. Testnet examples add shared
non-production conditions, which can reveal integration behavior without touching mainnet state.

On mainnet, Recycling and Burning examples should be read as live production token-flow behavior on
the production Bittensor network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, so outcomes from one environment should not be treated as
proof of behavior in another.
