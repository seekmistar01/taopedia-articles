# Fees

Fees are the TAO cost of network operations in Bittensor. Each state-changing action carries a fee
paid in TAO, covering the operation's inclusion in the Bittensor ledger
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Fee is cost vocabulary, not reward vocabulary. A validator dividend, miner incentive, or staking
yield is a role-facing reward. A fee is what a sender pays to complete an operation.

## Fee Scope

Fees apply to actions that change network state. Transfers, staking, unstaking, and subnet
registration are examples of fee-bearing operations
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

That scope distinguishes fees from emissions. Emission is value entering the network through
protocol allocation. A fee is a cost borne by the sender of an individual operation.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For fees, that
sequence changes how readers should interpret transaction-cost and staking-fee examples.

In localnet, fee mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet fee amounts do not
represent production network costs.

On testnet, transaction and staking fees can be exercised in a shared non-production network.
Testnet fee schedules are separate from mainnet chain state.

On mainnet, fees describe live production TAO costs for state-changing operations on the public
network. Observed amounts depend on the operation type and current network fee settings
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A fee example from one environment should not be read as
representing production transaction costs in another environment.

## Staking Operations Carry Two Fee Layers

[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) documentation separates
the cost of submitting a staking-related action from the cost tied to the liquidity being moved. A
staking, unstaking, or cross-subnet stake move therefore faces two layers: a blockchain transaction
fee based on compute and message size, and an additional liquidity fee measured as a documented
share of the transacted amount.

That split keeps fee vocabulary accurate when someone compares a simple transfer with a stake
change. The transaction fee answers what it costs to get the action recorded on
[Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor). The liquidity
fee answers what the staking path charges on the amount being converted, and guidance notes that
staking-side charges are taken from the
[Glossary: TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau) being committed while
unstaking-side charges are taken from the
[Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) being
withdrawn.

Fee guidance also notes an exception within one subnet. Moving stake between two hotkeys on the same
subnet still pays the transaction fee, but it does not add the separate liquidity swap fee that
cross-subnet moves can carry. That distinction helps explain why two stake actions can both be
called fees yet carry very different totals.

## Validator Weight Steps Are Fee-Free

[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) documentation states
that reading Bittensor chain state is always free, which is why lookups and previews do not by
themselves create a TAO fee charge. The same page also lists validator weight-setting and
concealed-weight commit and reveal work among the actions that pay no transaction fee at all.

That exception matters beside the article's general fee scope. Transfers, registrations, and staking
changes still belong to the fee-bearing group, but the consensus-facing steps validators use to
submit concealed weights and later reveal them are treated as fee-free network work rather than as
sender-paid ledger costs ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

Fee vocabulary still names TAO paid to complete a state change, yet fee rules explicitly exempt the
validator weight pipeline from that transaction-fee charge while other state-changing participation
steps remain in scope.

## Transaction Fees Combine Compute and Size

Each standard blockchain transaction fee on Bittensor combines two measured parts documented under
general transaction fees. A weight-based portion rises with the computational load of the action,
and a length-based portion adds a charge tied to the encoded size of the submitted message
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Lifecycle guidance states that this combined transaction fee is charged before the action executes,
withdrawn from the sender's
[Glossary: TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau) free balance, and
rejected immediately when that balance cannot cover the charge
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor: Example lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees#example-fees-in-the-lifecycle-of-a-transaction)).

That two-part structure explains why similar-looking operations can carry different transaction fees
without changing the separate liquidity charges that staking paths may add. Compute-heavy or larger
messages cost more to include even when the reader's goal is the same broad action type.

## Fees Are Deducted, Not Distributed as Rewards

A fee leaves the sender's balance to cover an operation, which is the opposite direction from an
emission that enters a participant's balance as a reward
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)). The collected amount can re-enter
future protocol accounting through recycling rather than disappearing permanently
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

For readers, that direction is the durable distinction. Fees describe what a sender pays to complete
a state change, while validator dividends, miner incentives, and staking yield describe value
flowing the other way. The same TAO can later be recycled, but the fee itself is always a deduction.

## Distinction from Emission

Emission adds value through protocol allocation. Fees deduct TAO from a sender to complete a
state-changing operation ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Use fees for operation cost; use emission for rewards credited to miners, validators, and stakers
after consensus and distribution steps complete
([Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

## Distinction from Recycling

A fee is the upfront cost of an operation. Recycling names what can happen to the collected amount
in later protocol accounting
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Fee vocabulary answers what the sender pays now. Recycling vocabulary answers how collected TAO may
re-enter protocol accounting later.

A collected fee can therefore re-enter Bittensor's future protocol accounting through recycling
rather than disappearing permanently from the emission picture
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Distinction from Mempool

Fees attach to state-changing actions. The mempool holds those actions while they wait for block
inclusion ([Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

A transaction can carry its fee while still pending. Inclusion in a block is when the action becomes
chain history.

## Distinction from Price Protection

A fee is the posted TAO cost of submitting an operation. Price protection is a caller-set floor on
the conversion outcome for swap-style actions such as staking and unstaking
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Both can apply to the same staking move. Fee vocabulary names the charge; price protection names the
minimum acceptable rate.

Price protection is a related setting for swap-style operations such as staking and unstaking. A
caller can set a minimum acceptable rate through price protection, separate from the fee the
operation incurs ([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

Fee and price protection serve different roles. A fee is the TAO cost of submitting an operation.
Price protection is the caller's floor on the conversion outcome. Both can apply to the same
operation without merging.

## Distinction from Slippage

A fee is an explicit charge on a state-changing action. Slippage is the price impact a swap creates
as it moves the pool's TAO-to-alpha ratio
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

- **Fees** — posted TAO cost to complete an operation.
- **Slippage** — execution effect from trade size relative to pool depth.

Staking moves can include a posted fee, a price-protection floor, and slippage from pool depth in
the same action without those labels meaning the same thing.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Fees are the TAO cost a sender pays to complete a state-changing operation on Bittensor. They name
operation cost vocabulary, not the on-chain step that turns validator weight submissions into
emission shares ([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Fee collection and recycling belong to transaction accounting;
Yuma Consensus settles scoring outcomes on a subnet
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep fee vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Fees answer what an operation
costs to submit; Yuma Consensus answers how validator weights become emission shares in a subnet
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Fees** — TAO cost of submitting a state-changing operation.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

Fees are concept vocabulary for the cost side of network operations. The term should not be read as
a validator incentive, a subnet reward, or a staking return
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Exact fee amounts depend on the network context. Mainnet, testnet, and localnet are separate
environments with different observed fee conditions
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).
