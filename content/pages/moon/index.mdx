**The Moon** is [[Earth]]'s only natural satellite and the fifth-largest moon in the Solar System.
It is a rocky, airless body whose surface records billions of years of impacts and volcanic
activity. The Moon is the brightest object in the night sky and has played a central role in
calendars, navigation, and culture throughout human history.

## Overview

The Moon formed early in Earth's history, most likely from debris produced by a giant impact between
the proto-Earth and a Mars-sized body. Its current average distance from Earth is about 384,400 km.
The same side of the Moon always faces Earth due to tidal locking.

## Orbital and Rotational Dynamics

- The Moon orbits Earth approximately every 27.3 days (sidereal month) and completes a full cycle of
  phases roughly every 29.5 days (synodic month).
- Tidal interactions between the Earth–Moon system transfer angular momentum, gradually increasing
  the Moon's orbital distance and slowing Earth's rotation over geologic time.
- The Moon's gravitational interaction with Earth produces ocean tides; spring and neap tides arise
  from the relative alignment of the Sun, Earth, and Moon.

## Surface and Composition

- The lunar surface is divided into dark basaltic plains known as maria and lighter, heavily
  cratered highlands.
- Regolith, a layer of fragmented rock and dust produced by impacts, covers the surface.
- The crust is primarily anorthositic; the mantle contains olivine and pyroxene; the small core is
  metal-rich.

## Gravity and Environment

The Moon's surface gravity is about 1.62 m/s², roughly one-sixth of Earth's. With no substantial
atmosphere or global magnetic field, surface temperatures vary widely and micrometeorite impacts are
frequent. The apparent phases of the Moon result from changing illumination geometry as it orbits
[[Earth]] and reflects sunlight.

## Exploration

- In 1959, the Soviet Luna program achieved the first flyby, impact, and far-side images.
- NASA's Apollo missions (1969–1972) performed the first crewed landings, returning rock and soil
  samples that revolutionized our understanding of lunar geology.
- Contemporary missions from multiple agencies investigate volatiles near the poles, subsurface
  structure, and potential resources for sustained exploration.

## Cultural and Scientific Significance

The Moon has shaped human culture, from mythology and art to timekeeping. Scientifically, it
provides a natural laboratory for studying planetary formation and early Solar System history. Its
role in Earth's climate stability and in generating tides links it closely to terrestrial life and
environments governed by [[Gravity]].
