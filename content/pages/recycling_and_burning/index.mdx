# Recycling and Burning

Recycling and burning are Bittensor token-accounting terms for tokens that leave ordinary spendable
circulation. The glossary distinguishes recycled tokens, which are subtracted from issuance so they
can be emitted again, from burned tokens, which remain in issuance but are not available for future
emission
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The distinction matters because both words describe removal, but not the same supply outcome. A
recycled amount can re-enter the emission path. A burned amount remains removed from effective
circulation without becoming re-emittable.

## Recycling Context

Recycling changes the issuance accounting for removed tokens. Transaction fees are a common
Bittensor example: the fee documentation says fees are recycled by deducting them from
`TotalIssuance`, making that amount available for future emission rather than treating it as a
permanent supply reduction
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

That makes recycling different from simply moving tokens out of a user's reach. The important
article-level claim is the accounting effect: recycled tokens are removed from current issuance and
can later be emitted again through the protocol's emission process.

## Burning Context

Burning also removes tokens from ordinary circulation, but the glossary gives it a different
accounting result. Burned tokens remain in the issuance record and are not returned for future
emission ([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Subnet stake burn is one Bittensor context where the distinction is useful. The stake-burn
documentation describes a mechanism where TAO moves into a subnet reserve while corresponding alpha
is removed from circulation and burned
([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

## Issuance Boundary

Issuance is the supply record that makes the distinction meaningful. The glossary describes issuance
as TAO circulating in the network, including TAO held in wallets and subnet liquidity pools
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

Recycling affects that record by subtracting removed tokens so they can be emitted again. Burning
does not make the removed tokens re-emittable. A clear supply statement should therefore say whether
it is discussing recycled amounts, burned amounts, current issuance, or effective circulation.

## Reading Supply Claims

Recycling and burning claims should stay attached to the mechanism being described. Transaction
fees, subnet stake burn, and emission accounting can all involve removed tokens, but they do not
necessarily imply the same future-emission result
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

That boundary keeps the article from treating every token sink as identical. The question is not
only whether tokens left ordinary circulation; it is whether the chain accounts for them as
re-emittable or permanently unavailable for future emission.

## Evidence Boundary

The recycling-and-burning glossary supports conceptual claims about supply accounting. It does not
by itself prove a current amount, diagnose a specific transaction, or explain every mechanism that
can move TAO or alpha tokens. Mechanism-specific claims need the relevant source for that mechanism.

For readers, the safe takeaway is to use recycling and burning as accounting labels. Recycling
points to re-emittable removal; burning points to removal without future re-emission
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
recycling and burning, that sequence determines which network's supply accounting is being
described.

In local testing, recycling and burning can be observed against a locally running chain's issuance
record. Localnet accounting reflects a user-controlled supply state and does not represent
production Bittensor TAO or alpha-token accounting.

On testchain, recycling and burning can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet
accounting helps confirm how fee recycling, alpha burning, and creator-emission burning behave under
shared chain conditions while staying separate from mainnet supply state.

On mainchain, recycling and burning describe how production Bittensor fees, subnet-creation costs,
and creator-emission burning affect the live TAO and alpha supply records. The
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) documentation explains
that transaction fees are recycled by deducting them from `TotalIssuance`, making the same quantity
available for future emission rather than permanently removing it
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A recycling or burning figure from one environment should
not be read as evidence of the same supply-accounting outcome in another environment.
