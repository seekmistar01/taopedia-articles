# Dynamic TAO (dTAO) in Bittensor

Dynamic TAO (often abbreviated as dTAO) is the evolving, integrated tokenomics and governance model
of the Bittensor network. Its goal is to decentralize how value is measured and distributed across
the ecosystem—removing centralized judgement and enabling subnets to price and incentivize the work
they need.

- **Purpose:** Replace centralized, root-level control with market-based, decentralized mechanisms
  that align incentives for miners, validators, subnet creators, and stakers.
- **Scope:** Governs emissions (TAO issuance), validator and miner incentives, and subnet-level
  autonomy.

## Key Concepts

- **Subnets as markets:** Each subnet specifies the kind of digital commodity it wants (e.g., model
  outputs, retrieval, compute). Subnet creators define incentive mechanisms and evaluation criteria.
- **Decentralized valuation:** Rather than a central actor assigning value, validators on each
  subnet evaluate miners and submit weights. Emissions flow according to these weights via
  consensus.
- **Evolving mechanism design:** Dynamic TAO is designed to be extensible—emission and governance
  mechanisms can be upgraded to better price work, reduce capture, and improve decentralization over
  time.

[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight),
[The Bittensor Standard (bittensor.com)](https://bittensor.com/content/the-bittensor-standard)

## Participants and Roles

- **Subnet creators:** Define the incentive mechanism and evaluation tasks for a subnet. They do not
  centrally pick winners; they design the rules by which validators evaluate miners.
- **Validators:** Evaluate miners’ outputs using the subnet’s mechanism and submit weights that
  determine relative emissions.
- **Miners:** Provide the requested commodity (e.g., model outputs) and compete for higher validator
  scores to earn emissions.
- **Stakers:** Delegate TAO to validators. Staking can influence validator prominence and reward
  share.

Reference: [Docs Home (learnbittensor.org)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/)

## Emissions under Dynamic TAO

Bittensor issues TAO over time. Emissions are allocated to participants based on subnet-level
evaluations and network rules.

- **Liquidity Injection to Subnets:** Newly emitted TAO is injected into subnet pools according to
  network rules.
- **Allocation via Validator Weights:** Validators score miners; emissions distribute in proportion
  to these scores (after consensus aggregation).
- **Stake and Validator Dividends:** Validators and their stakers receive a portion aligned with
  their performance and stake.

Reference: [Emission (learnbittensor.org)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Alpha Reserve and Outstanding Context

Dynamic TAO gives each subnet a market context where subnet-specific alpha can sit either inside the
subnet pool or outside that pool with participants. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes subnet pools with TAO reserves and alpha reserves, while the
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation separates alpha reserve
injection from alpha outstanding injection.

For readers, alpha reserve belongs to the pool side of Dynamic TAO accounting, while alpha
outstanding belongs to participant-side stake and emission context. That split helps keep subnet
liquidity, staking, and reward statements from being collapsed into one alpha-token balance.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Governance Trajectory

Dynamic TAO is tied to decentralizing governance over time.

- **From Foundation to Community:** Bittensor governance is designed to transition from foundation
  guidance to community-driven decision-making.
- **Bicameral legislature (in stages):** Proposals, review, and ratification mechanisms aim to
  distribute control and responsibility.

Reference: [Governance Overview (learnbittensor.org)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)

## Why Dynamic TAO Matters

- **Aligns incentives to real value:** Subnets can specialize and reward precisely the work they
  need.
- **Resists capture:** No single centralized authority sets value; validators and staking align
  incentives.
- **Supports rapid evolution:** Mechanisms (e.g., emission shares, evaluation functions) can evolve
  as the ecosystem learns.

## Common Questions

- **Is dTAO a separate token?** No. Dynamic TAO describes the policy and mechanisms governing TAO
  emissions and governance, not a separate asset.
- **Does the root network judge value?** The direction is to minimize centralized root judgement;
  valuation happens at the subnet level via validators and consensus.

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Dynamic TAO gives each subnet its own alpha token and market context. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-creator) notes that subnet
creators can configure multiple incentive mechanisms to distribute emissions across different types
of work or evaluation criteria.

For readers, Dynamic TAO still describes network-wide tokenomics and governance. Individual subnets
can run more than one incentive mechanism inside that subnet market design.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Subnet Creator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-creator)

## Relationship to Yuma Consensus

Dynamic TAO and Yuma Consensus are related but different parts of Bittensor vocabulary. Dynamic TAO
names the network-wide tokenomics design that governs how TAO and subnet alpha tokens are created
and weighted across subnets, while Yuma Consensus is the per-subnet aggregation step that turns
validator weight signals into miner incentives and validator dividends. The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes how newly
created TAO and alpha tokens are distributed across subnets, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes the clipping,
bonding, and emission calculation that runs inside each subnet.

For readers, Dynamic TAO names the cross-subnet token economics, while Yuma Consensus names the
within-subnet process that resolves validator weights into the emissions those tokenomics
distribute.

References: [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Crowdloans

Dynamic TAO and crowdloans address related aspects of subnet economics in Bittensor. Dynamic TAO is
the network-wide tokenomics design that gives each subnet its own alpha token and market context,
while crowdloans are a collective funding mechanism through which TAO holders can contribute to a
subnet's registration or ongoing funding and receive a share of emissions in return. The
[Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans) documentation describes crowdloans
as enabling community-level participation in subnet funding: contributor shares are recorded on
chain, emissions are distributed pro-rata, and the subnet's alpha emissions are swapped to TAO
before distribution, so contributors receive TAO rather than alpha tokens directly.

For readers, Dynamic TAO names the framework—subnet alpha tokens, market-based valuation, and
decentralized emissions—while crowdloans name one participation path that lets TAO holders fund
subnets without holding an operator role. Both belong to the same broader goal of decentralizing
subnet economics: Dynamic TAO creates the market structure, and crowdloans extend who can fund and
participate in that structure beyond the core miner and validator roles. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
provides the subnet-market context that frames both topics.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## Relationship to Coinbase

Dynamic TAO and coinbase are related but different parts of Bittensor network vocabulary. Dynamic
TAO is the evolving tokenomics and governance model that gives each subnet its own alpha token and
market context, decentralizing emissions and subnet valuation across the network. Coinbase is the
per-block protocol mechanism that executes the block-level work Dynamic TAO depends on: it
accumulates pending emissions each block, injects TAO and alpha into subnet pool reserves through
reserve injection, and checks each subnet's epoch boundary to trigger the Yuma Consensus round that
allocates those emissions to validators, miners, and stakers. The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes how the Dynamic
TAO emission system distributes TAO and alpha, and the
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block operation that runs those emission steps.

Dynamic TAO is the framework; coinbase is the engine that runs it each block. The subnet markets,
alpha token flows, and consensus-driven allocation that Dynamic TAO defines are realized through
coinbase's per-block operation. Dynamic TAO answers how value is measured and distributed across
subnets; coinbase answers what runs each block to advance those distribution steps.

References: [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
Dynamic TAO, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about subnet tokenomics and
governance decentralization.

In local testing, Dynamic TAO mechanics can be exercised inside an isolated environment to check
whether subnet markets, alpha tokens, and emission routing work correctly. That is useful for
verifying Dynamic TAO mechanics, but it remains isolated feedback from a local environment.

On testchain, Dynamic TAO behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives stronger
evidence about how subnet alpha token markets, emission flows, and validator stake weight interact
than a private local run, while still keeping the result separate from production mainchain
tokenomics.

On mainchain, Dynamic TAO governs live subnet economics, emission routing, and staking outcomes. The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes how Dynamic TAO
distributes TAO and alpha, so production Dynamic TAO context is tied to real subnet market activity
on the active network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. Dynamic TAO behavior observed in one environment should
not be read as identical evidence about subnet market dynamics in another environment.

For readers, this keeps Dynamic TAO examples from sounding stronger than their environment supports.
Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all describe the subnet tokenomics model, but they do
not carry the same interpretive weight.
