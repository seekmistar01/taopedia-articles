# Sybil Attack

A Sybil attack is an attempt by a single actor to gain outsized influence by creating many separate
identities that appear independent. In a Bittensor subnet, that would mean one party registering
many neurons or hotkeys hoping that more identities buy more rewards or consensus influence
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## What It Represents

The attack seeks leverage from numbers: many UIDs might hope to sway weights or capture more
emissions than a single honest participant. Bittensor weights influence by stake rather than
headcount, so low-stake identity floods carry little weight in Yuma aggregation
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Each registration also consumes TAO through a dynamic burn that rises with each successful
registration, bounded by documented `MinBurn` and `MaxBurn` parameters
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

Subnet slot ceilings such as `max_allowed_uids` further cap how many identities can coexist on one
subnet at once ([Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot)).

Examples tied to netuid 1 illustrate one subnet's Sybil-resistance path. They should not be read as
representing production registration economics on another network
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

## Distinction from Collusion

A Sybil actor multiplies identities at registration. Collusion coordinates validator weights toward
favored miners inside an existing participant set
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

- **Sybil attack** — one actor registering many identities to seek outsized influence.
- **Collusion** — validators coordinating inflated weights toward chosen miners.

## Distinction from Burn Cost

Burn cost is the TAO each registration burns. Sybil attack names the identity-flooding threat that
rising burn cost is meant to blunt
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

- **Sybil attack** — flooding a subnet with identities to seek disproportionate influence.
- **Burn cost** — per-registration TAO cost that raises the price of each new identity.

## Distinction from Register

Register is the subnet-entry process that assigns a UID slot after the registration burn. Sybil
attack names the multi-identity threat at that entry boundary
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

- **Sybil attack** — identity flooding to seek disproportionate influence.
- **Register** — controlled subnet-entry process that assigns a UID slot.

## Distinction from Self-Weighting

Self-weighting is one validator inflating weights for entities it already controls. Sybil flooding
adds identities at the registration boundary
([Yuma Consensus: Clipping excess weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#clipping-excess-weights)).

- **Sybil attack** — multiplying identities at registration to seek leverage from numbers.
- **Self-weighting** — one validator inflating weights without multiplying registrations.

## Distinction from Max Allowed UIDs

Max allowed UIDs is the hyperparameter ceiling on subnet population size. Sybil attack names the
attempt to occupy as many of those slots as possible
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

- **Sybil attack** — flooding a subnet with identities to seek disproportionate influence.
- **Max allowed UIDs** — hyperparameter ceiling on how many UIDs a subnet may hold.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Sybil attack names identity-flooding risk at registration. Yuma Consensus aggregates submitted
validator weights into emission shares each tempo
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Sybil attack** — many fake identities meant to distort incentives.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept and Bittensor's economic resistance at a high level. It does not claim
attacks are impossible or describe how to attempt one. Registration cost and stake distribution are
live chain state that vary over time
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).
