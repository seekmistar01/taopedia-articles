# Slippage

Slippage is the gap between an expected amount and the amount received when a conversion moves
through a pool ([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

In Bittensor, slippage can appear when staking or unstaking converts between TAO and subnet alpha
tokens through subnet pool reserves.

## Pool Conversion Context

Each Bittensor subnet has a TAO reserve and a subnet-specific alpha reserve. Those reserves form the
pool context for converting between network-level TAO and subnet-level alpha tokens
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Because the pool context is part of the conversion, slippage belongs with subnet liquidity pools and
alpha-token accounting rather than with a simple one-token amount view.

## Constant Product Mechanics

Slippage documentation describes subnet pools through constant-product mechanics. The reserve
relationship changes as TAO or alpha moves through the pool, so staking and unstaking pass through a
paired-reserve model rather than a fixed one-for-one conversion
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

For readers, slippage is not a separate fee label or account category. It is the received-amount
effect of converting through the pool.

## Conversion Size and Pool Liquidity

Slippage grows when the conversion is large relative to available pool liquidity. A larger movement
through thinner reserves changes the reserve relationship more, widening the difference between the
expected amount and the amount received
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

This is why slippage claims need direction and scale. Staking into alpha and unstaking back to TAO
can both show slippage through opposite conversion directions.

## Staking Direction

When TAO is staked into a subnet, the result is represented in that subnet's alpha-token context.
Staking documentation describes subnet-local staking, and slippage documentation explains why the
received amount can differ from a static expectation
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The key point is directional: staking moves TAO into a subnet pool context and receives
subnet-specific alpha-denominated stake.

## Unstaking Direction

Unstaking uses the same pool context in the other direction. Alpha-denominated stake is expressed
back into TAO terms through the subnet pool, so the received TAO amount can differ from a static
expectation for the same reserve-based reason
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

This keeps staking and unstaking slippage under one concept: both are conversion outcome
differences.

## Relationship to Reserve Ratio

Reserve ratio describes the pool relationship between TAO reserves and alpha reserves, while
slippage describes the outcome difference when staking or unstaking moves through that relationship
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Reserve ratio is the pool context. Slippage is the received-amount effect of using that context.

## Reader Boundary

Slippage should be read as a concept article term for pool-conversion outcome differences. It is not
a staking recommendation, fixed amount, subnet quality claim, or operations guide
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

The stable article-level point is that slippage appears because Bittensor subnet activity uses pool
reserves rather than a fixed one-for-one conversion.
