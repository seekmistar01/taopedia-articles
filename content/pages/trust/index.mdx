# Trust

Trust is a Bittensor consensus metric that describes how much of a miner's original validator
support remains after consensus clipping. The official glossary defines trust as the relationship
between final rank and pre-rank in Yuma Consensus.

References: [Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## What Trust Represents

Validators evaluate miner work by submitting weights. Before those signals become a consensus
result, outlier assignments can be clipped. Trust describes the effect of that clipping on the
evaluated miner: high trust means the original support was largely preserved, while lower trust
means more of that support was reduced by the consensus process.

References: [Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust),
[Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score)

## Relationship to Rank

Rank and trust are closely related but not the same concept. Rank is the consensus-adjusted
evaluation result. Trust describes how much the path from pre-rank to final rank changed during
consensus filtering. In practical reading, rank says where the miner landed after aggregation; trust
says how much clipping affected that result.

References: [Rank](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#rank),
[Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust)

## Relationship to Validator Trust

Trust is read from the evaluated miner's side of the process. Validator trust is read from the
validator's side, describing how much of a validator's submitted weight signal remains after
filtering. Keeping the two separate avoids treating trust as a general reputation score for every
participant.

References: [Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust),
[Validator Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-trust)

## Relationship to Consensus Score

Consensus score is the threshold-like agreement signal used during clipping. Trust is an outcome
metric after that filtering has affected an evaluated miner's support. The two concepts are related
because trust depends on how much validator support survives the consensus threshold.

References: [Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score),
[Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Trust is usually discussed for one incentive mechanism at a time, but subnets can also run multiple
mechanisms in parallel. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms) notes
that each mechanism operates with its own bond pool for Yuma Consensus calculations.

For readers, trust still describes how much consensus clipping affected a miner's evaluation, but
the mechanism context matters. Trust for one mechanism should be read separately when a subnet uses
more than one evaluation system.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)

## Careful Interpretation

Trust should be read inside a subnet's evaluation context. It is not a universal quality label, not
an operating instruction, and not a promise about future results. The Taopedia-level meaning is the
role it plays in explaining how consensus clipping changes miner-side evaluation.

References: [Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Subnet Deregistration

Trust and subnet deregistration address related but different levels of Bittensor removal
vocabulary. Trust is a per-miner consensus metric that describes how much of a miner's original
validator support remains after Yuma Consensus clipping inside one subnet, while subnet
deregistration removes an entire non-immune subnet from the network when the subnet limit is reached
and a new subnet registration attempts to enter, selecting the subnet with the lowest EMA of alpha
price. The [Glossary: Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust) defines trust
as the relationship between final rank and pre-rank in Yuma Consensus, and the
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes the network-level subnet removal process.

A miner's high trust value reflects strong consensus alignment inside one subnet but does not
protect that subnet from deregistration. Subnet deregistration uses the EMA of alpha price as its
selection signal — a network-level measure of subnet demand — rather than individual participant
trust or rank values. When a subnet is deregistered, every participant registered in it loses their
position simultaneously, including miners whose trust values indicated strong consensus alignment.

References: [Glossary: Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust),
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)

## Relationship to Coinbase

Trust and coinbase are related but different parts of Bittensor's consensus pipeline. Trust is a
per-neuron consensus metric describing how much of a miner's validator support remains after
consensus clipping. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission,
accumulates pending emissions, and triggers Yuma Consensus rounds at epoch boundaries, while the
[Glossary: Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust) describes the consensus
metric.

The connection is that trust is computed during the Yuma Consensus rounds coinbase triggers. When
coinbase runs a subnet's epoch round, the consensus computation produces trust values alongside the
round's other outputs; coinbase is the recurring engine that fires those rounds, not the formula
that defines trust. Trust describes a consensus-alignment outcome of the round, while coinbase
describes the per-block mechanism that drives the epoch cycle in which that outcome is produced.
