# Recycling

Recycling describes token reuse or re-emission in Bittensor supply accounting. It applies to token
flows that can return to future protocol distribution instead of being permanently removed from
spendable circulation.

## Distinction from Burning

Burning permanently removes tokens from spendable circulation. Recycling returns a deducted amount
toward future protocol emission instead
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

- **Burning** — no return path to spendable circulation.
- **Recycling** — deducted amount can be emitted again later.

The glossary pairs these two labels together because they are the contrasting outcomes for a
deducted amount. Recycling vocabulary belongs when the mechanism treats that amount as re-emittable,
such as transaction fees, while burning belongs when removal carries no return path
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Distinction from Issuance

Issuance tracks cumulative TAO created over time. Recycling adjusts that ledger when a deducted
amount is made available to be emitted again rather than treated as permanently gone
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Issuance names the supply record. Recycling names the reuse path for one deducted flow
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Distinction from Transaction Fees

A transaction fee is the cost of submitting an extrinsic. Recycling describes what happens to the
collected fee amount in protocol supply accounting after deduction
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

Fee prose answers why an amount was paid. Recycling prose answers whether that deducted amount can
return toward future emission.

## Distinction from Registration Allowed

Registration allowed is the per-subnet switch that decides whether new neurons may register.
Recycling describes the supply treatment of TAO deducted when a registration burn does occur
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

When registration allowed is off for a subnet, no registration burn can occur on that subnet.
Recycling vocabulary still belongs when explaining what happens to a burn that did occur while
registration was open
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

## Distinction from Halving

Halving cuts the per-block mint rate at documented supply thresholds. Recycling changes how quickly
issuance reaches those thresholds by returning deducted TAO toward the emission pool
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

More recycling can delay the next halving crossing. Halving vocabulary names the rate cut at the
crossing itself, not the reuse path for one deducted amount
([Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving)).

## Distinction from Burn Cost

Burn cost is the TAO a participant pays to register a neuron and claim a UID. Recycling is the
supply treatment that can return that registration payment toward future emission rather than
destroying it ([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

Burn-cost vocabulary names the payment size at registration time. Recycling vocabulary names what
happens to that payment in protocol accounting after the burn is recorded
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

## Distinction from Miner Registration

Recycling and miner registration describe the cost mechanism and the subnet-entry result. Recycling
is the mechanism by which the TAO paid to register is burned out of circulation, while miner
registration is the process that gives a miner a UID slot to take part in a subnet
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration)).

## Distinction from Block Reward

Recycling and block reward describe returned supply and new issuance. Recycling describes TAO
returned to the protocol's supply path, while block reward is the amount of new TAO the network
mints each block before any split across subnets
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Recycled TAO re-entering issuance can change how quickly the network reaches the next halving
threshold that steps the block reward down, so recycling names a return-to-supply movement while
block reward names the per-block mint rate that adds new TAO toward cumulative issuance
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

## Distinction from Maximum Supply

Recycling names the protocol reuse of deducted TAO, such as registration cost returned toward the
emission pool, while maximum supply names the protocol's 21 million TAO lifetime ceiling
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Maximum Supply](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#maximum-supply),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Reuse-path vocabulary and lifetime-cap vocabulary answer different questions. Recycling changes how
quickly issuance approaches the ceiling without raising or lowering the cap itself
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Maximum supply** — the fixed lifetime TAO ceiling issuance moves toward.
- **Recycling** — a supply-flow adjustment that changes how quickly issuance approaches that
  ceiling.

## Distinction from Bittensor Emissions

Recycling adjusts the TAO supply path so deducted amounts can re-enter future emission. Bittensor
emissions name the broader creation and routing of reward tokens into subnet pools
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Bittensor Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Recycling** — reuse of deducted TAO toward later protocol emission.
- **Bittensor emissions** — creation and routing of reward tokens into subnet distribution.

Supply-reuse vocabulary and reward-routing vocabulary sit at different layers of tokenomics
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Recycling describes how deducted TAO can return toward future protocol emission instead of leaving
spendable circulation permanently. That supply-accounting path is separate from the within-subnet
pass that converts validator weights into role shares each tempo
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Yuma Consensus aggregates submitted weights on a netuid and credits miner incentives and validator
dividends when the epoch closes. Recycling vocabulary does not replace that settlement step
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Burning and recycling sit on opposite sides of supply accounting after a deduction. Yuma sits on the
subnet evaluation side, turning weight signals into credited outcomes for miners and validators
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)).

Readers should not treat recycled supply as a direct miner payout. Credited incentives still follow
the documented weight-aggregation path on the relevant netuid
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Recycling** — supply reuse path for deducted TAO toward future emission.
- **Yuma Consensus** — tempo aggregation turning weights into role outcomes on a netuid.

## Reader Boundary

Recycling is tokenomics vocabulary, not market advice, transaction guidance, or a report of exact
network totals. Its stable meaning is that a deducted token amount remains available for future
protocol accounting rather than being treated as permanently removed
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That reading keeps recycling separate from broader supply statements. It can explain a protocol-side
outcome, but it does not predict emissions, set valuation expectations, or report a live supply
number.

## Distinction from Fees

Recycling and fees are different TAO outflows. Fees are the amounts paid to submit transactions
(extrinsics) on the network, while recycling describes registration-cost TAO that is deducted from
issuance and made available to future emission rather than charged per transaction
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

- **Fees** — per-transaction amounts paid to submit extrinsics.
- **Recycling** — registration-cost TAO returned to future emission.
