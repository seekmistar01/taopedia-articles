# Exponential Moving Averages

An exponential moving average, or EMA, is a smoothing method that blends a new measurement with a
running value from earlier steps. In Bittensor, EMAs help dampen brief fluctuations before they
affect subnet emissions, validator-miner bonds, and related consensus incentives
([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)).

The concept matters because Bittensor uses signals that can move abruptly. EMA smoothing lets those
signals respond over time without treating every short movement as equally durable
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Smoothing Method Context

EMA smoothing gives newer measurements more influence while retaining part of the earlier running
value. A larger smoothing factor reacts faster, while a smaller smoothing factor changes more slowly
([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)).

This makes EMAs useful when a process needs to respond to sustained movement without overreacting to
brief noise. The same general method can appear in more than one Bittensor context
([Glossary: Exponential Moving Average](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#exponential-moving-average-ema)).

## Subnet Flow Context

Bittensor emission uses EMA-smoothed net TAO flow when determining how emissions are allocated
across subnets. Smoothing makes allocation less sensitive to brief staking or unstaking swings
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

For readers, the important boundary is that the EMA is smoothing a flow signal. The allocation
meaning comes from the emission process, while the EMA describes how the signal is dampened over
time
([Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)).

## Bond Smoothing Context

EMAs also appear inside [[yuma_consensus|Yuma Consensus]] through validator-miner bond smoothing. A
bond should not jump entirely from one evaluation step, so smoothing lets the relationship evolve
gradually
([Glossary: Validator-Miner Bonds](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-miner-bonds)).

That puts EMA vocabulary inside the bond path rather than inside the submitted weight itself.
Validator weights are evaluation inputs; EMAs help smooth bond state that evolves from those inputs
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Liquid Alpha Context

Liquid alpha, also called consensus-based weights, changes the EMA smoothing factor for
validator-miner bonds according to consensus alignment. It is a per-pair adjustment inside the
bond-smoothing path
([Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights)).

This makes liquid alpha a specific use of EMA behavior rather than a separate smoothing method.
Exponential moving average names the general technique; liquid alpha names how the smoothing factor
can vary in Bittensor consensus
([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)).

## Multiple Mechanisms Context

When a subnet uses multiple incentive mechanisms, EMA smoothing should be read inside the mechanism
context being discussed. A bond pool for one mechanism is separate from another mechanism's bond
context
([Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets)).

This keeps EMA statements from becoming too broad. The smoothing method may be the same, but the
signal being smoothed and the mechanism using that signal still matter
([Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)).

## Reader Boundary

An EMA claim should identify what is being smoothed. In Bittensor, EMA language can refer to subnet
flow, validator-miner bonds, or another reward-related signal, and those uses are not
interchangeable ([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)).

The stable article-level point is the smoothing technique itself: an EMA blends new measurements
with prior history so Bittensor processes can respond to sustained changes without treating every
short movement as final ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For exponential
moving averages, this distinction is worth noting because EMA values accumulate chain history rather
than representing a fixed setting. A smoothed bond or emission signal is a product of the specific
sequence of weight submissions and emission rounds that have occurred on that chain.

In localnet, EMA mechanics can be exercised with direct control over inputs. Smoothed bond and
emission values reflect that local activity and do not transfer to other environments.

On testnet, smoothed values develop from testnet validator and miner activity. Because testnet
participants and history differ from mainnet, testnet EMA outputs are not representative of mainnet
bond state for the same subnet.

On mainnet, exponential moving averages are the live smoothing mechanism shaping validator-miner
bonds and emission signals in production Bittensor subnet operations. The
[Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema) documentation describes the
EMA mechanics in effect on the production network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates these environments. An EMA example should be read as a product of the chain history where
it was observed, not as a value that represents the same signal in a different environment.
