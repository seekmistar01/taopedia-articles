# Flow-Based Emissions

Flow-based emissions are Bittensor's model for allocating subnet-level emission injection from TAO
flow activity. The official
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)
documentation describes the model as using net TAO inflows from staking and unstaking activity
rather than relying on subnet token prices.

## Injection Context

Emission has two useful reading levels: injection into subnet liquidity contexts and later
distribution to participants. Flow-based emissions belong to the injection level. They describe how
the network decides a subnet's share of TAO reserve injection before the accumulated subnet rewards
are distributed through the participant-facing emission process.

This makes the term narrower than Bittensor emissions as a whole. It is about comparing subnets for
the injection stage, not about deciding a particular miner's incentive, a validator's dividend, or a
staker's final payout.

## Net Flow Signal

The flow-based model reads subnet demand through net TAO movement. The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection) documentation
frames the relevant signal as TAO flowing into a subnet through staking minus TAO flowing out
through unstaking. The docs also describe smoothing this signal with an exponential moving average
so short-term movement is not treated as the whole subnet picture, and the official
[Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema) explainer gives the broader
Bittensor context for EMA smoothing.

For readers, the important idea is direction rather than exact math: sustained net inflow supports
the subnet's emission-share context, while sustained net outflow weakens it.

## Difference from Price-Based Allocation

Flow-based emissions should not be confused with a price-only allocation model. The official
emission docs distinguish the flow-based model from the previous price-based approach, where subnet
emissions were tied to smoothed token prices. In the flow-based model, subnet allocation is framed
around user flow into and out of subnet staking contexts.

This distinction matters because a subnet token's price and a subnet's net TAO flow are different
signals. A price can move for market reasons, while flow-based emissions focus on whether TAO is
moving into or out of the subnet's staking context over time.

## Relationship to Distribution

After subnet-level injection, emissions still have to be distributed to network participants. The
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution) section
places miner, validator, staker, and subnet-owner outcomes in that later distribution flow.
Flow-based emissions do not replace Yuma Consensus or validator/staker distribution rules; they set
the subnet-level injection context those later mechanisms operate within.

## Participant Distribution Handoff

Subnet-level injection is only the first stage of Bittensor emissions. The official
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution) documentation
describes a later participant-facing flow where miner incentives, validator dividends, and staker
outcomes are allocated after injection context is resolved.

For readers, flow-based emissions explain how subnets compare for injection share, while Yuma
Consensus and distribution rules explain how that injected context becomes participant rewards.

References: [Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## EMA Smoothing Context

Flow-based allocation reads net TAO movement through a smoothed signal rather than a single burst of
staking or unstaking activity. The official
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection) documentation
describes smoothing net flow with an exponential moving average, and the
[Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema) page explains the broader
Bittensor context for EMA-based smoothing.

For readers, EMA smoothing belongs to the flow signal stage, not to participant payout tables.

References:
[Emission: TAO reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection),
[Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)

## Relationship to Bittensor Emissions

Flow-based emissions and Bittensor emissions address related but different parts of the emission
process. [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) describes how newly created TAO
and subnet alpha tokens enter subnet and participant contexts, while flow-based emissions name one
subnet-level injection model based on net TAO movement.

For readers, Bittensor emissions is the broader distribution topic, while flow-based emissions names
the subnet injection comparison model before participant distribution.

References: [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Flow-Based Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#flow-based-emissions)

## Relationship to Root Claims

Flow-based emissions and root claims address related but different parts of subnet flow accounting.
Flow-based emissions compare subnets using net TAO movement, while the
[Root Claim Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims) describes
how alpha dividends are handled when stake is placed through the Root Subnet.

For readers, flow-based emissions name subnet injection comparison, while root claims name dividend
handling that the official docs treat separately from subnet TAO outflows.

References:
[Root Claim Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Flow-based emissions compare subnets for TAO injection share. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that each
subnet has one or more incentive mechanisms.

For readers, flow-based emissions still describe subnet-level injection before participant
distribution. A subnet may run more than one incentive mechanism after injection reaches that subnet
market.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)

## Relationship to Subnet Weights

Flow-based emissions and subnet weights address related but different parts of Bittensor
cross-subnet tokenomics. Flow-based emissions name the injection model that compares subnets using
net TAO movement, while subnet weights name the relative emission importance assigned through that
flow signal. The
[Emission: Distribution across subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)
documentation describes the flow-based model, and the
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)
describes relative emission importance through net TAO flows.

For readers, flow-based emissions answer how subnets are compared for injection share, while subnet
weights answer the allocation measure built from that comparison.

References:
[Emission: Distribution across subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets),
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)

## Relationship to Exponential Moving Averages

Flow-based emissions and exponential moving averages address related but different parts of emission
signal processing. Flow-based emissions read subnet demand through net TAO movement, while
exponential moving averages smooth that flow signal so brief staking swings do not dominate
cross-subnet allocation. The
[Emission: TAO reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)
documentation describes EMA smoothing of net flow, and the
[Glossary: Exponential Moving Average (EMA)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#exponential-moving-average-ema)
describes the smoothing method applied to noisy measurements over time.

For readers, flow-based emissions name the flow comparison model, while exponential moving averages
name the smoothing technique applied to that flow signal before allocation.

References:
[Emission: TAO reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection),
[Glossary: Exponential Moving Average (EMA)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#exponential-moving-average-ema)

## Relationship to Yuma Consensus

Flow-based emissions and Yuma Consensus address related but different stages of Bittensor emission
flow. Flow-based emissions set subnet-level injection context from net TAO movement, while Yuma
Consensus aggregates validator evaluations into miner incentives and validator dividends after that
injection context is established. The
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution) documentation
places participant distribution after injection, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes within-subnet
aggregation of validator weight signals.

For readers, flow-based emissions belong to cross-subnet injection comparison, while Yuma Consensus
belongs to within-subnet evaluation aggregation.

References: [Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Netuid

Flow-based emissions and a netuid address related but different parts of Bittensor subnet
vocabulary. A netuid identifies a subnet on the network, while flow-based emissions describe how
subnets are compared for TAO injection share across the network. The
[Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid) places netuid at the
subnet level, and the
[Emission: Distribution across subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)
documentation describes cross-subnet allocation from net TAO flow.

For readers, a netuid answers which subnet is being discussed, while flow-based emissions answer how
identified subnets compare for injection share under the flow-based model.

References: [Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid),
[Emission: Distribution across subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)

## Reader Boundary

Flow-based emissions are not a live subnet ranking, an earnings quote, or a staking instruction.
They are a tokenomics concept for reading how subnet emission injection is allocated from net TAO
flow context. Exact formulas, parameters, current settings, and implementation details should be
checked in the official Bittensor documentation.
