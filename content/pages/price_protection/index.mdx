# Price Protection

Price protection is Bittensor's guardrail for adverse price movement during staking-related
activity. It is most relevant around staking and unstaking through subnet pools
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The concept sits between pool mechanics and price-movement handling. Slippage explains why a
received amount can differ from an expectation; price protection defines how much adverse movement a
staking or unstaking action is allowed to tolerate.

## What It Does

Price protection applies to adverse price movement while staking or unstaking through subnet pools.
It checks movement against a selected tolerance, then determines whether the staking or unstaking
action continues ([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The term is not a general promise that a pool price will stay fixed. It describes how Bittensor
handles price movement when a staking or unstaking action reaches the pool.

This makes the concept practical but narrow. Price protection can limit adverse movement handling,
but it does not remove slippage, quote a final amount, or replace broader staking and fee references
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

## Strict, Partial, and Unsafe

Price protection has three choices: strict, partial, and unsafe. Strict protection rejects the
action when price movement exceeds tolerance. Partial protection limits the action to the part that
remains within tolerance. Unsafe proceeds without that protection
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

Those choices describe outcome handling. They are not separate staking strategies and they do not
change the meaning of slippage; they determine how the action responds after adverse movement is
measured ([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Tolerance and Outcomes

Tolerance is the allowed adverse movement for the action. Slippage explains why pool conversions can
produce a different received amount, while price protection explains how the action reacts when that
difference is outside tolerance
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

That distinction keeps the page focused on the useful reading: price protection is about reaction to
adverse pool movement, not a guarantee that the received amount will match an initial expectation
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Pending Pool Movement

Pool conditions can change while a staking or unstaking action is pending or being processed. Price
protection compares movement across that interval rather than assuming the initial pool state is
unchanged ([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Concrete examples need the staking direction, selected tolerance, protection choice, pool state, and
network where the action occurs. Without those details, a numeric price-protection example can be
too broad for pool-specific behavior.

## Price Protection Limits Losses But Does Not Hide Pending Trades

[Managing Your Stakes](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/managing-stake-sdk)
documentation states that price protection caps how much adverse price movement a staking or
unstaking action will accept. The same guidance states that price protection does not prevent
[sandwich attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)
entirely, while [MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield) hides sensitive
pending transaction contents until block inclusion.

Price protection therefore reacts when movement is measured at execution time. It does not stop
another party from seeing a pending stake or unstake in the
[mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool) and ordering around it
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Staking In and Unstaking Out Can Both Use Price Protection

The [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) applies to adverse
price movement during staking-related activity through subnet pools. The
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) and
[Glossary: Unstaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#unstaking) entries both
note that Bittensor provides price-protection mechanisms for those conversions.

Whether TAO moves into a subnet pool or alpha moves back out, price protection names how much
measured movement the action will tolerate once the pool shift is known
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Slippage, Tolerance, and Protection Mode in One Staking Pipeline

On a subnet such as netuid 1, staking or unstaking moves value through paired TAO and alpha
reserves, which can produce [slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage)
between expected and received amounts
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

[Price protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) compares that measured
movement against a selected tolerance, then applies strict, partial, or unsafe handling. A pending
stake or unstake may still be visible in the
[mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool) before inclusion, which is why
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield) addresses a different part of the
risk area than tolerance handling at execution time.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For price
protection on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how pool-movement examples should be
read.

In localnet, price protection can be tested in isolation on netuid 1. Localnet tolerance outcomes
reflect that test chain's pool state rather than production staking behavior.

On testnet, price protection on netuid 1 applies in a shared non-production network where reserve
ratios and pending pool movement are separate from mainnet.

On mainnet, price protection on netuid 1 concerns live production staking and unstaking conversions
through the selected subnet pool
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. Measured price-protection outcomes from one environment
should not be read as pool behavior on another network.

## Partial Protection Fills What Tolerance Allows

Among the three protection choices, partial is the one that keeps part of an action rather than
rejecting it outright. Documentation describes partial protection as limiting the action to the
portion that remains within tolerance when measured movement exceeds the selected limit
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

For readers, this is the middle ground between strict and unsafe. Strict rejects the whole action
when movement is too large, while partial lets the within-tolerance portion proceed and drops only
the excess ([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

That behavior is why tolerance and protection mode are read together. Slippage explains why the
received amount can differ from an expectation, while the protection choice explains whether the
action is rejected, partially filled, or allowed to proceed once that difference is measured
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Distinction from Front-Running

Price protection and front-running sit on opposite sides of the same pool conversion. Front-running
names the ordering tactic that observes a pending swap and acts ahead to profit from the price move
it will cause, while price protection names the tolerance the trader sets so a staking or unstaking
action fails or only partially fills when measured pool movement exceeds the selected limit
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

- **Front-running** — ordering ahead of a visible pending swap to profit from its price move.
- **Price protection** — the trader-set tolerance that bounds how much movement the action accepts.

Price protection does not detect an attacker; it limits how far execution may move on the trader's
own order. Front-running vocabulary names the extraction pattern that pending visibility and
pool-moving conversions can enable
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Distinction from Slippage

Slippage and price protection answer different questions during pool conversions. Slippage names the
gap between expected and received amounts as a pool move executes, while price protection names the
tolerance the trader sets for how much adverse movement a staking or unstaking action will accept
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage)).

- **Slippage** — the conversion gap between expected and received amounts.
- **Price protection** — the trader-side tolerance response when movement exceeds the selected
  limit.

Slippage can occur on any pool conversion. Price protection explains what happens to the trader's
own action when measured movement crosses that configured tolerance, whether the action is rejected,
partially filled, or allowed to proceed under the selected protection mode
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

Network fees are a separate cost from this tolerance: fee references explain network transaction
costs, while price protection only bounds adverse pool-price movement on the trader's own action
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

## Distinction from MEV Shield

Price protection and MEV Shield address different parts of the same safety area. MEV Shield hides
sensitive pending transaction contents before block inclusion, while price protection sets how much
adverse pool movement a staking or unstaking action will tolerate once execution is measured
([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

- **MEV Shield** — pending encryption for eligible coldkey-signed submissions.
- **Price protection** — tolerance limits on adverse movement during pool conversions.

MEV Shield limits what observers can read while a transaction waits. Price protection limits how far
execution may move on the trader's own order after that movement is measured
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Distinction from Mempool

Price protection sets bounds on acceptable execution rates during staking-related pool conversions.
The mempool is the holding area where submitted transactions wait before block inclusion
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool)).

- **Price protection** — trader-side tolerance on adverse pool movement during conversion.
- **Mempool** — pending transactions awaiting inclusion before execution is measured.

A pending stake can be visible in the mempool while price protection addresses how much measured
movement the action will tolerate once the pool shift is known
([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

## Distinction from Impermanent Loss

Impermanent loss and price protection both concern pool price movement, but from opposite roles.
Impermanent loss is the drift risk a liquidity provider carries while TAO and alpha sit in the pool
and the ratio rebalances against simply holding. Price protection is the slippage tolerance a trader
sets on a single swap; it can bound execution cost for that trade but does not offset a provider's
ongoing pool drift
([Understand Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Impermanent loss** — a provider's ongoing hold-versus-pool drift risk.
- **Price protection** — a trader's per-swap tolerance bounding execution cost.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Price protection is a fee-related mechanism that limits how far execution can move from an expected
price during certain operations. It names transaction protection vocabulary, not the on-chain step
that turns validator weight submissions into emission shares
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Price protection guards individual operation outcomes; Yuma
Consensus settles scoring outcomes on a subnet
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep price-protection vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Price protection
answers how far an operation may deviate from an expected price; Yuma Consensus answers how
validator weights become emission shares in a subnet
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Price protection** — fee mechanism limiting execution price deviation.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

Price protection does not name a fixed pool price, a slippage calculator, MEV Shield, or a guarantee
that the received amount will match an initial expectation
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Use the term for how a staking or unstaking action reacts when measured pool movement exceeds the
trader's selected tolerance on their own order.
