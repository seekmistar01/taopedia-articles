# Alpha Tokens

Alpha tokens are subnet-specific assets in Bittensor. They are separate from TAO and give each
subnet its own tokenomics context for emissions, staking, and liquidity pools
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The term is broad alpha-side vocabulary. It names the subnet-specific asset family rather than one
pool reserve, one price, or one staking action.

## Subnet Asset

An alpha token represents value inside a specific subnet rather than across the whole Bittensor
network. TAO remains the network asset, while alpha tokens belong to subnet-level tokenomics
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

This keeps TAO and alpha-token language separate. A TAO supply or emission statement is not
automatically a statement about a subnet's alpha-token context.

The subnet label is part of the asset context. Two subnets can both use alpha-token vocabulary
without sharing the same pool, reserve, staking, or emission setting.

## Emissions and Pools

Bittensor emission uses both TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens. TAO enters subnet contexts, while
alpha tokens appear in subnet-level reward and reserve flows
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)).

Subnets use paired TAO and alpha reserve contexts. Each subnet can be read as an automated market
maker with TAO and subnet-specific alpha reserves
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

That means alpha tokens are not only reward vocabulary. They are also part of the subnet pool design
that supports Dynamic TAO tokenomics. TAO is network-scoped; alpha tokens are subnet-scoped assets
used inside each subnet's emission and pool context.

## Reserve and Outstanding Context

Alpha reserve is related but narrower than alpha tokens. It points to the pool-held side of a
subnet's alpha context, while alpha tokens refer to the subnet-specific asset more broadly
([Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Alpha outstanding is another narrower term. It refers to alpha outside the reserve pool, while alpha
reserve refers to alpha inside the pool
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Together, reserve and outstanding vocabulary split where subnet alpha sits. Alpha-token vocabulary
stays wider: it names the asset that can appear in either context.

## Staking and Delegation

Outside the root subnet, stake can be represented in a subnet's alpha units. Delegation places
subnet alpha in staking context, while slippage explains why pool conditions matter when TAO and
alpha interact ([Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

This keeps alpha-token staking language scoped. It describes subnet-denominated stake context, not
TAO itself.

When TAO and alpha interact through a pool, the pool context matters for how the relationship is
read. Alpha-token vocabulary should stay attached to the subnet and pool setting being discussed.

## Halving and Price Context

Alpha tokens also have subnet-level halving context. Halving mechanisms distinguish network-level
TAO halving from subnet-level alpha halving
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Alpha Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-halving)).

That distinction matters because the word "halving" can apply at different asset scopes. A TAO
halving statement and an alpha halving statement should identify the asset and subnet context.

Alpha price is another narrower alpha-side term. It is the TAO-denominated quote derived from a
subnet's TAO reserve and alpha reserve, while alpha tokens refer to the asset being priced
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Reader Boundary

Alpha tokens are the right term when the focus is the subnet-specific asset. If the focus is
network-scoped TAO, TAO is more precise. If the focus is the pool-held side of the subnet asset,
alpha reserve is more precise. If the focus is cross-subnet emission share, subnet weights are more
precise
([Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Those distinctions keep alpha tokens focused on subnet asset vocabulary, not a staking walkthrough,
pool calculation, or full explanation of Dynamic TAO.

The term also should not be used as a shortcut for every alpha-side concept. Alpha price, alpha
reserve, alpha outstanding, and alpha halving each name a more specific part of the alpha-token
system.

## Environment Boundary

Bittensor separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet environments. Alpha-token examples from one
environment should not be treated as evidence for another because subnet pools, reserve values, and
staking conditions can differ
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).

Localnet examples are isolated development examples. Testnet examples are shared non-production
examples. Mainnet alpha-token interpretation concerns production Dynamic TAO behavior.
