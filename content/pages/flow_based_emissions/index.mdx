# Flow-Based Emissions

Flow-based emissions are the subnet-level allocation model that compares net TAO movement for TAO
reserve injection before reward allocation happens inside a subnet
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

This article scopes the term to TAO reserve injection. The later emission path can include
subnet-specific alpha-token and reward allocation after the subnet-level injection context is set
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Injection Context

Emission has two useful reading levels: subnet-level injection and later reward allocation inside
the subnet. Flow-based emissions belong to the first level because they compare subnets for TAO
reserve injection
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

That makes the term narrower than Bittensor emissions as a whole. Flow-based emissions do not decide
one miner's incentive or one validator's dividend; they set the subnet-level context those later
steps operate within ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Net Flow and Smoothing

The flow signal compares TAO entering a subnet through staking with TAO leaving through unstaking.
The net direction of that movement is the demand signal used for subnet comparison
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

For readers, the key idea is direction rather than exact arithmetic: sustained net inflow supports a
subnet's injection-share context, while sustained net outflow weakens it
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

The flow signal is smoothed so that a brief burst of staking or unstaking does not dominate
cross-subnet allocation. Bittensor uses exponential moving averages in several contexts for this
kind of noisy-signal smoothing
([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)).

In this article, EMA context belongs to the flow comparison stage. It does not describe the final
reward table inside a subnet
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

## Negative Flow Context

The emissions documentation describes a zero-injection outcome for subnets whose smoothed net flow
is negative. That outcome is part of subnet comparison before later reward allocation
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

Zero injection should therefore be read as an injection-stage result, not as a separate miner,
validator, or staker penalty rule ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

When TAO injection does not grow a subnet pool, the emissions documentation links that condition to
pool-side effects such as harder staking conditions and higher slippage
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

Those effects are downstream of the injection model. They help explain why net flow matters without
changing what flow-based emissions mean
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Distribution Handoff

After subnet-level injection is resolved, reward allocation still happens through within-subnet
logic. Yuma Consensus describes how validator evaluations become miner incentives and validator-side
outcomes ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Flow-based emissions therefore sit before that handoff. They compare subnets; they do not replace
the later consensus and reward-allocation steps
([Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

## Reader Boundary

Flow-based emissions should be read as a tokenomics concept for subnet comparison. It is not a
miner-ranking rule, validator-dividend formula, or staking tutorial
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

The stable article-level point is that net TAO movement helps determine subnet-level injection
before rewards are allocated inside the selected subnet
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For flow-based
emissions, that sequence changes how readers should interpret examples of net TAO flow signals and
subnet injection share results.

In localnet, TAO flow between staking and unstaking reflects only local testing activity. Localnet
flow-based emission comparisons do not represent production subnet allocation outcomes.

On testnet, TAO flow is generated by testnet participants in a shared, non-production network.
Testnet subnet injection shares are separate from mainnet allocation results.

On mainnet, flow-based emissions are the live mechanism that compares net TAO movement across
subnets to determine real TAO reserve injection shares in production Bittensor operations. The
[Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)
documentation describes the flow comparison that applies on the production network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A flow-based emission example from one environment should
not be read as representing net TAO flow or injection shares in another environment.
