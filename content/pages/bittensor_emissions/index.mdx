# Bittensor Emissions

Bittensor emissions describe how newly created TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens enter the
network's reward system. The term connects token creation, subnet context, and reward allocation
rather than naming one balance or one role outcome.

## Asset Scope

Emissions can involve TAO and subnet alpha tokens. TAO belongs to the network-wide side of Bittensor
tokenomics, while alpha tokens belong to individual subnet contexts.

This matters because the asset being discussed changes the reading. TAO emission context and subnet
alpha-token context are different parts of the same tokenomics topic rather than one undivided
reward amount.

Emissions are also different from plain issuance. Issuance names a circulating supply measure, while
emissions describe creation and distribution flows.

## Injection and Distribution

Injection and distribution are separate parts of the emission flow. Injection describes value
entering subnet economic settings. Distribution describes how rewards are allocated after the subnet
context exists.

For readers, injection and distribution answer different questions. Injection explains how emissions
enter a subnet context; distribution explains how rewards are allocated once the subnet context is
in view.

Flow-based emissions are one example of terminology that depends on subnet context rather than only
on a network-wide release schedule.

## Subnet Allocation

Subnets give emissions a work-and-evaluation setting. Subnet incentives connect useful work,
validator evaluation, and reward outcomes.

That makes emissions more than accounting terminology. The emitted assets matter because they are
routed through subnet-specific incentive systems rather than distributed as a generic supply faucet.

## Staking Context

Staking connects token holders to validator-side reward outcomes, so emissions often appear beside
staking and delegation terms. Staking is one place emissions can be observed, not the whole
emissions system.

Emission terminology keeps the broader tokenomics context attached: reward creation and allocation
remain larger than the staking surface where some outcomes appear.

## Halving and Flow

Halving and flow-based emissions refine different sides of the broader emissions topic. Halving
changes emission rate. Flow-based emissions describe a subnet-context distribution model.

Those ideas stay separate. Halving changes emission rate, flow-based emissions describe subnet
allocation context, and Bittensor emissions names the broader reward creation and distribution
topic.

That distinction helps readers place tokenomics terms in order. Halving adjusts the rate, injection
and distribution describe movement into and through subnet contexts, and Yuma Consensus explains
allocation outcomes.

## Dynamic TAO Context

Bittensor emissions fit inside the Dynamic TAO model, where the network allocates TAO to subnet
reserves and subnets emit alpha tokens for subnet reward distribution. Dynamic TAO places emissions
inside that allocation structure: TAO is allocated to subnet reserves, while subnet-side emissions
produce alpha-token rewards for validators, miners, and stakers.

That architecture connects two emission dimensions. TAO emissions describe the network-level flow
from the TAO supply to subnet contexts, while alpha emissions describe the subnet-level flow from
subnet-side issuance to role-facing subnet rewards.

Reserve vocabulary keeps that architecture readable. TAO reserve and alpha reserve describe pool
sides, while alpha outstanding and distributed rewards describe alpha outside the reserve side of
the subnet economy.

The useful reading is the two-level structure: network-level TAO allocation and subnet-level alpha
distribution. Bittensor emissions names the broader topic that connects those levels without turning
every reserve, staking, or consensus term into a synonym for emissions.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Bittensor
emissions, that sequence changes how readers should interpret TAO injection, alpha distribution, and
subnet allocation examples.

In localnet, emission flows can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet coinbase cadence and
subnet allocation examples do not represent production tokenomics behavior.

On testnet, emission and consensus distribution can be exercised in a shared non-production network.
Testnet reserve injection and role-facing rewards are separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, Bittensor emissions describe live production value creation and allocation across
subnets. Observed injection shares and role outcomes depend on the selected subnet, epoch timing,
and network conditions at the time ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An emissions example from one environment should not be
read as representing production allocation behavior in another environment.

## Coinbase Keeps the Block-Cadence Process Moving

The coinbase mechanism runs every block, injecting TAO and subnet alpha into each subnet's liquidity
pools and accumulating pending emissions, also called alpha outstanding, for distribution during the
subnet's next epoch
([Coinbase documentation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Coinbase also triggers each subnet's Yuma Consensus epoch, the process that distributes accumulated
emissions to miners and validators. Epochs across subnets are staggered, so coinbase is recurring
block-level processing rather than a single payout event.

## Issuance Measures Supply; Emissions Describe Flow

[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes reward creation
and allocation, while
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance) names the
circulating TAO supply measure. Bittensor emissions vocabulary therefore sits on the flow side: it
explains how newly created TAO and subnet
[protocol alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) enter subnet
contexts and reach role-facing outcomes, not the total TAO counted as circulating at a moment in
time ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

For readers, that keeps emissions separate from supply accounting. Issuance answers how much TAO is
circulating; emissions answer how reward material is created, injected, and distributed through
subnet incentive systems.

## Distinction from Tempo

Bittensor emissions are newly created TAO and subnet alpha entering the reward system. Tempo is the
block interval that defines each subnet epoch and paces release and distribution
([Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

## Distinction from Recycling

Bittensor emissions and recycling move TAO in opposite directions. Emissions create new TAO and
subnet alpha and route them into the reward system, while recycling removes TAO from current
issuance and circulation so an equivalent amount can be emitted again later, such as the TAO
consumed by a registration cost ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Recycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycle)).

- **Bittensor emissions** — newly created TAO and alpha entering reward distribution.
- **Recycling** — TAO removed from current issuance so an equivalent amount can be emitted again.

## Distinction from Dividend

Bittensor emissions and a dividend differ as the whole reward flow and one share of it. Emissions
name the creation and distribution of new TAO and alpha across subnet incentives, while a dividend
is the portion of those distributed rewards paid to a validator and its stakers for their stake
([Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution),
[Staking and Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

- **Bittensor emissions** — the overall creation and distribution of reward tokens.
- **Dividend** — the stake-based share of distributed rewards a validator and its stakers receive.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Bittensor emissions name the full value-creation and allocation flow for newly created TAO and
subnet alpha across network and subnet contexts. Yuma Consensus is one subnet-level step inside that
broader process: it converts eligible validator weights into miner incentives and validator
dividends each tempo ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

That keeps emission language separate from consensus-result language: a rank, trust value, or
dividend is downstream of mechanism context, while emissions describe the rewards being allocated
([Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)).

Emission documentation separates injection from distribution. Coinbase runs each block to inject TAO
and subnet alpha into pools and to trigger the Yuma epoch that distributes accumulated value to
miners and validators on a subnet
([Coinbase documentation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Flow-based emissions compare net TAO movement across subnets before reserve injection is assigned.
That cross-subnet comparison sits upstream of Yuma settlement, which still allocates role shares
from validator weights on the selected netuid
([Flow-Based Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#flow-based-emissions),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

Halving mechanisms change TAO and subnet alpha emission rates at supply milestones. Yuma still
performs weight-to-share settlement inside whatever emission setting those schedules produce
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Issuance vocabulary counts circulating TAO supply, while Bittensor emissions vocabulary describes
reward creation and movement through subnet incentive systems rather than one balance snapshot
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha)).

- **Bittensor emissions** — full generation and allocation of network rewards.
- **Yuma Consensus** — within-subnet settlement from validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

Bittensor emissions are tokenomics and reward-distribution terminology, not reward estimates,
staking guidance, validator guidance, or network summaries
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

Exact observed values belong to the specific network context that produced them.
