# Alpha Reserve

An alpha reserve is the pool-held side of a subnet's alpha token in Bittensor's Dynamic TAO
tokenomics. The official
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes each subnet as an automated market maker with two liquidity reserves: a TAO reserve and a
subnet-specific alpha reserve.

## Pool Context

Alpha reserve belongs to the subnet liquidity-pool context. The same official subnet overview
describes alpha reserves as the amount of alpha available for purchase, while TAO reserves are the
TAO staked into the subnet. Together, those two reserves define the pool side of a subnet's economy.

This makes alpha reserve different from a participant balance. It is not the alpha held by subnet
participants as stake or rewards; it is the alpha held inside the pool that pairs with the subnet's
TAO reserve.

## Price Context

The subnet overview explains that a subnet pool's reserve ratio determines the price of its alpha
token. In reader terms, alpha reserve is one side of that ratio. A change in the reserve context can
affect the pool relationship between TAO and alpha, but the article-level concept is simply that the
alpha reserve is the pool-held alpha side.

This does not make alpha reserve a live market quote. Exact reserve amounts and prices are current
state values, while the concept describes where the alpha sits in the subnet tokenomics model.

## Emission Context

The [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)
documentation describes alpha reserve injection as adding alpha to the subnet's reserve in
proportion to TAO injection so the pool can grow without changing the alpha token price from the
injection itself.

For readers, the useful distinction is between pool-side and participant-side emission paths. Alpha
reserve injection supports the pool side, while alpha outstanding injection is the participant-side
context for rewards.

## Relationship to Alpha Outstanding

Alpha reserve and alpha outstanding describe where subnet alpha sits in Dynamic TAO accounting.
Alpha reserve is the pool-held alpha side paired with TAO reserve, while alpha outstanding is alpha
held outside that reserve context by subnet participants. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
uses both terms when explaining subnet liquidity and participant-held stake.

For readers, alpha reserve answers how much alpha is in the subnet pool, while alpha outstanding
answers how much subnet alpha is outside that pool. They are two locations for the same
subnet-specific alpha asset, not separate assets.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Relationship to ADR

Alpha reserve is part of the pool side behind Alpha Distribution Ratio. The
[Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)
entry compares alpha emitted to participants with alpha injected into the automated market maker
pool. Alpha reserve belongs to that pool-side comparison, not to the participant-held side.

## Relationship to TAO Reserve

Alpha reserve and TAO Reserve are related but distinct pool-side terms in Bittensor subnet
tokenomics. Alpha reserve names the pool-held subnet-specific alpha side of a subnet's automated
market maker, while TAO Reserve names the pool-held TAO side of the same subnet. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes each subnet as an automated market maker with paired TAO and subnet-specific alpha
reserves, and the [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)
documentation describes TAO reserve injection as the process that adds TAO to the pool side that
pairs with the alpha reserve.

For readers, alpha reserve names one side of the subnet pool, while TAO Reserve names the other.
Together they define the pool side of a subnet's economy; the reserve ratio between them is the
price-setting context described on the official subnet overview. The two are not interchangeable:
alpha reserve is the subnet-specific asset side, TAO Reserve is the network asset side.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission: TAO reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Alpha reserve names the pool-held side of subnet alpha in Dynamic TAO. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that when
multiple incentive mechanisms are used, each operates independently, allowing subnet creators to
distribute emissions across different types of work or evaluation criteria.

For readers, alpha reserve still describes alpha held inside the subnet pool. When a subnet runs
more than one incentive mechanism, participant-side emission paths can differ, but alpha reserve
remains the pool-held alpha side of that subnet market.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)

## Relationship to Protocol Alpha

Alpha reserve and protocol alpha are connected through the reserve injection process that feeds the
pool side of subnet tokenomics. Alpha reserve is the pool-held subnet alpha that pairs with the
subnet's TAO reserve inside an automated market maker, while protocol alpha is subnet alpha that the
Bittensor protocol itself accumulates as a byproduct of that injection. The
[Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha)
describes protocol alpha as alpha tokens the protocol gathers as a result of chain-side TAO buys
during reserve injection, cached per subnet rather than immediately recycled, while the
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)
documentation describes how alpha enters the reserve side of a subnet pool alongside TAO injection.

For readers, alpha reserve names the pool-held alpha that exists inside the subnet's liquidity
context, while protocol alpha names a portion of that pool-side activity's byproduct—alpha the
protocol retains from its own chain-side participation in the injection mechanism. The glossary
further notes that at subnet dissolution, protocol alpha participates in pro-rata TAO settlement,
reducing staker payouts proportionally while the protocol's TAO share returns to the chain. Alpha
reserve and protocol alpha are therefore related concepts within the same injection process: the
reserve is the pool context, and protocol alpha is the protocol's own accumulated stake that arises
from prior injections into that context.

References:
[Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha),
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)

## Relationship to Coinbase

Alpha reserve and coinbase are related but different parts of Bittensor subnet pool vocabulary.
Alpha reserve is the pool-held subnet alpha that pairs with a subnet's TAO reserve inside the AMM
pool — the supply side of the liquidity context that determines alpha pricing through pool ratios.
Coinbase is the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission, injects TAO into subnet pool
reserves each block through reserve injection, accumulates pending alpha emissions, and checks each
subnet's epoch boundary to trigger Yuma Consensus rounds. The
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)
documentation describes how alpha enters the reserve side of a subnet pool alongside TAO injection,
and the
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes the per-block operation that drives that injection process.

Coinbase is what actually performs the per-block reserve injection that changes alpha reserve
balances. Each block, coinbase adds TAO to the subnet reserve and simultaneously adjusts the alpha
reserve as part of that injection step. Alpha reserve names the pool-held alpha quantity at any
given moment; coinbase names the per-block mechanism whose reserve injection step modifies it.

References:
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection),
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)

## Relationship to Conviction and Locked Stake

An alpha reserve and conviction both involve subnet alpha, but they sit on different sides of the
subnet. An alpha reserve is the pool-held side of a subnet's alpha token — the alpha liquidity held
in the subnet's automated market maker — as the
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes. Conviction and locked stake, by the
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation, describe locking subnet alpha held as stake by participants so that it builds a
time-based commitment score and the staked balance cannot be silently reduced below the locked
amount.

For readers, an alpha reserve names alpha sitting inside the pool, while conviction names a lock
placed on alpha that participants hold as stake outside the pool. The two are not interchangeable:
the alpha reserve is pool liquidity used for pricing and swaps, whereas conviction is a commitment
lock on participant-held staked alpha. Pool-held alpha and locked staked alpha are different
positions of the same subnet asset; conviction applies to the staked side, not to the pool reserve.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Reader Boundary

Alpha reserve is not a separate token, a staking instruction, or a recommendation about a subnet. It
is a tokenomics term for the pool-held alpha side of a subnet's liquidity context. Live reserve
amounts, prices, and trading outcomes should be read from current official tools or chain data.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For alpha
reserve, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about pool-held subnet alpha.

In local testing, alpha reserve can show how much subnet alpha sits in the pool side of a subnet's
automated market maker inside an isolated environment. That is useful for checking reserve mechanics
and the price relationship, but it remains isolated feedback from a local environment.

On testchain, alpha reserve behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives
stronger evidence about how the pool-held alpha side responds to reserve injection and staking
interactions than a private local run, while still keeping the result separate from production
mainchain pool values.

On mainchain, alpha reserve reflects live pool-held subnet alpha tied to actual AMM activity. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes each subnet as an automated market maker with paired TAO and subnet-specific alpha
reserves, so production alpha reserve context is tied to real pool state on the active network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An alpha reserve value observed in one environment should
not be read as identical evidence about pool-held subnet alpha in another environment.

For readers, this keeps alpha reserve examples from sounding stronger than their environment
supports. Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all describe pool-held subnet alpha, but they
do not carry the same interpretive weight.
