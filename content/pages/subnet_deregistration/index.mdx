# Subnet Deregistration

Subnet deregistration is the Bittensor mechanism that removes a subnet from the network to make room
for a new one. The official
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes it as a lifecycle process that maintains network quality by removing weaker subnets so new
subnets can register within the network's fixed subnet limit.

## Why Subnets Get Deregistered

Bittensor caps how many subnets can occupy the network at once. The
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
states the network supports up to 128 subnet slots, while
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) provides the
broader subnet context. Once that limit is full, a new subnet cannot register unless an existing one
leaves.

Deregistration addresses that pressure by efficiency. The documentation frames it as removing
subnets that consume TAO emissions without providing value and unlocking the TAO resources locked in
those subnet pools, so capacity flows toward subnets that hold more market value.

## How a Subnet Is Selected

The process is triggered by demand for a slot. The
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
explains that when the subnet limit is reached and a new subnet attempts to register, the network
selects a subnet to remove rather than rejecting the newcomer outright.

The selection metric is market price, not a generic performance grade. The documentation states that
the subnet chosen for deregistration is the non-immune subnet with the lowest EMA price, meaning its
exponential moving average of alpha price. Here, "underperforming" is a conceptual shorthand: the
actual selection compares EMA price across eligible subnets. The on-chain behavior behind this lives
in the Subtensor
[network source code](https://github.com/opentensor/subtensor/blob/main/pallets/subtensor/src/subnets/subnet.rs).

## Immunity and Rate Limiting

New subnets are not removed immediately. The
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes an immunity period, currently about four months, during which a freshly registered subnet
is protected from deregistration, giving it time to establish its EMA price.

The mechanism is also paced. The documentation notes a rate-limiting window of about two days
between registrations and deregistrations, which connects subnet lifecycle changes to the broader
[Rate Limits in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/chain-rate-limits) model that
spaces sensitive chain actions over time.

## What Happens When the Cap Is Full

The 128-subnet limit is not just a registration quota on paper. When every slot is occupied, a new
subnet creator cannot add another market unless something else leaves the network. The official
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes that pressure as the trigger: a registration attempt when the cap is full can cause the
network to remove an existing subnet rather than reject the newcomer outright.

That makes deregistration part of subnet creation economics, not only subnet failure vocabulary. A
creator evaluating whether to register should understand that the cap can force lifecycle turnover
through market-price comparison among eligible subnets, not through a manual review of subnet
content. Current slot occupancy and immunity status belong in live chain data and official
references rather than in a static article quote.

References: [Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## How EMA Price Drives Selection

Selection uses the lowest exponential moving average of alpha price among non-immune subnets. The
documentation uses market price rather than a subjective performance score, which keeps the removal
rule tied to the alpha-price context described in Bittensor tokenomics vocabulary. A subnet still
inside its immunity window is excluded from that comparison so a newly registered market has time to
establish price history.

Readers should not treat "lowest EMA price" as a promise about any specific subnet's future or as a
live ranking table. It is the documented selection signal when the cap binds and a new registration
needs a slot. Exact immunity lengths, rate limits, and market conditions can change on chain, so
operational decisions should use current official references.

References: [Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Glossary: Alpha Price](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-price),
[Subtensor network source code](https://github.com/opentensor/subtensor/blob/main/pallets/subtensor/src/subnets/subnet.rs)

## Subnet Removal Versus UID Removal

Subnet deregistration removes an entire subnet to free a network slot. Miner or validator
deregistration, by contrast, removes a participant from a UID slot inside a subnet that continues to
exist. The official subnet deregistration page describes whole-subnet lifecycle removal, while
mining and validating documentation describe participant replacement when registration pressure or
performance rules require a slot change inside an active market.

Mixing those levels makes subnet policy harder to read. A low-performing miner can be replaced
without dissolving the subnet itself. A subnet deregistration event changes which netuid markets
exist at the network level. Keeping the two lifecycle events separate prevents a participant removal
story from being mistaken for an entire subnet sunset.

References: [Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Miner Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)

## Related Concepts

The [Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) lifecycle
should be separated from participant deregistration. Subnet deregistration removes an entire subnet
to free a network slot, while
[deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration) can also refer
to removing a miner or validator from a UID slot inside a subnet.

The selection rule depends on alpha-price context, not a manual review of subnet content. The subnet
deregistration documentation describes the target as the non-immune subnet with the lowest EMA
price, while the [Alpha Price](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-price)
glossary entry defines alpha price as the value of a subnet's alpha token relative to TAO. The
non-immune qualifier matters because a new subnet can be protected by an
[immunity period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period) while it has
time to establish market history.

Rate limits and settlement context describe what happens around the lifecycle event.
[Rate limits](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/chain-rate-limits) pace subnet registrations and
deregistrations; they do not decide which subnet is selected. If a subnet dissolves,
[protocol alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) is part of the
tokenomics settlement context rather than an ordinary participant-held stake.

## Reader Boundary

Subnet deregistration should not be read as a prediction about any specific subnet, a live ranking,
or a step-by-step guide. The subnet limit, immunity period, and rate-limit values can change on
chain, so current figures belong in official references and live chain state. This article describes
the concept of removing the lowest-EMA-price subnet to free a slot.

## Relationship to Subnet Registration

Subnet deregistration and subnet registration are related but distinct lifecycle events. The
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes deregistration as triggered when the 128-subnet limit is reached and a new subnet attempts
to register, with the network removing the lowest-EMA-price non-immune subnet so the registration
can succeed. The [Create a Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet)
documentation describes registration as the on-chain creation step that pays the registration burn
and assigns a new netuid.

For readers, the two act on the 128-slot cap from opposite sides. Subnet registration is the act
that tries to add a subnet to the network, while subnet deregistration is the act that can fire when
that addition would exceed the limit. Registration produces the netuid; deregistration removes it. A
reader who understands subnet registration as the lifecycle's "in" event can use subnet
deregistration as the corresponding "out" event that keeps the cap from binding new creators.

References: [Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Create a Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet)
