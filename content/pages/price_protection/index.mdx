# Price Protection

Price protection is Bittensor's guardrail for staking-related actions that can face adverse price
movement while they are pending or being processed. It is most relevant around staking and
unstaking, where subnet pool conditions can affect the final result. The
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) docs describe the feature
as a way to control how staking and unstaking handle adverse price movement, and
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) explains why received
amounts can differ from a static expectation.

## Why price movement matters

Staking and unstaking interact with subnet pool prices. If the pool changes before or during
processing, the final result can differ from what a user expected when the action was prepared. That
is the practical reason price protection belongs next to slippage, MEV, and staking context rather
than being a general wallet feature. The
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) page ties the feature to
adverse price movement, while
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) explains the
expected-versus-received gap in Bittensor staking and unstaking.

## Protection choices

Bittensor documentation describes three price-protection choices: strict, partial, and unsafe.
Strict protection rejects an action when the resulting price movement is outside the allowed
tolerance. Partial protection allows only the portion that stays within the allowed tolerance.
Unsafe handling ignores the price-protection guardrail. The key distinction is the behavior, not
client syntax. The [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) docs
define these choices and their high-level outcomes.

## Submission Price Versus Execution Price

Price protection compares the market price at execution against the price when the staking or
unstaking action was submitted. The
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) documentation describes
strict and partial modes in terms of how far final execution can move from that submission reference
before the action is rejected or limited. Readers should treat the submission moment as the baseline
for tolerance checks rather than as a guarantee that pool conditions will stay fixed while the
action is pending.

That pending window matters because subnet pool prices can change before execution completes. Price
protection is therefore a guardrail around movement during staking-related processing, not a
separate token type or wallet feature.

References: [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)

## Strict Rejection Versus Partial Fill

Strict protection rejects the entire action when execution would move the final price beyond the
selected tolerance from the submission price. Partial protection still executes, but only up to the
maximum amount that keeps the final price inside that tolerance band. The
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) docs contrast these
outcomes when a full unstake would push the market farther than the tolerance allows.

Readers choosing between the two modes should decide whether a failed action is preferable to a
partially filled one. Strict mode protects the full requested amount or nothing; partial mode
accepts a smaller executed amount so some of the action can complete inside the tolerance boundary.

References: [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)

## Unsafe Mode and Complementary Protections

Unsafe mode proceeds without the price-protection guardrail, which the official documentation treats
as convenient for development and testing but inadvisable when moving real-value liquidity on
mainnet. Price protection therefore names a user-selected response to adverse movement, while
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/) addresses a different part of the
risk area by hiding sensitive pre-inclusion details.

Readers should not treat unsafe execution, strict tolerance, partial fill, and MEV Shield as
interchangeable substitutes. Price protection decides how much adverse movement an action may
tolerate; MEV Shield reduces exposure of pending transaction details. Both can matter around staking
and unstaking, but they answer different questions.

References: [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/)

## Relationship to slippage

Slippage is the difference between an expected amount and the amount actually received through a
pool conversion. Price protection is a control around that kind of movement: it decides whether the
action should continue, be limited, or proceed without the protection guardrail. In other words,
slippage describes the outcome difference, while price protection describes how a staking-related
action handles unacceptable movement.
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) defines the staking and
unstaking slippage concept, and
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) defines the protection
choices.

## Relationship to MEV Shield

Price protection and MEV Shield address different parts of the same risk area. Price protection
controls adverse price-movement handling. MEV Shield hides sensitive pre-inclusion details so that
pending information is less exposed. A reader should not treat one as a replacement for the other.
The [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) docs point to MEV
Shield as additional protection against MEV attacks, and the
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/) docs explain encrypted mempool
protection.

## Careful interpretation

Price protection is not a guarantee of a desired result. It does not remove pool movement or decide
whether a staking choice is useful. It only defines how an action should respond when price movement
crosses the selected protection boundary. The
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) docs define that boundary
behavior, while [Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) explains
why the received amount can still depend on pool conditions.

## Relation to nearby concepts

Price protection sits near [[staking_and_delegation|Staking and Delegation]],
[[dynamic_tao|Dynamic TAO]], and [[wallets|Wallets]]. Staking and delegation provide the user-facing
staking context. Dynamic TAO explains the broader subnet pool and tokenomics context. Wallets are
where users initiate staking-related activity, but price protection is narrower: it is about adverse
price movement during staking-related actions.
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) and
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) define that narrower scope.

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Price protection limits adverse price movement during staking and unstaking through subnet pools.
The [Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that
each subnet has one or more incentive mechanisms.

For readers, price protection still names a staking guardrail. It belongs to shared pool conversion
context rather than to one mechanism label.

## Relationship to Extrinsics

Price protection and extrinsics are related but different parts of Bittensor chain action
vocabulary. Extrinsics name signed transactions and calls submitted to Subtensor, while price
protection names guardrails for how staking-related extrinsics handle adverse price movement during
processing. The
[Glossary: Extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#extrinsics) describes
signed transactions submitted to the chain, and the
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) documentation describes
strict, partial, and unsafe protection modes for staking-related actions.

For readers, extrinsics name the submitted chain actions, while price protection names tolerance
rules that can apply while those staking-related actions are processed.

References: [Glossary: Extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#extrinsics),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)

## Relationship to Subtensor

Price protection and Subtensor are related but different parts of Bittensor chain vocabulary.
Subtensor names Bittensor's layer 1 blockchain and system of record, while price protection names
client-side guardrails enforced around staking-related actions processed on that chain layer. The
[Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor) describes the
blockchain layer, and the [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)
documentation describes how staking and unstaking handle adverse price movement.

For readers, Subtensor names the chain layer, while price protection names a guardrail applied
around staking-related activity on that layer.

References: [Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)

## Relationship to Block

Price protection and a block are related but different parts of Bittensor chain timing vocabulary. A
block names one unit of chain progression on Subtensor, while price protection can matter while a
staking-related action is pending or being processed across one or more blocks. The
[Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block) describes a block as a
unit of chain data, and the
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) documentation describes
guardrails for adverse price movement during staking-related processing.

For readers, a block marks chain progression, while price protection names how a staking-related
action should respond if pool prices move beyond the selected tolerance during processing.

References: [Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)

## Relationship to Transaction Fees

Price protection and transaction fees are related but different parts of Bittensor chain vocabulary.
Transaction fees name the costs attached to state-changing actions on Subtensor, while price
protection names guardrails for adverse price movement during staking-related actions rather than
the base fee model. The [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)
documentation describes fee-bearing actions, and the
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) documentation describes
strict, partial, and unsafe protection modes.

For readers, transaction fees belong to the cost model for chain actions, while price protection
belongs to pool-price tolerance during staking-related processing.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)
