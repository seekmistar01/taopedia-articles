# Digital Commodities

Digital commodities are the useful outputs that Bittensor subnets are built to produce. A subnet
sets the work target, miners produce work for that target, and validators evaluate the results:
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets).

The term keeps attention on the product of a subnet, not only on the token incentives around it.
Different subnets can pursue different outputs while still using Bittensor's shared miner,
validator, and incentive structure.

## Subnet Work Context

A digital commodity is subnet-specific. One subnet may target a model, data, inference, compute, or
other digital service, while another subnet can define a different kind of useful output:
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism).

That subnet boundary matters because the commodity is not a universal object across the whole
network. It is the work category a particular subnet asks its miners to produce and its validators
to judge.

## Miner Production

Miners are the role family that produces the requested digital work. Their output is meaningful
inside the standards of the subnet that requested it:
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Mining and Validating](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners).

This makes digital commodities a work-output concept. The article topic is not the miner software
itself, but the useful product that miner behavior is meant to supply for a subnet.

## Validator Evaluation

Digital commodities need evaluation as well as production. Validators compare miner outputs against
the subnet's incentive rules and express those evaluations through weights:
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus).

That evaluation layer is what separates a digital commodity from an arbitrary file, answer, or
service. In Bittensor, the output becomes economically meaningful because validators score it under
the subnet's rules.

## Incentive Mechanism Context

The incentive mechanism links the commodity to rewards. It specifies what useful work counts, how
that work is evaluated, and how scores feed the reward process:
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions).

This is why digital commodities sit near subnet creation and incentive design. A subnet is not only
a topic label; it is a market for a defined kind of useful digital work, backed by a scoring and
emission process.

## Multiple-Mechanism Context

Some subnets can use multiple incentive mechanisms. In that case, each mechanism can define a
separate work-and-evaluation path, so readers should keep the relevant commodity context tied to the
mechanism being discussed:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism).

The broader idea stays the same. A digital commodity is the useful output a subnet asks for, while
the mechanism defines how that output is assessed and rewarded.

## Reader Boundary

This article defines digital commodities as a Bittensor subnet concept. It does not rank subnets,
measure output quality, or claim that every submitted miner output is useful:
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism).

Readers should interpret commodity claims through the relevant subnet and incentive mechanism. The
stable point is the relationship among requested work, miner production, validator evaluation, and
emission incentives.
