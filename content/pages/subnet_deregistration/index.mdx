# Subnet Deregistration

Subnet deregistration is the Bittensor subnet-lifecycle mechanism that frees a subnet slot when the
network is full. The core case is simple: if the 128-slot cap is already occupied, adding another
subnet requires one eligible subnet to leave first
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)).

## Why the Cap Matters

Bittensor treats subnet capacity as a bounded resource. The subnet cap keeps the network from
growing by unlimited slot creation, while the deregistration rule gives the system a way to rotate
capacity toward subnets with stronger market support
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

This makes deregistration part of subnet lifecycle vocabulary rather than a general failure label.
It is about freeing one subnet slot under a documented cap, not about judging every miner,
validator, or application running inside every subnet.

## Selection Rule

When the cap is full, the eligible subnet with the lowest exponential moving average price is the
removal candidate. EMA price is a smoothed alpha-price signal, so the rule is tied to market value
over time rather than a one-moment price tick
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Understanding Subnets: Price/rate of alpha tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#pricerate-of-alpha-tokens)).

That distinction matters for interpretation. "Lowest EMA price" is the documented comparison, while
"weak subnet" is only shorthand. The mechanism does not require a manual content review, a social
vote, or a broad quality essay about the subnet.

That EMA is taken over a subnet's alpha-token price, which
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#pricerate-of-alpha-tokens)
describes as the ratio of TAO reserve to alpha reserve in the subnet pool. Using a moving average
rather than a single price tick makes the rule resistant to momentary swings, which is why immunity
windows and rate limits sit alongside it rather than letting a brief dip mark a subnet for removal
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)).

## Creation Pressure

Subnet deregistration is coupled to subnet creation pressure. A full cap creates the need for slot
turnover; without that pressure, the concept is not just a standing ranking table. The lifecycle
pair is easier to read as "create a slot" and "free a slot" rather than as unrelated operations
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Create a Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet)).

This boundary separates deregistration from ordinary subnet analysis. A reader can understand the
mechanism without listing every possible subnet, comparing teams, or turning the article into a
status dashboard.

## Development Stage Context

Localnet, testnet, and mainnet examples do not carry the same weight. A localnet example can show
how the lifecycle rule behaves in an isolated development environment, while testnet and mainnet
belong to separate network contexts with separate histories
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).

Localnet examples are isolated development examples. Testnet examples are shared non-production
examples. Mainnet deregistration interpretation concerns production subnet lifecycle on the active
network.

## A Full Cap With All Subnets Immune Blocks Entry

[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes a case where every occupied subnet is still inside its immunity window. When that happens,
the selection step returns no removal candidate, registration fails with a subnet-limit error, and
no subnet is deregistered until at least one becomes eligible.

That edge case keeps deregistration vocabulary tied to eligibility rather than to automatic
turnover. A full 128-slot network can still refuse a new subnet registration if every incumbent
remains immune.

## Liquidation Converts Alpha Back to TAO Holders

When a subnet is deregistered,
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
states that alpha in the subnet is converted through the pool and distributed to alpha holders, with
the subnet's TAO reserve allocated pro-rata. Alpha tokens in that subnet are destroyed as part of
dissolution rather than continuing as a live subnet asset.

Deregistration therefore ends the subnet market and settles holder balances. It is not the same as
miner or validator deregistration inside a subnet that continues to exist
([Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

## Zero Emissions Do Not Automatically Remove a Subnet

[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation notes that emissions and
de-registration are intentionally decoupled: subnets with zero flow-based injection can still remain
registered, while subnet deregistration continues to use lowest alpha-price EMA among eligible
subnets when the cap is full.

That split keeps two vocabulary paths separate. Negative or zero cross-subnet emission share does
not, by itself, trigger the subnet-slot removal rule described in deregistration documentation.

## Distinction from Miner Deregistration

Subnet deregistration and miner deregistration remove different things. Subnet deregistration frees
an entire subnet slot when the 128-slot cap is full, selecting the eligible subnet with the lowest
alpha-price EMA, while miner deregistration removes an individual participant's UID inside a subnet
that keeps running
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

- **Subnet deregistration** — removal of a whole subnet slot under the cap.
- **Miner deregistration** — removal of one UID inside a continuing subnet.

## Distinction from Immunity Period

Subnet deregistration is the removal mechanism, while an immunity period is the protection that
delays it. The documentation describes an immunity window that keeps a newly created subnet out of
the removal comparison while it establishes price history
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period)).

- **Subnet deregistration** — the rule that frees a slot when the cap is full.
- **Immunity period** — the window that keeps a new subnet out of the removal comparison.

Newer subnets can be protected by an immunity window, which keeps a freshly created subnet out of
the removal comparison while it has time to establish price history. Deregistration is also paced by
rate limits, so slot turnover belongs to the same family of delayed, bounded network actions as
other sensitive subnet lifecycle changes
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Rate Limits in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/chain-rate-limits)).

The practical reading is that selection is not just "lowest price wins removal." Eligibility,
immunity, and timing are part of the concept. A subnet can have a low market signal and still be
outside the comparison if the protected window applies.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Subnet deregistration names the removal of an entire subnet when the global subnet cap binds. Yuma
Consensus is the separate mechanism that converts validator weight submissions into emission shares
each tempo on subnets that remain active
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)).

- **Subnet deregistration** — lifecycle removal of a whole subnet at the cap.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain mechanism that uses validator weights to determine emission shares
  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).
  Readers should keep subnet-lifecycle vocabulary separate from settlement vocabulary. Subnet
  deregistration removes an entire subnet at the global cap; Yuma continues settling weights on
  subnets that remain active
  ([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
  [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Lifecycle removal changes which netuids exist. Yuma still runs on active subnets each tempo
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Netuid** — numeric identifier for one subnet.

## Distinction from Miner Registration

Miner registration gives a miner a UID slot after paying a registration burn to enter a subnet.
Subnet deregistration is the network mechanism that removes an entire non-immune subnet when the
subnet limit is reached, selecting the subnet with the lowest EMA of alpha price for removal
([subnet deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration)).

Miner deregistration vacates one low-performing miner slot while the subnet continues. Subnet
deregistration removes the entire subnet and every UID assignment within it at once.

- **Miner registration** — participant-level entry that assigns a miner UID slot on a subnet.
- **Subnet deregistration** — network-level removal of an entire subnet and all its UID slots.

## Reader Boundary

Subnet deregistration removes a subnet slot when the 128-slot cap is full. It should be kept
separate from miner or validator deregistration inside a continuing subnet, from zero-emission
removal, and from predictions about future removals
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

The documented comparison is lowest alpha-price EMA among eligible subnets, not a manual quality
review or social vote. Immunity windows, rate limits, and creation pressure are part of the concept,
not optional timing details
([Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Rate Limits in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/chain-rate-limits)).
