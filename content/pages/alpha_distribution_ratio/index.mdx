# Alpha Distribution Ratio

Alpha Distribution Ratio, often shortened to ADR, is a Bittensor tokenomics measure for comparing
distributed subnet alpha with alpha injected into a subnet's automated market maker pool. It names
the split between outside-pool alpha emissions and pool-facing alpha injections
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Emission: injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

The term is useful because Dynamic TAO has both outside-pool and pool-side alpha paths. ADR gives
those paths a compact comparison point without turning the ratio into a total supply figure, a pool
balance, or a payout estimate.

## Distribution and Injection

ADR compares two subnet-alpha paths. One path is distribution, where alpha is allocated outward as
part of subnet emissions. The other path is injection, where alpha is added to the subnet pool
alongside TAO reserve activity
([Emission: injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

That makes ADR a comparison measure rather than a balance, ranking, or payout table. The ratio is
about where subnet alpha is directed: outward through distribution or inward through pool injection
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

The broader emissions split gives the two sides. Injection adds liquidity to subnet pools, while
distribution allocates value to subnet roles
([Emission: injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

The injection side is pool-facing, and the distribution side is outside-pool-facing. ADR names that
split without becoming a total supply number or a specific reward result
([Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection),
[Emission: Alpha outstanding injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-outstanding-injection)).

## Halving Link

ADR is tied to halving vocabulary. The ADR glossary entry relates the ratio to the gap between the
global TAO halving index and a subnet's alpha halving index, so the term sits at the intersection of
system-wide TAO emissions and subnet-level alpha emissions
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

This keeps ADR separate from a simple emissions total. TAO halvings operate at system scope, while
alpha halvings operate at subnet scope; ADR is shaped by how those halving scopes differ
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

The halving link also explains why ADR is not just a pool snapshot. It reflects how TAO and subnet
alpha emission schedules can diverge, then expresses the resulting distribution-versus-injection
split for subnet alpha.

## Reserve and Outstanding

ADR is scoped to subnet-level alpha. Flow-based emissions compare subnets for allocation, while ADR
compares alpha paths inside a subnet
([Emission: distribution across subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

That avoids mixing two levels of analysis. Flow-based allocation answers how emission enters
subnets; ADR answers how subnet alpha is split after the article is already discussing one subnet
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

The injection side of ADR connects directly to alpha reserve. Alpha reserve is the pool-held alpha
side of a subnet market, and alpha reserve injection adds alpha to that pool-side reserve
([Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)).

ADR does not replace alpha reserve. Alpha reserve names where pool-side alpha sits; ADR names the
relationship between that pool-side injection path and the distribution path
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Alpha outstanding belongs on the other side of the comparison. The subnet overview places alpha
outstanding outside the reserve pool, so ADR is best read as pool-injected alpha versus distributed
outside-pool alpha
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission: Alpha outstanding injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-outstanding-injection)).

Reserve and outstanding terms identify locations. ADR identifies the ratio between two movement
paths that can affect those locations.

## Protocol Alpha

Protocol alpha is related to ADR because it can arise from reserve-injection activity. During
coinbase-driven reserve injection, the protocol can accumulate alpha that is cached by subnet rather
than immediately recycled
([Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)).

That does not make ADR a protocol-alpha inventory. ADR is the distribution-versus-injection ratio,
while protocol alpha names a retained-alpha category within reserve-injection mechanics
([Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha)).

## Liquidation Interpretation

ADR also has a liquidation-side reading. A higher ADR means liquidation happens at a deeper discount
to spot price, so the ratio shows how much of the subnet's alpha has left the pool-side path
relative to the alpha that remains in the pool
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

That makes ADR a liquidity-pressure indicator as well as a distribution-versus-injection ratio. A
high ADR means the outside-pool side is large relative to pool-injected alpha, which is the
condition the glossary associates with a deeper liquidation discount. A lower ADR points to a
smaller relative outside-pool side
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

The liquidation reading is still a glossary-level interpretation, not a conversion rule. It explains
why the ratio matters: ADR connects the accounting split to the discount pressure that can appear
when outside-pool alpha is liquidated against the pool.

That keeps ADR separate from alpha price. Alpha price is read from the TAO reserve and alpha reserve
relationship, while ADR is the distribution-versus-injection ratio that can affect how outside-pool
alpha relates back to pool liquidity
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Halving Index Gap Shapes the ADR Formula

The [Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)
relates the ratio to the gap between the global TAO halving index and a subnet's alpha halving
index. TAO halvings operate at system scope, while alpha halvings operate at subnet scope
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

ADR therefore reflects how those halving schedules can diverge, then expresses the resulting
distribution-versus-injection split for subnet alpha.

## Comparing ADR Across Subnets Requires Matching the Shared Index

The ADR formula's halving-index gap has one term that is shared network-wide and one term that is
specific to a subnet. The global TAO halving index applies the same way to every subnet's ADR
calculation, while each subnet supplies its own alpha halving index
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

A side-by-side ADR comparison between two subnets is therefore a comparison of their alpha halving
indexes against that same shared global term, not a comparison of two unrelated formulas. Reading
two ADR figures together still requires knowing which subnet's alpha halving index produced each
one.

## Injection, Distribution, and ADR on a Subnet Pool

Dynamic TAO emission on a subnet such as netuid 1 splits alpha across injection and distribution
paths before ADR summarizes the relationship between them
([Emission: injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

Injection adds alpha to the subnet pool through
[alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)
and related reserve-side activity, while distribution allocates alpha outward to subnet roles and
[alpha outstanding](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-outstanding-injection)
context. ADR compares those two movement paths rather than naming a single pool balance or reward
result
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

The halving-index gap then shapes how that ratio evolves as global TAO halving progress and a
subnet's alpha halving index diverge
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)). A higher ADR can also
signal stronger outside-pool pressure relative to pool-injected alpha when liquidation vocabulary
enters the discussion
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For ADR on a
subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how distribution-versus-injection ratios should be
read.

In localnet, ADR can be observed on isolated subnet pools such as netuid 1. Localnet halving
progress and reserve balances reflect that test chain's emission history rather than production
tokenomics.

On testnet, ADR on netuid 1 applies in a shared non-production network where alpha halving indexes
and pool conditions are separate from mainnet.

On mainnet, ADR on netuid 1 concerns live production subnet tokenomics, where injection,
distribution, alpha reserve, and alpha outstanding all move through the paired pool on the selected
subnet ([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An ADR example from one environment should not be read as
subnet tokenomics state on another network.

## Distinction from Protocol Alpha

The alpha distribution ratio and protocol alpha are different parts of subnet alpha accounting. ADR
is the measure comparing distributed subnet alpha against alpha injected into the subnet's automated
market maker pool, while protocol alpha is subnet alpha the Bittensor protocol accumulates during
reserve injection and caches per subnet
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha),
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)).

Ratio vocabulary and cached-balance vocabulary answer different accounting questions. ADR compares
distribution against pool injection; protocol alpha names alpha the protocol retains during reserve
injection rather than the comparative ratio itself
([Emission: injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

- **Alpha distribution ratio** — the ratio comparing distributed alpha to pool-injected alpha.
- **Protocol alpha** — alpha the protocol caches per subnet during reserve injection.

## Distinction from Slippage

The alpha distribution ratio and slippage both relate to a subnet pool but measure different things.
ADR is an emissions-side ratio comparing distributed alpha against pool-injected alpha, while
slippage is the difference between the expected and received amounts when a single trade moves
through the pool's reserves
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage)).

- **Alpha distribution ratio** — an emissions ratio of distributed alpha to pool-injected alpha.
- **Slippage** — the expected-versus-received gap on one trade through the pool.

## Distinction from Tempo

The alpha distribution ratio and tempo name a ratio and an interval. ADR compares two alpha movement
paths within a subnet's tokenomics, while tempo is the block interval that defines a subnet's epoch
and paces when emissions are released and distributed
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)).

- **Alpha distribution ratio** — the distribution-versus-injection ratio for subnet alpha.
- **Tempo** — the block interval that paces a subnet's epoch and emission release.

## Distinction from Emission

The alpha distribution ratio compares distributed subnet alpha against alpha injected into the pool.
Emission is the broader process by which TAO and subnet alpha enter circulation through protocol
schedules
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Alpha distribution ratio** — comparative measure of distribution versus pool injection.
- **Emission** — protocol process that schedules TAO and alpha entering circulation.

Ratio vocabulary names one accounting comparison; emission vocabulary names the wider issuance path
([Emission: injection and distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)).

## Per-Subnet Live Value Boundary

Alpha distribution ratio is per-subnet chain state. The ratio comparing distributed alpha against
injected alpha on one netuid reflects that subnet's pool accounting, but the live ratio can differ
from reference defaults and from the ratio on another netuid
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

This article's infobox uses netuid 1 as an example label when discussing alpha movement on Finney
mainnet. That example anchors discussion; it does not prove identical pool ratios everywhere
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

Alpha distribution ratio describes pool accounting; Yuma Consensus still converts validator weights
into emission shares each tempo
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Alpha distribution ratio** — distribution-versus-injection ratio for subnet alpha.
- **Per-subnet boundary** — live value is chain state on one netuid.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Alpha distribution ratio compares distributed subnet alpha against alpha injected into the pool.
Yuma Consensus is the separate tempo process that converts validator weight submissions into
emission shares each epoch
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Alpha distribution ratio** — distribution-versus-injection ratio for subnet alpha.
- **Yuma Consensus** — mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

ADR is a subnet tokenomics ratio. It is not a staking instruction, a yield promise, or a universal
comparison across every subnet. It explains how subnet alpha is divided between distribution and
injection once the relevant subnet is established
([Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)).

Halving indexes help shape it, flow-based emissions establish allocation across subnets, and alpha
reserve names the pool-side location, but ADR itself is the distribution-versus-injection ratio
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).
