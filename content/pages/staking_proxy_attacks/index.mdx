# Staking Proxy Attacks

Staking proxy attacks are a Bittensor wallet-safety concept about misused delegated staking
authority. The official
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)
page frames the issue around a key boundary: a staking proxy is not a direct transfer key, but its
authority can still matter if the delegate is compromised.

This makes the topic narrower than full wallet compromise. It is about the risk left inside a
permission that is intentionally limited to staking-related activity.

Reference:
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)

## Delegated Staking Authority

The official [Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies) explains proxy
relationships as scoped delegation between accounts. In that model, a staking proxy has less
authority than a wallet with full control, but it still has access to staking-related actions for
the protected account.

That distinction is the core of staking proxy attack risk. Reduced authority is not the same thing
as no authority.

References: [Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies),
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)

## Value-Loss Context

The official page connects staking proxy compromise to unfavorable staking and unstaking outcomes
rather than to a simple direct transfer. That places the concept near Bittensor's pool-conversion
topics.

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) explains why staking and
unstaking can produce received amounts that differ from a static expectation. Staking proxy attacks
are a safety context where delegated staking authority intersects with that kind of outcome risk.

References:
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)

## Relation to Nearby Protections

Staking proxy attacks are adjacent to MEV Shield and price protection, but they are not the same
concept. [MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/) concerns pre-inclusion
transaction visibility. [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)
concerns adverse price-movement handling around staking-related actions.

Staking proxy attacks name the delegated-authority risk. MEV Shield and price protection describe
other risk controls in nearby transaction and staking contexts.

References: [MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)

## Reading Boundary

The term should not be read as a claim that proxy wallets are inherently unsafe. Proxy wallets are a
normal Bittensor security pattern when their authority is understood. Staking proxy attacks describe
the narrower case where delegated staking authority is misused or exposed.
