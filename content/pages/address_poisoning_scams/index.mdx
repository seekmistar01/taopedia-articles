# Address Poisoning Scams

Address poisoning is a wallet scam that uses lookalike destination addresses to take advantage of
routine transaction habits. In Bittensor, this matters because
[wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) sign transactions, access TAO, and manage
stake, so a copied or selected address must be the destination the user actually intended to use.
The Bittensor
[address-poisoning guide](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/address-poisoning-scams) frames the
risk as a wallet-safety problem rather than a failure of address validity.

## How address poisoning works

The risk is not that a Bittensor address stops being cryptographically valid. The
[address-poisoning guide](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/address-poisoning-scams) warns about
similar-looking addresses that can appear familiar during routine wallet use, especially when a
wallet or explorer shortens long addresses for display. A user who trusts the familiar-looking entry
may send to the wrong destination.

## Why Bittensor users should care

Bittensor [wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) are used for signing transactions,
accessing TAO, and managing stake in subnets. That makes address review part of wallet safety rather
than a cosmetic detail. Address poisoning belongs next to [[wallets|Wallets]] and
[[wallets_coldkey_hotkey|Bittensor's wallet/key model]] because a mistaken destination can affect
value-bearing wallet actions.

## Wallet history and lookalike risk

Address poisoning targets a simple habit: copying or selecting a destination from recent wallet
history without checking the full address. The
[Bittensor guide](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/address-poisoning-scams) connects that risk
to recent activity and abbreviated address display. A lookalike entry can feel trustworthy because
it appears near legitimate activity, but the visual similarity is the trap. The safer mental model
is to treat wallet history as a convenience layer, not as proof that a destination is correct.

## Verification concepts

At a concept level, address-poisoning defense is about destination verification. The
[address-poisoning guide](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/address-poisoning-scams) points back
to checking the destination before acting on a transaction. The important questions are whether the
destination came from a trusted record, whether the full address was checked, and whether the user
is relying on abbreviated display text or recent-history convenience. Those concepts apply before
the transaction is signed, not after it has already been sent.

## Recovery expectations

Wrong-destination wallet transactions should be treated as serious because later intervention may
not be available. The
[address-poisoning guide](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/address-poisoning-scams) warns that
outside organizations may not be able to recover tokens lost through theft, scam, or accidental key
loss. That warning is why the topic is best understood as preventive wallet safety.
