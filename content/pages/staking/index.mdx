# Staking

Staking is the process of attaching TAO support to a validator in Bittensor
([Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).
It is process vocabulary. Stake is the related amount vocabulary after support is attached
([Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake)).

The term should stay tied to validator support. It is not a validator recommendation, balance
report, fee explanation, or full staking guide.

## Validator Support

Staking is read through the relationship between a TAO holder and a validator. Delegation describes
staking TAO to a validator, while staking is the broader process name
([Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

This makes staking different from the validator role itself. The validator performs validation;
staking attaches support to that validator's subnet position.

That distinction also keeps staking separate from the resulting stake amount. The process attaches
support; the amount records the support after the process has happened.

## Stake and Stake Weight

Staking and stake are closely related but not identical. Staking names the process, while stake
names the associated token amount after support is attached to a validator position
([Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake),
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking)).

Stake weight is another related term. It is the interpreted stake measure used for validator
consensus power and emissions in a subnet
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)).

The useful boundary is process, amount, and weighting: staking attaches support, stake names the
attached amount, and stake weight is the downstream consensus measure.

## Consensus Context

Staking matters because validator stake contributes to consensus and emissions. Bittensor consensus
uses validator signals and stake context when calculating subnet emissions
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That does not turn staking into an emissions calculation. Staking names the support process; reward
outcomes still depend on validator behavior, subnet context, and the broader emission system.

## Autostaking Context

Staking and autostaking describe related paths for attaching support. Staking is the general process
of attaching TAO support to a validator, while autostaking routes miner emissions into a staking
position automatically
([Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking)).

The distinction is trigger rather than destination. Autostaking routes proceeds into a staking
position; it does not create a separate validator-support category.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For staking on a
subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret pool conversions,
delegation support, and stake-weight influence examples.

In localnet, staking examples can be tested in an isolated environment. Local validator support and
pool reserves reflect local chain configuration rather than production delegation state.

On testnet, staking can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet stake readings on a
selected netuid are separate from mainnet metagraph and pool state
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

On mainnet, staking is the live process of attaching TAO support to validators on the production
Bittensor network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A staking example from one environment should not be read
as representing production delegation behavior on another network.

## Staking Converts TAO Through Subnet Pool Reserves

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) describes staking and
unstaking as conversions that pass through subnet pool reserves. On a subnet such as netuid 1, TAO
delegated to a validator moves through that subnet's TAO and
[alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) reserves rather than as a
fixed one-for-one exchange
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

That pool path keeps staking in tokenomics vocabulary alongside consensus support. Staking attaches
support to a validator, while the pool conversion explains why received subnet-denominated stake can
differ from a static pre-trade estimate
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Slippage Can Change the Received Staking Amount

The [Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage) names the gap
between expected and received amounts when a conversion moves through subnet pool reserves. Staking
documentation treats that gap as part of ordinary pool behavior rather than as a separate fee class.

[Price protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) sits beside slippage as a
response when measured movement exceeds a selected tolerance. Slippage explains the conversion
outcome; price protection explains how the staking action proceeds once movement is measured
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Stake Weight Scales Validator Influence in Consensus

[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)
documentation describes stake weight as the influence measure used when Yuma Consensus merges
validator signals on a subnet. Staking therefore feeds consensus power as well as delegation support
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The distinction keeps process and influence separate. Staking names attaching TAO support; stake
weight names how strongly that supported validator's signals count inside the aggregation step that
follows weight submission.

## Stake Names the Attached Amount

Staking is action vocabulary and [stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake)
is amount vocabulary. A holder performs staking to support a validator; the validator's stake is the
resulting quantity that can include both own and delegated support
([Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking)).

[Delegate stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate-stake) and
[effective stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#effective-stake) answer
follow-up amount questions about validator-supplied TAO versus nominated support from others.

## Unstaking Reverses the Support Path

[Unstaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#unstaking) names removing previously
staked TAO from a validator position. Staking increases delegated or self-staked support behind a
hotkey; unstaking reduces it. They belong to the same staking lifecycle but point in opposite
directions on the same subnet relationship
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

## Staking Converts TAO Through the Subnet Pool on Alpha Subnets

On alpha subnets, documentation describes staking as passing through the subnet's liquidity pool
rather than placing raw TAO directly on a hotkey. TAO enters the pool and is converted into subnet
alpha before the staked position is recorded behind the validator
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation#staking-into-subnets),
[Understanding Subnets: Liquidity Pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools)).

For readers, this is why staking is always subnet-local. The conversion happens in that subnet's
pool, so the same holder staking on two subnets ends up with two separate alpha-denominated
positions
([Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha)).

Root subnet staking is the contrasting path documentation keeps in TAO. Naming the subnet tells a
reader whether the position is root-side TAO or subnet-side alpha after pool conversion
([Glossary: Root Subnet/Subnet Zero](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero)).

## Distinction from Delegation

Staking names attaching TAO support to a validator hotkey. Delegation names the support relationship
by which delegated TAO is recorded behind that hotkey
([Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation)).

Staking is the action; delegation is the recorded support relationship.

## Distinction from Effective Stake

Effective stake names the combined delegate-owned and delegated TAO behind a hotkey. Staking names
the act of adding that support
([Glossary: Effective Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#effective-stake)).

Readers comparing hotkey totals should use effective stake after support is recorded.

## Distinction from Stake

Stake names the amount of tokens delegated to a validator. Staking names the process of attaching
that support ([Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake)).

Stake is the quantity; staking is the action that creates or changes it.

## Distinction from Liquidity Positions

Liquidity positions commit range-based TAO and alpha depth inside a subnet pool. Staking converts
TAO through the pool into a delegated validator position
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Pool-depth supply and validator support are separate entry paths.

## Distinction from Slippage

Slippage names the conversion gap when TAO moves through a subnet pool. Staking names the delegation
action that triggers that pool move
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Staking names intent; slippage names the received-amount difference on the conversion.

## Distinction from Dividends

Dividends name validator-side rewards from consensus outcomes. Staking names how TAO support is
attached behind a validator hotkey
([Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Support attachment and reward payout are separate questions on the same netuid.

## Distinction from Delegate Stake

Delegate stake names only the TAO a validator stakes from their own holdings. Staking names the
broader act of attaching support behind a hotkey
([Glossary: Delegate Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate-stake)).

Delegate stake is validator-supplied TAO; staking can also describe delegated support from others.

## Distinction from Token Bridging

Token bridging concerns EVM-side token movement and representation. Staking is native TAO support
attached to a validator hotkey on Bittensor
([Token Bridging](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/evm-tutorials/bridge-vtao)).

Bridging and staking are separate operations even when vTAO links them conceptually.

## Distinction from Moving Stake

Staking adds new TAO to a validator's hotkey on a subnet, while moving stake rearranges stake that
is already delegated, re-pointing the delegation to a different hotkey and/or subnet
([Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Staking brings new value into a position; moving stake re-points an existing attachment without
adding fresh TAO.

## Distinction from TAO Reserve

Staking routes TAO into subnet alpha through the paired reserves, which changes TAO reserve
balances, while the TAO reserve is the pool quantity that action shifts
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Staking names the delegation action; TAO reserve names the pool quantity that action can move.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Staking names the process of committing TAO to subnet validators or liquidity positions. Yuma
Consensus is the separate mechanism that converts validator weight submissions into emission shares
each tempo
([Staking and Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Staking** — committing TAO to validators or subnet pools.
- **Yuma Consensus** — mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

Staking names the process of attaching TAO support to a validator, not a validator recommendation,
balance report, fee explanation, or complete staking guide
([Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake)).

Staking attaches support; stake names the attached amount; stake weight is the downstream consensus
measure. [Autostaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking) routes miner emissions
into a staking position but does not create a separate validator-support category.
