# Staking vs Liquidity Provision

Staking and liquidity provision are two different ways a TAO holder can put capital to work in a
subnet, for example netuid 1. They are easy to confuse because both involve committing TAO to a
subnet, but they earn from different sources and carry different risks, so it helps to read them
side by side
([Staking and Delegation Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

## Staking: Earning From Emissions

Staking places TAO behind a validator. In return, the staker earns a share of the rewards that
validator produces, in proportion to its stake and after the validator's take. The income source is
the subnet's emission flowing through validation, so a staker's outcome tracks the validator's
performance and the value of the position held
([Staking and Delegation Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That emission path runs through
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus), which turns validator
evaluation into miner and validator-side rewards before delegated stake receives its share. Staking
therefore ties returns to consensus-mediated emissions rather than to swap fees.

## Liquidity Provision: Earning From Fees

Providing liquidity instead supplies a subnet's pool so others can trade between TAO and alpha. The
income source is trading fees collected from activity in the provider's price range, not emissions.
The matching risk is impermanent loss, the exposure to price drift a provider carries while its
capital sits in the pool
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## The Trade-off

The two are not the same bet. Staking rewards come from emissions and depend on a validator and on
consensus; liquidity rewards come from trading fees and depend on how much trading flows through a
provider's range. Their risks differ in the same way: a staker is exposed to validator performance,
while a provider is exposed to price drift. Reading them together clarifies that committing TAO to a
subnet is not a single action but a choice between distinct roles
([Staking and Delegation Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Staking Converts TAO Through the Subnet Pool

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) documentation describes
staking on an alpha subnet as a conversion that passes through the subnet's paired TAO and
[alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) reserves. Placing TAO
behind a validator moves value through the pool rather than bypassing it.

Liquidity provision also commits capital to the same pool, but through a position range rather than
a validator hotkey. Staking enters subnet activity as delegated support that converts through the
automated market maker, while liquidity provision supplies tradable reserves for others to use
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Liquidity Fees Come From Trading Activity in the Range

[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions) documentation
explains that providers earn fees from swaps and related activity that pass through their chosen
price range. The income depends on how much trading volume flows through that range, not on the
subnet's emission schedule.

A staker earns from emission routed through validation and delegation, while a liquidity provider
earns when other participants trade through the supplied range. The emissions-versus-fees
distinction is therefore tied to two different documented payment paths.

## Root Subnet Staking Stays in TAO Units

[Understanding Subnets: Root subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#root-subnet)
documentation notes that stake on the root subnet is recorded in
[TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau), while stake on other subnets is
recorded in that subnet's alpha units after TAO enters the pool. The comparison between staking and
liquidity provision therefore depends on which subnet is in scope.

On an alpha subnet such as netuid 1, staking and liquidity provision both interact with
alpha-denominated pool mechanics. On the root subnet, staking follows the documented TAO-denominated
path instead, which is a separate context from alpha-subnet liquidity provision
([Glossary: Root Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For staking versus
liquidity provision, that sequence changes which subnet pool and emission context a reader should
assume when comparing the two roles
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

In localnet, staking and liquidity examples can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet
validator performance and pool activity do not represent production returns.

On testnet, both paths can be exercised on a shared non-production network. Testnet emissions and
trading volume are separate from mainnet subnet state
([Staking and Delegation Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

On mainnet, staking draws from live validator emissions and liquidity provision earns from live pool
fees on the selected [netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid).

A staking-versus-liquidity example from one environment should not be read as describing production
outcomes on another network.

## Distinction from Slippage

Staking and liquidity provision are roles for deploying TAO in a subnet, while slippage is a cost
that appears when value converts through the subnet pool. Slippage documentation describes staking
on an alpha subnet as a conversion that passes through the pool's paired TAO and alpha reserves,
where the executed rate can move against a conversion as it passes through those reserves
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Staking and Delegation Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

- **Staking / liquidity provision** — roles for deploying TAO in a subnet.
- **Slippage** — the price impact incurred when value converts through the pool's reserves.

## Distinction from Impermanent Loss

Liquidity provision is the role that earns trading fees, while impermanent loss is the risk that
role carries rather than a separate way to earn. Liquidity-position documentation describes
impermanent loss as the exposure to price drift a provider carries while its capital sits in the
pool ([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Liquidity provision** — supplying pool reserves to earn trading fees.
- **Impermanent loss** — the price-drift risk a provider carries while capital is in the pool.

## Distinction from Staking

Staking versus liquidity provision compares two ways TAO holders can deploy capital, while staking
names attaching TAO support to validators in the delegation path
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking)).

- **Staking vs liquidity provision** — comparison article for two capital-deployment paths.
- **Staking** — attaching TAO support to validators.

## Distinction from Liquidity Positions

This comparison article and liquidity positions differ in scope. Liquidity positions name the
pool-side positions holders take in subnet liquidity pools, while this article contrasts that path
with validator staking rather than defining pool mechanics alone
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Glossary: Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#liquidity-positions)).

- **Staking vs liquidity provision** — side-by-side comparison of staking and LP paths.
- **Liquidity positions** — pool-side position vocabulary in Dynamic TAO markets.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Staking versus liquidity provision names two ways TAO can support subnet tokenomics. Staking commits
TAO toward validators and delegation markets; liquidity provision supplies alpha in subnet pools
([Staking and Delegation Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Staking vs liquidity provision** — two TAO support paths in subnets.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement that turns validator weights into emission shares.
  Staking and liquidity provision name support paths; Yuma processes validator weights into rewards
  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).

## Reader Boundary

This page compares the two mechanisms at a high level and is not financial advice. Which one suits a
given holder, and what either actually returns, depends on goals, risk tolerance, and live
conditions such as validator performance, trading activity, and prices, all of which change over
time. The durable point is the distinction: emissions through staking versus fees through liquidity.
