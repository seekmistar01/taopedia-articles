# Sybil Attack

A Sybil attack is an attempt by a single actor to gain outsized influence over a network by creating
many separate identities that appear independent. In a Bittensor subnet, that would mean one party
registering many neurons or hotkeys, for example on a subnet such as netuid 1, hoping that
controlling more identities translates into controlling more of the rewards or consensus
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## What It Would Target

The goal of a Sybil attack is leverage from numbers. In Bittensor terms, an attacker holding many
UIDs might hope to sway the weights that feed consensus, or to capture a larger share of emissions
than an honest single participant. The attack only works if having more identities, on its own, buys
more influence
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## Why Identity Count Alone Does Not Win

Bittensor weights influence by stake rather than by headcount. The documentation describes Yuma
Consensus using the stake associated with each UID when it calculates emissions, so a crowd of
low-stake identities carries little weight. Gaining real influence still requires proportional
stake, which cannot be conjured simply by registering more keys. This stake weighting is a core
reason identity flooding does not pay off
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## The Cost of Each Identity

Numbers also are not free. Registering a neuron carries an economic cost, since registration
consumes TAO, so every additional identity an attacker creates has a real price. Combined with stake
weighting, this means a Sybil attempt pays a growing cost for identities that, without matching
stake, add little influence. The economics are arranged so that flooding a subnet with identities is
expensive and weak rather than cheap and powerful
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

## Identity Attacks, Collusion Resistance, and Yuma Anti-Gaming Pipeline

Sybil resistance sits in a broader incentive-defense pipeline on a subnet such as netuid 1. An
identity-flooding actor tries to multiply
[UID slots](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot); a
[collusion](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism) actor instead
tries to coordinate
[validator weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector) toward favored
miners. The two threats use different tools and should not be read as the same attack pattern
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

The useful pipeline order is therefore registration cost and slot ceilings at entry, stake-weighted
validator signal submission, [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
aggregation with consensus clipping against inflated weights, and tempo-bound
[emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) distribution. Sybil flooding is blunted
upstream by per-identity TAO cost and downstream by stake weighting; collusion is blunted by
clipping and stake requirements inside the same consensus pass
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

A Sybil actor therefore pays for each identity without automatically gaining proportional consensus
influence. A colluding validator group must still hold enough stake to move the stake-weighted
agreement point and survive clipping before extra reward can land at the tempo boundary
([Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)).

## Each Successful Registration Raises the Next Identity's Price

The per-identity cost described above is not a flat fee. The registration burn is bounded by
documented `MinBurn` and `MaxBurn` parameters, decays over time when registrations are quiet, and
rises with each successful registration through a documented increase multiplier
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

That rising-price behavior targets exactly the Sybil pattern: an actor registering many identities
in quick succession drives the price of each subsequent identity higher, on top of stake weighting
already limiting what those identities can achieve once registered.

## Subnet Slot Ceilings Cap Total UID Count

Each subnet has a configurable maximum number of UID slots. The
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters) reference
includes `max_allowed_uids` as that ceiling. A Sybil actor cannot accumulate unlimited on-subnet
identities without either winning open slots or displacing existing participants under documented
[deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration) rules
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

That capacity limit is separate from stake weighting. Even before emissions are calculated, the slot
ceiling bounds how many registrations can coexist on one subnet at once
([Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot)).

## Neuron Lifecycle Includes a Post-Registration Immunity Window

[Understanding Neurons](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/neurons) lists registration and UID
assignment as the first lifecycle steps, followed by an
[immunity period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period) during which a
newly registered neuron is protected from ordinary pruning. Performance metrics build after that
grace window rather than at the instant of registration.

Each additional identity an attacker registers therefore pays its own entry cost and enters with the
same post-registration immunity rules as any other neuron. This page does not treat that window as a
complete Sybil defense on its own
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For a Sybil attack
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how the economic resistance should be read,
because registration cost and stake only carry production weight on mainnet
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

In localnet, registering many identities happens in an isolated environment with no production cost,
so identity flooding there demonstrates mechanics rather than real-world resistance on netuid 1.

On testnet, registrations and stake exist on a shared non-production network whose TAO is not
mainnet value, so the cost of identities is not a production economic barrier.

On mainnet, each registered identity consumes real TAO and influence is weighted by real stake, so
the cost-and-stake resistance described here applies to live subnets such as netuid 1
([Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

The strength of Sybil resistance observed in one environment should not be read as the resistance on
another.

## Stake Weight Limits Influence After Each Registration

Documentation treats subnet entry cost and validator weight submission as sequential steps.
Registration makes each identity expensive to create, while stake-weighted weights and Yuma clipping
limit how much any one identity can affect consensus outcomes after it exists
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight)).

A Sybil actor therefore cannot assume that registering many low-stake neurons automatically produces
proportional validator agreement. Consensus aggregates stake rather than raw UID count when
emissions are finalized at tempo boundaries
([Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Distinction from Burn Cost

A Sybil attack and the burn cost meet at the economic barrier to fake identities. A Sybil attack is
one actor registering many neurons to gain outsized influence, while the burn cost is the TAO each
registration burns, so the per-registration burn cost makes mass identity creation expensive and
blunts the attack
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

Root-network stake and subnet registration sit on different layers. A Sybil actor on a subnet such
as netuid 1 still pays a per-identity registration burn for each UID slot it tries to occupy, even
when the same operator holds root stake elsewhere
([Glossary: Root](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root),
[Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

For readers, root stake changes validator influence in the root subnet context, while Sybil flooding
on a child subnet still multiplies entry burns at the subnet registration boundary
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration)).

## Distinction from Collusion

A Sybil attack and collusion sit in the same anti-gaming pipeline but use different tools. A Sybil
actor multiplies identities at registration, hoping that more UID slots buy more influence, while a
colluding group coordinates validator weights toward favored miners inside an existing participant
set. Identity flooding raises entry cost per UID; coordinated weighting raises the stake required to
move the agreement point and survive clipping
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

For readers, the defenses also split by stage. Registration burn, slot ceilings, and stake weighting
blunt Sybil flooding upstream, while consensus clipping and stake thresholds blunt collusion inside
the weight-aggregation pass. Similar-looking bad outcomes can therefore come from different exploit
patterns until actor count and registration history are included
([Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot),
[Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight)).

- **Sybil attack** — one actor registering many identities to seek outsized influence.
- **Collusion** — multiple validators coordinating inflated weights toward chosen miners.

## Distinction from Self-Weighting

A Sybil attack and self-weighting both try to capture more reward than honest participation would
allow, but they scale influence differently. Sybil flooding adds identities at the registration
boundary, while self-weighting is one validator inflating weights for entities it already controls
without multiplying registrations
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus: Clipping excess weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#clipping-excess-weights)).

For readers, Sybil resistance is mostly economic at entry — burn cost, slot limits, and low-stake
headcount — whereas self-weighting is resisted inside consensus clipping after the validator already
holds a permit. A lone self-favoring validator lacks coalition stake; a Sybil actor lacks automatic
stake per new identity even after paying each burn
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

- **Sybil attack** — multiplying identities at registration to seek leverage from numbers.
- **Self-weighting** — one validator inflating weights for its own side without a coordinating
  group.

## Distinction from Max Allowed UIDs

A Sybil attack and max allowed UIDs meet at subnet capacity, but they name different sides of the
same ceiling. A Sybil actor tries to occupy as many UID slots as possible, while max allowed UIDs is
the owner-settable hyperparameter that caps how many neurons can coexist on the subnet at once. The
ceiling bounds how large an identity-flooding attempt can grow even before stake weighting is
applied ([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot)).

For readers, max allowed UIDs is a protocol limit on population size, not an attack pattern. A Sybil
actor still competes for open slots and documented
[deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration) displacement
rules when the subnet is full, so slot pressure and per-identity burn cost work together rather than
either one replacing the other
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

- **Sybil attack** — flooding a subnet with identities to seek disproportionate influence.
- **Max allowed UIDs** — the hyperparameter ceiling on how many UIDs a subnet may hold.

## Distinction from Register

Sybil attack is when one actor creates many identities to gain disproportionate influence. Register
is the subnet-entry process that assigns a UID slot after a participant pays the registration burn
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

Subnet registration rules make each entry consume a UID slot and a dynamic burn on netuid 1. Each
additional identity an attacker registers therefore raises its own burn price. A newly registered
identity also starts with an immunity window before pruning applies. Sybil attack names the
multi-identity threat, while register names the controlled entry path that limits new identities
through rising cost
([Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration),
[Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

- **Sybil attack** — flooding a subnet with identities to seek disproportionate influence.
- **Register** — the subnet-entry process that assigns a UID slot after the registration burn.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

A sybil attack names an attempt to gain disproportionate influence by controlling many identities.
Yuma Consensus is the separate mechanism that aggregates stake-weighted validator signals and clips
outliers before converting weights into emission shares each tempo
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Sybil attack** — many identities used to gain disproportionate influence.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement that turns validator weights into emission shares. Sybil
  attack names identity flooding risk; Yuma processes validator weights into rewards
  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).

- **Consensus score** — benchmark Yuma uses when clipping out-of-consensus weights.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept and Bittensor's economic resistance at a high level. It does not claim
attacks are impossible, quantify any specific defense, or describe how to attempt one. The exact
registration cost and stake distribution that determine how strong the resistance is on any subnet
are live chain state and vary over time
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## Stake Weight Caps Influence After Registration

Documentation separates identity cost at registration from stake-weighted influence inside
consensus. A Sybil actor may register many neurons, but validator weights and Yuma aggregation still
scale influence by stake rather than by raw UID count
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight)).

For readers, that is why Sybil resistance is a pipeline rather than a single gate. Registration burn
and slot ceilings raise the price of identities, while stake weighting limits how much each identity
can move emissions even after it exists on the metagraph
([Subnet Metagraph](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/metagraph),
[Understanding Neurons](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/neurons)).

Collusion resistance sits later in the same pipeline when validators coordinate weights rather than
multiply registrations
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).
