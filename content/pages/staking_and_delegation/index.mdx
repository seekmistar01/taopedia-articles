# Staking and Delegation (TAO)

Staking and delegation describe how TAO holders attach support to validators in Bittensor.
Delegation is the holder-to-validator path, staking is the action of attaching support, and stake is
the amount associated with the resulting validator position
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking),
[Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake)).

The term is broad. It can refer to the staking area, the validator support relationship, or the
stake position that results after TAO is attached. A precise use should keep the asset, validator,
and network context visible.

## Validator Support

Stake is the amount of TAO associated with a validator position. Staking is the act of attaching
that TAO to a validator. Delegation is the common holder-to-validator path: a TAO holder supports a
validator without becoming that validator
([Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation),
[Glossary: Subnet Validator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-validator)).

Related staking terms name different parts of that relationship. A nominator delegates to a
validator. Delegate stake names stake supplied by a delegate validator itself. Effective stake is
the combined stake measure used when self-stake and delegated support are considered
([Glossary: Nominator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominator),
[Glossary: Delegate Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate-stake),
[Glossary: Effective Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#effective-stake)).

## Rewards and Stake Measures

Staking does not promise a fixed return. Validator dividends depend on the network's emission and
consensus mechanics, validator behavior, and the stake relationship around a validator
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Stake also differs from stake weight. Stake names the validator-associated amount, while stake
weight is the interpreted measure used for consensus and emission context
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)).

The useful boundary is action versus amount versus weighting. Staking attaches support, stake names
the attached amount, and stake weight is the downstream consensus measure.

## Root and Alpha Paths

Staking and delegation can describe more than one staking path. Root Subnet, also called Subnet
Zero, is the subnet-agnostic staking venue for root validators and TAO holders. An alpha staker
belongs to the subnet-local alpha-token side of staking rather than the Root Subnet side
([Glossary: Root Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution),
[Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha)).

Root claims are downstream of Root Subnet staking. Alpha dividends earned through root stake can be
swapped into TAO or kept as subnet alpha, and that claim-handling step is separate from the initial
act of staking to a validator
([Root Claim Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

## Automatic Routing

Autostaking is related to staking and delegation but names a different route into a validator
position. Staking and delegation begins with a TAO holder who decides to commit TAO to support a
validator. Autostaking routes a miner's own emission proceeds to a validator position automatically
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Autostaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)).

Both paths can result in TAO staked to a validator, but the source differs. One names the holder's
staking path; the other names the miner's automatic emission routing.

## Unstaking Versus Moving Stake

Unstaking and moving stake both change an existing validator support position, but they name
different outcomes. On an alpha subnet, unstaking withdraws delegated stake and converts subnet
alpha back toward TAO through the subnet pool on the way to the coldkey; Root Subnet positions are
already held in TAO, so that conversion step does not apply there. Moving stake reassigns support to
another validator or subnet without first returning TAO to the coldkey
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Unstaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#unstaking)).

The distinction keeps exit language separate from rearrangement language. A holder who wants TAO
back in unstaked form is describing unstaking. A holder who keeps support committed but attached to
a different validator is describing moving stake instead.

## Wallet and Environment Scope

Staking actions are tied to wallet authority. The validator receives stake support, while the holder
controls the wallet authority used to add, move, or remove that support
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Managing Your Stakes](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/managing-stake-sdk)).

Choosing a validator, evaluating risk, and monitoring rewards remain separate decisions. They should
not be implied by the definition of staking and delegation itself.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For staking and
delegation on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes which network a reader should assume
when interpreting TAO support attached to validators.

In localnet, staking and delegation flows can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet stake
positions on netuid 1 reflect that test chain's validator relationships rather than production
outcomes.

On testnet, holders can delegate on a shared non-production network. Testnet stake and emission
context for netuid 1 are separate from mainnet state
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

On mainnet, staking and delegation concern live production TAO attached to validators on the
connected network, including Root Subnet and alpha-subnet paths such as netuid 1
([Glossary: Root Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A staking example from one environment should not be read
as describing production stake outcomes on another network.

## Distinction from Stake

Staking and delegation describe actions and relationships, while stake names the amount associated
with a validator position after support is attached
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake),
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking)).

- **Staking and delegation** — how TAO holders attach support to validators.
- **Stake** — the TAO amount tied to the resulting validator position.

A holder delegates TAO to a validator, which increases the validator's supported stake and feeds
into [effective stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#effective-stake) when
self-stake and delegated support are combined
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

On an alpha subnet such as netuid 1, that support path passes through subnet pool reserves when TAO
is converted into subnet alpha for the validator position
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)). Emission then reaches
the validator first, validator take is applied, and the remainder returns to nominators in
proportion to their delegated stake ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Validator dividends and nominator returns therefore sit downstream of delegation and take rules,
while [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) names the shared
aggregation step that turns validator weight signals into subnet incentive outcomes on alpha
subnets. Staking vocabulary names the support relationship; consensus vocabulary names how
validator-side signals are processed after that support is in place.

## Distinction from Stake Weight

Staking and delegation are upstream of stake weight. Staking attaches support and stake names the
amount, while stake weight is the downstream consensus measure derived from that staking context
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)).

- **Staking and delegation** — holder-to-validator support actions.
- **Stake weight** — computed influence used in consensus and emission context.

## Distinction from Validator Take

Staking and delegation describe attaching support, while validator take describes how the resulting
emissions are split. Validator take is the commission a validator keeps from delegated-stake
emissions before the remainder returns to stakers in proportion to their stake
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Validator Take %](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-take-)).

- **Staking and delegation** — attaching support to a validator.
- **Validator take** — the validator's commission on delegated-stake emissions.

The staking overview describes emissions reaching a validator first and then returning to delegators
after the validator extracts its take, so staking attaches support while take and proportional
returns describe how emission proceeds are split afterward
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Validator Take %](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-take-)).

## Distinction from Autostaking

Staking and delegation begin with a holder choosing to commit TAO to a validator, while autostaking
routes a miner's own emission proceeds to a validator position automatically
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Autostaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)).

- **Staking and delegation** — a holder committing TAO to a validator.
- **Autostaking** — automatic routing of a miner's emissions to a validator.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Staking and delegation name how TAO holders attach support to validators without becoming validators
themselves. Delegation is the common holder path, while staking names the act of committing that
support behind a validator hotkey
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation)).

- **Staking and delegation** — attaching TAO support to validators.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement that turns validator weights into emission shares.
  Staking and delegation attach TAO support; Yuma processes validator weights into rewards
  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).

## Reader Boundary

Staking and delegation describe how TAO holders attach support to validators. The terms are not
fixed-return promises, validator rankings, or complete operator guides
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation)).

Staking attaches support; stake names the amount; stake weight is the downstream consensus measure.
Root Subnet TAO staking and alpha-subnet staking follow different token paths
([Glossary: Root Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero),
[Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha)).
