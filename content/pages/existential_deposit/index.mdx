# Existential Deposit

Existential deposit is the minimum TAO balance threshold for retaining a Bittensor account. The
official [Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit) defines
it as the minimum amount of TAO required for an account to exist.

## Dust-Account Boundary

The glossary explains that accounts below this threshold can be removed to conserve resources and
avoid dust accounts. For readers, the important point is that existential deposit is an
account-retention threshold. It is not a transaction fee, a staking amount, or a participant score.

## Amount Boundary

A static article should not state a live existential deposit amount. The useful Taopedia-level
meaning is the role of the threshold: it marks the minimum balance needed for an account to remain
represented in Bittensor account records. Live amounts belong in official references.

## Reaping Context

The
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)
explains that accounts with balances below the existential deposit threshold can be reaped — removed
from chain state — to conserve network resources and prevent dust-account buildup. This reinforces
that the threshold is an account-existence boundary rather than a staking or fee parameter.

References:
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)

## Relationship to Transfer

The existential deposit and a transfer are related but different parts of Bittensor vocabulary. The
existential deposit names the minimum balance an account must keep to remain in chain state, while a
transfer names the act of moving TAO between addresses. The
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)
describes the minimum-balance threshold, and the
[Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer) describes moving
funds between wallet addresses.

For readers, the existential deposit names a balance boundary that a transfer must respect: a
transfer that would leave an account below the threshold interacts with that account-existence rule.
One names the action of moving funds; the other names the minimum balance that action cannot quietly
ignore.

References:
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit),
[Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer)

## Relationship to TAO

Existential deposit and TAO are related but different parts of Bittensor account vocabulary. TAO
names the network token, while existential deposit names the minimum TAO balance threshold required
for an account to exist on the Bittensor blockchain. The
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)
defines the threshold in TAO terms, and the
[Glossary: TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao) describes TAO as the token
used in Bittensor incentives.

For readers, TAO names the asset, while existential deposit names the minimum account-retention
amount measured in that asset.

References:
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit),
[Glossary: TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao)

## Relationship to Wallet Address

Existential deposit and wallet address are related but different parts of Bittensor wallet
vocabulary. A wallet address identifies where TAO can be sent, while existential deposit names the
minimum balance boundary that determines whether an account remains represented in chain state. The
[Glossary: Wallet Address](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#wallet-address)
describes wallet addresses as TAO destinations, and the
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)
describes the account-existence threshold.

For readers, wallet address names the destination identifier, while existential deposit names a
balance condition that can affect whether the account behind that destination remains in state.

References:
[Glossary: Wallet Address](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#wallet-address),
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)

## Relationship to Subtensor

Existential deposit and Subtensor are related but different scopes in Bittensor chain vocabulary.
Subtensor names the layer 1 blockchain and system of record, while existential deposit names an
account-retention rule within that chain state. The
[Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor) describes the
blockchain layer, and the
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)
explains that accounts below the threshold can be reaped to conserve network resources.

For readers, Subtensor names the chain layer, while existential deposit names one balance threshold
that affects account state on that layer.

References: [Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor),
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)

## Relationship to Subtensor Constants

Existential deposit and Subtensor constants are related because the threshold is exposed as a named
runtime constant. The official
[Subtensor Constants](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/constants) reference lists
`balances.existentialDeposit` as the minimum amount required to keep an account open, while the
glossary explains the same concept as the minimum TAO amount required for an account to exist.

For readers, existential deposit names the account-existence concept, while Subtensor constants name
the runtime-reference place where the current constant is exposed. A static article should explain
the concept rather than hard-code a live value.

References: [Subtensor Constants](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/constants),
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)

## Reader Boundary

Existential deposit should be read as a balance-threshold concept, not as account-management advice.
This article does not describe operational steps, live balances, or recovery behavior for accounts
below the threshold.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
existential deposit, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about account
balance thresholds.

In local testing, the existential deposit threshold applies inside an isolated environment. That is
useful for checking account-existence mechanics, but the threshold value and account behavior in a
local environment may differ from production network parameters.

On testchain, existential deposit behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives
stronger evidence about how the minimum balance threshold interacts with account lifecycle on-chain
than a private local run, while still keeping the result separate from production mainchain account
rules.

On mainchain, the existential deposit is the live minimum TAO balance threshold required for
Subtensor account existence. The
[Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)
defines it as the minimum TAO amount required for an account to exist, so production context is tied
to actual chain parameters on the active network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An existential deposit threshold observed in one
environment should not be read as identical evidence about account balance rules in another
environment.

For readers, this keeps existential deposit examples from sounding stronger than their environment
supports. Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all describe account-balance thresholds, but
they do not carry the same interpretive weight.
