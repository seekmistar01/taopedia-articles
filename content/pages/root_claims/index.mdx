# Root Claims

Root claims handle alpha dividends earned through Root Subnet staking. They decide whether earned
alpha remains as subnet alpha or is converted into TAO and restaked on Root
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The mechanism is narrow. It is about dividend treatment after rewards accrue, not about moving the
original root stake or explaining a general staking procedure.

## Keep and Swap Outcomes

Root claims have two outcomes. Keep leaves earned alpha as the alpha of the subnet that produced it.
Swap converts the earned alpha into TAO and restakes that dividend output on Root
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Both outcomes apply to dividends, not to the original root stake. This keeps root claims separate
from unstaking, moving stake, or changing delegation.

That distinction is important when reading root-staking material: a claim changes the treatment of
earned dividend output, while the root stake remains the staking position that produced access to
those dividends.

## Claim Type Context

Root claim type is the preference that determines which outcome a root claim uses. Keep and Swap are
routing choices for dividend output, while a root claim is the handling of accumulated dividends
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Glossary: Root Subnet/Subnet Zero](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero)).

The important concept is the dividend outcome: Keep preserves alpha exposure from the producing
subnet, while Swap routes the dividend into TAO on Root.

## Claim Processing

Root claims can be processed automatically by the chain or manually through the root-claim extrinsic
path. In both cases, the configured Keep or Swap preference is what determines how the claim treats
accumulated alpha dividends
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims)).

This keeps the processing method separate from the outcome choice. Automatic and manual claims
describe how a claim is triggered, while Keep and Swap describe what happens to the dividend output.

## Emission Scope

Root-claim swaps sit inside Bittensor emission accounting. They handle dividend output, while
flow-based emissions track subnet inflows and outflows for emission distribution
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

This keeps root claims narrower than emissions overall. Root claims explain what happens to
dividends from Root Subnet staking after they accrue; they do not describe every reward distribution
mechanism in Bittensor.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For root claims,
that sequence changes how readers should interpret alpha-dividend handling and Root Subnet staking
examples.

In localnet, root-claim examples can be exercised in an isolated environment. Local claim settings
and dividend outcomes reflect local chain configuration rather than production Root Subnet state.

On testnet, root claims can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet Root Subnet
dividend context is separate from mainnet staking and claim history
([Root Claim Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims)).

On mainnet, root claims describe live dividend-handling choices for Root Subnet staking on the
production Bittensor network
([Glossary: Root Subnet/Subnet Zero](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A root-claim example from one environment should not be
read as representing production dividend outcomes in another environment.

## Original Root Stake Stays Staked in Both Modes

[Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims) documentation
states that the initial TAO staked to a validator on the
[Root Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero) remains
staked on root in both Keep and Swap modes. Only the alpha dividends earned from other subnets are
routed differently.

Root claims vocabulary therefore applies to dividend handling, not to moving the underlying root
stake position that produced those dividends.

## Swap Claims Do Not Count as Subnet TAO Outflows

The same root-claims reference notes that Swap-mode conversions do not count as TAO outflows for
flow-based [emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) calculations on the subnet
that produced the alpha. Claiming and converting alpha dividends to TAO therefore does not, by
itself, reduce that subnet's net-flow allocation signal.

Keep mode instead accumulates alpha on the producing subnet, while Swap mode restakes the converted
TAO on root. The emission exception applies to the Swap path's accounting rather than to every
root-staker action.

## Automatic Claims Use a Minimum Dividend Threshold

Root-claims documentation describes automatic processing only when accumulated alpha dividends
exceed a minimum threshold, documented as 500,000 RAO (0.0005 TAO equivalent). Manual claims can be
triggered through the `claim_root` extrinsic, specifying up to five subnets per call.

That split keeps threshold and timing separate from the Keep-or-Swap preference. Both automatic and
manual paths apply the configured claim type once dividends are processed
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims)).

## Keep and Swap Change Dividend Exposure, Not Root Stake

Both Keep and Swap apply only to alpha dividends earned through Root Subnet staking, not to the
original TAO staked on the
[Root Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero). Keep
preserves subnet-alpha exposure from the producing subnet, while Swap converts dividend output to
TAO restaked on Root, so the two modes differ in what form rewards take after claiming rather than
whether the underlying root position remains staked
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims)).

For readers, root claims vocabulary sits between emission (which generates dividends) and root
staking (which holds the original TAO position). The claim mechanism routes dividend output; it does
not unstake or move the root stake that produced access to those dividends.

## Distinction from Root Proportion

Root claims and root proportion both concern root-subnet staking but measure different things. Root
proportion is the relative weight a validator's stake carries through the root subnet rather than
through a subnet's alpha side, while root claims handle the alpha dividends that root staking earns
— whether they stay as alpha or convert to TAO and restake on root. One measures root-routed stake
weight; the other handles the dividends it produces
([Glossary: Root Proportion](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-proportion),
[Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims)).

## Distinction from TAO Weight

Root claims and TAO weight both relate to Root Subnet staking but act on different things. Root
claims handle the alpha dividends earned through Root Subnet staking, deciding whether they stay as
alpha or convert to TAO and restake on Root, while TAO weight is the global multiplier that sets how
much root (TAO) stake counts relative to a subnet's own alpha stake when stake weight is computed
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Glossary: TAO Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-weight)).

- **Root claims** — the handling rule for alpha dividends earned through Root staking.
- **TAO weight** — the multiplier that scales how much root stake counts against alpha stake.

## Distinction from Issuance

Root claims and issuance describe a dividend-handling mechanism and a supply measure. Root claims
route the alpha dividends earned through Root Subnet staking into either alpha or TAO, while
issuance is the circulating TAO supply counted across the network, so one decides the form a reward
takes and the other counts total tokens in circulation
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

- **Root claims** — the rule that routes earned alpha dividends to alpha or TAO.
- **Issuance** — the circulating TAO supply measured network-wide.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Root claims name how earned alpha dividends are handled after root-network staking. Yuma Consensus
is the on-chain mechanism that converts validator weight submissions into emission shares each tempo
and is not a dividend claim-setting workflow
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Root-claims vocabulary belongs to dividend handling after rewards accrue. Yuma vocabulary belongs to
how validator weights become emissions in the first place.

- **Root claims** — rule for routing earned alpha dividends to alpha or TAO.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Distinction from Root Subnet

The Root Subnet, also called Subnet Zero, is the special subnet where validators can register and
TAO holders can stake in a subnet-agnostic way. Root claims exist because stake placed through that
Root Subnet path can receive alpha dividends from other subnets while the underlying stake remains
associated with the Root Subnet
([Glossary: Root Subnet/Subnet Zero](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero),
[Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims)).

That separation gives root claims their role. The original stake remains tied to Root Subnet, while
earned alpha needs a handling rule once it is claimable.

## Reader Boundary

Root claims should be read as dividend-handling vocabulary for Root Subnet staking. The claim type
sets how accumulated alpha dividends are handled after they are claimable; it does not rename the
original root stake, guarantee future dividend amounts, or describe a general unstaking action
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Glossary: Root Subnet/Subnet Zero](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero)).
