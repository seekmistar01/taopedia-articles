# Digital Commodities

Digital commodities are the useful work products that Bittensor subnets ask miners to produce. A
subnet narrows the kind of work being requested, while validators measure the quality of that work
against subnet-specific standards.
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) describes each
subnet as producing a specific kind of digital commodity related to artificial intelligence, and
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) uses the same
miner-and-validator frame.

## Subnet-defined work

A digital commodity is not one universal output across all of Bittensor. Each subnet defines its own
target work, which gives the subnet a distinct purpose inside the broader network. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) source connects
digital commodities to individual subnets, and
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) explains that
each subnet has its own goal and standards.

## Miner production

Miners are the role family that produces the requested work. The commodity can differ by subnet, so
the important article-level idea is the relationship between a subnet's request and the miner work
that answers it.
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) describes
miners as performing useful work defined by the subnet's incentive mechanism.

## Validator evaluation

Digital commodities need evaluation, not just production. Validators measure miner work according to
the subnet's standards and turn those evaluations into weights that can feed consensus. The
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)
source explains that incentive mechanisms define both the miner work and how validators evaluate
that work.

## Why the term matters

The term digital commodity keeps the focus on work output instead of only on tokens, tools, or
roles. It helps explain why subnets can differ while still sharing Bittensor's broader incentive
model: each subnet can ask for a different kind of useful work, and validators evaluate that work
under that subnet's rules. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) and
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)
sources support this work-and-evaluation frame.

## Careful interpretation

A digital commodity is not a token category or proof that every miner output is useful. It names the
work category a subnet is trying to produce and evaluate. Actual quality still depends on the
subnet's incentive mechanism and validator evaluation. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) and
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)
sources define those boundaries.

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Digital commodities are usually discussed for one incentive mechanism at a time, but subnets can
also run multiple mechanisms in parallel. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that when
multiple incentive mechanisms are used, each operates independently, allowing subnet creators to
distribute emissions across different types of work or evaluation criteria.

For readers, a digital commodity still names work a subnet asks miners to produce, but the mechanism
context matters. The work category for one mechanism should be read separately when a subnet uses
more than one evaluation system.

## Relationship to Coinbase

Digital commodities and coinbase are related but different parts of Bittensor subnet vocabulary.
Digital commodities are the useful outputs that Bittensor subnets define and miners produce — the
specific kind of AI service, compute, data, or other item a subnet is designed to generate, as
evaluated by that subnet's validators. Coinbase is the per-block protocol mechanism that accumulates
pending emissions each block, injects TAO and alpha into subnet pool reserves, and checks each
subnet's epoch boundary to trigger the Yuma Consensus round that distributes emission rewards to the
miners and validators who produced and evaluated those commodities. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) frames digital
commodities as what subnets exist to produce, and the
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes the per-block mechanism that delivers the emission result of that
production.

Digital commodities name what miners are producing and validators are evaluating; coinbase names
what drives the per-block cycle that converts those evaluations into emission outcomes. Miners
compete to produce the best commodity; validators score that production; coinbase triggers the
epoch-boundary round where those scores become TAO and alpha rewards.

References: [Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction),
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
