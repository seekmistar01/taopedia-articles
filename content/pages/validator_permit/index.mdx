# Validator Permit

A validator permit is a Bittensor flag for validation rights inside a subnet
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: VPermit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#vpermit)).

The term is scoped to the subnet where the permit is being discussed. It is not a universal
validator label across every subnet.

In plain language, a validator permit answers: does this UID hold the right to submit validator
weights in this subnet? It does not answer whether the validator is active, profitable, or highly
ranked. The permit is a binary rights flag, not a performance measure
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit)).

For a reader, the difference from stake weight matters most when interpreting a validator's subnet
position. Stake weight explains how much consensus influence the validator's stake produces; a
validator permit records only whether the weight-submission right exists in that subnet
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight)).

## Subnet Permission Flag

Validator permit is a one-subnet rights concept. It tells readers whether validation rights exist
for the relevant subnet
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: VPermit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#vpermit),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The subnet context is part of the meaning because miners and validators operate inside specific
subnet markets. A validator permit discussed for one subnet does not automatically describe the same
validator's position in another subnet.

## VPermit Aggregates Subnet Permits

Validator permit and VPermit describe nearby ideas at different scopes. Validator permit names the
one-subnet flag, while VPermit gives the broader authorization view across subnets
([Glossary: VPermit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#vpermit),
[Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit)).

For readers comparing one delegate across subnets, validator permit answers permission inside a
single subnet metagraph and VPermit summarizes where those subnet permits apply for that delegate
([Glossary: Subnet Validator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-validator)).

A VPermit list does not replace per-subnet validator permit reading. Each subnet still requires its
own permit flag check when the article focuses on weight submission rights inside one netuid.

## Top Stake Neurons Earn the Permit

The
[Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit)
describes the flag as awarded to the top neurons by
[stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight) on the subnet. It is
a rights marker tied to stake position, not a separate performance score.

Stake weight is the computed stake value used for validator consensus power and emissions inside a
subnet. That keeps permit reading separate from rank or emission outcomes. Stake weight explains
influence; the permit records whether weight submission rights exist for the subnet being discussed.

## Registration Does Not Grant the Permit

Registration and validator permit are separate subnet states. Registration places a participant on a
subnet UID slot through a registered hotkey, while validator permit describes whether validation
rights exist for that subnet
([Validating: Validator registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators#validator-registration),
[Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit)).

For readers, registration should not be read as automatic permission to validate. The permit term
answers the narrower rights question after the subnet context is known.

## Setting Weights Requires the Permit Flag

The same glossary entry states that a validator permit is required for setting weights and
participating in consensus. Registration places a participant on a subnet; the permit answers
whether that participant may submit
[validator weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector) that count in
the aggregation step
([Validating: Validator registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators#validator-registration)).

Readers should not treat registration as automatic validation rights. The permit is the binary flag
that gates weight submission inside the subnet context.

## Consensus Ignores Weights Without a Permit

Epoch documentation lists pre-consensus filtering steps that decide which submitted weights can
enter [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus). Only rows from
validators holding a validator permit influence the consensus pass
([Implementation of the Yuma Consensus Epoch](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/epoch)).

A neuron can hold a UID slot and still sit outside consensus if it lacks the permit flag. Permit
vocabulary therefore marks eligibility for weight influence, not merely subnet registration.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For the validator
permit on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret which
neurons hold the flag and the metagraph permit fields.

In localnet, validator-permit assignments can be exercised in an isolated environment. Local permit
holders reflect local chain state rather than production subnet consensus.

On testnet, validator-permit status can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet
permit assignments on a selected netuid are separate from mainnet metagraph state
([Subnet Metagraph](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/metagraph)).

On mainnet, the validator permit is the live flag marking which neurons hold weight-setting rights
in production subnet consensus on the connected Bittensor network
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A validator-permit observation from one environment should
not be read as representing production permit assignments on another network.

## The Permit Is Earned by Standing, Not Requested

A point worth making explicit is that a validator does not apply for a permit. Documentation
describes it as a flag granted to the top stake-weighted neurons on a subnet, so it is conferred by
relative standing rather than by a separate request or approval step
([Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

For readers, this is why registration alone is not enough. Registering places a neuron on the
subnet, but the permit depends on where its stake weight ranks against everyone else competing for
the limited validator slots
([Glossary: Subnet Validator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-validator)).

That standing-based grant also explains why the permit can be lost without any action by the holder.
Because it tracks relative stake weight, others gaining standing can displace a permit holder even
if its own stake is unchanged, which is what links the permit to ongoing competition rather than a
one-time grant
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit)).

## Reader Boundary

Validator permit names validation rights inside one subnet. Stake weight names stake-derived
validator influence, and VPermit names broader authorization context
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Glossary: VPermit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#vpermit)).

The term is narrow: a validator permit is the subnet-scoped rights flag, not a full validator guide
or a ranking of subnet performance.

## Relationship to Max Validators

Validator permit and max validators are related but different subnet terms. A validator permit is
the flag that grants weight-setting rights to higher-stake neurons, while max validators is the
hyperparameter that caps how many of those permits can exist on the subnet at once
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

For readers, the permit names which neurons may set weights, while max validators names the ceiling
the permit system fills. The cap bounds how many neurons end up validating on the subnet
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

## Relationship to Activity Cutoff

A validator permit and activity cutoff are two different gates on whether a validator's weights
count in a subnet's consensus pass. The permit is a permission flag that records whether a UID may
submit validator weights, while activity cutoff is the early-epoch block window that sets when those
weights must arrive to be included
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Subnet Hyperparameters: Activity Cutoff](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#activitycutoff)).

A permitted validator still has to submit inside that window. Weights from a validator that holds
the permit but arrives after the activity-cutoff window can miss the current consensus pass, so the
permission gate and the timing gate are checked separately
([Implementation of the Yuma Consensus Epoch](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/epoch),
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

Use validator permit when covering whether weight submission is allowed in the subnet; use activity
cutoff when covering when that submission must happen inside the epoch
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

Permission and timing answer different questions on the same weight-submission path
([Implementation of the Yuma Consensus Epoch](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/epoch)).

## Relationship to Deregistration

A validator permit and deregistration act on the same subnet UID from opposite ends. The permit is
the rights flag, reassigned each epoch to the top neurons by stake weight, that records whether a
UID may submit counting weights, while deregistration removes a neuron from its UID slot when the
subnet is full, ending any permit attached to that slot
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

- **Validator permit** — the per-epoch right to submit counting weights, held only while the UID
  stays registered.
- **Deregistration** — removal of the UID slot, which ends any permit attached to it.

## Relationship to Weights Version Key

A validator permit and the weights version key can both block a weights update at submission time,
but for different reasons. The validator permit authorizes a neuron to set validator-style weights
on other neurons at all, while the weights version key check rejects a `set_weights` call whose
declared version is below the subnet's required minimum
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

For readers, the permit is an authorization gate and the version key is a compatibility gate. A
submission that sets non-self weights without a permit is rejected with a no-validator-permit error,
and a submission carrying a stale version is rejected by the version check, so a validator generally
needs both a current version and a permit before its weights are accepted and able to take part in
consensus ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Relationship to Stake

Stake and the validator permit sit on the same validation-rights path but name different things. The
validator permit is the binary flag awarded to the top neurons by stake weight that records whether
they may submit counting validator weights, while stake, interpreted as stake weight, is the
validator-associated amount that ranks a neuron's consensus influence
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: Stake Weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake-weight),
[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight)).

- **Validator permit** — the binary right to submit counting weights that top-stake neurons earn.
- **Stake / stake weight** — the validator-associated amount and its consensus weighting.

## Relationship to Register

Register and the validator permit are separate subnet states a UID can hold. The validator permit is
the rights flag, awarded to the top neurons by stake weight, that records whether that registered
UID may submit counting validator weights, while register is the subnet-entry action that places a
hotkey on a UID slot, so registration alone does not grant the permit
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register),
[Validating: Validator registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators#validator-registration)).

- **Validator permit** — the separate right to submit counting weights, earned by top stake weight.
- **Register** — the entry action that places a hotkey on a subnet UID slot.

## Relationship to Min Allowed Weights

A validator permit and min allowed weights are two separate requirements behind a counting weight
submission. The validator permit is the on-chain flag granting the right to submit weights that take
part in consensus, while min allowed weights sets how many targets a submission must include to be
valid
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

For readers, the two gate different things. The permit decides whether a validator's weights are
allowed to count at all on the subnet, and min allowed weights decides whether a given submission is
structurally valid once that right exists. A permitted validator still has to meet the minimum
count, and meeting the count does not by itself grant the permit
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Distinction from Validator Take

A validator permit and validator take both carry "validator" but answer different questions on a
subnet such as netuid 1. A validator permit is the on-chain right to submit counting weights and
take part in consensus, granted to the higher-stake-weight neurons; validator take is the commission
rate a validator keeps from its delegated-stake dividends before the remainder reaches its
delegators, with a documented default of 18%. One decides whether a UID may validate at all on
netuid 1; the other decides how the dividends from that validation are split
([Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit),
[Glossary: Validator Take %](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-take-)).

A permit is a prerequisite, not a payout. A UID on netuid 1 needs the permit before it earns
validation dividends, and the take only applies once those dividends exist, so setting a take does
not grant a permit and holding a permit does not fix any particular take
([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

- **Validator permit** — the right to submit counting weights on a subnet such as netuid 1.
- **Validator take** — the commission a permitted validator keeps from delegated-stake dividends
  (default 18%).
