# Staking Proxy Attacks

Staking proxy attacks are a Bittensor wallet-safety concept about misused delegated staking
authority. The official
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)
page frames the issue around a key boundary: a staking proxy is not a direct transfer key, but its
authority can still matter if the delegate is compromised.

This makes the topic narrower than full wallet compromise. It is about the risk left inside a
permission that is intentionally limited to staking-related activity.

## Delegated Staking Authority

The [Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies) explains proxy relationships as
scoped delegation between accounts. In that model, a staking proxy has less authority than a wallet
with full control, but it still has access to staking-related actions for the protected account.

That distinction is the core of staking proxy attack risk. Reduced authority is not the same thing
as no authority.

## Relation to Nearby Protections

Staking proxy attacks are adjacent to MEV Shield and price protection, but they are not the same
concept. [MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/) concerns pre-inclusion
transaction visibility. [Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)
concerns adverse price-movement handling around staking-related actions.

Staking proxy attacks name the delegated-authority risk. MEV Shield and price protection describe
other risk controls in nearby transaction and staking contexts.

A compromised staking proxy also creates a risk that MEV Shield does not cover by itself: an
attacker can repeat the same staking maneuver across many separate transactions, draining value a
little at a time, rather than relying on a single transaction MEV Shield could otherwise protect
([Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)).

## Practical Risk Reduction

The guidance frames staking proxy attacks as preventable through how proxy authority is granted and
monitored. Proxies are a normal Bittensor pattern when their scope is understood, but a staking
proxy should be paired with operational habits that match its authority: knowing which account can
act, what staking actions it can trigger, and whether announcements or delays are in use for
sensitive proxy types where applicable.

The useful reader takeaway is separation of roles. The protected coldkey remains the custody anchor,
the proxy account performs scoped work, and compromise of the proxy account becomes a
staking-activity problem rather than an immediate full-wallet transfer problem — but only if the
proxy type and surrounding controls were chosen with that boundary in mind.

## How This Differs From Transfer Compromise

Staking proxy attacks are intentionally narrower than coldkey compromise or a proxy with transfer
authority. A coldkey loss threatens balances, ownership, and hotkey management. A transfer-capable
proxy can move funds according to its permissions. A staking proxy, by contrast, exposes the
protected account mainly to staking-path manipulation.

MEV Shield and price protection address adjacent transaction and conversion risks, but they do not
replace careful proxy design. Staking proxy attacks name delegated-authority misuse; the other tools
address visibility and price-movement handling in nearby contexts. All three can matter to a wallet
strategy, but they solve different parts of the problem.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For staking proxy
attacks, that sequence changes how readers should interpret examples of delegated staking authority
and proxy misuse outcomes.

In localnet, proxy-wallet and delegation setups can be exercised in an isolated environment.
Localnet examples reflect local chain state and locally configured proxy parameters rather than
production wallet behavior.

On testnet, proxy-authority examples can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet
examples show how delegated authority, proxy permissions, and slippage exposure behave under shared
chain conditions while staying separate from mainnet wallet activity.

On mainnet, staking proxy attacks describe live delegated-authority exposure on the production
Bittensor network. The
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)
reference describes the operational guidance that applies on the production network, and the
[Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies) reference explains the proxy
authority model those attacks target.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A proxy-authority observation from one environment should
not be read as evidence of production delegated-staking behavior in another environment.

## Distinction from Phishing

Staking proxy attacks and phishing both threaten a wallet, but through different routes. A staking
proxy attack misuses authority the owner already delegated to a proxy account, while phishing tries
to trick the owner into revealing a secret such as a seed phrase
([Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks),
[Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)).

- **Staking proxy attack** — misuse of authority already granted to a proxy.
- **Phishing** — deception that tries to obtain a wallet secret.

## Distinction from Delegation

A staking proxy attack and delegation both involve "delegating," but different things. A staking
proxy is an account granted authority to perform staking actions for a protected wallet, while
delegation is committing TAO as stake behind a validator
([Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

- **Staking proxy attack** — misuse of delegated authority to act on staking.
- **Delegation** — committing stake to support a validator.

## Distinction from Proxy Wallets

Staking proxy attacks and proxy wallets are related but different parts of Bittensor wallet-security
vocabulary. Proxy wallets are the scoped-delegation pattern where one account acts for a protected
wallet with limited authority, while staking proxy attacks name the threat scenario where a
compromised staking proxy misuses that delegated authority to perform unfavorable staking operations
([Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies),
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)).

- **Proxy wallets** — scoped delegation where one account acts for a protected wallet.
- **Staking proxy attack** — misuse of a compromised staking proxy's delegated authority.

A staking proxy does not receive full wallet authority, but compromise is still meaningful. The
official
[Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)
page explains that an attacker with a compromised staking proxy can perform staking-related actions
on the protected account. Those actions can include unfavorable staking or unstaking patterns that
change how the account's stake is positioned, rather than sending TAO directly to an unrelated
address.

That outcome is why the concept sits next to slippage and pool-conversion topics. Staking and
unstaking can produce amounts that differ from a static quote, and a malicious actor acting through
a staking proxy can move the protected account through those operations. The harm is value
positioning and loss through adverse conversions, not necessarily a clean transfer drain.

Readers should therefore treat limited authority as a reduction in risk, not as proof the protected
wallet is safe while the proxy is exposed.

The guidance is procedural once compromise is suspected: rotate or remove the affected staking proxy
rather than treating limited authority as a reason to wait, and assume stake-moving activity
occurred until verified otherwise
([Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks)).

The working-with-proxies reference describes removal as immediate and independent of any configured
delay: removing a proxy revokes the delegate's permission to act on the primary account right away,
even if that proxy was originally set up with a delay before it could act
([Working with proxies](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies/working-with-proxies)).

That immediacy matters for staking proxy attacks specifically, because it means a protected account
does not have to wait out a proxy's configured delay window to cut off further staking-related
misuse — removal takes effect on submission, separate from whatever delay governed the proxy's own
actions.

## Distinction from Slippage

A staking proxy attack misuses delegated staking authority granted to a proxy account. Slippage is
the gap between expected and received amounts when a pool conversion executes during staking actions
([Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Staking proxy attack** — misuse of delegated authority to perform unfavorable staking
  operations.
- **Slippage** — conversion gap when pool reserves move during a staking action.

Proxy misuse and pool-movement exposure are separate risks on the same staking path
([Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies)).

Staking proxy attacks sit where delegated staking authority intersects that conversion variance, but
slippage vocabulary describes pool-move outcomes during stake paths and does not by itself prove a
proxy was compromised
([Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Distinction from Nominator

A staking proxy attack is about authority: a delegate account misusing the staking permission an
owner granted, acting on stake it does not own. A nominator is a role, not a threat — an account
that stakes its own TAO behind a validator and keeps control of those tokens while earning dividends
([Glossary: Nominator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominator)).

- **Staking proxy attacks** — a delegate acting on stake it was permitted to touch but does not own.
- **Nominator** — a holder who stakes their own TAO and retains ownership of it.

## Distinction from Regenerating a Key

Removing or rotating a compromised staking proxy cuts off further misuse going forward, but it does
not rebuild a lost key. Regenerating a key is the separate recovery step of recreating a lost
coldkey or hotkey from its mnemonic — a restore path, not a way to revoke a delegate
([Glossary: Regenerating a Key](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#regenerating-a-key)).

- **Staking proxy attacks** — handled by revoking the delegate's authority, not by restoring keys.
- **Regenerating a key** — restoring a lost key from its mnemonic, which neither grants nor removes
  proxy authority.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Staking proxy attacks describe threat patterns where compromised or mis-scoped proxy permissions are
abused against staked positions. They name security-risk vocabulary, not the on-chain step that
turns validator weight submissions into emission shares
([Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies),
[Working with proxies](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies/working-with-proxies)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Proxy abuse targets delegated signing permissions; Yuma Consensus
settles scoring outcomes on a subnet
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep proxy-threat vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Staking proxy attacks
answer what can go wrong with proxy permissions; Yuma Consensus answers how validator weights become
emission shares in a subnet ([Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Staking proxy attacks** — threat patterns against mis-scoped proxy permissions.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

Staking proxy attacks should not be read as a claim that proxy wallets are inherently unsafe. Proxy
wallets are a normal Bittensor security pattern when their authority is understood. Staking proxy
attacks describe the narrower case where delegated staking authority is misused or exposed
([Avoid Staking Proxy Attacks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/avoid-staking-proxy-attacks),
[Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies)).
