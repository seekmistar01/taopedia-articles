# Autostaking

Autostaking is a Bittensor feature that automatically stakes a miner's earnings to a validator the
miner chooses. The official
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking) documentation
describes it as a way for miners to automatically stake mining income to a validator of their
choice, streamlining compound staking without manual intervention.

## Why Autostaking Exists

Mining produces a stream of emissions over time, and converting those earnings into stake normally
takes repeated manual action. The autostaking documentation frames the feature as removing that
friction, so a miner's rewards are continuously reinvested into the network instead of sitting idle
between manual stake operations.

For readers, autostaking is best understood as a standing instruction rather than a one-time action.
Once set, it keeps directing new mining emissions into stake without further steps.

References: [Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)

## How Autostaking Behaves

The autostaking documentation explains that a miner sets a destination validator, and as the miner
earns emissions from subnet participation, those emissions are automatically staked to that
validator. This lets a miner grow stake as it is earned, without repetitive manual stake movements.

The destination is configured per subnet for a given wallet, so the choice of validator can follow
each subnet the miner participates in. For readers, the key idea is continuity: autostaking turns
ongoing earnings into ongoing stake under the miner's chosen delegation target.

References: [Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Glossary: Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking)

## How It Relates to Delegation

Autostaking routes earnings into the same staking-and-delegation system that other participants use.
The
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
describes how stake is delegated to validators, and autostaking simply automates the miner side of
that flow by sending fresh emissions to a chosen delegate.

For readers, autostaking does not introduce a new reward type. It is a convenience layer that feeds
a miner's emissions into ordinary delegation rather than requiring manual staking each time.

References:
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Glossary: Delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate)

## Relationship to Staking

Autostaking and staking are related but different parts of Bittensor reward handling. Staking names
the act of committing TAO to support a validator and participate in rewards, while autostaking names
the automatic routing of a miner's new emissions into that staking path. The
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking) documentation
describes automatic staking of mining income, and the
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
describes the broader staking system.

For readers, staking names the destination activity, while autostaking names the automation that
keeps sending new miner earnings there.

References: [Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)

## Relationship to Emission

Autostaking and emission are related because autostaking acts on mining emissions after they are
earned. Emission names the Bittensor process of generating and allocating rewards to participants,
while autostaking names one way a miner can automatically route those rewards into stake. The
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) overview explains participant reward
allocation, and the autostaking documentation describes automatically staking mining income.

For readers, emission names the reward flow, while autostaking names an optional treatment of a
miner's received share of that flow.

References: [Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)

## Relationship to Delegate

Autostaking and a delegate are related but different roles in the staking path. A delegate is the
validator that receives staked TAO from delegators, while autostaking is the feature that sends a
miner's new earnings to a chosen validator. The
[Glossary: Delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate) defines the
delegate role, and the autostaking documentation describes choosing a destination validator.

For readers, delegate names the validator-side recipient of stake, while autostaking names the
automatic miner-side routing of earnings toward that recipient.

References: [Glossary: Delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate),
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)

## Relationship to Subnet Miner

Autostaking and a subnet miner are related because autostaking is framed around miner earnings. A
subnet miner performs work for a subnet and can receive emissions, while autostaking names the
automatic staking of those miner earnings to a validator. The
[Glossary: Subnet Miner](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-miner) describes
the miner role, and the autostaking documentation describes the feature as applying to mining
income.

For readers, subnet miner names the role that earns the rewards, while autostaking names one
automatic way those rewards can be re-used in staking.

References:
[Glossary: Subnet Miner](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-miner),
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)

## Relationship to Subnet Stake Burn

Autostaking and subnet stake burn are related but distinct parts of Bittensor subnet tokenomics.
Autostaking is the miner-side feature that automatically routes new mining emissions into stake on a
chosen validator, while subnet stake burn is the subnet-owner-side mechanism that adds TAO to a
subnet's reserve and burns the corresponding alpha. The
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking) documentation
describes autostaking as feeding miner emissions into ordinary staking, and the
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn) documentation describes the
burn flow as a voluntary subnet buyback.

For readers, autostaking is the miner-side path that grows a validator's stake from incoming
emissions, while subnet stake burn is the subnet-owner-side path that removes alpha from circulation
in exchange for adding TAO to the subnet's reserve. The two are not interchangeable: autostaking
increases validator-side stake from new emissions, subnet stake burn reduces circulating alpha
supply by way of a pool action. Both involve subnet-level tokenomics, but they operate on different
actors and different sides of the supply picture.

References: [Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
autostaking, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about automatic staking of
miner emissions.

In local testing, autostaking can show whether miner earnings are being routed to a chosen validator
inside an isolated environment. That is useful for checking the automatic staking path, but it
remains isolated feedback from a local environment.

On testchain, autostaking behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives stronger
evidence about how miner emissions are routed into stake than a private local run, while still
keeping the result separate from production mainchain incentives.

On mainchain, autostaking routes live miner earnings into stake on a chosen validator. The
[Auto Staking for Miners](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking) documentation
describes autostaking as automatically staking mining income to a validator of the miner's choice,
so production autostaking context is tied to actual miner emission routing on the live network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An autostaking result observed in one environment should
not be read as identical evidence about miner emission routing in another environment.

For readers, this keeps autostaking examples from sounding stronger than their environment supports.
Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all describe automatic staking of miner earnings, but
they do not carry the same interpretive weight.

## Reader Boundary

Autostaking should not be read as a yield promise, a validator recommendation, or a step-by-step
setup guide. Which validator to choose and whether to automate staking are decisions for each miner,
and current behavior belongs in official references and live chain state. This article describes the
concept of automatically staking mining emissions to a chosen validator.
