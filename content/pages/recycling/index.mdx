# Recycling

Recycling describes token reuse or re-emission inside Bittensor tokenomics. It is supply-accounting
vocabulary for token flows that can return to future emission rather than being permanently removed
from circulation
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The term is easiest to read by contrast with burning. Recycling keeps a token amount available to
the protocol's future emission accounting, while burning emphasizes removal from spendable
circulation.

## Tokenomics Role

Recycling is a supply-flow concept. It describes what happens after tokens enter a protocol-level
accounting process, not an ordinary transfer between users
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

For readers, recycling answers a narrow question: whether the deducted amount can return through
future emission accounting.

That makes recycling useful for separating protocol accounting from custody movement. A transfer
changes who controls tokens, while recycling describes whether a deducted amount remains available
for later protocol distribution.

The distinction is narrow but important. Recycling is about supply accounting after a deduction; it
is not the same thing as spending, transferring, or receiving a token amount
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

## Issuance Record

Recycled tokens are deducted from Bittensor's issuance record so the same quantity can be emitted
again later
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

This keeps recycling connected to supply accounting rather than to user-facing payment language. The
important point is not who receives a token next, but how the protocol accounts for the amount.

The issuance connection also explains why recycling is not a new issuance event by itself. The
deducted amount is kept available for future emission accounting rather than counted as a separate
fresh supply concept
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That makes issuance the accounting record and recycling the return path. The same deducted amount
can be removed from the issuance record while remaining available for later emission accounting
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Burning Contrast

Recycling and burning are related but different tokenomics terms. Burning removes tokens from
spendable circulation, while recycling allows deducted tokens to be reused or emitted again
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

That contrast prevents every token sink from being read as permanent destruction. Recycling is a
reuse concept; burning is a removal concept.

The combined recycling-and-burning glossary entry is useful because both terms describe what happens
after a token amount leaves ordinary circulation. Recycling points to later reuse or re-emission,
while burning points to removal from spendable circulation
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Fee Flow

Transaction fees can feed recycling accounting. Bittensor fee documentation treats transaction fees
as costs attached to state-changing actions, while recycling-and-burning terminology explains how
collected fees can be accounted for after collection
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

For readers, transaction fees name the cost side of an action. Recycling names the supply-accounting
side after those amounts enter the protocol flow.

That distinction keeps fee language from doing too much work. A fee-bearing action explains why a
cost was paid, while recycling explains how the resulting deducted amount can remain part of future
protocol emission accounting
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

## Emission Return

Emission is the creation and allocation side of Bittensor tokenomics. Recycling matters because
deducted amounts can return to future emission accounting instead of disappearing from the emission
picture ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

This makes recycling part of the broader supply-flow vocabulary around issuance, fees, and emission.
It does not by itself define subnet scoring or reward distribution.

In that supply flow, recycling is best read as a return path. It does not decide which miner,
validator, or staker receives emission later; it only explains that the amount can be available to
the protocol's future emission process ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

## Reading Recycling

Recycling is high-level Bittensor tokenomics terminology. It is not a valuation claim, market
forecast, or report of exact network totals
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The stable point is that recycling keeps a deducted token amount available for future protocol
accounting rather than treating it as permanently removed.

That reading also keeps recycling separate from operational advice. The term can explain the
protocol-side supply outcome, but it does not recommend a transaction, predict emissions, or report
current supply totals.

## Network Examples

Bittensor separates localnet, testnet, and mainnet environments. Recycling examples from one
environment belong to that environment because supply state and testing conditions can differ
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).

Localnet examples are isolated development examples. Testnet examples are shared non-production
examples. Mainnet recycling interpretation concerns production Bittensor tokenomics behavior.

## Supply Cycle

Recycling is supply-cycle accounting, not an ordinary transfer or a permanent burn. The glossary
places recycling beside burning, while emissions documentation explains how available token amounts
can flow back through protocol distribution
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That distinction keeps tokenomics language precise. Recycling describes a reusable accounting path,
while burning describes removal from spendable circulation and transfers describe custody changes
between accounts.

Specific totals or examples need the network, source, and timing that produced them. The recycling
concept travels across environments; measured supply state belongs to one environment.
