# Sandwich Attack

A sandwich attack is a transaction ordering exploit where an attacker places two transactions around
a target transaction — one before and one after — to profit from the price movement the target
transaction causes
([Glossary: Sandwich attack](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

## What It Represents

An attacker observes a pending target trade that will move a pool price, submits a transaction ahead
of the target, lets the target execute, then submits a second transaction to close the position at
the improved rate
([Glossary: MEV (Maximal Extractable Value)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

Documentation describes sandwich attacks around visible staking and unstaking activity that moves
subnet pool pricing while details may still be readable in the mempool
([Managing Your Stakes](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/managing-stake-sdk)).

Examples tied to netuid 1 illustrate one subnet's pool-ordering risk path. They should not be read
as representing production mempool behavior on another network
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

## Distinction from MEV

MEV names the broader category of value extractable through transaction ordering and inclusion. A
sandwich attack is the specific before-and-after pattern around a victim trade
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **MEV** — general value extractable by ordering or inserting transactions.
- **Sandwich attack** — specific MEV strategy that brackets a victim's swap.

## Distinction from Slippage

Slippage is ordinary price impact when a trade moves through pool depth. A sandwich attack adds
adversarial ordering around a victim trade so the attacker profits from that movement
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Slippage** — received-amount gap from pool depth during a conversion.
- **Sandwich attack** — adversarial before-and-after ordering around a visible pending trade.

## Distinction from Front-Running and Back-Running

Front-running names a before-only ordering attack. Back-running names an after-only ordering profit.
A sandwich attack wraps the victim with trades on both sides, so front-running is one half of the
sandwich rather than the whole maneuver
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/)).

- **Front-running** — similar transaction submitted ahead of the target.
- **Back-running** — profit taken immediately after the target trade.
- **Sandwich attack** — front-run plus back-run around one victim trade.

## Distinction from Price Protection

Price protection caps how much adverse price movement a staking or unstaking action will accept. It
does not hide pending trade details from observers
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

- **Price protection** — tolerance on execution outcomes for the caller's own order.
- **Sandwich attack** — adversarial ordering around someone else's visible trade.

## Distinction from MEV Shield

MEV Shield encrypts sensitive pending transaction contents until block inclusion. Sandwich attack
names the ordering exploit that protection is meant to reduce
([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

- **MEV Shield** — encrypted mempool protection.
- **Sandwich attack** — adversarial ordering around a visible pending trade.

## Distinction from Mempool

The mempool holds pending transactions before block inclusion. A sandwich attack depends on seeing
and ordering around a pending victim trade in that queue
([Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool)).

- **Mempool** — pending transaction queue before inclusion.
- **Sandwich attack** — adversarial ordering around a visible pending trade.

## Distinction from Arbitrage

Arbitrage profits from genuine price differences without targeting a specific victim. A sandwich
attack brackets one victim's pending trade to extract value from the move it causes
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **Arbitrage** — profit from existing price differences, tending to correct them.
- **Sandwich attack** — bracketing a specific victim's trade.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

A sandwich attack names mempool and pool ordering exploit vocabulary. Yuma Consensus converts
submitted validator weights into emission shares after evaluation completes
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Sandwich attack** — predatory pool ordering around a victim's pending trade.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement from validator weights to emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

Sandwich attack is concept vocabulary for a transaction-ordering exploit in pool operations. It
should not be read as a consensus failure, a validator scoring issue, or a description of normal
slippage
([Glossary: Sandwich attack](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).
