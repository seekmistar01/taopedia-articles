# Reserve Ratio

A reserve ratio is the relationship between the TAO reserve and alpha reserve inside a Bittensor
subnet pool. Each subnet pool pairs TAO with a subnet-specific alpha token, and the ratio between
those reserve sides is central to Dynamic TAO pool behavior
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission: TAO reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection),
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)).

## Root Subnet Skips Paired Reserves

The [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)
documentation describes the root subnet, also called subnet zero, as the only subnet without its own
alpha currency. Validators can register there and TAO holders can stake to them, but that path does
not create the paired TAO-and-alpha reserve structure used by alpha subnets
([Glossary: Root Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#root-subnetsubnet-zero)).

Reserve ratio therefore fits alpha subnets that maintain both reserve sides inside an AMM pool. Root
staking stays in subnet-agnostic TAO stake language rather than in reserve-ratio reading, because
there is no alpha reserve side to pair with TAO reserve on subnet zero.

That boundary keeps reserve-ratio claims scoped to subnets that actually run paired pool reserves,
not to every staking context on the network.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For reserve ratio
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how paired reserve examples should be read.

In localnet, reserve ratio on netuid 1 can be observed in an isolated subnet pool. Localnet TAO and
alpha reserve balances reflect that test chain rather than production pool state.

On testnet, reserve ratio on netuid 1 applies in a shared non-production network where reserve pairs
and slippage outcomes are separate from mainnet.

On mainnet, reserve ratio on netuid 1 concerns live production paired reserves, alpha price, and
pool conversions on the selected subnet
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An observed reserve-ratio value from one environment
should not be read as pool state on another network.

## Distinction from Impermanent Loss

Reserve ratio and impermanent loss describe a pool's balance and a cost its drift can create.
Reserve ratio is the proportion of TAO to alpha a subnet pool holds, while impermanent loss is the
value drift a liquidity provider can bear as that ratio moves away from where the provider entered,
so reserve ratio names the balance and impermanent loss names a cost of its change
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity Pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

The two answer different questions. Reserve ratio names the pool balance at any moment, while
impermanent loss names the provider-facing cost of holding through a change in that balance, so the
ratio can shift continuously while impermanent loss describes what that shift means for a position
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Reserve ratio** — the proportion of TAO to alpha a subnet pool holds.
- **Impermanent loss** — the drift cost a liquidity provider can bear as that ratio moves.

## Distinction from Nearby Pool Labels

Reserve ratio compares TAO and alpha reserves in a subnet pool. Nearby labels such as alpha
outstanding name other positions in the same tokenomics vocabulary
([Glossary: Reserve Ratio](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#reserve-ratio),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

- **Reserve ratio** — comparative measure of paired pool reserves.
- **Nearby pool labels** — related alpha-position terms in the same pool.

Ratio vocabulary names one comparison; neighboring labels name other pool positions
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Distinction from Alpha Price

Alpha price is the TAO-denominated quote derived from paired reserves. Reserve ratio names the
proportion between those reserve sides before a reader turns the pool state into a per-alpha quote
([Understanding Subnets: Price/Rate of Alpha Tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#pricerate-of-alpha-tokens)).

## Distinction from Staking and Emission

Staking and unstaking move value through the paired reserves and can shift the reserve ratio during
user-driven conversions. Matched emission injection can deepen both reserve sides together without
changing the documented quote at the injection step
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)).

Those flows change pool depth or proportion; they are not substitutes for reserve-ratio vocabulary.

## Distinction from Liquidity Positions

Liquidity positions add range-based depth inside a subnet pool. Reserve ratio still describes the
paired TAO-and-alpha proportion that staking, unstaking, and swaps move through
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

A provider position can earn fees while the underlying reserve ratio continues to shift with pool
activity on the same netuid.

## Distinction from Alpha Outstanding

Reserve ratio compares pool-held reserves. Alpha outstanding names alpha outside the reserve pool
([Glossary: Reserve Ratio](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#reserve-ratio),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Reserve ratio** — comparative pool reserve measure.
- **Alpha outstanding** — alpha held outside the reserve pool.

In-pool comparison vocabulary and out-of-pool position vocabulary answer different accounting
questions ([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Reserve ratio names the numeric relationship between TAO and alpha sides of a subnet liquidity pool.
Yuma Consensus is the separate mechanism that converts validator weight submissions into emission
shares each tempo
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Reserve ratio** — numeric TAO-and-alpha reserve relationship.
- **Yuma Consensus** — mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Distinction from Pool Pair

Reserve ratio belongs to the paired reserve model. The TAO side and alpha side must be read together
because the pool relationship gives subnet alpha its TAO-denominated pricing context.

This makes the ratio a relationship term rather than an asset term. TAO reserve identifies the TAO
side, alpha reserve identifies the subnet-token side, and reserve ratio identifies how those sides
relate inside the pool.

The ratio does not include every alpha amount associated with a subnet. Alpha outstanding is outside
the reserve pool, while reserve ratio is about the TAO and alpha quantities inside the paired pool
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Distinction from Slippage

Reserve ratio describes the TAO-to-alpha proportion in a subnet pool. Slippage is the
received-amount gap when a conversion moves through that pool
([Glossary: Reserve Ratio](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#reserve-ratio),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Reserve ratio** — paired reserve proportion in the pool.
- **Slippage** — conversion gap after pool movement.

Pool-proportion vocabulary and execution-gap vocabulary answer different reader questions
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

## Distinction from Emission Injection

Emission can change reserve context. TAO reserve injection adds TAO to subnet reserves, and alpha
reserve injection grows the paired alpha side in relation to the TAO injection
([Emission: TAO reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection),
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)).

The reserve ratio is therefore useful when reading emission mechanics, but it is not the same as
emission distribution. It describes the pool relationship, while emission distribution describes how
value reaches miners, validators, stakers, and subnet creators
([Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Reserve ratio is also different from TAO reserve injection and alpha reserve injection individually.
Those terms describe additions to one reserve side; reserve ratio describes the relationship between
the sides after the reserve quantities are considered together.

## Reader Boundary

Reserve ratio is a reference term for the relationship between TAO and alpha reserves inside a
subnet pool. Concrete examples require the selected subnet, network environment, and source for the
pool context
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The narrow use is the clearest one: reserve ratio means the relationship between the TAO reserve and
alpha reserve in a subnet pool. Emissions, slippage, liquidity positions, and incentive mechanisms
add nearby context.
