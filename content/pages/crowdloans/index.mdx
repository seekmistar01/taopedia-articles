# Crowdloans

A crowdloan is a Bittensor feature that lets a group collectively fund an on-chain action, such as
registering a new subnet, and share the result. The official
[Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans) documentation describes it as a way
for contributors to pool funds toward an extrinsic execution or a balance transfer to a specific
address, rather than a single sponsor paying the full cost up front.

## Why Crowdloans Exist

Standing up a subnet carries a registration cost that one participant may not want to cover alone.
The crowdloan documentation frames the feature as a collaborative alternative: a creator opens a
crowdloan with a funding cap and an end block, and contributors deposit funds until the cap is met.

This makes subnet bootstrapping a shared effort. Instead of concentrating the lease cost on one
sponsor, the cost and the later upside are spread across everyone who contributed.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## How Finalization Works

A crowdloan resolves at its end block. The documentation states that on success the pallet finalizes
the crowdloan by executing an on-chain call that registers a leased network, which stands up the
subnet and activates emissions for the group. At creation, exactly one of a call or a target address
is specified, and that choice is immutable to prevent mid-campaign repurposing.

If the cap is not met by the end block, the documentation notes that anyone can trigger refunds, and
once all contributors are refunded the creator can dissolve the crowdloan.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)

## Beneficiary Proxy and Emission Sharing

Crowdloan finalization separates control from custody. The documentation describes how finalization
creates a dedicated proxy, the subnet lease beneficiary, authorized to operate the subnet, such as
configuring parameters, without holding custody of contributor funds or emission splits.

Rewards follow contribution. When a lease is created, the documentation states that emissions flow
to contributors pro-rata based on each person's contributed share, tying the upside of the subnet
back to who funded it.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Working with Proxies](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies/working-with-proxies)

## Relationship to Subnet Creation

Crowdloans and subnet creation are related because a crowdloan can collectively fund the
registration of a new subnet. The [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans)
documentation gives subnet registration as the main example of a crowdloan-funded action, while
[Create a Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet) describes subnet creation
as the process of bringing a new subnet onto the network.

For readers, subnet creation names the network outcome, while a crowdloan names one funding path
that can make that outcome possible for a group of contributors.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Create a Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet)

## Relationship to Extrinsics

Crowdloans and extrinsics are related but different parts of Bittensor chain-action vocabulary. The
crowdloan documentation describes a campaign as funding either an extrinsic execution or a balance
transfer to a target address. An extrinsic is the submitted chain action; the crowdloan is the
collective funding structure that can pay for one at finalization.

For readers, an extrinsic names the action submitted to the chain, while a crowdloan names the
group-funded campaign that can eventually execute that action.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Glossary: Extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#extrinsics)

## Relationship to Transfer

Crowdloans and transfers are related because a crowdloan can also fund a balance transfer to a
specified address. The crowdloan documentation separates this transfer path from the extrinsic
execution path, while the
[Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer) describes a
transfer as sending TAO between wallet addresses.

For readers, transfer names a movement of TAO, while crowdloan names a shared funding campaign that
can use pooled contributions for that movement when the campaign finalizes.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer)

## Relationship to Proxy Wallets

Crowdloans and proxy wallets are related through the lease beneficiary role. The crowdloan
documentation describes finalization as creating a dedicated proxy authorized to operate the subnet
without holding contributor funds or emission splits. The
[Working with Proxies](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies/working-with-proxies) guide
explains the broader idea of one account acting for another under a proxy relationship.

For readers, a proxy wallet names the delegated-control pattern, while the crowdloan beneficiary
proxy is the specific crowdloan-side use of that pattern for subnet operation.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Working with Proxies](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies/working-with-proxies)

## Relationship to Emission

Crowdloans and emission are related because successful leased-subnet crowdloans can share resulting
emissions among contributors. The crowdloan documentation states that emissions flow pro-rata based
on contribution share after a lease is created, while the
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) overview explains participant reward
allocation in Bittensor.

For readers, emission names the reward flow, while a crowdloan names the contribution structure that
can define how a leased subnet's emission upside is shared.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Reader Boundary

Crowdloans should not be read as an investment recommendation, a yield quote, or a step-by-step
funding tutorial. Caps, end blocks, and emission shares depend on each campaign and on live chain
state. This article describes the concept of pooling funds to register and operate a subnet
collectively.
