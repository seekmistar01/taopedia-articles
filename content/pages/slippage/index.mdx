# Slippage

Slippage is the gap between an expected amount and the amount received.

In Bittensor, that gap can appear when staking or unstaking converts between TAO and subnet alpha
tokens through subnet pool reserves.
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) places the concept in TAO
and alpha-token conversions, while
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) gives the
subnet-pool context behind those conversions.

## Pool conversion context

Each Bittensor subnet has a TAO reserve and a subnet-specific alpha reserve. Those reserves form the
pool context for converting between network-level TAO and subnet-level alpha tokens. Because the
pool context is part of the conversion, slippage belongs with subnet liquidity pools and alpha-token
accounting, not with a simple one-token balance view.

Sources: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)

## Relationship to Reserve Ratio

Reserve ratio describes the pool relationship between TAO reserves and alpha reserves, while
slippage describes the outcome difference when staking or unstaking moves through that relationship.
The [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)
documentation anchors the reserve relationship, and
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) explains the conversion
effect.

For readers, reserve ratio is the pool context; slippage is the received-amount effect of using that
context.

## Staking direction

When TAO is staked into a subnet, the result is represented in that subnet's alpha-token context.
The [staking overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation) describes
subnet-local staking and alpha-denominated stake, and
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) explains why the received
amount can differ from a static expectation. For a Taopedia reader, the key point is directional:
staking moves from TAO into a subnet pool context and comes out as subnet-specific alpha-denominated
stake.

## Unstaking direction

Unstaking uses the same pool context in the other direction. Alpha-denominated stake is expressed
back into TAO terms through the subnet pool, so the received TAO amount can differ from a static
expectation for the same reason.
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) anchors this concept in
staking and unstaking, while
[Staking/Delegation Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
connects it to subnet-local staking.

## Why amount and pool context matter

Slippage is not just another name for the final amount. It is a property of converting through
reserves whose balance changes as staking or unstaking is processed. Larger changes and different
reserve contexts can therefore produce different outcomes.
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) is the source for
calculation details, but a concept article should keep the distinction simple: pool context affects
the received amount.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
slippage, that stage boundary matters because the pool reserves and conversion direction belong to a
specific network environment.

A local example can explain why staking or unstaking through a pool can produce a received amount
different from the expected amount. It remains local evidence unless the source is about a public
Bittensor network and a specific subnet pool.

If a slippage example comes from testchain, it should be read as shared testing context. It can help
separate the concept from a private local run, but it should not be cited as production mainchain
pool behavior.

If a claim comes from mainchain, the production context should stay visible. The selected subnet,
staking or unstaking direction, pool state, and source for any numeric amount are part of what makes
the claim meaningful.

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) defines the received-amount
effect in TAO and alpha-token conversions, while
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) gives the
subnet-pool context. The
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference keeps
mainnet, testnet, and localnet separate.

This keeps slippage from being read as a universal number or a staking recommendation. The article
topic is the conversion outcome difference; the network, subnet, and pool context explain where a
particular example applies.

That distinction also keeps slippage separate from price protection. Slippage describes the outcome
difference, while price protection describes a guardrail around adverse movement during
staking-related actions.

## Relationship to Price Protection

Slippage describes the difference between an expected amount and the amount actually received
through a pool conversion. Price protection is a control around that movement: it decides whether a
staking-related action should continue, be limited, or proceed without the guardrail when the
slippage would be unacceptable.
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) defines the staking and
unstaking slippage concept, and
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection) defines the protection
choices.

For readers, slippage names the outcome difference, while price protection names how an action
handles that difference. The two describe different sides of a pool conversion: the movement itself
and the guardrail around it.

## Reading slippage claims

A slippage claim should say whether it is about staking into alpha-denominated stake, unstaking back
to TAO, or general pool-conversion context. Claims about specific figures, reserve balances, or a
particular subnet need the exact source behind those figures. The safer general reading from
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) and
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) is that
slippage appears because Bittensor subnet activity uses pool reserves rather than a fixed
one-for-one conversion.

## Relationship to MEV Shield

Slippage and MEV Shield address different parts of adverse movement risk during Bittensor activity.
Slippage names the difference between an expected pool-conversion amount and the amount actually
received, while [MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/) encrypts
sensitive pre-inclusion transaction details so pending information is less exposed.

For readers, slippage describes a pool-conversion outcome difference, while MEV Shield is a
pre-inclusion protection against MEV exposure. Price protection is the closer guardrail for slippage
itself; MEV Shield addresses a different layer of risk.

References: [Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Slippage appears when staking or unstaking converts through a subnet pool's TAO and alpha reserves.
The [Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that
each subnet has one or more incentive mechanisms.

For readers, slippage still names a pool-conversion outcome difference. A subnet may run more than
one incentive mechanism, but slippage belongs to the shared pool context rather than to one
mechanism label.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)
