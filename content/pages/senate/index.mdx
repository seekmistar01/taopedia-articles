# Senate

The Senate is the Bittensor governance body responsible for approving or disapproving proposals made
by the Triumvirate. The [Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate)
describes it as a group of elected delegates that reviews Triumvirate proposals.

## Governance Role

The [Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance) places the Senate after the
Triumvirate in the proposal flow: the Triumvirate creates proposals, and the Senate approves or
rejects them. The [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) documentation provides
the Senate-side context for proposal review and voting.

## Voting Mechanism

The [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) documentation describes how senators
cast votes on Triumvirate proposals: approval or disapproval votes are submitted using the
`btcli sudo senate-vote` command, and each vote is included in the next block after it is cast. This
places Senate voting within Bittensor's block-based state update model rather than taking effect
instantly.

## Governance Position

The Senate is one side of Bittensor's bicameral governance structure. The
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance) describes the Triumvirate as the
body that creates proposals and the Senate as the body that approves or rejects them. The
[Glossary: Bicameral Legislature](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#bicameral-legislature)
frames that pairing as a two-tier proposal-approval system.

In that structure, the Senate is not the origin of the proposal. It is the approval body that
reviews the item created by the Triumvirate. A proposal can therefore be understood as the
governance item, the Triumvirate as the origin body, and the Senate as the review body.

This separation also matters after a vote. The governance overview says a proposal must obtain a
Senate majority and then be closed by a Triumvirate member before execution. Senate approval is a
required gate in the process, but the proposal lifecycle still includes the closing step described
in the official governance documentation.

References: [Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Glossary: Bicameral Legislature](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#bicameral-legislature)

## Proposal Records

The Senate documentation distinguishes public visibility from voting authority. Anyone can view
proposals currently before the Senate, while only senators vote on them. This means a visible
proposal is not automatically an approved proposal, and a proposal under review is not the same
thing as a completed governance action.

Proposal hashes provide the identifier used during that review process. The
[Glossary: Proposal hash](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal-hash) defines
the proposal hash as the unique identifier for a proposal used in voting. The hash identifies the
item under review; it is separate from the Senate, the proposal body, and the vote result.

Together, proposal records and proposal hashes make Senate review traceable without changing the
Senate's role. The Senate remains the body that records approval or disapproval, while the proposal
and proposal hash identify what is being reviewed.

References: [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate),
[Glossary: Proposal hash](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal-hash)

## Representation Boundary

The Senate documentation describes senators as delegates who have elected to participate in
proposals and control a significant portion of total network stake. It also notes that holders who
delegate stake to Senate members are represented by the party controlling the delegate they chose.

This makes Senate participation stake-mediated rather than open voting by every account. A holder
can inspect proposals publicly, but governance voting through the Senate is attached to the senator
role and the delegated-stake relationship described in the official Senate documentation.

Reference: [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)

## Security Role

The governance overview explains why Senate approval matters for privileged changes. Before the
governance protocol, administrative actions such as changing hyperparameters, creating subnetworks,
or chain upgrades depended on a single privileged `sudo` key. The governance protocol adds proposal
review involving both Triumvirate and Senate participation for the proposal path described there.

That makes the Senate a review safeguard, not a privileged key holder. Sudo names administrative
authority, while the Senate names the approval body that must support a governance proposal before
the proposal can advance through the documented governance flow.

References: [Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Glossary: Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo)

## Relationship to Triumvirate

The Senate and the Triumvirate are the two bodies in Bittensor's governance flow, but they play
different roles. The
[Glossary: Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate) describes
the Triumvirate as the body that creates proposals, while the
[Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate) describes the Senate
as the elected delegates who approve or reject them. The
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance) places the Triumvirate first as
the proposing body and the Senate second as the reviewing body.

For readers, the Triumvirate originates and later closes proposals, while the Senate is the approval
gate in between. A proposal needs Senate majority support and then Triumvirate closure before
execution, so the two bodies act at different stages of the same flow rather than as interchangeable
roles.

References: [Glossary: Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)

## Relationship to Senator

The Senate and a senator are related as the body and its members. The
[Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate) describes the Senate
as the group of elected delegates that reviews proposals, while a senator is one such delegate
seated in that body. The [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) documentation
describes individual senators casting approval or disapproval votes on proposals.

For readers, the Senate names the collective governance body and its majority-vote outcome, while a
senator names a single voting member within it. A senator's vote is one input; the Senate's decision
is the aggregate the governance flow acts on.

References: [Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)

## Reader Boundary

This article describes the Senate as a governance role. It does not list active senators, proposal
outcomes, or participation requirements. Readers should use official governance references for
current proposal records and detailed Senate context.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For the Senate,
that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about governance body activity and
proposal review.

In localnet, Senate governance mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet Senate
votes and proposal outcomes do not represent production governance decisions.

On testnet, Senate activity can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet Senate
votes are separate from mainnet governance state and live proposal outcomes.

On mainnet, the Senate is the live Bittensor governance body whose approval is required before
Triumvirate proposals can execute on-chain. The
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) documentation describes the Senator role
and voting process that applies on the production network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A Senate example from one environment should not be read
as representing governance authority or voting outcomes in another environment.

## Review Boundary

The Senate should be described as the review and approval body for Triumvirate proposals, not as the
sole origin or executor of governance changes. Governance documentation separates proposal creation
by the Triumvirate, Senate voting, and subsequent closure or execution steps
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

For readers, a Senate vote is an important gate, but it is not the whole lifecycle. A precise claim
should identify whether it concerns review, approval, rejection, closure, or execution.
