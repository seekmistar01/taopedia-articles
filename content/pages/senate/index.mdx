# Senate

The Senate is the Bittensor governance body responsible for approving or disapproving proposals made
by the Triumvirate. It is the review and voting side of Bittensor's bicameral governance structure
([Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

The term names the collective review body. It is different from senator, which names an individual
delegate role inside that body ([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

## Governance Role

The Senate sits after the Triumvirate in the proposal path. The Triumvirate creates proposals, and
the Senate reviews those proposals by voting to approve or disapprove them
([Glossary: Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

This gives the term a narrow role boundary. Senate names the review side of governance, while
Triumvirate names the proposal-origin side
([Glossary: Bicameral Legislature](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#bicameral-legislature)).

## Voting Mechanism

Senate voting is a governance review action. Senators cast approval or disapproval votes on
Triumvirate proposals, and the vote result determines whether the proposal clears the Senate review
step ([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

That makes Senate voting different from public proposal visibility. Proposals can be inspected, but
the authority to approve or disapprove belongs to the senator role described in the Senate
documentation ([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

## Governance Position

The Senate is one side of Bittensor's bicameral governance structure. Bicameral legislature names
the two-body arrangement, Triumvirate names the proposal-origin side, and Senate names the
approval-side review body
([Glossary: Bicameral Legislature](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#bicameral-legislature),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

In that structure, a proposal is the item under review. A proposal hash is the identifier for that
item. The Senate is the body that records approval or disapproval of the item during review
([Glossary: Proposal Hash](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal-hash)).

This is why Senate language works best after the proposal-origin context is clear. It identifies the
review side of the path, not the whole governance system
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

## Proposal Records

Proposal records and proposal hashes make Senate review traceable without changing the Senate's
role. A visible proposal, a Senate-approved proposal, and a completed governance change are separate
parts of the governance path ([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Glossary: Proposal Hash](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal-hash)).

This status boundary keeps Senate approval in context. Senate review is an important gate, while
proposal status and later application require the surrounding governance record.

## Security Role

Senate approval matters because governance replaced direct sudo-style privileged authority with a
proposal review path. Sudo names the older privileged-authority concept; Senate names the approval
body in the governance process
([Governance Overview: security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance#security),
[Glossary: Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo)).

That makes the Senate a review safeguard, not a privileged key holder. Privileged authority belongs
to the governance path, not to an exposed administrative key in this article's scope.

## Network Scope

Bittensor documentation separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet environments
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).
Senate examples need that environment attached because local tests, testnet activity, and mainnet
governance have different scope.

A local example can illustrate mechanics. A testnet example can show shared non-production behavior.
Mainnet Senate activity is the production governance-review context.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Senate, that
sequence changes how an example should be read, because the surrounding network state differs at
each stage.

In localnet, senate can be exercised in an isolated development environment, where the surrounding
chain state reflects local configuration rather than production history.

On testnet, senate can be observed in a shared, non-production network whose state is kept separate
from mainnet.

On mainnet, senate applies on the live, production Bittensor network, where the surrounding state is
real and persistent.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A senate example from one environment should not be read
as representing another environment.

## Delegated Stake Extends Senate Representation

The [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) page describes senators as delegates
who control a significant portion of total network
[stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake). That stake weight is not limited
to what each senator holds directly. Network members who
[delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation) stake to a seated senator
are represented through the delegate they chose.

Wording ties holder opinion to delegate choice rather than to a separate Senate ballot for every
account. A holder can make their governance preference known by delegating with an organization
whose Senate positions align with their interests
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

This extends the Senate beyond its seated members without changing who casts votes. Individual
senators still approve or disapprove proposals, while delegated stake connects broader network
participation to those seated delegates.

## Stake Threshold Governs Senate Membership

Senate participation is not automatic for every registered participant. The
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) requirements page states that a coldkey
must register on a [subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet) as a
[hotkey-coldkey pair](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey-coldkey-pair), hold
more than two percent of the network's total stake through self-stake or delegation, and elect to
participate before that pair can vote on Triumvirate proposals.

The [Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate) adds that the body
is formed from the top delegate hotkeys. When all twelve Senate seats are filled, a new eligible
delegate replaces the lowest-stake seated member rather than adding a thirteenth seat
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

Those rules connect Senate vocabulary to documented weight and capacity limits. The term names a
review body whose seated membership reflects stake ranking and an opt-in choice, not merely public
access to proposal records.

## Majority Is Calculated From Seated Senators

Senate approval depends on how many senators are actually seated, not on the body's maximum size.
The [Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance) states that a proposal needs
more than half of seated senator approvals before it can move toward execution, and that twelve
seats may exist without all twelve being occupied at once.

The same page illustrates the arithmetic with a smaller seated set. When only three senators hold
seats, the approval threshold becomes two approvals even though the Senate can grow larger over time
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

That seated-count rule keeps Senate review language precise. A proposal hash can accumulate visible
votes, but clearing the Senate gate means reaching a majority among delegates currently seated in
the body, not among an assumed full roster of twelve.

## Distinction from Triumvirate

The Senate and Triumvirate sit on opposite sides of Bittensor's bicameral governance path. The
Triumvirate creates proposals, while the Senate reviews those proposals by voting to approve or
disapprove them ([Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Glossary: Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

- **Senate** — the approval-side review body that votes on Triumvirate proposals.
- **Triumvirate** — the proposal-origin side that creates items for Senate review.

## Distinction from Senator

The Senate and senator differ in scope. The Senate names the collective governance body, while a
senator names one seated delegate who can cast approval or disapproval votes inside that body
([Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

- **Senate** — the aggregate review body in bicameral governance.
- **Senator** — one delegate member who votes within the Senate.

That keeps body language and member language separate: the Senate names the review body's aggregate
role, while senator names an individual delegate who can cast a vote
([Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

The [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) page separates public inspection
from senator voting authority. Anyone can view proposals currently before the Senate through the
unpermissioned `btcli sudo proposals` command, while senator voting is limited to eligible seated
senators.

Senate voting documentation states that a senator uses `btcli sudo senate-vote`, enters the proposal
hash from the proposals overview, and then chooses approval or disapproval. Once confirmed, the vote
is included in the next block and counted in the vote
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

That split keeps Senate vocabulary precise. Public visibility supports review of what is before the
body; senator votes record whether an eligible delegate approved or disapproved a specific proposal
hash.

## Distinction from Bicameral Legislature

The Senate and bicameral legislature differ in scope. Bicameral legislature names the paired
two-body governance structure made of the Triumvirate and the Senate, while the Senate names only
the approval-side review body that votes on Triumvirate proposals
([Glossary: Bicameral Legislature](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#bicameral-legislature),
[Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

Structural vocabulary names the full origin-and-review arrangement; Senate vocabulary names the
review body inside it.

- **Bicameral legislature** — the full two-body governance arrangement.
- **Senate** — the approval-side review body within that arrangement.

## Distinction from Proposal Hash

The Senate is the governance body that approves or disapproves Triumvirate proposals. A proposal
hash identifies the specific proposal item senators vote on
([Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Glossary: Proposal Hash](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal-hash),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

- **Senate** — the review body that votes on proposals.
- **Proposal hash** — the identifier for the proposal under review.

Body vocabulary and item-identifier vocabulary answer different governance questions
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

## Distinction from Sudo

Sudo and the Senate name a legacy authority and an approval body. Sudo is the legacy single-key
authority that could enact privileged changes on its own, while the Senate is the elected body that
votes to approve or reject proposals in the governance model that replaced it
([Glossary: Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

- **Sudo** — the legacy single-key authority that acted unilaterally.
- **Senate** — the elected body that votes on proposals in its place.

## Distinction from Nominator

A nominator is a TAO holder who stakes with a validator. The Senate is the governance body formed
from the top delegate keys by stake. Delegated stake a nominator supplies counts toward the total
stake that can qualify a delegate for a Senate seat, but nomination does not make the nominator a
senator ([Glossary: Nominator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominator),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate),
[Glossary: Delegate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegate)).

- **Nominator** — TAO-holder role supplying delegated stake to a validator.
- **Senate** — governance body whose seats are gated by delegate stake totals.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

The Senate names one chamber in Bittensor's bicameral on-chain governance system. Proposal review
and voting there are separate from the tempo pass that converts validator weight submissions into
emission shares on a subnet
([Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

Yuma Consensus reads submitted weights, applies clipping and bonding, and credits miner incentives
and validator dividends when the epoch closes
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

A senator seat is a governance role tied to top delegate keys. It is not a weight-submission or
emission-settlement action on a subnet market
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

- **Senate** — governance chamber in Bittensor's on-chain legislature.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

Senate should be read as governance-body vocabulary. The term points to proposal review and voting
inside the bicameral governance structure
([Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

When the focus is proposal origin, Triumvirate is more precise. When the focus is an individual
delegate, senator is more precise. When the focus is the two-body structure, bicameral legislature
is more precise.
