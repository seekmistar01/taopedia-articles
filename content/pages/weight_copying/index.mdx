# Weight Copying

Weight copying is a Bittensor consensus problem where a validator copies public weight information
instead of independently evaluating miners. Validators are expected to score miners based on the
quality of their work, and those weights feed into [[yuma_consensus|Yuma Consensus]] for incentive
allocation. The
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor) page
defines the free-riding behavior, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) explains how validator
weights become part of miner and validator emissions.

## Why independent evaluation matters

Independent validator evaluation is the quality-control layer for a subnet. When validators produce
their own miner scores, the consensus process receives multiple views of miner performance. When a
validator copies another validator's weights, the copied signal looks like participation but adds
less independent information. The
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor) page
frames this as free-riding on other validators' evaluation work.

## Consensus impact

Weight copying matters because validator weights are an input to consensus, not just private notes.
Yuma Consensus combines validator rankings into emission outcomes for miners and validators, so a
copied weight vector can make the consensus signal less diverse than it appears. The
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) page describes the weight
matrix and emission vectors, while the
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)
connects copied weights to degraded subnet quality.

## Delayed visibility

The main protocol-side defense is delayed visibility of fresh weights. Commit Reveal introduces a
waiting period between when validator weights are committed and when they are revealed publicly for
consensus. That delay makes the newest weight information less useful to copy, because a copied view
is already stale by the time it can be reused. The
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal) page explains this delayed
reveal model, and the
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor) page
places it in the weight-copying context.

## Relationship to Consensus-Based Weights

Consensus-based weights, also called liquid alpha, are a second protocol-side tool against weight
copying. Where delayed visibility makes fresh weights less useful to copy, consensus-based weights
adjust bond smoothing by consensus alignment, so out-of-line — including copied or stale — weights
carry less bond influence where liquid alpha is active. The
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor) page
places liquid alpha alongside delayed weight visibility as tools that help validators identify
useful miners and bond to them quickly, and the
[Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights) page
defines the mechanism.

For readers, delayed visibility and consensus-based weights are complementary defenses rather than
alternatives: one limits how current a copied view can be, while the other can limit how much a
copied view shapes bond outcomes.

## What copying does not prove

Similar weights do not automatically prove malicious copying. Validators can legitimately converge
when they evaluate the same miners and observe similar performance. The safer distinction is between
independent evaluation and dependence on public weight information. The
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor) page
focuses on copied or derived weights, while
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes why consistent
validator signals can also be part of normal consensus.

## Relationship to Coinbase

Weight copying and coinbase are related but different parts of Bittensor's evaluation and emission
pipeline. Weight copying is the problem behavior where a validator relies on public weight
information instead of independently evaluating miners. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission,
accumulates pending emissions, and triggers Yuma Consensus rounds at epoch boundaries, while the
[The Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)
documentation describes the copying behavior.

The connection is that coinbase-triggered consensus rounds rely on the validator weights as their
input, and weight copying degrades the quality of that input. When coinbase runs a subnet's Yuma
Consensus round each epoch, it works from the weights validators submitted; copied weights look like
participation but add less independent information for that round to draw on. Coinbase still
executes normally, but the incentive outcomes it produces reflect weaker signal. Coinbase is the
mechanism that consumes validator weights to distribute emissions; weight copying is a quality
problem in the weights it consumes.
