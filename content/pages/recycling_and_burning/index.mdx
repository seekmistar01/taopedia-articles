# Recycling and Burning

Recycling and burning are Bittensor token-accounting terms for different kinds of removal from
ordinary circulation
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The distinction matters because both terms describe removal, but they do not describe the same
supply outcome. Recycling keeps the removed amount available for later protocol distribution;
burning leaves the removed amount unavailable for future emission.

The pair is useful because Bittensor sources can use familiar removal labels in different ways. A
reader needs the accounting treatment, not only the casual verb attached to the event
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Two Accounting Outcomes

The shared starting point is that tokens leave ordinary circulation. The accounting question is what
happens after that removal: can the amount return through future emission, or is it unavailable for
that route?
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Recycling answers yes to future re-emission. Burning answers no. Keeping that contrast explicit
prevents every token-removal event from being described with the same broad "burn" language.

This turns recycling and burning into supply-accounting terms. The same movement away from a user,
pool, or action can have different long-term meaning depending on whether the protocol can emit the
amount again.

## Recycled Tokens

Recycled tokens are subtracted from issuance so they can be emitted again
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Transaction fees are one official example. Bittensor treats fees as recycled rather than permanently
removed from future emission
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

For readers, recycling is therefore a reuse concept. A recycled amount leaves ordinary circulation
for the moment, but the accounting treatment lets that amount return through protocol emission
later.

That makes recycling different from a simple sink. The removed amount is not immediately available
to the holder or actor involved in the event, but it remains available to the protocol's later
emission flow.

Burn cost is another useful example. The subnet-creation label includes "burn," while local subnet
creation guidance presents the cost as recycled rather than destroyed
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Local subnet creation: Subnet creation cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/local-build/create-subnet#subnet-creation-cost)).

That example keeps the accounting treatment separate from the label. The event can be called burn
cost while the TAO treatment remains recycling.

## Burned Tokens

Burned tokens remain in issuance while unavailable for future emission
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Subnet stake burn is one place where the distinction is useful. In stake burn, TAO moves into a
subnet reserve while corresponding alpha is removed from circulation and burned
([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

For readers, burning is therefore a removal concept. It is not the same as a temporary deduction,
ordinary transfer, or recycled fee flow.

The important distinction is future availability. A burned amount can still be part of issuance
accounting, while the term means it is unavailable for future emission.

## Issuance and Emission

Issuance gives recycling and burning their accounting meaning. Issuance covers TAO circulating in
the network, including TAO held in subnet liquidity pools
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

Recycling affects emission accounting by making removed tokens re-emittable. Burning removes tokens
without making them available for later emission.

That makes issuance the shared accounting backdrop. Recycling changes how a deducted amount can
return to emission; burning explains why a removed amount no longer effectively circulates even
though issuance accounting still records it
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Emission is the return path for recycled amounts. Recycling can make removed tokens available for
future emission, while burning keeps removed tokens out of that future-emission route
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Mechanism Examples

Removed-token statements belong with the mechanism being described. Transaction fees, subnet stake
burn, burn cost, and emission accounting can all involve removed or redirected tokens, but they do
not imply the same later-emission result
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The useful question is not only whether tokens left ordinary circulation. It is whether the relevant
mechanism treats the removed amount as re-emittable or unavailable for future emission.

This keeps fee language, stake-burn language, and emission language from collapsing into one token
sink label. Fees illustrate recycling, stake burn illustrates burning, and burn cost illustrates a
label whose accounting treatment is recycling.

## Fees Illustrate Recycling, Stake Burn Illustrates Burning

[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) and
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn) sit on opposite sides of
the accounting contrast. Fees illustrate amounts that can return through future emission, while
stake burn illustrates removal from spendable circulation
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

The two sides also differ in which asset is removed: transaction fees recycle TAO, while subnet
stake burn removes alpha from circulation as TAO moves into the subnet reserve
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

## Development Stage Context

Bittensor separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet environments. Recycling and burning examples from
one environment belong to that environment because supply state and mechanism observations can
differ ([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).

## Distinction from Recycling

Recycling and burning keeps two supply-accounting outcomes distinct. Recycling names a deducted
amount that can return toward future protocol emission, while burning names removal without that
return path ([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

- **Recycling** — deducted amount can be emitted again later.
- **Burning** — removed amount stays unavailable for future emission.

This pair article explains both labels together. Recycling vocabulary belongs when the mechanism
treats the deducted amount as re-emittable, such as transaction fees
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)).

## Distinction from Transaction Fees

Recycling and burning name a supply-accounting outcome, while a transaction fee names a charge. A
transaction fee is the cost charged to process an action; its collected amount is then handled
through recycling rather than the fee itself being a removal category
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

- **Transaction fee** — the cost charged to process an action.
- **Recycling/burning** — how the removed amount is treated for future emission.

## Distinction from Issuance

Recycling and burning are removal treatments, while issuance is the supply record they adjust.
Issuance tracks TAO circulating in the network; recycling subtracts an amount so it can be emitted
again, and burning leaves an amount in issuance accounting but unavailable for future emission
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

- **Issuance** — the circulating-supply record.
- **Recycling/burning** — the removal treatments that adjust how supply can re-emit.

## Distinction from Burn Cost

Recycling and burning describes how TAO returns to circulation or leaves supply through protocol
flows. Burn cost is the dynamic TAO amount paid to register on a subnet
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

- **Recycling and burning** — supply return and removal through protocol flows.
- **Burn cost** — dynamic registration price for subnet entry.

Macro supply vocabulary and registration-price vocabulary answer different tokenomics questions
([Validating: Validator registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators#validator-registration)).

## Distinction from Halving

Recycling and burning and halving both shape how much TAO ultimately circulates, but they act on
different levers. Recycling and burning are removal treatments applied to specific amounts that have
left ordinary circulation, deciding whether a deducted amount can be emitted again or stays
unavailable for future emission, while halving is the process where Bittensor's daily token emission
rate cuts in half, reducing the rate of new TAO entering circulation overall
([Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving)).

- **Recycling and burning** — removal treatments deciding whether a specific deducted amount can
  re-emit.
- **Halving** — a scheduled cut to the rate at which new TAO enters circulation.

## Distinction from Existential Deposit

Recycling and burning and an existential deposit both relate to TAO leaving an account or
circulation, but they answer different questions. Recycling and burning are supply-accounting
treatments for amounts removed through protocol flows, classifying whether the removed amount is
re-emittable or permanently unavailable for future emission, while an existential deposit is the
minimum amount of TAO an account must hold to exist on the blockchain, below which the account can
be reaped to prevent dust accumulation
([Glossary: Existential Deposit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#existential-deposit)).

- **Recycling and burning** — accounting treatment for protocol-removed amounts and their
  re-emission.
- **Existential deposit** — the minimum account balance below which an account is reaped.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Recycling and burning are paired supply-accounting labels that classify whether a deducted amount
can return toward future emission or is permanently unavailable. They name token reuse outcomes, not
the on-chain step that converts validator weights into emission shares
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes on a subnet. Recycling and burning describe what happens to
removed amounts in issuance accounting; Yuma Consensus performs weight aggregation and emission
settlement ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep supply-outcome vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Recycling and
burning answer whether a deducted amount can be re-emitted; Yuma Consensus answers how validator
weights become emission shares in a subnet
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Recycling and burning** — paired labels for re-emittable vs permanently removed amounts.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Distinction from Burn Cost Labels Recycling in Registration

The [Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost) describes
registration-related burn vocabulary whose accounting treatment is recycling rather than permanent
burning
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

That keeps fee language, stake-burn language, and registration burn language from collapsing into
one token sink label.

## Reader Boundary

Recycling and burning are conceptual supply-accounting terms
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The pair does not by itself prove an exact amount, diagnose a transaction, or explain every
mechanism that can move TAO or alpha. Transaction fees, stake burn, burn cost, and emissions provide
the surrounding event-specific details.
