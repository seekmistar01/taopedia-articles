# Subnet Markets

Subnet markets are the economic and evaluation context for Bittensor subnets. A subnet is an
incentive-based competition marketplace for a specific digital commodity: miners produce the
commodity, validators evaluate the work, and subnet incentives connect that evaluation to rewards.
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) and
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) anchor this market
view across the protocol.

## Market role

Each subnet gives Bittensor a separate market for useful digital work. The market is not the same as
the whole network: the broader Bittensor platform supplies blockchain context and TAO incentives,
while the subnet narrows the work category and evaluation context.
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) separates subnets
from the blockchain layer, and
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) ties each
subnet to a market for a particular digital commodity.

## Work and evaluation

Subnet markets need both production and evaluation. Miners produce the commodity, validators measure
quality under the subnet's incentive mechanism, and Yuma Consensus turns evaluator rankings into
miner and validator emission outcomes.
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)
connects incentive mechanisms to validator evaluation, while
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) connects validator rankings
to emissions.

## Token context

Each subnet market has a token side as well as a work side. In Dynamic TAO, subnet-specific alpha
tokens sit next to TAO in subnet pool context, giving the market a way to represent subnet-level
demand. That token context belongs with [[dynamic_tao|Dynamic TAO]] and
[[staking_and_delegation|Staking and Delegation]], while the subnet market view connects work
demand, evaluation, and staking context.
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) provides the
TAO reserve, alpha reserve, and alpha outstanding framing, and
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) links TAO and alpha-token emissions to
subnet rewards.

## Emissions and demand

Emissions make subnet markets economically visible inside Bittensor. The emission process
distributes newly created TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens to roles that contribute value
through mining, validation, staking, and subnet creation. In market terms, emissions connect
subnet-level demand and evaluation outcomes to rewards.
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) covers TAO and alpha-token emissions,
and [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) gives the
subnet context behind that distribution.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
subnet markets, that sequence changes how readers should interpret examples of market activity.

In local testing, a subnet market example can show how work, evaluation, and rewards are modeled
inside an isolated environment. That is useful for checking the market structure, but it does not
show live public demand, emissions, or participant behavior.

On testchain, subnet market behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives
stronger evidence about how miners, validators, token context, and subnet rules interact than a
private local run, while still keeping the result separate from production mainchain activity.

On mainchain, a subnet market belongs to live Bittensor economic state. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes subnets as specialized competition markets, so production readings should keep the
selected subnet, token context, emission state, and block timing attached.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A subnet-market example in one environment should not be
read as identical evidence about market behavior in another environment.

This distinction matters because a subnet market is both an evaluation context and an economic
context. The network environment explains what kind of evidence the example provides, while the
selected subnet explains which work, participants, and token state are in scope.

For readers, this keeps subnet-market examples from sounding stronger than their environment
supports. Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all contain subnet market language, but they
do not carry the same interpretive weight.

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Subnet markets connect miner work, validator evaluation, and emissions inside a subnet. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms) notes
that validators must evaluate miners separately for each mechanism, and the
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets)
docs explain how more than one incentive mechanism can operate inside a subnet.

For readers, a subnet market still names one subnet's economic context. That market may run more
than one incentive mechanism with separate evaluation paths.

## Relationship to Dynamic TAO

Subnet markets and Dynamic TAO are related but distinct parts of Bittensor network vocabulary.
Subnet markets name the per-subnet incentive-based competition market where miners produce and
validators evaluate a specific digital commodity, while Dynamic TAO names the protocol design that
gives each subnet its own alpha token in addition to network-level TAO. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) and
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) documentation
describe how subnets operate as specialized markets, and the same sources place those markets inside
the Dynamic TAO design that lets each subnet carry its own subnet-specific token alongside TAO.

For readers, a subnet market names the per-subnet economic context, while Dynamic TAO names the
broader design choice that gives every subnet its own token side. The two are not interchangeable:
the market names the market, Dynamic TAO names the tokenomics design that supports per-subnet
markets with their own alpha tokens. Subnet markets existed conceptually before Dynamic TAO, but the
Dynamic TAO design is what currently gives each subnet market its own token side.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction)

## Relationship to Subnet Deregistration

Subnet markets and subnet deregistration address related but different parts of Bittensor subnet
vocabulary. A subnet market is a Bittensor subnet viewed as a competition context — miners produce a
digital commodity, validators evaluate it, and emissions connect evaluation to rewards. Subnet
deregistration is the process that removes an entire non-immune subnet from the network when the
subnet count reaches the network limit and a new subnet registration attempts to enter, with the
subnet showing the lowest EMA of alpha price selected for removal. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes how each subnet operates as a specialized competition market, and the
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes the network-level removal process.

Subnet deregistration applies at the subnet level, not to individual participants inside a subnet.
When subnet deregistration occurs, the subnet itself is removed from the network, and all
participants registered in that subnet — miners, validators, and stakers — lose their positions in
it because the subnet context no longer exists. This is different from participant deregistration,
where one miner or validator loses a slot while the subnet continues operating. The
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) docs define
which subnets are eligible for removal and how selection works.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)

## Relationship to Coinbase

A subnet market and coinbase are related but different parts of how a subnet's economy operates. A
subnet market is the per-subnet competition context that links miner work, validator evaluation, and
emission rewards for a specific digital commodity. Coinbase is the per-block protocol mechanism that
drives TAO emission, injects TAO into emitting subnets' pool reserves, accumulates pending
emissions, and checks epoch boundaries to trigger Yuma Consensus rounds. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes the subnet as a market connecting work and rewards, and the
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes the per-block emission operation.

Coinbase is the mechanism that makes a subnet market economically active rather than merely defined.
A subnet market sets up the demand, evaluation, and roles, but it is coinbase that supplies the TAO
and alpha emissions those roles compete for and runs the epoch round that pays them out. The reward
leg of the market — the part that turns validator evaluation into emission outcomes for miners,
validators, and stakers — is exactly what coinbase executes. The market names the economic context;
coinbase names the protocol process that funds and settles it.
