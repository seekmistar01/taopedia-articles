# Subnet Stake Burn

Subnet stake burn is a Bittensor mechanism that adds TAO to a subnet reserve while permanently
removing alpha from circulation. The official
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn) page describes it as a
voluntary subnet buyback mechanism.

The term belongs to subnet tokenomics. It should be read as a pool and supply-accounting mechanism,
not as a general staking tutorial or a complete explanation of every reserve update that can happen
inside a subnet.

## Reserve Flow

The source describes a flow where TAO is added to the subnet's reserve in its automated market maker
pool. The pool then determines the corresponding alpha amount, removes that alpha from its reserve,
and burns it within the same chain action
([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

That reserve flow is the core article-level point. TAO enters the subnet reserve context, while
alpha leaves circulation. The mechanism changes pool composition and alpha supply at the same time.

## Supply Accounting

Subnet stake burn is one concrete use of burning vocabulary. The recycling-and-burning glossary
separates re-emittable recycled tokens from burned tokens, while the stake-burn documentation
describes a mechanism that burns alpha
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

For readers, the useful distinction is accounting scope. Subnet stake burn names the mechanism;
burning names the supply outcome for the removed alpha.

## Alpha Outstanding Boundary

Alpha outstanding describes alpha in circulation for a subnet. Subnet stake burn describes a
mechanism that can reduce that circulating alpha by burning a calculated amount
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

The concepts are connected but not identical. Alpha outstanding is a supply quantity, while subnet
stake burn is a mechanism that changes supply through a reserve action.

## Tempo Boundary

The official stake-burn page describes the mechanism as rate-limited per subnet and tied to tempo
([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)). That timing detail is
a protocol boundary, not an invitation to reproduce execution steps in a concept article.

For Taopedia, the important point is that subnet stake burn is not an unrestricted repeated action.
Its use is bounded by the source mechanism and should be interpreted through the official stake-burn
documentation.

## Conviction Boundary

Subnet stake burn and conviction both involve subnet alpha, but they describe different outcomes.
Stake burn removes alpha from circulation, while conviction and locked stake describe locking subnet
alpha so that a staked balance cannot be reduced below the locked amount
([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)).

This boundary matters because burning and locking are not interchangeable. A burn reduces
circulating alpha; a lock constrains movement of alpha that remains staked.

## Distinction from Burn Cost

Burn cost and subnet stake burn are two different burns that should not be conflated. Burn cost is
the TAO amount recycled when creating a new subnet market, while subnet stake burn is a voluntary
subnet buyback that adds TAO to a subnet reserve while permanently removing alpha from circulation
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

Creation vocabulary names the one-time cost to open a new netuid; stake-burn vocabulary names
ongoing reserve and supply adjustment on an existing subnet.

- **Burn cost** — recycled TAO required to create a new subnet market.
- **Subnet stake burn** — voluntary alpha buyback that adds TAO to subnet reserve.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Yuma Consensus and subnet stake burn describe related but different parts of Bittensor's incentive
system. Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that aggregates validator weight signals within a
subnet into miner incentives and validator dividends through clipping, bonding, and emission
calculation, while subnet stake burn names a voluntary tokenomics mechanism that adds TAO to a
subnet reserve while permanently removing alpha from circulation
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

Consensus-process vocabulary and stake-burn vocabulary answer different questions on the same
incentive landscape. Yuma Consensus names how validator weights become incentives and dividends;
subnet stake burn names reserve and supply-accounting movement rather than consensus math
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Distinction from Coinbase

Coinbase and subnet stake burn are adjacent mechanisms that move through different paths. Coinbase
is the per-block protocol process for TAO emission, pending-emission accounting, and epoch-boundary
activity, while subnet stake burn is a voluntary subnet buyback that adds TAO to a subnet reserve
while permanently removing alpha from circulation
([Coinbase documentation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

Both can be discussed near subnet reserves, but coinbase is part of recurring emission flow while
subnet stake burn is a separate alpha-burning mechanism.

- **Coinbase** — per-block emission process that advances TAO and alpha accounting.
- **Subnet stake burn** — voluntary alpha buyback that adds TAO to subnet reserve.

## Distinction from Slippage

Subnet stake burn is a voluntary alpha buyback that adds TAO to a subnet reserve while removing
alpha from circulation. Slippage is the gap between expected and received amounts when a pool
conversion executes ([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Subnet stake burn** — owner-initiated reserve and supply adjustment mechanism.
- **Slippage** — conversion gap during the staking step inside that operation.

Stake-burn documentation warns that the staking step can move the alpha price when liquidity is
thin, so slippage tolerance belongs to execution reading rather than to the burn label itself
([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)). Because of that,
official guidance recommends limit-price or slippage-tolerance controls on the operation, since the
alpha amount acquired for burning can differ from a pre-trade estimate when the pool moves during
execution ([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Distinction from Pool

Subnet stake burn belongs beside subnet-pool vocabulary because it updates reserve context. A subnet
liquidity pool names the paired TAO and alpha reserve structure, while subnet stake burn names one
mechanism that can add TAO to that structure and remove alpha from circulation
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

That distinction prevents pool state and burn mechanism from being collapsed into one term. The pool
is the reserve structure; stake burn is one mechanism that can change it.

## Reader Boundary

Subnet stake burn should be read as a subnet tokenomics mechanism. It adds TAO to subnet reserve
context, removes alpha from circulation, and is bounded by the official stake-burn rules
([Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn)).

The stable article-level point is that subnet stake burn connects reserve adjustment with permanent
alpha removal. Exact execution behavior, rejected attempts, and troubleshooting details belong in
the official mechanism documentation.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Subnet Stake
Burn, this sequence gives readers a boundary for interpreting stake-burn examples and
subnet-registration notes.

Localnet examples are isolated and reflect local chain state, so they are useful for controlled
experiments rather than evidence of live Bittensor behavior. Testnet examples add shared
non-production conditions, which can reveal integration behavior without touching mainnet state.

On mainnet, Subnet Stake Burn examples should be read as live production subnet-registration
behavior on the production Bittensor network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, so outcomes from one environment should not be treated as
proof of behavior in another.

## Source Scope

The official references scope Subnet Stake Burn to a specific Bittensor context rather than a
universal account, market, or operational signal. The article's primary source is
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn), while
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
supplies the adjacent system context used to interpret the term.

For readers, the useful boundary is that Subnet Stake Burn explains the referenced mechanism or
concept at the level the sources describe it. Current chain values, wallet state, subnet-specific
outcomes, or production measurements still belong to the relevant network, block, account, or tool
output at the time of observation.

## Owner Calls add_stake_burn Once Per Tempo

Official [Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn) documentation
states that the `add_stake_burn` extrinsic is rate-limited per subnet to one call each
[tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo), about 360 blocks. On a subnet
such as netuid 1, a second call inside the same tempo returns a rate-limit error.

That cap keeps stake burn tied to subnet-owner timing rather than to unlimited repeated burns on the
same [netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid).

## TAO Enters the Reserve While Alpha Burns

The stake-burn reference describes a sequence on a subnet such as netuid 1 in which submitted TAO is
added to the subnet's TAO reserve while an equivalent
[alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) amount is removed from
circulation within the same transaction.

Subnet stake burn therefore changes both sides of the pool ratio: more TAO in reserve, less
circulating
[alpha outstanding](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-outstanding-injection).
The price update follows the new reserve balance rather than leaving alpha supply unchanged.
