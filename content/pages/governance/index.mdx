# Bittensor Governance

Bittensor governance is the proposal and approval path for privileged network changes. The process
separates proposal creation by the Triumvirate from review and approval by the Senate
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

The structure is useful because it keeps proposal origin and proposal approval distinct. A proposal
can be brought forward for review without being the same thing as an approved change.

That makes Bittensor governance a process term. It connects the body names, proposal identifiers,
and approval status without collapsing them into one concept.

## Triumvirate Role

The Triumvirate is the proposal-origin side of the governance process. It creates proposals that can
then be reviewed by the Senate ([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

That means the Triumvirate should not be collapsed into the approval body. Its role is proposal
initiation, not final approval
([Glossary: Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate)).

This gives governance a directional flow. Proposal creation starts with the Triumvirate, while
approval belongs to Senate review.

## Visibility and Voting

Governance records can be visible without making every reader a voter. The
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) documentation separates public proposal
visibility from voting, which is restricted to senators.

That distinction prevents two different access ideas from being merged. Public visibility supports
inspection; voting authority belongs to the Senate role.

This matters for reader interpretation because a visible proposal is still only a governance item
under review. Voting authority and proposal status come from the Senate and governance record
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

## Privileged Change Scope

Governance applies to privileged network changes such as parameter changes, upgrades, and related
administrative actions ([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

This is a narrower scope than general Bittensor activity. Governance is not a validator-scoring
mechanism, a staking flow, or an ordinary subnet work loop; it is the review path for elevated
network changes.

The security context is why Sudo belongs nearby. Sudo names the older privileged-authority concept,
while governance names the proposal-and-review process that replaced direct single-key
administration ([Glossary: Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo),
[Governance Overview: security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance#security)).

That makes governance the review path for elevated changes, not the mechanism for ordinary subnet
work. Subnet incentives and governance can interact, but they answer different system questions
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Proposal Flow Boundary

Bittensor governance claims should keep the proposal flow visible. The governance overview separates
Triumvirate proposal creation, Senate approval or rejection, and later finalization or application
steps ([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

Approval by one body is not the same as a completed governance action. Precise governance language
should identify whether it is discussing proposal creation, Senate review, voting result,
finalization, or application.

This keeps governance references from overstating a proposal's status. The proposal flow supplies
the structure, while the specific governance record supplies the status for a particular change.

## Foundation-to-Community Transition

Bittensor's governance protocol is designed to move network management from foundation-centralized
control toward community ownership over time
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

The first step in that transition is a
[bicameral legislature](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#bicameral-legislature).
In that opening stage, the
[Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate) creates
[proposals](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal) and the
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate) reviews them for approval or
rejection. The two-body arrangement is therefore an early governance structure, not the final
endpoint of decentralization.

That framing helps when governance language appears beside older privileged-authority vocabulary.
[Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo) names the earlier single-key
administration model, while governance names the review path that is meant to broaden control over
time ([Governance Overview: security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance#security)).

## Majority Plus Close Before Execution

Governance documentation states that a proposal is executed only after two conditions are both
satisfied: the Senate reaches a majority of approvals, defined as more than half of seated senators,
and a Triumvirate member closes the proposal
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

That pairing is tighter than Senate approval alone. A proposal can accumulate enough senator
approvals and still remain unexecuted until the close step occurs on the Triumvirate side. Execution
vocabulary therefore belongs to the moment when both gates have passed, not to the vote tally by
itself.

The same rule also keeps execution separate from review language. Senate records show whether enough
[senators](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate) approved or disapproved a
[proposal hash](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal-hash) reference; the
close step records whether the approved action was actually carried out
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

## Public Viewing and Senator Voting Use Different Paths

The [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) page separates inspection from
voting authority. Anyone can view proposals currently before the Senate through the unpermissioned
`btcli sudo proposals` command, while senator voting uses `btcli sudo senate-vote` and is limited to
senators.

Senate voting documentation states that a senator is prompted for the proposal hash, which can be
obtained from the proposals overview, and then prompted to cast approval or disapproval. Once
confirmed, the vote is included in the next block and counted in the vote
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

That split keeps governance reading precise. Public visibility supports review of what is before the
Senate, while senator voting records whether an eligible delegate approved or disapproved a specific
proposal hash. Neither path replaces Triumvirate proposal creation or the close step described in
governance documentation ([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

## Governance Versus Subnet Incentives on netuid 1

On a subnet such as netuid 1, ordinary subnet work is evaluated through incentive mechanisms,
[validator weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector), and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Governance sits on a separate path for privileged network changes. A senator may need subnet
registration and stake weight to participate in Senate review, but Senate approval is not the same
process as validator scoring or emission allocation on netuid 1
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That boundary keeps governance claims from being read as subnet incentive outcomes, and keeps subnet
performance claims from being read as governance approval.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For governance on
a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how proposal and Senate examples should be read.

In localnet, governance mechanics can be exercised in isolation. Localnet proposal records and
Senate examples reflect that test chain rather than production governance history.

On testnet, governance activity on netuid 1 applies in a shared non-production network where
proposal records and subnet incentive state are separate from mainnet.

On mainnet, governance on netuid 1 concerns production proposal review, privileged changes, and
Senate approval on the live network
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A governance example from one environment should not be
read as representing another environment.

## Distinction from Senate

The Senate reviews proposals for approval or rejection in Bittensor's bicameral governance stage
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate),
[Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate)).

Governance names the overall proposal-and-approval process; Senate names the reviewing body in that
process.

The [Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) page sets participation requirements
beyond the general review role described elsewhere. A coldkey-hotkey pair must be registered on a
[subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet), hold more than two percent of
the network's total [stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake) through
self-stake or [delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation), and elect
to participate in Senate review ([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

The [Glossary: Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#senate) also describes the
body as formed from the top delegate hotkeys. When all twelve Senate seats are occupied, a new
eligible delegate replaces the lowest-stake seated member rather than expanding the body beyond that
cap ([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

Those stake and seat rules connect Senate membership to network weight. Public proposal visibility
remains open to inspection, but seated senator status depends on documented stake thresholds and
seat limits rather than on read access alone.

The Senate is the approval body in the governance process. The
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate) reference describes senators as the
voting group that reviews proposals.

This makes Senate approval a governance threshold. Proposal creation starts the review path, while
Senate voting determines whether the proposal clears that approval step.

Senate language should therefore stay attached to review and voting. It should not be used as a
replacement for the proposal itself or for the wider bicameral structure
([Glossary: Bicameral Legislature](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#bicameral-legislature)).

The distinction also keeps Senate and senator vocabulary separate. Senate names the collective
review body; senator names the individual delegate role that votes in that body
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

## Distinction from Triumvirate

The Triumvirate creates proposals that the Senate reviews. Governance vocabulary covers both steps
without collapsing them into one role
([Glossary: Triumvirate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#triumvirate),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

Proposal creation and Senate review answer different questions in the same governance path.

## Distinction from Proposal

A proposal is the privileged change package under review. Governance names the wider process that
includes creation, review, and execution gates
([Glossary: Proposal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#proposal)).

Readers should name the specific proposal record when citing a hash or outcome.

## Distinction from Sudo

Sudo names the earlier single-key administration model. Governance names the review path meant to
broaden control over privileged changes
([Glossary: Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo),
[Governance Overview: security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance#security)).

Sudo vocabulary belongs to privileged administration history; governance vocabulary belongs to
proposal review and approval.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Governance names the Triumvirate-and-Senate path for privileged network changes such as parameter
updates and chain upgrades. Yuma Consensus names the tempo-bound process that converts validator
weight submissions into miner incentives and validator dividends on a selected netuid
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Proposal-review vocabulary and subnet-settlement vocabulary answer different questions. Governance
tracks whether a privileged change cleared Senate approval; Yuma aggregates eligible validator
weights through clipping and bonding at epoch boundaries
([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate),
[Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).

Senate voting authority is restricted to senators and concerns network-level proposals. Validator
weight submission and Yuma settlement concern subnet evaluation and emission shares rather than
governance proposal status ([Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate),
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

An approved governance proposal may change hyperparameters or chain logic, but that administrative
outcome is separate from how Yuma credits miner and validator rewards from weights in a given tempo
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

- **Governance** — proposal review and approval for privileged network changes.
- **Yuma Consensus** — aggregation that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Distinction from Subnet Incentives

Subnet incentives name miner and validator outcomes inside a subnet's mechanism. Governance names
network-level proposal review for privileged changes
([Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance)).

A governance outcome is not evidence of subnet performance quality on its own.

## Distinction from Proposal Review

A governance proposal is a review item for a privileged change. The
[Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance) frames governance around proposal
creation, voting, and approval rather than ordinary subnet operation.

That scope keeps governance vocabulary separate from day-to-day subnet incentives. Governance
concerns privileged network changes; subnet incentive mechanisms concern how subnet work is
evaluated and rewarded
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Proposal review also keeps status language separate from process language. A submitted proposal, an
approved proposal, and an applied network change are related, but they are not the same governance
state.

That status boundary matters when reading governance summaries. The proposal path tells readers
where a change sits in review, while the specific proposal record explains the requested change.

## Reader Boundary

Bittensor governance should be read as proposal-and-approval vocabulary. Triumvirate proposal
creation and Senate approval are distinct parts of the process
([Governance Overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance),
[Senate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/governance/senate)).

It does not provide voting instructions, proposal strategy, operational steps, or point-in-time
governance results. Exact proposal outcomes belong to the specific governance record being
consulted.
