# Issuance

Issuance is the circulating amount of TAO in the Bittensor network. The
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance) places issuance in
circulating-supply vocabulary, including TAO held directly and TAO held in subnet liquidity pools.

## Circulating TAO

Issuance is a supply measure, not a reward category. It tells readers which TAO is counted as part
of network circulation. The
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance) includes both
directly held TAO and TAO held in subnet pool contexts.

That makes issuance broader than a single role's received rewards. It is the supply-side term for
circulating TAO, not a label for one payout path.

## Subnet Pool Context

Subnet pool TAO belongs to issuance vocabulary because it remains part of circulating supply. The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes TAO entering
subnet contexts, while the [Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)
glossary entry includes TAO held in subnet liquidity pools.

This boundary keeps pool-held TAO from being mistaken for a separate supply category. It is still
part of the circulating issuance picture, even though it sits in a subnet reserve context.

Pool-held TAO also keeps issuance separate from wallet-balance language. A unit of TAO can be part
of circulating issuance without being held directly by an individual wallet. The glossary's
pool-held wording makes issuance a network supply term rather than a personal balance term
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

That distinction is useful when reading subnet liquidity material. A subnet pool can hold TAO as
part of the subnet's reserve structure, while issuance still names the broader circulating TAO
amount that includes those reserves ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Emission Boundary

Issuance and emission answer different tokenomics questions. Emission names the process that creates
and allocates new token amounts over time
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)). Issuance names the circulating TAO
supply state that those processes affect.

In short, emission is process vocabulary and issuance is supply-state vocabulary. A statement about
new rewards reaching a role is not the same as a statement about circulating TAO supply.

This makes issuance useful as context around emission without replacing the emission process. Newly
created TAO can affect the supply picture, but issuance itself names the amount being counted, not
the validator, miner, staker, or subnet creator path that received emitted tokens
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The same separation helps when RAO appears in token amounts. RAO gives the smaller denomination of
TAO, while issuance identifies which TAO supply is in circulation
([Glossary: RAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#rao)).

## Halving Boundary

TAO halving rules use issuance milestones. The
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving) documentation describes
network-level halving as a reduction in TAO emission rate after total issuance reaches supply-based
milestones.

That makes issuance important for halving context, but it does not make issuance the same as
halving. Issuance names the supply measure; halving names the rate-change rule that refers to that
measure.

Halving language is therefore clearest when the supply measure and the rate change stay separate.
Issuance gives the supply-side milestone context, while halving describes the emission-rate change
that follows the milestone ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

This also keeps TAO issuance distinct from subnet alpha-token language. TAO issuance describes the
network token's circulating-supply context; alpha tokens belong to subnet-specific tokenomics
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Recycling and Burning

Recycling and burning affect supply records in different ways. The
[Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
glossary entry describes recycling as subtracting tokens from the issuance record so the same
quantity can be emitted again later. Burning removes tokens from ordinary circulation without making
them available for future emission in the same way.

These terms should stay separate from issuance itself. Issuance names the circulating-supply record;
recycling and burning describe supply-flow changes that can affect how that record is interpreted.

That distinction keeps supply-accounting language precise. A recycling statement is about tokens
leaving the issuance record for later emission, while a burning statement is about removal from
ordinary circulation
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Issuance remains the counted supply context around those flows, not the name of either flow.

## Reading Boundaries

Issuance is the right term when the focus is circulating TAO supply. If the focus is token creation
and distribution, [emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) is more precise. If
the focus is scheduled emission-rate reduction,
[halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving) is more precise. If the focus is supply
removal or reuse,
[recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning) is
more precise.

These boundaries keep issuance focused on supply records, not staking returns, subnet rankings, or a
full explanation of every tokenomics flow.
