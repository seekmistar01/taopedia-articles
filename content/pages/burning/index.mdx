# Burning

Burning is the removal of tokens from spendable circulation. In Bittensor tokenomics, the term is
useful because it separates permanent circulation removal from recycling, emission, staking, and
ordinary transfer activity
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The key distinction is what happens after the token flow enters protocol accounting. Burning means
the amount is not available for ordinary transfer or later re-emission, while recycling keeps a
deducted amount available for later protocol distribution.

## Supply Context

Burning is a supply-side tokenomics concept. It describes a token amount being removed from
spendable circulation rather than being paid to another user or returned through future emission
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

For readers, burning answers a narrow question: whether the token amount remains usable in normal
circulation. It does not explain who earned a reward, which subnet produced work, or how emissions
were allocated.

That narrow scope keeps burning from becoming a catch-all label for every token sink. A token amount
can leave one account, pay a fee, be recycled, or be burned; those are related movements, but they
are not the same tokenomics outcome
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The spendable-circulation boundary is the key article-level point. Burning concerns the status of
the deducted amount after the protocol accounts for it, not the immediate action that caused the
deduction.

## Recycling Boundary

Burning and recycling are related but different terms. Burning removes tokens from spendable
circulation, while recycling keeps deducted amounts available for future protocol accounting
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)).

That contrast prevents every token sink from being read the same way. Burning is a removal concept;
recycling is a reuse concept.

The distinction also explains why the phrase "recycling and burning" appears as a combined
tokenomics topic. Both terms describe token flows after a deduction, but they point to opposite
outcomes for whether the amount can later return through protocol distribution
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

This boundary matters when a source uses burn-like language near protocol accounting. The safe
reading is to check whether the amount is removed from spendable circulation or kept available for
future emission through recycling.

## Issuance and Emission Context

Burned tokens remain counted in the record of token issuance even though they no longer effectively
circulate
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

This keeps burning distinct from recycling. A recycled amount can be emitted again, while a burned
amount is not made available for future emission by the recycling rule.

Emission is the creation and allocation side of Bittensor tokenomics. Burning concerns a different
supply-flow direction: removal from spendable circulation
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)).

For readers, this distinction keeps reward distribution separate from supply removal. Burning does
not by itself explain subnet scoring, validator dividends, or miner incentives.

That also keeps issuance language clear. Burning does not erase the historical fact that tokens were
issued; it describes their later removal from ordinary spendable circulation
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Fee Accounting Context

Transaction fees can sit near recycling-and-burning terms because fees are token flows attached to
state-changing actions. The fee itself names a cost, while burning names one possible
supply-accounting outcome
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

This keeps fee language separate from burning language. A fee describes what an action costs;
burning describes removal from spendable circulation.

Fee handling should therefore be read through the fee article first. Burning can describe the
tokenomics outcome of a deducted amount, but it does not replace the separate explanation of why a
fee exists or how a fee-bearing action is represented
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

A fee-bearing action is therefore not automatically a burn statement. The fee source explains the
cost side of the action, while the recycling-and-burning vocabulary explains the supply-accounting
status of the deducted amount after collection.

## Burn-Cost Label Context

Burning language can appear near terms that do not always mean permanent destruction. Burn cost is
the clearest example: Bittensor documentation uses the familiar burn-cost label for subnet creation,
while also noting that the subnet creation cost is technically recycled rather than burned
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Local subnet creation: Subnet creation cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/local-build/create-subnet#subnet-creation-cost)).

That makes burning useful as a vocabulary boundary. A label that includes "burn" should still be
read with the supply-accounting treatment in the relevant source. If the amount can return through
recycling, the tokenomics result is different from burning even when the user-facing label uses burn
language
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Environment Boundary

Burning is a conceptual tokenomics term, not an environment-status report. Bittensor network labels
such as mainnet, testnet, and localnet tell readers where an observation belongs, while burning
itself describes the supply outcome
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)).

That boundary matters for static reference prose. A local or testing example can illustrate the
term, but the durable article point is the tokenomics distinction between removal and recycling, not
a specific token total.

## Reading the Term

Burning should be read as high-level Bittensor tokenomics terminology. It is not a report of exact
network totals, advice about token movement, or a shortcut for every supply-flow concept
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The stable article-level point is that burning removes tokens from spendable circulation rather than
returning them to future emission accounting.

That reading is intentionally narrower than strategy or valuation language. Burning can explain a
supply-accounting outcome, but it does not predict token value, recommend a transaction, or report a
current network total.

## Halving and Supply Context

Halving relates to burning in the broader supply picture through a different mechanism. Halving
reduces the emission rate rather than destroying existing tokens, so burning and halving describe
different supply-reduction paths. Burning removes tokens from spendable circulation immediately;
halving reduces the rate at which new tokens are created going forward
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

The burning and recycling distinction also matters in the context of specific protocol actions.
Bittensor documentation describes certain token-flow outcomes as burning and others as recycling,
depending on whether the amount is removed from spendable circulation or returned for future
protocol distribution
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

For readers, burning belongs on the removal side of supply accounting, distinct from both halving
and recycling. A token that is burned is not halved, and a token that is recycled is not removed
from future emission. That three-way distinction keeps supply vocabulary precise and prevents
burning from being conflated with rate reductions or return flows
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).
