# Bittensor Emissions

Bittensor emissions describe how newly created TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens enter the
network's reward system ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The term is broader than a single token amount. Emissions connect token creation, subnet context,
and reward allocation rather than only describing a supply counter.

Emissions are easiest to read as a flow: newly emitted value enters the relevant network or subnet
context, then moves through subnet incentive and consensus machinery
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

That flow keeps supply language and allocation language connected. Emissions describe the reward
side entering the system, while incentive and consensus context explain how rewards are handled
after they reach a subnet context.

## Asset Scope

Emissions can involve TAO and subnet alpha tokens. TAO belongs to the network-wide side of Bittensor
tokenomics, while alpha tokens belong to individual subnet contexts
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Alpha Tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-tokens)).

This matters because the asset being discussed changes the reading. TAO emission context and subnet
alpha-token context are different parts of the same tokenomics topic rather than one undivided
reward amount.

Emissions are also different from plain issuance. Issuance names a circulating supply measure, while
emissions describe creation and distribution flows
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Injection and Distribution

The [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) reference separates the idea of
emission entering subnet contexts from the later distribution of rewards. Injection describes how
new emissions reach the relevant subnet context; distribution describes how those rewards are
allocated after the subnet context exists.

For readers, injection and distribution answer different questions. Injection explains how emissions
enter a subnet context; distribution explains how rewards are allocated once the subnet context is
in view.

Flow-based emissions are one example of terminology that depends on subnet context rather than only
on a network-wide release schedule
([Flow-Based Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#flow-based-emissions)).

## Subnet Allocation

Subnets give emissions a work-and-evaluation setting. Subnet incentives connect useful work,
validator evaluation, and reward outcomes
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

That makes emissions more than accounting terminology. The emitted assets matter because they are
routed through subnet-specific incentive systems rather than distributed as a generic supply faucet.

## Consensus Context

Yuma Consensus explains how validator evaluation signals are aggregated into subnet outcomes
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)). Emissions are related
because rewards need consensus-driven allocation context.

The two concepts answer different questions. Emissions explain what value enters the reward system,
while Yuma Consensus explains how subnet evaluation signals shape reward allocation after validator
signals are available.

This keeps emission language separate from consensus-result language. A rank, trust value, or
dividend is downstream of mechanism context; emissions describe the rewards being allocated
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Staking Context

Staking connects token holders to validator-side reward outcomes, so emissions often appear beside
staking and delegation terms. The
[Staking and Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
documentation describes that staking context, while the
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) reference keeps the broader emission
frame around reward creation and distribution.

For readers, staking is one place emissions can be observed, not the whole emissions system.
Emission terminology keeps the broader tokenomics context attached.

## Halving and Flow

Halving and flow-based emissions refine the broader emissions topic. The
[Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving) reference describes supply-rate
reductions, while the
[flow-based emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#flow-based-emissions) section
describes a subnet-context distribution model.

Those ideas stay separate. Halving changes emission rate, flow-based emissions describe subnet
allocation context, and Bittensor emissions names the broader reward creation and distribution
topic.

That distinction helps readers place tokenomics terms in order. Halving adjusts the rate, injection
and distribution describe movement into and through subnet contexts, and consensus context explains
allocation outcomes ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Reader Boundary

Bittensor emissions are a tokenomics concept, not an environment report. Mainnet, testnet, and
localnet tell readers where an observation belongs, while emissions describe the reward creation and
distribution topic being discussed
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That network label matters when an example includes observed values. The durable framing is the
relationship among TAO, subnet alpha, injection, distribution, and subnet incentives.

Bittensor emissions are tokenomics and reward-distribution terminology. The stable framing is that
emissions connect newly created TAO and subnet alpha tokens to subnet reward outcomes
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Bittensor emissions do not provide reward estimates, staking guidance, validator guidance, or
network summaries. Exact observed values belong to the specific network context that produced them.

## Dynamic TAO Context

Bittensor emissions fit inside the Dynamic TAO model, where the network allocates TAO to subnet
reserves and subnets emit alpha tokens for subnet reward distribution. Dynamic TAO places emissions
inside that allocation structure: TAO is allocated to subnet reserves, while subnet-side emissions
produce alpha-token rewards for validators, miners, and stakers
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

That architecture connects two emission dimensions. TAO emissions describe the network-level flow
from the TAO supply to subnet contexts, while alpha emissions describe the subnet-level flow from
subnet-side issuance to role-facing subnet rewards
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Reserve vocabulary keeps that architecture readable. TAO reserve and alpha reserve describe pool
sides, while alpha outstanding and distributed rewards describe alpha outside the reserve side of
the subnet economy
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The useful reading is the two-level structure: network-level TAO allocation and subnet-level alpha
distribution. Bittensor emissions names the broader topic that connects those levels without turning
every reserve, staking, or consensus term into a synonym for emissions.
