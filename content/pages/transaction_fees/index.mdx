# Transaction Fees

Transaction fees are costs the Bittensor chain can attach to state-changing network actions. The
official [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) page separates
base chain transaction fees from swap fees that can apply when stake-related activity interacts with
subnet liquidity. It also notes that reading chain state is free.

## General Transaction Fees

The official fees page describes many state-changing actions as fee-bearing. Their base transaction
fee has two parts: a weight component, tied to computational load, and a length component, tied to
the size of the submitted action. At a Taopedia level, the important point is the distinction
between the base chain fee and other economic effects that may appear around a specific Bittensor
action.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Swap Fees

The same source treats swap fees as separate from the base transaction fee. Stake and unstake
activity can also involve an amount-based swap fee when liquidity is transacted through subnet
markets. This means a stake-related action can have both a base chain fee and a liquidity-linked
fee, while ordinary chain reads have neither.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Fee-Free Activity

Bittensor documentation identifies chain reads as free and lists some weight-setting and
commit-reveal actions as fee-free. The practical distinction is not whether an action feels small to
a user, but whether the chain classifies that action as fee-bearing. This keeps fee-free activity
separate from actions that change state and consume chain resources.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Grouped Actions

The official page also explains that grouped actions inherit fee behavior from their component
actions. If a grouped action contains a fee-bearing component, the group can carry a fee; if every
component is fee-free, the group can remain fee-free. This helps readers avoid assuming that
grouping actions always removes or duplicates fees.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Fee Recycling Context

The
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
describes collected transaction fees as recycled tokens deducted from the record of token issuance
for future emission. In Taopedia prose, that links base chain fees to recycling vocabulary rather
than treating every fee as burned or permanently removed.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Relationship to Mempool

Transaction fees and the mempool address different parts of a submitted chain action. The
[Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool) is where pending
transactions wait before block inclusion, while
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) describes the costs the
chain can attach to state-changing actions, including weight and length components.

For readers, the mempool names the pending holding period, while transaction fees describe cost
accounting for fee-bearing actions that may wait there before inclusion.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool)

## Relationship to Extrinsics

Transaction fees and extrinsics address related but different parts of a submitted chain action. An
extrinsic is a state-changing runtime request, while
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) describes the costs the
chain can attach to those actions, including weight and length components.

For readers, an extrinsic names the submitted action, while transaction fees describe the fee
accounting that may apply when that action is processed.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subtensor extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/extrinsics)

## Relationship to Blocks

Transaction fees and blocks address related but different parts of chain activity. The
[Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block) describes a block as
containing a collection of transactions, while
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) describes the costs the
chain can attach to state-changing actions that may be included there.

For readers, a block names chain ordering and inclusion, while transaction fees describe fee
accounting for fee-bearing actions recorded inside that block.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block)

## Relationship to Recycling

Transaction fees and recycling address related but different parts of Bittensor token flow.
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) describes costs on
state-changing actions, while the
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
describes collected transaction fees as recycled tokens deducted from the issuance record for future
emission.

For readers, transaction fees name the cost layer on submitted actions, while recycling describes
what can happen to collected fees after they enter issuance accounting.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)

## Relationship to Subtensor

Transaction fees and Subtensor address related but different parts of Bittensor chain economics. The
[Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor) defines
Subtensor as Bittensor's layer 1 blockchain and system of record for transactions, while
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) describes the costs the
chain can attach to state-changing actions processed inside that layer.

For readers, Subtensor names the blockchain layer where fees are enforced, while transaction fees
describe the cost accounting applied to fee-bearing actions on that layer. Chain reads inspect
Subtensor state without incurring those base transaction fees.

References: [Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Relationship to Subtensor Error Codes

Transaction fees and Subtensor error codes address related but different parts of a failed chain
action. [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) describes costs
on state-changing actions, while the [Subtensor Error Codes](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/errors)
overview groups failure families returned when an action is rejected, including balance and
fee-related conditions.

For readers, transaction fees explain what a successful fee-bearing action may cost, while error
codes explain why a submission failed before completing. A rejected extrinsic can surface an error
code even when the fee model itself is understood.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subtensor Error Codes](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/errors)

## Relationship to Network Context

Transaction fees are evaluated inside a specific Bittensor network environment. The
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet as deployment contexts, and fee-bearing actions are
processed against whichever network endpoint is being used. Balances, subnet markets, and fee
outcomes belong to that network's chain history.

For readers, this matters when estimating or comparing costs across environments. A fee-bearing
action on testnet uses testnet balances and state even when the fee rule family matches mainnet
documentation.

References: [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Relationship to Chain Reads

Transaction fees and chain reads address opposite sides of Subtensor interaction. The official
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) page identifies chain
reads as free, while state-changing actions can carry base transaction fees with weight and length
components. The [Subtensor extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/extrinsics)
reference lists submitted runtime methods that change state.

For readers, a chain read inspects current state without submitting a fee-bearing extrinsic.
Transaction fees belong to the submission path that asks the runtime to record a change, not to the
inspection path that queries existing state.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subtensor extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/extrinsics)

## Careful Interpretation

Transaction-fee details can depend on the action being evaluated and on current chain behavior.
Taopedia readers should use this article as conceptual orientation, not as a fee calculator or an
operating guide. For exact current behavior, examples, and estimation paths, use the official
Bittensor documentation.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)
