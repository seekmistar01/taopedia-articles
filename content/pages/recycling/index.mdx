# Recycling

Recycling is a Bittensor tokenomics concept for reusable token supply. It covers token amounts that
return to issuance accounting instead of being treated as ordinary wallet-to-wallet transfers. The
official glossary places recycling beside burning because both terms explain what happens after a
token amount leaves normal spendable circulation:
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning).

The important distinction is reusability. A burned amount is removed from spendable circulation,
while a recycled amount can be accounted for so the same quantity becomes available to emit again.
That makes recycling a supply-flow term, not a payment term and not a wallet action.

## Supply Accounting Context

Recycling helps readers follow Bittensor supply accounting when token amounts pass through protocol
processes. The concept is concerned with where the amount goes after collection, especially when the
amount is deducted from issuance accounting and can later return through emission:
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance).

This framing keeps recycling separate from normal transfers. A transfer changes custody between
accounts, while recycling changes how a protocol-captured amount is represented in the supply cycle.
The same token amount can leave ordinary circulation without being permanently destroyed.

## Recycling and Burning

Recycling and burning are adjacent concepts because both concern token amounts removed from ordinary
use, but they answer different questions. Burning emphasizes removal from spendable circulation.
Recycling emphasizes whether the protocol can return an amount to future emission:
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling).

For article readers, the distinction prevents a common tokenomics error. Not every sink-like flow is
destruction. Some flows reduce ordinary spendability while preserving a path back into the emission
cycle. Recycling names that reusable side of the accounting boundary.

## Relation to Emission

Emission is the process through which Bittensor distributes newly available token amounts over time.
Recycling matters because a recycled amount can be deducted from issuance accounting and then become
available for emission again: [Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning).

That relationship makes recycling part of the supply cycle rather than a separate rewards system.
Emission explains how tokens are paid out by protocol mechanisms; recycling explains how some
previously captured amounts can re-enter what the protocol may emit later.

## Subnet Cost Context

One place recycling appears in Bittensor vocabulary is around costs associated with joining subnet
capacity. The glossary links the recycle concept to amounts paid into protocol processes rather than
to ordinary purchases or manual redistribution:
[Glossary: Recycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycle),
[Glossary: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost).

The reader-facing point is not the transaction procedure. It is the accounting outcome: the amount
can be handled as part of the protocol's tokenomics cycle. That is why recycling belongs with
supply, emission, and burning concepts rather than with operational wallet instructions.

## Network Context

Bittensor documentation separates local, test, and main network environments. Recycling examples
should be read in that environment context, because supply accounting in one environment is not a
statement about another:
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development).

This boundary is especially useful for interpreting examples and experiments. A local or test
environment can demonstrate mechanics, but the article topic remains the tokenomics meaning of
recycling: reusable supply accounting for protocol-captured amounts.

## Reader Boundary

This article defines recycling as a Bittensor tokenomics concept. It does not provide wallet
instructions, estimate future supply conditions, or recommend financial decisions:
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions).

Readers who need exact values or implementation details should use the relevant official
documentation for that specific task. The stable purpose of this page is conceptual: to explain why
recycling is different from burning, transfers, and ordinary fee payment.
