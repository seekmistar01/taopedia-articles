# Wash Trading

Wash trading is trading with yourself, buying and selling the same asset between accounts you
control, to manufacture the appearance of activity. In Bittensor it would mean swapping a subnet's
alpha back and forth in its pool so that trading looks busier or a price looks more active than real
demand would make it.

## Why It Is Costly Here

Bittensor prices a subnet's alpha through an automated market maker pool, where every swap moves the
price and the trader absorbs slippage. A buy followed by a sell returns the trader to roughly where
they started, minus that slippage and any transaction costs. So a round-trip is not a way to gain
value; it is a way to spend it, and the spending repeats with every cycle of the wash.

## What It Can and Cannot Do

Wash trading cannot create real value or directly earn protocol rewards, because Bittensor pays
emissions for mining and validation rather than for trading volume. What it can do is inflate
surface-level numbers, such as how much a token appears to be traded, which is the usual motive for
doing it. That cosmetic effect, though, is bought at a cost that grows with every round-trip rather
than for free.

## Distinction from Sandwich Attack

Wash trading is self-directed and self-funded, while a sandwich attack exploits the ordering of
someone else's trade in the mempool
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Self-dealing vocabulary and third-party-exploitation vocabulary answer different questions. Wash
trading manufactures volume by acting as both buyer and seller; sandwich attacks front-run and
back-run another participant's swap.

- **Wash trading** — self-dealing round-trips that fake volume at the actor's cost.
- **Sandwich attack** — ordering exploit that extracts value from someone else's pending trade.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For wash trading,
that sequence changes how readers should interpret pool depth, slippage, and apparent trading volume
examples.

In localnet, round-trip swap costs can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet pool depth
does not represent production slippage on live subnets.

On testnet, wash-trading mechanics can be exercised in a shared non-production network. Testnet pool
conditions are separate from mainnet state
([Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

On mainnet, wash trading concerns live production subnet pools where self-funded round-trips absorb
real slippage on the selected [netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A wash-trading example from one environment should not be
read as describing production pool behavior in another environment.

## Liquidity Positions Exclude Self-Funded Fee Loops

[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions) documentation notes
that a position does not accumulate fees for staking operations performed by the coldkey that owns
it. A provider therefore earns trading fees from other participants' activity, not from routing its
own stakes through its own range.

That rule closes an obvious self-dealing loop. Wash trading tries to manufacture activity by acting
as both sides of a trade, while the liquidity model is designed so a provider cannot pay swap fees
back to itself through its own coldkey activity.

## Price Discovery Needs Independent Trades

Price discovery names how a subnet's alpha price emerges from independent buying and selling in the
pool. [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)
documentation describes each subnet's dynamic currency as that subnet's alpha token, priced through
its own automated market maker pool. Wash trading is the opposite pattern: the same controller acts
on both sides to simulate activity without adding a genuine outside valuation signal.

That contrast keeps wash trading in cosmetic-volume vocabulary. A discovered price reflects what
unrelated participants were willing to trade at, while wash trading only repeats a self-funded
round-trip that the pool charges for through slippage
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Distinction from Arbitrage

Wash trading and arbitrage both involve repeated pool swaps, but only one responds to a real price
signal. Arbitrage trades on an actual gap between a subnet's pool price and broader market pricing,
buying the cheaper side and selling the dearer side until the gap closes, while wash trading has no
gap to close: the same controller buys and sells its own alpha to simulate activity and absorbs
slippage on every round-trip, so arbitrage corrects price while wash trading only manufactures the
appearance of volume
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Arbitrage** — trading that closes a real price gap between markets.
- **Wash trading** — self-dealing that fakes volume with no real gap, paying slippage each cycle.

## Distinction from MEV

Wash trading and MEV both describe ways value moves around pool trades, but they target different
things. Wash trading is self-dealing, the same controller buying and selling its own alpha to
simulate volume, while MEV (maximal extractable value) is value extracted by controlling the
ordering or inclusion of other participants' transactions, such as front-running or sandwiching a
pending swap
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **Wash trading** — self-dealing that fakes volume on one's own trades.
- **MEV** — value extracted by ordering or including other participants' transactions.

## Distinction from Slippage

Wash trading and slippage name a strategy and the cost it keeps paying. Wash trading is the pattern
of round-tripping one's own alpha to manufacture volume, while slippage is the difference between
the expected and received amounts when a trade moves through the pool's reserves, the very cost each
wash round-trip absorbs
([Glossary: Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage)).

- **Wash trading** — the self-dealing pattern that fakes volume.
- **Slippage** — the expected-versus-received gap each round-trip pays through the pool.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Wash trading names self-dealing round-trips that make a subnet pool look busier than independent
demand would support. Each buy-and-sell cycle moves the pool price and pays slippage, so the pattern
buys appearance rather than real value
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Bittensor [incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives) flow to miners
and validators through [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
ranking rather than through raw trading volume in a subnet pool.
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) material describes subnet rewards as
tied to mining, validation, and staking roles inside consensus timing. Wash trading therefore cannot
substitute for that documented reward path: inflated swap counts do not replace the rank-based miner
and validator outcomes that emission vocabulary actually describes.

Yuma Consensus converts validator weight submissions into miner incentives and validator dividends
each tempo. That settlement step reads evaluation signals, not how many times alpha was swapped in a
pool during the interval ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Price discovery reflects what unrelated participants trade at in the pool. Wash trading uses the
same controller on both sides, so it simulates activity without adding a genuine outside valuation
signal ([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Liquidity-position rules also block one self-dealing loop: a provider does not accumulate fees from
staking operations run by the coldkey that owns the position. That design keeps provider rewards
tied to other participants' activity rather than to self-routed swaps
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Wash trading** — artificial volume meant to mislead markets.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement that turns validator weights into emission shares, not
  pool swap volume
  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level. It is not a claim that wash trading never happens or
any kind of trading advice, and it does not quantify the cost, which depends on live pool depth and
prices that change over time. The durable point is the shape of the incentive: the pool design makes
faking activity an expense the faker keeps paying.
