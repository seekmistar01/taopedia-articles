# Yuma Consensus in Bittensor

Yuma Consensus (YC) is Bittensor’s mechanism for aggregating validator-submitted weights into final
incentives for miners and dividends for validators. Validators evaluate miners’ outputs on a subnet,
submit weights that reflect relative performance, and Yuma Consensus combines these signals into a
robust outcome that resists manipulation and rewards honest, high-quality work.

- **Role:** Translates validator evaluations into emissions (TAO) distribution and validator
  dividends.
- **Scope:** Operates per subnet, using weights that validators submit after running the subnet’s
  incentive mechanism.

References:
[Yuma Consensus (learnbittensor.org)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Yuma overview (taostats.io docs)](https://docs.taostats.io/docs/consensus)

## Inputs to Yuma

- **Validator weights:** Each validator derives a vector of weights for miners after evaluating
  their performance per the subnet’s mechanism.
- **Mechanism independence:** Subnets can define their own incentive mechanisms; YC is agnostic to
  how validators score miners, focusing instead on reliably aggregating the submitted weights.

Reference:
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)

## Desired Properties

- **Robustness:** Downweights inconsistent or adversarial validators; rewards validators whose
  evaluations trend with the cohort.
- **Convergence:** Encourages validators to produce timely, consistent evaluations that predict the
  eventual consensus outcome.
- **Alignment:** Incentivizes miners to produce outputs that score well across honest evaluators,
  not just a single party.

Reference:
[Yuma Consensus (learnbittensor.org)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Outcomes of YC

- **Miner incentives:** A miner’s emissions share is proportional to their aggregate weight after
  YC.
- **Validator dividends:** Validators receive dividends based on their contribution and (in some
  archetypes) their stake, encouraging accurate and timely evaluations.
- **Subnet autonomy:** Each subnet’s consensus is computed per its submitted weights, enabling
  specialization and local optimization.

Reference: [Consensus summary (taostats docs)](https://docs.taostats.io/docs/consensus)

## Interaction with Dynamic TAO

Dynamic TAO (dTAO) provides the broader tokenomics and governance model. YC fits within that model
as the method for converting evaluations to payouts, while dTAO defines how emissions are injected
and how governance evolves.

- **dTAO governs emission policy and decentralization trajectory.**
- **YC aggregates evaluation signals to allocate those emissions.**

References:
[Understanding Subnets: Validator stake weight](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#validator-stake-weight),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Practical Implications

- **For miners:** Optimize for evaluation criteria defined by the subnet; consistent performance
  across validators improves your YC outcome.
- **For validators:** Provide honest, consistent, timely evaluations; YC rewards validators whose
  weights align with the eventual consensus trend.
- **For subnet creators:** Design mechanisms that are measurable, evaluable, and hard to game; YC
  will then aggregate validator signals effectively.

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Yuma Consensus is usually discussed for one incentive mechanism at a time, but subnets can also run
multiple mechanisms in parallel. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms) defines
multiple incentive mechanisms as independent evaluation systems, each with its own bond pool for
Yuma Consensus calculations.

For readers, Yuma Consensus still turns validator weights into miner incentives and validator
dividends, but the mechanism context matters. Consensus inputs and outcomes for one mechanism should
be read separately when a subnet uses more than one evaluation system.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)

## Relationship to Validator Weights

Yuma Consensus and validator weights address related but different parts of Bittensor subnet
incentive flow. Validator weights are the evaluation signals validators submit after judging miner
work, while Yuma Consensus aggregates those submitted signals into miner incentives and validator
dividends. The
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights)
describes the per-validator evaluation inputs, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes how those inputs
become consensus outcomes.

For readers, validator weights name the subnet-specific evaluation submissions, while Yuma Consensus
names the aggregation step that turns many validator submissions into shared incentive results.

References:
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Commit Reveal

Yuma Consensus and Commit Reveal address related but different parts of validator-weight processing.
Commit Reveal can delay when fresh validator weights become publicly visible before consensus uses
them, while Yuma Consensus combines the revealed weight signals into incentive allocations. The
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal) documentation describes
concealed weight submission and delayed reveal, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes aggregation after
validators submit weights.

For readers, Commit Reveal names a visibility and timing layer around weight submission, while Yuma
Consensus names the aggregation layer that consumes those weights for emissions.

References: [Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Tempo

Yuma Consensus and tempo are related but different parts of subnet timing vocabulary. Tempo names
the block-based cadence that governs epoch transitions inside a subnet, while Yuma Consensus runs on
the validator-weight inputs collected during that subnet's periodic work. The
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo) describes tempo as the
subnet-specific cadence for epochs, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes how submitted
weights feed consensus outcomes within subnet operation.

For readers, tempo names the subnet's periodic cadence, while Yuma Consensus names how validator
evaluations are combined into incentives across that cadence.

References: [Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Epoch

Yuma Consensus and an epoch address related but different parts of subnet consensus timing. An epoch
names the consensus period inside a subnet, while Yuma Consensus describes how validator weights
submitted during that period are aggregated into miner and validator reward outcomes. The
[Glossary: Epoch](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#epoch) describes the subnet
consensus period, and [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
describes the weight-aggregation step that turns evaluations into emissions within that period.

For readers, an epoch names the time window for subnet consensus work, while Yuma Consensus names
the mechanism that converts validator evaluations into incentive results inside that window.

References: [Glossary: Epoch](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#epoch),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
