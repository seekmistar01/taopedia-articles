# Weight Copying

Weight copying is a consensus-quality problem where a validator relies on another validator's weight
information instead of independently evaluating miners. It is framed as free-riding on validator
work because copied weights can imitate evaluation without adding a new assessment of miner
performance
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The term is about the source of a weight signal. Agreement between validators is not the issue by
itself; the concern is dependence on copied information rather than independent subnet evaluation.

That makes weight copying an input-quality problem. The copied signal can enter the same
validator-weight path as an independently produced signal, but it contributes less new information
about miner performance
([Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

## Independent Evaluation

Independent validator evaluation gives a subnet multiple assessments of miner performance. When a
validator copies another validator's weights, the copied signal can look like another assessment
without adding new information about the miners
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

That weakens the evaluation layer that downstream consensus receives. The important distinction is
evaluation independence, not whether two validators happen to produce similar rankings.

This is why weight copying belongs near validator-weight vocabulary. Validator weights are useful
because they represent miner-work evaluation; copied weights weaken that input quality
([Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

Independent evaluation is still allowed to converge. Two validators can evaluate the same miner work
and reach similar conclusions; weight copying is the failure mode where similarity comes from
dependence on another validator's information.

## Consensus Impact

Weight copying matters because validator weights are inputs to consensus. Yuma Consensus aggregates
validator rankings of miner performance into incentive and dividend outcomes, so copied weights can
make the input set less independent than it appears
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

This places weight copying in consensus vocabulary rather than only validator-behavior vocabulary.
The risk is not only that a validator avoids work; it is that copied signals can reduce the quality
of information available to consensus.

Rank, trust, and validator trust are downstream terms. Weight copying affects the quality of the
validator-weight inputs that are later filtered and aggregated into those consensus readings
([Glossary: Rank](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#rank),
[Glossary: Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust),
[Glossary: Validator Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-trust)).

This keeps the problem upstream of final outcomes. A rank or trust result is not itself a
weight-copying finding; it is a later consensus reading that depends on the submitted weight inputs.

## Delayed Visibility

Delayed visibility is one defense against copied weights. Commit Reveal separates commitment to
weights from later visibility, which reduces the value of copying recently visible weight
information ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

The useful idea is timing. If a validator cannot immediately see and reuse another validator's
weight information, copied weights become less useful as a substitute for independent scoring.

Delayed visibility does not change what validator weights are. It changes when weight information
can be observed, which matters because copied weights depend on visibility
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

Drand time-lock encryption sits next to this visibility defense. It helps keep committed weights
unreadable until the relevant reveal timing, which supports Commit Reveal's copied-weight protection
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

## Consensus-Based Weights

Consensus-based weights, also described through liquid alpha, are another related defense. They
adjust bonds around consensus alignment, while the weight-copying reference places the mechanism
beside delayed visibility as a response to copied or stale weights
([Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

Delayed visibility and consensus-based weights address different parts of the same problem. One
limits the usefulness of copying fresh weight information; the other changes how aligned or stale
signals affect validator bond outcomes.

The connection is consensus quality. Both ideas make copied or stale weights less attractive than
independent, timely miner evaluation.

## Evidence Limits

Similar weight patterns do not automatically show copying. The weight-copying problem concerns
dependence on copied or derived weights, while honest validators can still converge when they
evaluate miner performance similarly
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

That keeps interpretation precise. Weight copying is about lack of independent evaluation, not a
general label for every similar-looking validator score.

A similar rank or trust outcome does not by itself identify weight copying. The term concerns how
the validator-weight information was produced.

## Weight Signal

Weight copying stays separate from the weight signal itself. Validator weights are the signals used
for consensus, while weight copying names a failure mode in how those signals may be produced
([Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

This distinction separates a normal consensus input from a risk affecting the quality of that input.
Validator-weight language covers the signal type; weight-copying language covers dependence on
copied information.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For weight
copying, that sequence changes how readers should interpret validator-weight visibility and
consensus input examples.

In localnet, weight-copying defenses can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet validator
weight visibility does not represent production consensus behavior.

On testnet, validator weight patterns can be exercised in a shared non-production network. Testnet
consensus outcomes are separate from mainnet subnet state
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

On mainnet, weight copying concerns live production validator weights and Yuma Consensus on the
selected subnet. Observed weight visibility and consensus values depend on that subnet's current
chain state ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A weight-copying example from one environment should not
be read as representing production consensus behavior in another environment.

## Public Weights Create the Copying Risk

[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal) documentation explains that
each validator's weight vector on a subnet joins others in a
[weight matrix](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix). That matrix feeds
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus), which turns
[validator weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector) into incentive
and dividend outcomes for the subnet.

The matrix must remain public information so
[emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) across the Bittensor
platform can be transparently fair. Transparent reward accounting and open validator scores are
linked design goals, not optional extras.

That openness is also what makes weight copying possible. When recent validator scores are readable,
one validator can substitute another validator's visible evaluation for independent miner
assessment. Weight copying therefore arises from the same transparency property that lets
participants audit how rewards were assigned
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

## Stake-Weighted Prediction Can Outperform Honest Scoring

Weight-copying documentation describes a profitable copying pattern in which validators submit
weights designed to maximize alignment with Yuma Consensus. One described approach uses the
stake-weighted median of other validators' miner scores to predict the
[consensus score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score) that Yuma
Consensus will form.

Yuma Consensus rewards validators based on how closely their submitted weights align with emerging
consensus. A copier who forecasts that consensus from stake-weighted aggregation can therefore
appear better aligned than an honest validator who evaluates miners independently and disagrees with
peers on some scores.

Optimized copiers can achieve higher validator dividends per stake than honest validators in the
described pattern. Weight copying can become more profitable than evaluation work when visible
consensus is predictable from aggregated scores, not merely cheaper in operational effort
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

That profitability gap is part of why subnet owners are expected to configure defenses such as
Commit Reveal. Independent validation must remain the stronger economic path if subnets are to keep
producing quality
[digital commodities](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets).

## Concealed Weights Wait Outside Consensus

Commit Reveal adds a waiting interval between when a validator commits weights and when those
weights become public. During that interval, committed weights stay encrypted on chain and are
therefore not included in Yuma Consensus
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

After the concealment interval elapses, the weights are revealed and then enter Yuma Consensus for
the next epoch calculation, the same role they would have played if submitted without concealment.
The validator-weight path therefore has a hidden commitment phase followed by a consensus-input
phase.

Concealed weights are excluded from the aggregation step that turns validator signals into
[rank](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#rank),
[trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust), and emission outcomes until
reveal completes. Copiers looking for a current consensus input cannot treat another validator's
still-concealed commitment as live consensus material until those weights enter the Yuma calculation
window for the relevant [tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo).

## Defenses Address Visibility and Bond Alignment

Commit Reveal limits when committed weights become visible, while
[consensus-based weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights) adjust
bonds around consensus alignment. Both reduce the value of copied or stale signals, but they address
different parts of the same input-quality problem
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

## Weight Vector Provenance Is the Distinguishing Factor

Each validator submits a
[weight vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector) that scores miner
performance, and those vectors join into the
[weight matrix](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix) that
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) aggregates. Weight copying is
not a property of the vector's values but of where those values came from.

For readers, two validators can submit near-identical weight vectors while only one is copying.
Provenance, not similarity, is what separates an independently derived vector from one that reuses
another validator's published scores
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

## Visible Matrix Timing Differs From Concealed Commitments

[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal) documentation explains that
each validator's [weight vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)
joins others in a [weight matrix](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix)
that feeds [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus). The matrix must
stay public so emission allocation remains auditable, which is also what makes recent validator
scores copyable when they are visible
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

Commit Reveal adds a waiting interval between commitment and visibility. During concealment,
committed weights stay encrypted on chain and are excluded from Yuma Consensus until reveal
completes
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

For readers, the visible matrix therefore represents revealed weights in the consensus window, not
every validator's current hidden commitment. A copier looking for live consensus input cannot treat
another validator's still-concealed commitment as matrix material until those weights enter the Yuma
calculation after reveal ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

After the concealment interval elapses, revealed weights take the same consensus-input role they
would have had without concealment, but the delay reduces the value of copying the most recently
visible scores
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

That timing defense sits upstream of rank, trust, and validator-trust outcomes. Weight copying
concerns how validator-weight information was produced; delayed visibility changes when that
information can be observed, not what a weight vector is
([Glossary: Weight Copying](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-copying)).

Independent validators can still converge without copying; the matrix shows submitted signals, while
provenance distinguishes honest evaluation from dependence on another validator's published weights
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Distinction from Similar Validator Rankings

Similar validator rankings do not by themselves show weight copying. Honest validators can evaluate
the same miner work independently and still converge, while weight copying is dependence on another
validator's published scores rather than independent evaluation
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Similar rankings** — independent validators reaching similar conclusions from miner work.
- **Weight copying** — reusing another validator's visible scores without independent evaluation.

## Distinction from Validator Trust

Validator trust measures how consistently a validator's clipped weights align with consensus over
time. Weight copying reuses visible weight signals instead of independently evaluating miners, yet a
copier forecasting consensus can still score high on alignment
([Glossary: Validator Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-trust),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

- **Validator trust** — consensus-alignment measure over time.
- **Weight copying** — reusing another validator's published scores rather than independent
  evaluation.

## Distinction from Collusion

Weight copying is when a validator reuses visible weight signals instead of independently evaluating
miners. Collusion is when validators coordinate inflated weights toward chosen targets
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

- **Weight copying** — dependence on another validator's published scores.
- **Collusion** — coordinated inflation toward favored miners.

## Distinction from Self-Weighting

Self-weighting is when one validator inflates weights for entities it controls. Weight copying
instead reuses another validator's visible signals rather than favoring one's own miners directly
([Yuma Consensus: Clipping excess weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#clipping-excess-weights),
[Glossary: Weight Copying](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-copying)).

Similar-looking weight patterns therefore remain ambiguous until provenance is known. Copied
alignment, coordinated coalition, and lone self-preference are separate failure modes addressed by
different defenses in documentation
([Glossary: Trust](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#trust),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

Commit Reveal limits when weights become visible; that timing defense is separate from proving
whether a similar matrix row came from copying, collusion, or self-preference
([Glossary: Weight Matrix](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix)).

Evidence limits still apply: similar ranks or trust outcomes do not, by themselves, identify which
provenance pattern produced the weights
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

## Distinction from Commit Reveal

Commit reveal is a subnet mechanism that delays when fresh validator weights become readable. Weight
copying is the problem where a validator reuses another validator's visible weight information
instead of evaluating miners independently
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

The defense and the failure mode sit at different points on the same path. Commit reveal changes
visibility timing; weight copying names dependence on copied signals once they are visible.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Yuma Consensus aggregates revealed validator signals into subnet incentive outcomes. Weight copying
concerns whether those submitted signals came from independent evaluation
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Glossary: Weight Copying](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-copying)).

Consensus can still process copied weights as ordinary inputs. The copying label therefore belongs
to provenance and evaluation quality, not to the aggregation step itself.

## Distinction from Consensus Score

Weight copying is when a validator submits another validator's weights instead of evaluating miners
itself. Consensus score is the stake-weighted benchmark those submitted weights are measured against
([Glossary: Consensus Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#consensus-score),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Copied weights often aim to track that benchmark without independent work. The exploit names
dependence on another validator's signal; the benchmark names the alignment target.

## Distinction from Consensus-Based Weights

Consensus-based weights adjust validator bonds around consensus alignment as a copier deterrent.
Weight copying names dependence on another validator's published scores
([Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

Bond adjustment and visibility defenses address different parts of the same problem. Weight copying
is the upstream provenance failure; consensus-based weights change how aligned or stale signals
affect bond outcomes.

## Distinction from Weight Setting

Weight setting is the validator action of assigning scores to miners. Weight copying is dependence
on another validator's published scores instead of performing that evaluation
([Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

Scoring action and provenance failure answer different reader questions on the same validator-weight
path.

## Reader Boundary

Weight copying should not be read as a synonym for agreement, rank, trust, validator trust, or every
repeated-looking score. It names a consensus-quality risk where one validator's submitted weights
depend on another validator's weight information rather than on independent miner evaluation
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Glossary: Weight Copying](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-copying)).

The useful boundary is evaluation independence. Similar-looking validator scores can still come from
honest convergence; weight copying concerns dependence on copied or derived weight information
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).
