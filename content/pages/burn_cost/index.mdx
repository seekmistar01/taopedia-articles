# Burn Cost

Burn cost is the TAO amount associated with creating a new Bittensor subnet. Bittensor vocabulary
uses the term for TAO that is recycled during subnet creation, even though the amount is commonly
discussed through the familiar "burn cost" label
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

The term is narrower than a general fee. Burn cost belongs to the subnet creation path, not to every
transfer, validator action, or user interaction. It gives readers a specific name for the dynamic
TAO amount tied to bringing a subnet into existence
([Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

## Creation Amount

Burn cost is part of the subnet creation flow. The subnet creation guide presents it as the amount
of TAO required for creating a subnet, with the amount shown by tooling rather than fixed inside the
concept article
([Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost),
[BTCLI: subnets burn-cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/btcli/#btcli-subnets-burn-cost)).

That makes the term useful before a reader looks at a live value. Burn cost names the creation-side
requirement, while the network or tool output supplies the observed number for a particular
environment.

The creation-side scope also keeps burn cost separate from ordinary transfer fees. It is tied to
subnet creation, not to every TAO movement or every account action.

## Dynamic Pricing

Burn cost is dynamic rather than fixed. The subnet creation guide presents burn cost as a value that
can decrease over time and rise when additional subnets are created, so the durable concept is the
pricing mechanism rather than a quoted number
([Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

That makes burn cost a moving creation amount rather than a stable value for a reference page. A
source-stable description can explain what the amount means, why it changes, and where it belongs
without preserving a number that can become stale.

The dynamic behavior also separates burn cost from ordinary labels such as fee or balance. A burn
cost statement belongs with subnet creation and the network where the amount was observed.

## Recycling Treatment

The name includes "burn," but Bittensor documentation treats subnet creation cost as recycled TAO.
The local subnet creation guide calls out that the label remains "burn cost" even though the TAO is
technically recycled rather than destroyed
([Local subnet creation: Subnet creation cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/local-build/create-subnet#subnet-creation-cost)).

Burn cost is the familiar subnet-creation label; recycling is the accounting treatment for the TAO
involved. The word "burn" alone is not enough to describe whether the TAO is permanently removed
from all future issuance
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Recycling vocabulary is therefore the more precise accounting description. Burn cost identifies when
the TAO is required; recycling explains how the protocol treats that TAO after the subnet creation
event.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For burn cost,
that sequence changes how readers should interpret subnet-creation price examples.

In localnet, burn cost can be tested in an isolated environment. Local creation costs reflect local
chain configuration rather than production subnet registration economics.

On testnet, burn cost can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet burn readings are
separate from mainnet subnet creation state.

On mainnet, burn cost is the live recycled-TAO requirement for opening a new subnet market on the
production network
([Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A burn cost observation from one environment is not
evidence for all others.

## Subnet Registration Pays Burn Cost

[Subnet registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet) is the process that
pays burn cost to open a new incentive market and receive a unique
[netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid). Burn cost names the TAO amount;
subnet registration names the creation event that pays it.

## Neuron Register Uses a Separate Burn Path

The [Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register) describes
purchasing a UID slot on an existing subnet through a dynamic neuron registration burn. That burn
path is separate from subnet-creation burn cost, which funds an entirely new subnet rather than
entry into one that already exists.

Burn cost names network expansion; register names participant entry into an existing netuid.

## Coinbase Drives Ongoing Emissions After Creation

Burn cost sits on the input side of subnet creation: TAO is required for a new subnet and is treated
as recycled. [Coinbase](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
sits elsewhere in the supply flow, as the per-block mechanism involved in TAO emission and epoch
processing after the subnet exists.

Burn cost is tied to creation of a subnet; coinbase concerns ongoing emission mechanics after the
network is producing blocks and distributing rewards. Recycling describes how creation TAO returns
to issuance accounting; coinbase describes recurring emission processing
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Subnet Deregistration Can Follow Creation at Capacity

When the subnet cap is full, a new subnet registration attempt can trigger
[subnet deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration), removing the
eligible subnet with the lowest EMA of alpha price so the incoming subnet can take the slot. Burn
cost names the price of entry; deregistration names the market-removal step that can make room when
no free slot remains.

## Distinction from Min Burn

Burn cost in this article names the recycled TAO amount for subnet creation. Min burn is a separate
neuron-registration hyperparameter that sets the lower bound on the dynamic TAO burn charged when a
hotkey registers on an existing subnet
([Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#minburn)).

- **Burn cost (subnet creation)** — recycled TAO required to open a new subnet market.
- **Min burn** — floor for neuron registration burn on an existing subnet.

The two should not be conflated when reading registration economics on a netuid that already exists
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

## Distinction from Max Burn

Subnet-creation burn cost is separate from max burn, the neuron-registration hyperparameter that
caps the dynamic registration burn on an existing subnet
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#maxburn),
[Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

Subnet creation burn opens a new netuid; max burn bounds per-entry registration price inside a
subnet that already exists
([Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

Readers comparing creation and entry costs should keep the lifecycle stage explicit. Burn cost funds
a new subnet market; max burn bounds joining a subnet that already has a netuid
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Distinction from Subnet Stake Burn

Burn cost and subnet stake burn are two different burns that should not be conflated. Burn cost is
the TAO amount recycled when creating a new subnet market, while subnet stake burn is a voluntary
subnet buyback that adds TAO to a subnet reserve while permanently removing alpha from circulation
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Subnet Stake Burn](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/stake-burn),
[Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

Creation vocabulary names the one-time cost to open a new netuid; stake-burn vocabulary names
ongoing reserve and supply adjustment on an existing subnet.

- **Burn cost** — recycled TAO required to create a new subnet market.
- **Subnet stake burn** — voluntary alpha buyback that adds TAO to subnet reserve.

## Distinction from Crowdloans

Burn cost names the TAO amount required for subnet creation, while a crowdloan names one collective
funding path that can help contributors pool TAO toward that creation action
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

A crowdloan can be a path for meeting a creation cost, while burn cost remains the creation-side TAO
amount regardless of whether one creator or multiple contributors provide the funds.

- **Burn cost** — the dynamic TAO amount required to create a new subnet market.
- **Crowdloan** — collective funding toward a campaign-defined creation action.

## Chain Reads for netuid 1

Readers can verify live subnet registration burn bounds for the documented example netuid with
`btcli subnet hyperparameters --netuid 1 --network finney`
([Subnet Hyperparameters: View hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#view-hyperparameters)).

That read path keeps live registration-burn claims tied to a parseable netuid.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Burn cost names the recycled TAO required to create a new subnet market. Yuma Consensus is the
separate tempo process that converts validator weight submissions into emission shares on subnets
that already exist
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Burn cost** — recycled TAO required to create a new subnet market.
- **Yuma Consensus** — mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

Burn cost is a conceptual and accounting term, not timing advice, a price forecast, or a general
transfer fee. It names the dynamic TAO amount required for subnet creation, and Bittensor
documentation treats that TAO as recycled rather than simply destroyed
([Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost),
[Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

Live values belong in the tool or source that reports the relevant environment at the time of
observation.
