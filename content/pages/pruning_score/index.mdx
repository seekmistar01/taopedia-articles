# Pruning Score

A pruning score expresses how a subnet decides which participant makes way when it is full and a
newcomer wants in. It captures a simple principle of Bittensor: a place in a subnet is held by
continuing to earn, and the participant that has earned the least is the one asked to leave. On a
subnet such as netuid 1, that is the participant with the lowest pruning score
([Mining in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners)).

## A Place That Must Be Earned

A subnet has only so many places, so admitting a new participant means an existing one gives way.
The pruning score is how that choice is framed: it stands for how much each participant has been
earning, and the one earning the least is the one displaced. Holding a place is never permanent; it
depends on continuing to contribute, which keeps a subnet's limited capacity in the hands of those
actively earning rather than those who simply arrived first.

## What the Score Reflects

The pruning score reflects a neuron's emissions, so it mirrors how much the network has been
rewarding that participant rather than any outside rating. A participant earning well sits far from
being displaced, while one earning little stays close to the edge. It is a standing that follows
from performance, not a label applied independently of how a participant is doing.

## A Grace Period for Newcomers

A newly admitted participant is shielded by an
[immunity period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period) before this
pressure applies. That grace window gives a newcomer time to establish itself and begin earning
before its standing can put its place at risk. Once the grace period passes, the same expectation
applies to everyone: a place is kept by earning.

## Why It Matters

The pruning score turns a subnet's limited places into a continuously contested resource. Who holds
a place is aligned with who is currently contributing, and a participant that stops earning
gradually moves toward giving its place to someone who will. That keeps incentives pointed at
sustained contribution.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For pruning score
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret subnet slot
competition examples.

In localnet, pruning mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet pruning scores do
not represent production subnet participation.

On testnet, slot competition can be exercised in a shared non-production network. Testnet pruning
outcomes are separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, pruning score describes live production subnet slot competition. Observed rankings
depend on the selected subnet's current emission and participation context.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A pruning-score example from one environment should not be
read as representing production slot competition in another environment.

## Full Subnets Remove the Lowest-Scoring Incumbent

When a subnet is full,
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)
identifies the existing participant with the lowest pruning score as the displacement candidate for
an incoming registration. The newcomer receives the slot after that incumbent is removed, not
because the newcomer itself held the lowest score
([Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

Pruning score therefore belongs on the incumbent side of registration pressure.

## Immunity Exceptions Can Affect Eligibility

[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration) lists
immunity-period exceptions among the eligibility rules that can affect which participant is removed
when a subnet is full. Immunity does not change what pruning score measures; it changes who is
eligible for removal at a given moment while the score still reflects emission standing.

## UID Trimming Ranks by Emission Instead

[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming) describes a separate
owner-initiated bulk reduction ranked by emission scores. Pruning score names the standing used when
a full subnet must displace one participant to admit a new registration.

Trimming is a manual resize with compression rules; pruning score is the performance signal cited in
mining documentation for single-slot replacement on a full subnet.

## Emissions From Yuma Feed the Score

Yuma Consensus aggregates validator weight signals within a subnet into miner
[incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives) and validator dividends.
Pruning score reflects emission standing that follows from that consensus path rather than from a
separate manual quality label
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Pruning score names displacement standing; Yuma Consensus names how validator evaluations become the
emission outcomes that standing tracks.

## Distinction from Activity Cutoff

Pruning score and activity cutoff are different subnet signals. Pruning score reflects emission
standing used when a full subnet must displace one participant to admit a new registration, while
activity cutoff is the early-epoch block window within which a validator must submit weights to
count in the current Yuma Consensus pass
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#activitycutoff)).

- **Pruning score** — earnings-based standing for single-slot displacement on a full subnet.
- **Activity cutoff** — epoch block window for weight submission to count.

Activity cutoff therefore concerns validator participation timing within an epoch, not the
displacement ranking described by pruning score
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Distinction from Active UID

An active UID records whether a neuron currently meets activity conditions on the metagraph. Pruning
score instead names earned emission standing when slot displacement is driven by registration
pressure on a full subnet
([Glossary: Active UID](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#active-uid),
[Mining in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners)).

Active UID answers whether a slot currently satisfies activity rules. Pruning score answers which
incumbent looks weakest when documented deregistration ranking is needed to free one slot
([Subnet Metagraph](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/metagraph),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

## Reader Boundary

This page describes the concept at a high level. Which participant actually stands lowest on a given
subnet at a given time is live state that shifts as earnings change, and is best read from current
chain data at the time of observation. The durable point is the principle: a subnet place is earned,
the least-earning participant makes way, and a grace period protects newcomers first
([Mining in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners)).

## Relationship to Validator Deregistration

Pruning score and validator deregistration act on the same removal as selector and action. The
pruning score is the per-neuron value that ranks which UID is most expendable when a full subnet
must displace one, while validator deregistration is the validator-specific removal that frees the
slot
([Validating: Validator deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators#validator-deregistration)).

The two also differ in scope. Pruning score is role-neutral: the neuron with the lowest score, miner
or validator, risks replacement when all UID slots are occupied, so validator deregistration is only
the validator-side case of a ranking that applies to any neuron
([Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

## Relationship to Subnet Validator

Pruning score and the subnet validator meet at deregistration eligibility. Pruning score is the
per-neuron value that ranks which UID is removed first when the subnet is full, while a subnet
validator is a neuron that evaluates miners and submits weights in a subnet
([Glossary: Subnet Validator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-validator)).

A subnet validator is not exempt from that ranking. A low-scoring validator outside its immunity
window can be displaced like any other low scorer, so pruning score applies to validators as well as
miners
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

## Relationship to Deregistration

Pruning score reflects how much a subnet participant has been earning when slots are contested.
Deregistration is the removal of a low-performing entrant when capacity and replacement rules apply
([Mining in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

Readers comparing standing to removal need both terms on the same netuid. Pruning score names the
earnings-based standing that can put a participant closest to displacement, while deregistration
names the replacement event when a slot must be freed
([Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot),
[Glossary: Incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives)).

Use pruning score when discussing earned standing among participants; use deregistration when
covering the removal step rather than the standing metric itself
([Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Standing metric and removal event answer different reader questions when subnet capacity is full
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).
