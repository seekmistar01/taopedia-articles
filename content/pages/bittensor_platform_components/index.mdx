# Bittensor Platform Components

Bittensor has several major platform components. This page maps those pieces before more detailed
articles discuss them:
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction),
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks).

The components answer different questions. Subnets define useful work. The blockchain records state.
TAO and alpha tokens carry incentives. Roles perform and evaluate work, while tools provide the
interface layer.

## Subnets

Subnets are Bittensor's specialized work markets. Each subnet can define a digital commodity,
connect miners and validators around that work, and use an incentive mechanism to score useful
output: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism).

This makes subnets the application layer of the platform. They are where Bittensor's broad incentive
structure becomes a specific work-and-evaluation problem.

## Blockchain and Tokens

The blockchain is the shared record for balances, stake, subnet state, and incentive activity. TAO
is the network token, while subnet-specific alpha tokens appear in subnet emission contexts:
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions).

Together, the chain and tokens give the platform its accounting layer. They do not define the
subnet's useful work by themselves; they make that work measurable and rewardable inside the
network.

## Role Families

Bittensor role families map to the work cycle. Miners produce subnet outputs, validators evaluate
those outputs, subnet creators shape incentive rules, and stakers support validation:
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction),
[Mining and Validating](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets).

Keeping the roles distinct prevents a platform overview from collapsing into one generic actor
model. Each role connects to a different part of production, evaluation, design, or support.

## Tools and Interfaces

Tools and interfaces sit around the protocol pieces. They make it possible to interact with subnets,
chain state, and role-specific software without changing what those platform components mean:
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction).

This separates interface vocabulary from incentive vocabulary. A tool can expose a workflow, but the
subnet, chain, token, and role concepts explain why that workflow matters.

## Network Environments

Bittensor concepts depend on environment. Mainnet, testnet, and localnet are different contexts for
interpreting examples, experiments, and subnet behavior:
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development).

That distinction keeps a platform map from mixing production, shared testing, and local development
meanings. The same component name can appear in each environment, but evidence from one environment
should not be treated as evidence from another.

## How the Pieces Fit Together

A subnet defines work. Miners produce it, validators evaluate it, the blockchain records relevant
state, and TAO plus alpha tokens provide incentive accounting. Tools provide access to those
components, and network labels tell readers which environment is being discussed:
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions).

That map is intentionally broad. More detailed articles can focus on Yuma Consensus, staking,
emissions, or subnet design without repeating the entire platform structure.
