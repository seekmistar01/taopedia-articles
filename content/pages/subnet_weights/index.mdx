# Subnet Weights

Subnet weights are Bittensor's way of expressing how much relative importance each subnet has when
emissions are allocated across subnets. The
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights) entry
defines the term through net TAO flows, while
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) explains how that flow signal is used
inside the broader emission process.

## Flow-based meaning

Under the flow-based emission model, subnet weights are tied to net TAO movement into and out of a
subnet through staking and unstaking activity. This makes the term different from a simple market
price label: it describes an allocation signal used to distribute emissions, not just the spot value
of a subnet's alpha token.

The official emission docs describe the flow-based model as replacing the previous price-based
approach for distributing TAO emissions across subnets:
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets).

## Smoothing context

Subnet weights should be read with their smoothing context. Bittensor does not treat every short
staking or unstaking movement as an immediate durable signal. The emission docs describe an
exponential moving average of net TAO flows, and the
[Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema#subnet-flow-emission-smoothing)
docs explain why this slow-moving signal reduces the effect of brief swings.

This means a subnet weight is best interpreted as a protocol-level allocation measure built from
sustained flow behavior, not as a live popularity score.

## Relationship to emissions

Subnet weights sit in the first stage of the emission process: allocation across subnets. After
liquidity is injected into subnet pools, Bittensor's participant-level distribution still depends on
subnet consensus and the rules that determine rewards within each subnet. The weight therefore
answers a narrow question: how much of the cross-subnet emission share is associated with a subnet.

The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#injection-and-distribution-two-stages-of-the-emissions-process)
docs separate this allocation stage from the later distribution stage.

## Reader boundary

Subnet weights are not validator weights, weight vectors, or the weight matrix used inside subnet
consensus. Those terms concern how validators evaluate work within a subnet. Subnet weights concern
how Bittensor compares subnets when allocating emissions across the network.

## Staking Flow Signal

Subnet weights reflect net TAO movement from staking and unstaking activity across subnets. The
official [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes how
those flows feed the cross-subnet allocation stage, and the
[Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema) docs explain how short-term
swings are smoothed before they affect allocation.

For readers, inflow and outflow measure participation in subnet liquidity, not a subnet's task
quality or a validator's rank inside consensus.

## Relationship to Netuid

Subnet weights and a netuid are related but different parts of Bittensor tokenomics vocabulary. A
netuid identifies a subnet on the network, while subnet weights describe that subnet's relative
share of cross-subnet emission allocation under the flow-based model. The
[Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid) places netuid at the
subnet level, and the
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)
describes relative emission importance assigned through net TAO flows.

For readers, netuid answers which subnet is being discussed, while subnet weights answer how much
cross-subnet emission share that identified subnet receives under flow-based allocation.

References: [Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid),
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)

## Relationship to Exponential Moving Averages

Subnet weights and exponential moving averages address related but different parts of emission
signal processing. Subnet weights are the allocation measure built from net TAO flow behavior, while
exponential moving averages smooth that flow signal so brief staking swings do not dominate
cross-subnet allocation. The
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)
describes the relative emission importance signal, and the
[Glossary: Exponential Moving Average (EMA)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#exponential-moving-average-ema)
describes the smoothing method applied to noisy measurements over time.

For readers, subnet weights name the allocation outcome, while exponential moving averages name the
smoothing technique that helps that outcome reflect sustained flow rather than brief noise.

References:
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights),
[Glossary: Exponential Moving Average (EMA)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#exponential-moving-average-ema)

## Relationship to Validator Weights

Subnet weights and validator weights address related but different scopes in Bittensor weight
vocabulary. Subnet weights compare subnets when allocating emissions across the network, while
validator weights are evaluation signals validators submit for miners inside one subnet's consensus
process. The
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)
describes cross-subnet allocation importance, and the
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights)
describes per-validator evaluation inputs used by Yuma Consensus.

For readers, subnet weights belong to cross-subnet emission allocation, while validator weights
belong to within-subnet evaluation and consensus. The two terms should not be read as
interchangeable weight labels.

References:
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights),
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights)

## Relationship to Alpha Tokens

Subnet weights and alpha tokens are related but different parts of subnet tokenomics vocabulary.
Subnet weights describe cross-subnet emission allocation from net TAO flow, while alpha tokens name
the subnet-specific token used in each subnet's pool and staking context. The
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)
defines allocation through net TAO flows, and the
[Glossary: Alpha Tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-tokens) describes
subnet-specific tokens tied to individual subnets.

For readers, subnet weights name how much cross-subnet emission share a subnet receives, while alpha
tokens name the subnet-level asset context that staking flows move through inside that subnet.

References:
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights),
[Glossary: Alpha Tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-tokens)

## Relationship to Coinbase

Subnet weights and coinbase are related but different stages of Bittensor's emission process. Subnet
weights describe each subnet's relative cross-subnet emission allocation, derived from net TAO flows
smoothed by an exponential moving average. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission,
accumulates pending emissions, and triggers Yuma Consensus rounds at epoch boundaries, while the
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)
describes the cross-subnet allocation signal.

The two operate at different layers of emission. Subnet weights influence how much emission each
subnet receives relative to others, while coinbase is the per-block mechanism that produces
emissions and, within each subnet, distributes them to participants through Yuma Consensus rounds.
Subnet weights are about the cross-subnet split; coinbase is about generating and distributing the
emissions once that split applies. One describes a subnet's share of the overall flow, while the
other is the recurring process that produces and routes that flow.
