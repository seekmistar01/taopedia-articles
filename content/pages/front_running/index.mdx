# Front-Running

Front-running is observing a pending transaction and submitting a similar transaction with higher
priority so that it executes first. In Bittensor it appears around a subnet's automated market maker
pool: a swap that is waiting to execute is visible before it lands, and another trader can order
their own swap ahead of it to profit from the price move the pending swap will cause
([Front-Running](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

## Why It Is Possible

A transaction does not execute the instant it is submitted. It first sits in the mempool, the
temporary holding area where pending and unconfirmed transactions wait before being included in a
block, and there its contents can be observed. Because a subnet prices alpha through a pool whose
price moves with every swap, seeing a large pending swap signals which way the price is about to
move and by roughly how much. That visibility is the raw material front-running depends on
([Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## The Sandwich Pattern

The best-known front-running pattern is the sandwich attack: placing transactions before and after a
target transaction to profit from the price movement that target causes. An attacker who sees a
pending buy places their own buy first, lets the victim's order push the price up, then sells into
that higher price. The victim's trade is wrapped, or sandwiched, between the attacker's two trades,
and the extra slippage the victim pays is the attacker's gain. Front-running is the front half of
that pattern
([Sandwich Attack](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## What Defends Against It

Two mechanisms reduce the payoff. Price protection lets a trader set a tolerance on the price they
will accept, so a swap that would execute worse than that limit fails or only partially fills rather
than completing at a front-runner's inflated price, which caps how much can be extracted. The MEV
shield attacks the information itself: transaction data is encrypted with a public key from the
blockchain validator and decrypted only just before the transaction is finalized, so a pending
swap's details stay hidden until it is already on-chain and there is nothing readable to trade ahead
of ([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For front-running
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret mempool visibility
and price-protection examples.

In localnet, front-running mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet pending
transaction visibility does not represent production ordering behavior on netuid 1 mainnet pools.

On testnet, MEV-related flows can be exercised in a shared non-production network. Testnet pool and
mempool conditions are separate from mainnet state even when the same netuid number appears in both
environments.

On mainnet, front-running concerns live production subnet pools and the protections traders use on
the public network ([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A front-running example from one environment should not be
read as representing production transaction behavior in another environment.

## Pending Staking Also Moves Pool Prices

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) describes staking and
unstaking as conversions that pass through subnet pool reserves, not only standalone swaps. A
pending stake or unstake that sits in the
[mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool) can signal an upcoming shift
in the TAO-to-[alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) ratio
before it lands.

Front-running therefore is not limited to swap extrinsics. Any pending conversion large enough to
move the pool can reveal direction and size to someone watching the mempool, which is the visibility
this page describes as the opening for ordering ahead
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Back-Running Takes the Slot After the Target

[Front-Running](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)
and [Back-Running](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)
both depend on seeing a pending conversion, but they sit on opposite sides of it. Front-running
executes before the target to profit from the move it is about to cause; back-running executes
immediately after to profit from the move it just caused.

A sandwich attack combines both legs around one victim trade. Read alone, front-running names only
the first leg that worsens the target's fill price, while back-running names the follow-up trade
that captures the dislocation left behind
([Sandwich Attack](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

## Slippage Measures the Pool Move Being Traded On

[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#slippage) names the difference between
expected and received amounts when a conversion moves through subnet pool reserves. Front-running
targets that movement: the attacker orders ahead because the pending transaction will change the
ratio and therefore the price the next trader receives.

Price protection and the MEV shield reduce how much can be extracted, but slippage vocabulary
describes the underlying pool shift that makes ordering ahead profitable in the first place
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Weight Copying Is an Analogous Timing Problem, Not Trading Front-Running

[Weight Copying](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor) describes a
validator reading the publicly available weight matrix and copying, or deriving from, other
validators' work instead of independently evaluating miners. It is a separate, non-trading problem,
not a form of front-running itself.

The two share a timing shape rather than a mechanism: both depend on someone else's information
becoming visible before a result is finalized.
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal) addresses weight copying the
way encryption addresses front-running, by keeping submitted weights concealed until a designated
reveal point so there is nothing public to copy before consensus runs.

## Visibility Creates the Attack Surface

Front-running in Bittensor is easiest to understand as a timing exploit around visible pending
activity. Documentation describes attackers acting on transactions they can see before they
finalize, which is why public mempool visibility and pool price movement matter more than the label
alone ([Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

For readers, this is why protection features target visibility and tolerance rather than banning
trades outright. Price protection and MEV Shield narrow the window in which a pending swap can be
exploited, rather than changing how subnets evaluate miners
([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

That framing also keeps MEV vocabulary grouped. Front-running, back-running, and sandwich attacks
are different shapes of the same extractable-value problem around observable order flow, which is
why documentation groups them under maximal extractable value
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

## Distinction from MEV

Front-running names the specific tactic of observing a pending transaction and submitting a
higher-priority one so it executes first. MEV (maximal extractable value) names the broader
value-extraction category built on transaction ordering and pre-block visibility
([Glossary: Front-Running](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

Front-running is one specific shape within MEV. MEV vocabulary groups three ordering shapes built on
a visible pending trade — front-running, sandwich attacks, and back-running — and front-running is
the case that submits ahead of the target
([Glossary: Sandwich Attack](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[Glossary: Back-Running](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **Front-running** — ordering ahead of a visible pending trade.
- **MEV** — the broader category covering front-running, sandwich attacks, and back-running.

## Distinction from Price Protection

Front-running and price protection sit on opposite sides of the same swap: one is the extraction
tactic, the other the trader's defense. Front-running observes a pending pool conversion and orders
ahead to profit from the price move it will cause, while price protection is the slippage tolerance
the trader sets so a swap fails or only partially fills instead of completing at a front-runner's
inflated price, so price protection does not detect the attacker — it caps how much a successful
front-run can extract ([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/)).

- **Front-running** — ordering ahead of a visible pending swap to profit from its price move.
- **Price protection** — the trader-set tolerance that bounds the loss such ordering can impose.

## Distinction from MEV Shield

Front-running and the MEV shield are the attack and a defense against it. Front-running observes a
pending swap and orders ahead of it, while the MEV shield encrypts pending transaction data so it
stays unreadable until the transaction is finalized, removing the visibility front-running depends
on
([Front-Running](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/)).

- **Front-running** — ordering ahead of a visible pending swap.
- **MEV shield** — encryption that hides pending data so there is nothing to order ahead of.

## Distinction from Mempool

Front-running and the mempool are a tactic and the place that enables it. The mempool is the holding
area where pending transactions wait before inclusion, while front-running is the tactic of reading
a pending swap there and submitting a higher-priority trade ahead of it
([Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool),
[Front-Running](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **Mempool** — the holding area where pending transactions are visible.
- **Front-running** — ordering ahead of a swap observed in that holding area.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level and is not trading advice or a claim that any specific
trade was front-run. Whether front-running is profitable in a given moment depends on live pool
depth, pending order flow, and the protections in force, all of which change continuously. The
durable point is the relationship: visible pending transactions plus price-moving pools create the
opening, and price protection and the MEV shield are what narrow it
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).
