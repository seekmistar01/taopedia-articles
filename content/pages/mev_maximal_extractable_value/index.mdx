# MEV (Maximal Extractable Value)

MEV, short for Maximal Extractable Value, is the value that can be extracted by controlling or
observing the order of transactions before they are included in a block
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

In Bittensor, MEV risk arises when pending transactions in the mempool expose enough information for
another party to profit by acting ahead of or around them. This is most relevant for subnet pool
operations where transaction contents can affect pool prices.

## Mempool Visibility

MEV opportunities begin with mempool visibility. When a transaction is pending but not yet included
in a block, its contents may be readable. A party observing those contents can act on that
information before the original transaction settles
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

This is the root of MEV risk in Bittensor. The value is not created by the attacking party — it is
transferred from the original transaction's sender to whoever acts on the ordering opportunity.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For MEV, that
sequence changes how readers should interpret mempool visibility and transaction-ordering examples.

In localnet, MEV-related mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet pending
transaction visibility does not represent production ordering behavior.

On testnet, mempool and MEV-shield flows can be exercised in a shared non-production network.
Testnet transaction ordering is separate from mainnet state.

On mainnet, MEV concerns live production subnet pools and pending-transaction ordering on the public
network ([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An MEV example from one environment should not be read as
representing production transaction behavior in another environment.

## Three Named Ordering Shapes

The
[MEV glossary entry](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)
groups three common ordering shapes under maximal extractable value. Front-running observes a
pending transaction and submits a similar one with higher priority to execute first. Sandwich
attacks place transactions before and after a target to profit from the price movement that target
causes. Back-running submits immediately after a target transaction to capitalize on its effects.

Those labels describe different positions around one visible pending trade. MEV vocabulary groups
them because each shape extracts value from ordering or observation before block inclusion, not
because every follow-up trade is adversarial.

## Staking and Unstaking Can Be Affected

The same glossary entry states that MEV attacks can affect
[staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#staking) and
[unstaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#unstaking) operations when knowledge
of pending transactions lets another party manipulate token prices. Those conversions pass through
subnet pool reserves, so a pending stake or unstake can signal an upcoming pool move before it lands
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

MEV therefore belongs to pool-moving transaction vocabulary, not only to standalone swap examples.
The risk starts when pending details are readable in the
[mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool), not when a final block record
is already settled.

## Slippage and MEV Answer Different Questions

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) discusses how pool reserves
and trade size affect execution prices during conversions. MEV vocabulary, by contrast, names value
that can be extracted when another party observes pending transaction details and acts on ordering
before inclusion
([MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

For readers, ordinary slippage can occur even without an adversarial observer, while MEV patterns
depend on foreknowledge of pending intent in the
[mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool). Price protection adds a third
layer by letting callers set acceptable execution bounds during pool operations
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Distinction from MEV Shield

MEV and MEV Shield answer different questions about pending transactions. MEV names the value that
can be extracted when transaction ordering or pending contents can be observed or influenced before
block inclusion, while MEV Shield names encrypted mempool protection that hides sensitive pending
transaction contents until inclusion
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

- **MEV** — the risk category tied to ordering and pending visibility.
- **MEV Shield** — pending encryption for eligible coldkey-signed submissions.

Put plainly, MEV names the problem while MEV Shield names one mechanism designed to reduce it, which
is why understanding MEV explains why MEV Shield exists
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

MEV vocabulary describes why ordering-sensitive exposure matters in pool and mempool context. MEV
Shield vocabulary describes one pre-inclusion defense that reduces what observers can read while a
transaction waits; it does not remove every execution or slippage concern after reveal
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool)).

The
[Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)
ties [MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield) to these attacks by
describing encryption that keeps transaction contents hidden until block inclusion. The shield page
explains that sensitive pending details stay encrypted while waiting and are revealed only when the
chain can process them.

That timing addresses mempool visibility rather than every execution outcome. Runtime rules still
decide whether a revealed action succeeds once included; MEV Shield changes what observers can read
during the pending window that ordering attacks depend on
([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

## Distinction from Sandwich Attack

MEV is the umbrella term for value extracted by controlling transaction ordering and inclusion. A
sandwich attack is one concrete way to capture it: an attacker places one transaction just before a
victim's swap and another just after, profiting from the price move the victim's trade causes
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

Category vocabulary and strategy vocabulary answer different questions on the same ordering path.
MEV names the general extractable value; a sandwich attack names the before-and-after bracketing
pattern around a visible pending trade.

- **MEV** — the general value extractable by ordering or inserting transactions.
- **Sandwich attack** — a specific MEV strategy that brackets a victim's swap.

## Distinction from Block

MEV and a block name different parts of the same ordering timeline. MEV is the value that can be
extracted by controlling or observing the order of transactions before they are included in a block,
while a block is a unit of data in the Bittensor blockchain that contains a collection of
transactions and a unique identifier, the block hash
([Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block)).

- **MEV** — value extractable from transaction ordering during the pending window before inclusion.
- **Block** — the data unit that bundles transactions and fixes their order once they are included.

## Distinction from Emission

MEV and emission name two different sources of value movement. MEV is value transferred from one
transaction's sender to whoever acts on a transaction-ordering opportunity, redistributed rather
than created, while emission is value injected by the protocol itself: each block injects TAO into
subnets, distributed each tempo to participants such as miners, validators, and stakers
([Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)).

- **MEV** — value redistributed between users through transaction ordering, not newly created.
- **Emission** — value newly injected by the protocol each block and distributed each tempo.

## Distinction from Pool

MEV risk is most discussed in the context of subnet pool operations such as staking and unstaking,
where transaction ordering can affect pool prices. A transaction that moves a pool price creates an
opportunity for someone observing the pending transaction to act first
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Price protection is a related tool that allows callers to set bounds on acceptable execution rates
for pool operations ([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

MEV names value extracted by reordering, inserting, or censoring pending transactions. That
mempool-ordering risk is separate from the tempo pass that converts validator weight submissions
into emission shares on a subnet
([MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Pool trading and pending extrinsics can create ordering profit opportunities. Yuma still credits
miner incentives and validator dividends from aggregated evaluation weights after inclusion
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

MEV Shield addresses pre-inclusion visibility. Yuma addresses post-inclusion weight aggregation on
the selected netuid ([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

- **MEV** — profit from manipulating transaction order or inclusion.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

MEV is concept vocabulary for value extraction through transaction ordering. It should not be read
as a consensus attack, a validator scoring issue, or a description of normal pool slippage
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

The MEV concept applies most precisely to contexts where transaction order is visible before
finalization. It is less relevant for operations that do not involve pool pricing or mempool
exposure.
