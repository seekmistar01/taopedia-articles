# Bittensor Platform Components

Bittensor is easier to read when its main pieces are separated. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) describes the
platform as a pool of subnets, a blockchain and TAO incentive layer, and open-source tools for
interacting with the system. Those pieces work together, but they answer different questions: what
work is being produced, where the record is kept, how incentives flow, and which environment a
reader is looking at.

## Subnets

Subnets are Bittensor's specialized markets. Each subnet defines a digital commodity related to
artificial intelligence, a miner community that produces that commodity, and a validator community
that measures the work against subnet-specific standards.
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) frames a
subnet as an incentive-based competition marketplace, while
[[subnet_creation_mechanisms|Subnet Creation and Incentive Mechanisms]] explains how subnet creators
shape the work and evaluation rules.

## Blockchain and TAO

The Bittensor blockchain is the system of record for balances, transactions, staking context, and
subnet-related activity. TAO is the network token used in that incentive context.
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) describes TAO as the
incentive for participation in subnet activity, and
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) explains emissions as the distribution
of newly created TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens to the role families that contribute through
mining, validation, staking, and subnet creation.

## Role Families

Bittensor documentation groups participation into several role families. Miners produce the work a
subnet asks for. Validators evaluate miner work and submit weights. Subnet creators define the
incentive mechanisms that describe what miners should produce and how validators should measure it.
Stakers support validators with TAO. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) lists these
personas, and [[mining_and_validating|Mining and Validating]] covers the miner and validator split
in more detail.

## Tools and Interfaces

The Bittensor SDK and related tools are the interface layer around those roles. They support
interactions between miners and validators inside subnets and provide blockchain access where
needed. At the article level, the important distinction is that tools are not a separate incentive
market: they are the interface layer around the platform components described above. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) places tools beside
subnets and the blockchain as a supporting component.

## Network Environments

Bittensor concepts also depend on network context.
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) separates mainnet,
testnet, and localnet as different environments. Mainnet is the production context, testnet is a
shared testing context, and localnet is a controlled local context for development and experiments.
This distinction keeps protocol concepts, examples, and subnet articles from mixing production,
testing, and local-development meanings.

## How The Pieces Fit Together

A subnet defines the work and evaluation game. Miners produce outputs, validators evaluate them, and
validator weights feed the reward process. The blockchain records the relevant state, while TAO and
subnet-specific alpha tokens provide incentive context. Network labels explain where activity is
being interpreted, and tools provide the interface layer.
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) and
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) provide this
platform frame, which sits behind articles on [[dynamic_tao|Dynamic TAO]],
[[staking_and_delegation|Staking and Delegation]], and [[yuma_consensus|Yuma Consensus]].

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Bittensor platform components include subnets where miners produce work and validators evaluate it.
The [Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that
each subnet has one or more incentive mechanisms.

For readers, the platform map still separates subnets, blockchain, TAO, and tools. A single subnet
market may run more than one incentive mechanism alongside those other components.

## Relationship to Coinbase

Bittensor platform components and coinbase sit at different levels: one is the high-level map of the
platform's pieces, the other is a specific mechanism inside the blockchain-and-TAO layer. The
platform components are subnets, the blockchain and TAO incentive layer, role families, tools, and
network environments. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission,
accumulates pending emissions, and triggers Yuma Consensus rounds at epoch boundaries, while the
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) describes the
platform pieces.

Coinbase belongs to the blockchain-and-TAO incentive layer among those components — it is the
per-block process that produces and advances the TAO emissions the platform's incentive context
depends on. The platform-components view names the major pieces and how they fit together; coinbase
names one engine operating inside the incentive layer that connects validator evaluation to emission
outcomes. One is the overall map, the other is a mechanism located within it.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Bittensor
platform components, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about how subnets,
incentive layers, and the blockchain infrastructure interact.

In localnet, platform components can be exercised in an isolated environment. Results reflect local
configuration and do not represent the interaction patterns that emerge across participants on a
production network.

On testnet, platform component interaction can be observed in a shared, non-production network. This
separates the result from a private local environment while keeping it outside mainnet subnet and
incentive activity.

On mainnet, platform components operate together in the live Bittensor network. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) describes how
subnets, validators, miners, stakers, and the blockchain layer interact in the production context.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A platform component example from one environment should
not be read as identical evidence about component interaction in another environment.

For readers, this keeps platform component examples from being treated as carrying more weight than
their environment supports. Localnet, testnet, and mainnet contexts can all describe component
interaction, but they carry different interpretive weight.
