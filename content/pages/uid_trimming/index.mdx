# UID Trimming

UID trimming is a subnet-owner operation that reduces the number of registered neuron UIDs on a
subnet down to a chosen maximum and compacts the remaining UIDs into consecutive indices. The
[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming) guide presents it as a way for
a subnet owner to manage the active participant base while keeping clean, sequential UID numbering.

## How Trimming Selects UIDs

Trimming removes the weakest participants first. The process starts from the lowest emitters and
works upward, ranking UIDs by emission scores and removing low performers until the remaining count
is at or below the chosen maximum. It is driven by emission rather than by a separate pruning score
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

## Compression of Remaining UIDs

After the low performers are removed, the survivors are compressed to the left so their indices stay
consecutive. If UIDs 5, 7, and 9 survive, they become 0, 1, and 2. This keeps the subnet's UID
numbering contiguous rather than leaving gaps where trimmed slots used to be
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

## Protected UIDs

Not every UID can be trimmed. Temporally immune UIDs, those still within the
[immunity period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period) after a recent
registration, and owner-owned UIDs are protected from trimming. The number of immune UIDs must not
exceed 80% of the maximum UID count
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

## Owner Control and Limits

Trimming is a [sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo) operation that only
the subnet owner, the wallet that created the subnet, can perform; it is triggered manually rather
than automatically. The guide lists a rate limit of 216,000 blocks, about 30 days, between trims,
and states that the minimum count an owner can trim a subnet down to is currently 64. These are
documented protocol limits, and the values in force should be read for the subnet in question.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For UID trimming
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret owner trim
examples, immune-UID limits, and compression outcomes.

In localnet, UID trimming can be tested in an isolated environment. Local trim results reflect local
chain configuration and locally configured UID limits rather than production subnet participant
state.

On testnet, trimming behavior can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet trim
outcomes and immune-UID counts are separate from mainnet subnet metagraph state
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

On mainnet, UID trimming is a live owner sudo action on the production Bittensor network that can
remove low emitters and compress surviving UIDs into consecutive indices.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A trimming example from one environment should not be read
as representing production UID layout or trim eligibility on another network.

## Trimming Stops at the Owner's Target Count

The owner chooses a maximum UID count, and trimming removes the lowest emitters until the subnet
reaches that ceiling or runs out of eligible removals. The operation is a bulk resize rather than a
single-slot replacement, which is why it can remove many low performers in one owner action instead
of waiting for registrations one at a time
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming),
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)).

Emission ranking decides who goes first, but the owner sets how small the active participant set
should become once trimming finishes.

## Too Many Immune UIDs Can Block Trimming

Temporarily immune registrations and owner-owned UIDs are protected from trimming. The trimming docs
also cap immune UIDs at 80% of the subnet's maximum UID count. When too many slots are protected,
the owner cannot remove enough participants to reach the chosen target in that pass.

The cap keeps bulk trimming from colliding with the grace period for newcomers. Immunity gives
recent registrations time to earn before they can be selected, while the 80% limit prevents that
protection from filling so much of the subnet that trimming cannot proceed.

## Trimmed Slots Lose Their On-Chain Neuron Data

When a UID is trimmed, its neuron record is permanently deleted from chain state rather than left as
an empty index. That deletion is stronger than ordinary deregistration language suggests on its own:
the trimmed participant does not simply yield a slot while leaving historical data in place.

Compression then renumbers the survivors into consecutive indices. Trimming therefore both removes
low emitters and clears their on-chain records before the remaining UIDs are packed into a
contiguous set ([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

## Deregistration Replaces One Slot at Registration Time

Trimming is distinct from
[deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration). Deregistration
replaces the single lowest performer when a new registration arrives on a full subnet, while
trimming is an owner-initiated bulk reduction of the UID set down to a target size. A trimmed UID's
data is permanently deleted from the blockchain rather than left as an empty index.

## Emission Ranking Connects Trimming to Yuma Outcomes

Trimming selects UIDs by emission scores, which reflect how Yuma Consensus and subnet emission
processing distribute rewards among participants after validator weights are aggregated
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)).

UID trimming names the owner resize operation; Yuma Consensus names how validator evaluations become
the emission outcomes that trimming uses as its ranking input.

## Trimmed Participants Must Re-Register

If a UID was trimmed, the participant must
[register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register) again to participate and
receives a new UID number. The trimmed neuron starts fresh with default scores rather than keeping
the old slot ([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

That recovery path differs from compression for survivors. Remaining UIDs are renumbered into
consecutive indices on a subnet such as netuid 1, while a trimmed participant leaves the subnet
snapshot until a new registration succeeds.

## Trimming Deletes Listed On-Chain Neuron Data

When a UID is trimmed, the trimming guide lists deleted material including UID-to-hotkey mapping,
[weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights) set to other
neurons, [bonds](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-miner-bonds) from
validators, [axon](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#axon) information, neuron
certificates, and [last update](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#last-update)
timestamps. The deletion is permanent rather than leaving an empty index.

Survivors keep associated state that the trim operation migrates during compression. Trimmed slots
therefore remove both the UID and the historical neuron record tied to it.

## Trim Operations Incur Transaction Fees

Trim operations incur [transaction fees](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) for the
underlying blockchain transactions they trigger. The owner-initiated resize therefore carries a
documented chain cost separate from registration burn economics.

Fee vocabulary stays separate from emission ranking. Trimming selects low emitters first, but
executing the owner sudo action still pays the fees the chain charges for the extrinsic.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level. It does not report which UIDs exist on any particular
subnet, which would be trimmed, or whether an owner has trimmed recently. Those are live chain state
and should be checked for the relevant netuid at the time of observation. The numbers here, such as
the 64 minimum and the roughly 30-day rate limit, are documented protocol values
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).
