# Miner Deregistration

Miner deregistration is the miner-specific case of deregistration in a Bittensor subnet.
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)
describes it as the removal of a low-performing miner when a subnet is full and a new miner is
registered.

## Miner-Specific Scope

The broader [Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
glossary entry applies to subnet miners or subnet validators. Miner deregistration narrows that
concept to miners: the participant being removed is a miner occupying a subnet UID slot.

This makes the term role-specific, not a separate scoring system. It describes which type of subnet
participant is removed, while the broader deregistration concept describes the general removal
process for low-performing subnet participants.

## UID Slot Context

The mining documentation places miner deregistration in the context of limited subnet capacity. When
a subnet is full, a new miner registration can replace an eligible miner with lower performance. For
readers, the important point is that miner deregistration is tied to subnet participation, capacity,
and miner performance; it is not a live ranking or a manual recommendation
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For miner
deregistration on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret
examples of miner removal.

In localnet, miner deregistration can show that miner-slot replacement logic is represented inside
an isolated subnet environment. That is useful for checking how a miner UID can be vacated, but it
does not show that a live public subnet miner has been removed.

On testnet, miner deregistration behavior can be observed in a shared non-production network.
Testnet removal outcomes are separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, miner deregistration belongs to live subnet state. Production readings should keep the
selected subnet, miner UID slot, block timing, and eligibility context attached
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A miner deregistration example in one environment should
not be read as identical evidence about miner removal in another environment.

## A New Registration Can Displace the Lowest Pruning Score

When subnet miner UID capacity is exhausted and a new miner still registers, the mining
documentation describes removal of the lowest-performing eligible miner so the incoming registration
can occupy a slot. When displacement is required, the participant with the lowest
[pruning score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#pruning-score) is the documented
displacement candidate
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

Successful miner registration on a full subnet can therefore displace an existing participant under
documented pruning and immunity rules. Registration burn payment and displacement eligibility are
linked decisions on a full subnet, not independent steps
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

## Immunity Period Can Delay Miner Removal

The [Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period)
describes temporary protection after registration. Immunity vocabulary names a timing boundary that
can delay ordinary displacement, not a permanent exemption from subnet performance rules.

A newly registered neuron may be shielded for a time before it can be treated as a displacement
candidate. Miner deregistration remains the miner-specific displacement path once eligibility rules
are satisfied.

## Multiple Mechanisms Keep Separate Performance Tracks

Subnets can run more than one incentive mechanism in parallel. The
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)
notes that miner performance in one mechanism does not affect their rating in another.

Miner deregistration still describes removal of a low-performing miner from a subnet UID slot. When
a subnet uses multiple mechanisms, performance for one mechanism does not by itself describe miner
standing in another
([Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets)).

## Deregistration Names the General Removal Process

The [Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
describes removing a subnet miner or subnet validator from a subnet due to poor performance.
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)
describes that removal process applied to a low-performing miner when a full subnet accepts a new
registration.

Deregistration is the general removal term; miner deregistration names the miner-specific case of
that process.

## UID Slot Is the Position That Gets Vacated

The [Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot) describes a
participant position inside a subnet, while miner deregistration describes removal of a
low-performing miner from that position when subnet capacity and replacement rules require it.

A UID slot names the occupied miner position; miner deregistration names the miner-specific process
that can vacate that position under subnet displacement rules.

## Netuid Selects Which Subnet Market Applies

A [netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid) selects which subnet context
is in scope, while miner deregistration describes miner removal inside that selected subnet when
eligibility rules are met.

Netuid answers which subnet market is being discussed; miner deregistration answers what can happen
to a low-performing miner inside that subnet.

## Participant Removal Versus Subnet Removal

Miner deregistration removes one miner from a UID slot inside a subnet that continues to exist.
[Subnet deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) removes an
entire subnet to free a network slot when the global subnet cap binds.

A low-performing miner can be displaced without dissolving the subnet itself. Miner deregistration
stays scoped to UID participation inside an active netuid rather than to network-wide subnet sunset
events.

## Coinbase Stops Paying After the Slot Is Vacated

Holding a UID slot is what makes a miner visible to
[coinbase](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)-triggered epoch
rounds. While registered, a miner can earn a share of the emissions coinbase allocates each epoch
based on validator evaluations; once deregistered, the freed slot no longer appears in the consensus
the coinbase round processes.

Coinbase determines the emissions a miner's slot can earn while occupied; miner deregistration ends
that eligibility by vacating the slot.

## Immunity Period Delays But Does Not Remove Pruning Pressure

Miner deregistration documentation pairs pruning-score ranking with an immunity window that shields
recently registered neurons from ordinary displacement. After immunity ends, the same pruning rules
apply to miners and validators alike when the subnet is at capacity
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration),
[Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period)).

For readers, immunity vocabulary explains why a new registrant is not immediately eligible to be
pruned, while pruning-score vocabulary explains who is removed first once the subnet is full and
protection expires
([Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

That timeline keeps registration cost, grace window, and displacement ranking as separate parts of
the same slot lifecycle ([Understanding Neurons](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/neurons)).

## Registration Burn and Displacement Are Linked on Full Subnets

Mining documentation describes registration as paying a dynamic TAO burn while competing for a UID
slot. When the subnet is already at its occupancy ceiling, a successful registration can displace an
existing neuron rather than add net-new capacity, which ties entry cost to pruning-ranked removal
([Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration),
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

For readers, miner deregistration is therefore an outcome of slot competition, not a separate
administrative action. The incoming registrant pays the live burn; the outgoing occupant is selected
by documented pruning and immunity rules when displacement is required
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Glossary: Max Allowed UIDs](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#maxalloweduids)).

That framing keeps registration economics, slot ceilings, and removal ranking on one lifecycle path
([Subnet Metagraph](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/metagraph)).

## Reader Boundary

This article defines the term at a high level. It does not list current miners, count available
slots, rank subnet participants, or describe registration steps. Current displacement eligibility
belongs in mining references and live subnet state at the time of observation
([Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

Miner deregistration should be read as removal from a subnet miner slot, not as proof that the
account has been removed from every Bittensor role or network context.
