# Conviction and Locked Stake

Conviction and locked stake describe a Bittensor staking feature where subnet alpha stake can be
locked so that it builds a time-based commitment score. The official
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation describes conviction as a public on-chain signal of long-term commitment that cannot
be silently reversed.

## Locked Stake

Locked stake is alpha stake that is bound inside a specific subnet lock. The same official
[conviction documentation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
explains that the lock prevents the staked alpha balance on that subnet from dropping below the
locked amount. Alpha above the locked amount remains outside the lock boundary.

For Taopedia readers, the important distinction is between stake that supports participation and
stake that has also been locked for commitment signaling. Locking adds a visibility constraint to a
portion of staked alpha; it is not a separate token and not a separate subnet role.

## Conviction Score

Conviction is the score that grows from locked stake over time. In broad terms, the longer alpha
remains locked, the more conviction it can build toward the locked amount. The
[official documentation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking#the-conviction-score)
details the exact curves and formulas; the concept-level reading is that conviction records maturing
commitment rather than a simple current-balance number.

This makes conviction useful as a signal. A large locked position can show that the position cannot
be fully unwound without first passing through the lock behavior described in the official staking
docs.

## Lock Modes

The
[conviction docs](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking#decaying-and-perpetual-modes)
describe two high-level lock modes: decaying and perpetual. In decaying mode, the locked amount
decreases over time. In perpetual mode, the locked amount does not decay, allowing conviction to
continue approaching the locked amount. Switching out of perpetual mode starts the decay process
from the current locked mass.

This mode distinction is why conviction should be read as a time-sensitive staking signal. It says
something about a lock's current commitment state, but it does not replace the current official
state for that subnet and position.

## Subnet Alpha Context

Conviction and locked stake apply to subnet alpha already staked inside a specific subnet. The
official [Glossary: Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha) describes
subnet alpha as the subnet-specific staking asset used when delegating to a subnet.

For Taopedia readers, this keeps conviction inside subnet alpha staking. Locking signals commitment
on alpha bound to one subnet; it is not a root-TAO lock and not a separate token layer from the
alpha balance being committed.

Reference:
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Conviction and locked stake signal long-term commitment on subnet alpha. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms) notes
that miner performance in one incentive mechanism does not affect their rating in another.

For readers, conviction still names a staking commitment on locked subnet alpha. Multiple mechanisms
change how miners are rated inside the same subnet, but conviction remains a separate staking signal
rather than a per-mechanism performance score.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)

## Reader Boundary

Conviction should not be read as a validator recommendation, a complete staking status, or
protection from subnet deregistration. The official
[conviction documentation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking#subnet-deregistration)
states that conviction locks do not protect against deregistration and that deregistration removes
the lock records before ordinary subnet dissolution handling.

Claims about exact lock amounts, current conviction, decay timing, or a particular subnet need a
current chain query or another direct source. This article only explains the concept and the
interpretive boundary.

## Relationship to Subtensor

Conviction and locked stake and Subtensor are related but different parts of Bittensor chain
vocabulary. Subtensor names Bittensor's layer 1 blockchain and system of record, while conviction
and locked stake name a public on-chain commitment signal recorded through subnet staking state on
that layer. The [Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor)
describes the blockchain layer, and the
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation describes conviction as a public on-chain signal of long-term commitment.

For readers, Subtensor names the chain layer, while conviction and locked stake name a staking
commitment signal recorded on that layer.

References: [Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Relationship to Netuid

Conviction and locked stake and a netuid are related but different parts of Bittensor vocabulary. A
netuid identifies a subnet on the network, while conviction and locked stake name a commitment
signal applied to subnet alpha already staked inside that subnet context. The
[Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid) places netuid at the
subnet level, and the
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation describes locked stake as bound inside a specific subnet lock.

For readers, a netuid identifies which subnet staking context is in scope, while conviction and
locked stake name a commitment signal on alpha locked within that context.

References: [Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Relationship to Stake

Conviction and locked stake and stake are related but different scopes in Bittensor staking
vocabulary. Stake names the currency-token amount delegated to a validator UID in a subnet, while
conviction and locked stake name a time-based commitment signal built from locked subnet alpha
within that staking context. The
[Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake) defines stake as
delegated currency-token amount in a subnet, and the
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation describes locked stake as alpha bound inside a subnet lock.

For readers, stake names delegated amount in a subnet, while conviction and locked stake name a
commitment signal applied to a locked portion of that subnet alpha context.

References: [Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Relationship to Extrinsics

Conviction and locked stake and extrinsics are related but different parts of Bittensor chain action
vocabulary. Extrinsics name signed transactions and calls submitted to Subtensor, while conviction
and locked stake name the on-chain commitment state produced when staking lock actions are processed
on chain. The [Glossary: Extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#extrinsics)
describes signed transactions submitted to the chain, and the
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation describes conviction as a public on-chain signal.

For readers, extrinsics name submitted chain actions, while conviction and locked stake name the
commitment state associated with lock-related staking actions among those submissions.

References: [Glossary: Extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#extrinsics),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Relationship to Subnet Deregistration

Conviction and locked stake and subnet deregistration are related but distinct parts of Bittensor
subnet lifecycle vocabulary. Conviction and locked stake name a per-staker commitment signal built
from locked subnet alpha, while subnet deregistration is the network mechanism that removes the
lowest-EMA-price non-immune subnet to free a slot inside the 128-subnet cap. The official
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
states that conviction locks do not affect the deregistration selection score — only the EMA price
matters — and that lock records are deleted before the dissolution sequence runs.

For readers, conviction and locked stake name the on-chain signal that holds alpha inside a subnet
even when a holder wants to move it, while subnet deregistration names the lifecycle event that can
remove the entire subnet regardless of locked-stake state. A subnet with large locked conviction is
no more protected from deregistration than one with none. When a subnet is deregistered, the locked
alpha is liquidated alongside the rest: conviction scores themselves have no refund value, and the
lock records are simply removed during dissolution.

References: [Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
