# Delegation

Delegation is the process of staking TAO to a validator. The official
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation) describes delegation as
staking TAO to a validator, increasing that validator's stake and helping secure a validator permit.

## Validator Support

Delegation is a support relationship between a TAO holder and a validator. The
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
explains that staking, also known as delegation, supports validators because their total stake in a
subnet includes stake delegated by others.

This makes delegation different from the validator role itself. A delegate is the validator
receiving delegated stake, while delegation is the process that attaches staking support to that
validator.

## Stake Context

Delegated TAO contributes to the validator's stake context. The
[Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake) entry defines stake as
the currency-token amount delegated to a validator UID in a subnet, including both the validator's
own stake and stake delegated from others.

For a reader, delegation should therefore be read as part of the stake picture, not as a separate
validation task. It changes the support associated with a validator position; it does not make the
delegator perform validation work.

## Consensus and Emissions Context

Delegation matters because validator stake affects subnet consensus and emission context. The
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
states that a validator's total stake helps determine consensus power and share of emissions. The
[Glossary: Validator Permit](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-permit)
also places stake weight in the context of validation rights.

This article keeps the concept at the reference level. It does not calculate rewards, rank
validators, or describe how to carry out staking actions.

## Nomination Handoff

Delegation names the support relationship between a TAO holder and a validator. The official
[Glossary: Nominate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominate) describes
nomination as staking TAO on a validator hotkey so token holders can participate through active
validators. That action vocabulary is the operational side of the delegation relationship defined
here.

For readers, delegation is the relationship concept; nominate is the action term used when
describing how nominators attach TAO to validators before stake amounts and emissions context are
interpreted.

References: [Glossary: Nominate](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominate),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Delegation attaches TAO support to a validator inside a subnet. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that each
subnet has one or more incentive mechanisms.

For readers, delegation still names staking support for a validator position in one subnet. That
subnet may run more than one mechanism, but delegated stake remains part of the same subnet context.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)

## Relationship to Dividends

Delegation and dividends are related but different parts of Bittensor staking vocabulary. Delegation
names the process of staking TAO to a validator, while dividends name the portion of a validator's
emissions that flows back to those who have delegated stake. The
[Glossary: Dividends](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#dividends) describes
dividends as the portion of emissions returned to delegators based on their delegated stake.

For readers, delegation names the support relationship and the staking action, while dividends name
a result that depends on how the validator's emissions are distributed. Delegation establishes the
position; dividends reflect how that position participates in emission sharing. One names the act of
staking support; the other names what that support can earn.

References: [Glossary: Dividends](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#dividends),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)

## Relationship to Moving Stake

Delegation and moving stake are related but distinct parts of Bittensor staking vocabulary.
Delegation names the support relationship that attaches TAO to a validator in a subnet, while moving
stake names a participant-facing operation that rearranges an existing delegated position between
hotkeys and/or between subnets in a single atomic transaction, without first withdrawing to the
coldkey. The
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation#moving-stake)
describes moving stake as a special operation that transfers stake between validators or subnets in
one transaction, listed alongside direct unstaking and transferring stake as one of the ways a
participant can rearrange an existing staked position.

For readers, delegation establishes the support relationship and the initial stake amount on a
validator, while moving stake changes which hotkey and/or subnet that already-delegated position is
attached to. Delegation is the act of attaching support; moving stake is one of the operations a
delegator can use to retarget that support without unstaking first. The two are not interchangeable:
delegation attaches new TAO support to a validator, while moving stake re-points existing delegated
support to a different validator or subnet.

References:
[Staking and delegation overview: Moving stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation#moving-stake),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)

## Relationship to Autostaking

Delegation and autostaking address related but different parts of Bittensor staking vocabulary.
Delegation names the process of staking TAO to a validator, while autostaking is a mechanism that
automatically routes a miner's emission proceeds into a staking position without a separate manual
staking step. The
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
describes delegation as staking TAO to a validator and contributing to that validator's stake, while
the [Autostaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking) documentation describes
autostaking as automatically staking a miner's emissions to a chosen validator.

For readers, delegation and autostaking both result in TAO being staked to a validator, but they
differ in origin. Delegation is the act of a TAO holder manually staking to a validator; autostaking
is the automated process that directs emission proceeds from mining into that same kind of staking
relationship.

References: [Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation),
[Autostaking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners/autostaking)

## Relationship to Conviction and Locked Stake

Delegation and conviction both involve staked positions, but they describe different things.
Delegation is the process of staking TAO to a validator, increasing that validator's stake, as the
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation) describes. Conviction and
locked stake, by the
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)
documentation, describe locking subnet alpha stake so that it builds a time-based commitment score
and cannot be silently reduced below the locked amount.

For readers, delegation answers where stake is directed — which validator it supports — while
conviction answers whether a staked position has been locked to signal long-term commitment. The two
are not interchangeable: delegation routes stake to a validator for consensus and emissions, whereas
conviction is a lock applied to staked alpha that builds a commitment score over time. A position
can be delegated without being locked, and locking adds a commitment constraint that delegation by
itself does not impose.

References: [Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation),
[Conviction and locked stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/conviction-staking)

## Reader Boundary

Delegation should not be read as a recommendation to support any particular validator or as a status
report on current stake. Exact balances, fees, slippage, validator take, and current subnet state
belong in current official staking references.

## Relationship to Coinbase

Delegation and coinbase are related but different parts of Bittensor's economics. Delegation is the
process of staking TAO to a validator, increasing that validator's stake. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation describes coinbase as the per-block protocol mechanism that drives TAO emission,
accumulates pending emissions, and triggers Yuma Consensus rounds at epoch boundaries, while the
[Glossary: Delegation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#delegation) describes the
staking process.

The connection is indirect. Delegation increases the stake behind a validator, which contributes to
its stake weight and consensus influence; coinbase separately triggers the epoch and emission
process from which the validator and its delegators can earn dividends. Delegation is a participant
action on the staking side that shapes consensus only through the resulting stake-weight
relationship, while coinbase is the per-block mechanism that runs the epoch cycle each block. One
names how support is attached to a validator; the other names the recurring engine that drives the
emission cycle that support takes part in.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
delegation, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence about stake support attached
to a validator.

In local testing, delegation can show whether stake is being attached to a validator inside an
isolated environment. That is useful for checking the relationship between staking and the validator
position, but it remains isolated feedback from a local environment.

On testchain, delegation behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives stronger
evidence about how staked TAO contributes to a validator's stake than a private local run, while
still keeping the result separate from production mainchain incentives.

On mainchain, delegation belongs to live validator stake context. The
[Staking/delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)
describes delegation as staking TAO to a validator and contributing to that validator's stake, so
production delegation context is tied to actual stake on the live network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A delegation example observed in one environment should
not be read as identical evidence about validator stake support in another environment.

For readers, this keeps delegation examples from sounding stronger than their environment supports.
Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all describe staking support attached to a validator,
but they do not carry the same interpretive weight.
