# Commit Reveal

Commit Reveal is a Bittensor mechanism that delays when validator weight signals become visible.
Fresh weights are concealed before they can be reused by observers
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#commit-reveal),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

The term is about visibility timing. It does not change what validator weights mean, and it does not
replace the consensus process that later interprets available validator signals.

Validators still set weights, while the chain handles the concealed commit and later reveal path.
The mechanism changes when weight data becomes readable and usable, not the basic meaning of the
submitted weights ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

That places Commit Reveal before the open consensus calculation. The mechanism protects the timing
of weight disclosure; Yuma Consensus later interprets available validator signals
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Timing Model

At a high level, the mechanism separates a concealed phase from a later reveal phase. That two-step
timing model is the core of the mechanism
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

The concealment period is measured through the subnet's configured Commit Reveal interval. During
that period, the committed weights remain encrypted and are not yet included in Yuma Consensus
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

After the interval elapses, the chain reveals the weights when the corresponding Drand beacon pulse
is available. The revealed weights then become public and can be used in the next consensus
calculation ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

That timing model keeps the concept distinct from consensus scoring. Commit Reveal answers when
weight information can be seen; available validator signals are aggregated into subnet outcomes
through Yuma Consensus ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Subnet Settings

The mechanism belongs to subnet-specific settings, and subnet hyperparameters provide the broader
vocabulary for subnet mechanism settings
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

That makes Commit Reveal a subnet mechanism rather than a universal description of every
validator-weight path. A subnet can be discussed in terms of whether this visibility delay is part
of its weight path, while the article definition stays focused on the mechanism itself.

The subnet setting controls whether the mechanism is active and how long the reveal interval lasts.
That places Commit Reveal beside subnet hyperparameter vocabulary without making it a general
subnet-quality label ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

This keeps the setting separate from the validator's evaluation. A subnet can configure the
visibility mechanism, while validators still supply the weight signals that later enter consensus.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For commit reveal
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret concealed weight
submission and reveal-timing examples.

In localnet, commit-reveal flows can be tested in an isolated environment. Local reveal intervals
and weight visibility do not represent production subnet behavior.

On testnet, commit-reveal hyperparameters can be exercised in a shared non-production network.
Testnet concealment windows on a selected netuid are separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, commit reveal is a live per-subnet mechanism on production networks. Observed reveal
timing and weight visibility depend on the selected subnet's hyperparameter settings
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A commit-reveal example from one environment should not be
read as representing production weight-disclosure behavior in another environment.

## Distinction from Activity Cutoff

Commit reveal and activity cutoff are different weight-timing rules. Commit reveal delays when
submitted validator weights become visible, concealing fresh weights for a reveal window, while
activity cutoff is the early-epoch block window within which a validator must submit weights to
count in the current Yuma Consensus pass
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

- **Commit reveal** — delays visibility of submitted weights until the reveal window.
- **Activity cutoff** — bounds how early in the epoch weights must arrive to count.

## Distinction from MEV Shield

Commit Reveal and MEV Shield are both pre-inclusion concealment mechanisms, but they protect
different things. Commit Reveal conceals validator weight submissions until a later reveal so
recently produced weights are harder to copy, while MEV Shield encrypts sensitive pending
transaction contents, such as staking and unstaking, until block inclusion so front-running and
sandwich attacks cannot read them in the mempool
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

The two answer different questions. Commit Reveal protects a validator's fresh weights from copiers,
while MEV Shield protects a trader's pending transaction from mempool observers, so one belongs to
weight-disclosure timing and the other to transaction-ordering safety even though both delay when
sensitive data becomes readable ([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

## Distinction from Weights Rate Limit

Commit reveal changes when committed weights become visible, while the weights rate limit changes
how frequently a validator can submit new commits. Commit reveal is a visibility-timing mechanism;
the weights rate limit is a submission-pacing hyperparameter
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

## Distinction from Weight Copying

Weight copying is reusing another validator's visible weights instead of independent evaluation.
Commit reveal delays when fresh committed weights become readable
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

- **Weight copying** — dependence on copied visible weight signals.
- **Commit reveal** — visibility delay before weights become readable.

Problem vocabulary names the exploit; commit-reveal vocabulary names the timing defense
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The weight-copying problem comes from visible weight information being useful to validators that did
not independently evaluate subnet work
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

Commit Reveal reduces that timing advantage by hiding fresh weight signals until a later reveal
point. The protection is narrow: it makes recent signals harder to copy before disclosure, while the
underlying evaluation meaning of validator weights remains separate.

The copying-resistance value comes from the delay. A validator that wants to copy fresh weights
cannot read those encrypted weights during the concealment interval, so the copied signal is less
useful for immediate weight-setting behavior
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

That makes Commit Reveal most relevant to fresh, recently produced weights. Historical public
weights can still be read, but the mechanism reduces the value of copying weights before their
intended reveal point ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Commit reveal delays when validator weight signals become visible to observers. Validators still
submit weights, but the chain conceals those signals until the configured reveal window opens
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#commit-reveal)).

Yuma Consensus is the later step that aggregates revealed validator signals into subnet incentive
outcomes at tempo boundaries. It concerns reward settlement once readable weights are present, not
the concealment schedule that precedes them
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

During the concealment interval, committed weights stay encrypted and are not yet part of the open
consensus calculation. After reveal, the same weights become ordinary validator signals for the next
Yuma pass on the selected netuid
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The weight-copying problem shows why that timing gap matters. Commit reveal makes recently produced
weights harder to reuse before disclosure, while Yuma still depends on revealed validator signals
when crediting subnet outcomes
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

Subnet hyperparameters configure whether commit reveal is active and how long the reveal interval
lasts on a netuid. Those settings change visibility timing; they do not replace Yuma settlement
inside the same weight path
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

- **Commit reveal** — delayed visibility for validator weight signals.
- **Yuma Consensus** — aggregation that turns revealed weights into emission shares.

Commit Reveal and Yuma Consensus sit at different points in the validator-weight path. Commit Reveal
concerns delayed visibility before weights are openly readable, while Yuma Consensus concerns
aggregation after validator signals are available for consensus processing
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Keeping that order clear prevents the terms from collapsing into one idea. Commit Reveal protects
the timing of weight disclosure; disclosed signals affect subnet incentives through Yuma Consensus.

The order is direct: weights are set, concealed, revealed, and then available for consensus
processing. Each step belongs to the validator-weight path, but only the aggregation step is Yuma
Consensus ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

That order also keeps weight-copying language precise. Commit Reveal reduces the usefulness of
recently visible weights; Yuma Consensus uses revealed validator signals in later aggregation
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Distinction from Weight Setting

Weight setting is the validator action that records miner scores on chain. Commit reveal delays when
those committed weights become visible to observers
([Glossary: Weight Setting](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-setting),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

- **Weight setting** — validator submission of miner scores.
- **Commit reveal** — concealment interval before weights are readable.

Action vocabulary names submission; commit-reveal vocabulary names delayed visibility
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

## Distinction from Commit Reveal Period

Commit reveal is the conceal-and-reveal weight scheme. The commit reveal period is the
hyperparameter that sets how many subnet tempos the hidden window lasts
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

The mechanism names the encrypted commit-and-reveal path; the period names its duration in tempos.
Precise timing claims should cite the subnet whose hyperparameters are being read.

The automatic reveal path is different from a manual disclosure step. Validators submit weights as
usual, and the chain performs the time-lock encryption and reveal process around those weights
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

This automatic path matters because revealed weights are still ordinary validator signals once they
become readable. Commit Reveal changes their visibility schedule; it does not turn them into a
separate kind of consensus input
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The reveal step therefore changes availability, not meaning. Once readable, the weights are still
validator-weight signals in the subnet's consensus path
([Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

## Distinction from Consensus-Based Weights

Consensus-based weights adjust validator bonds around consensus alignment as a separate copier
deterrent. Commit reveal limits when fresh weight signals become visible
([Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

Delayed visibility and bond adjustment address different parts of the weight-copying problem. One
changes readability timing; the other changes how aligned or stale signals affect bond outcomes.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus 3

Yuma Consensus 3 and Commit Reveal act at different points around validator weights. Commit Reveal
changes when weight information becomes visible, while YC3 changes how far an already submitted
weight influences bond formation after consensus is known
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[How Yuma Consensus 3 Makes Bittensor More Fair](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yc3-blog)).

- **Commit Reveal** — controls when weight information becomes visible.
- **Yuma Consensus 3** — controls how submitted weights shape bonds after consensus.

## Distinction from Drand Time-Lock Encryption

Commit Reveal is the commit-then-reveal weight submission process: validators submit concealed
weights first and reveal readable weight information later
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)). Drand time-lock
encryption is the timing layer inside that process — committed weight ciphertext stays unreadable
until the public Drand beacon reaches the target round, even for the validator who created it
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption),
[Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

- **Commit Reveal** — the two-step submission pattern that separates commit from later reveal.
- **Drand time-lock encryption** — the beacon-timed lock that keeps committed weights concealed
  until reveal.

Drand time-lock encryption prevents concealed weight data from being decrypted before the intended
reveal time ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

Here, Drand is timing infrastructure for hidden weights. The important concept is the visibility
gap: weight material can exist before observers can read it.

The Drand connection is therefore about unlock timing. The committed encrypted data is present
before the reveal, but the cryptographic material needed to read it arrives only when the relevant
beacon pulse is available ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

That timing relationship makes Drand time-lock encryption narrower than Commit Reveal. Commit Reveal
is the whole conceal-and-reveal mechanism; Drand time-lock encryption supplies the
delayed-readability piece.

In short, the two relate as a scheme and the cryptographic primitive that powers it: the committed
weights decrypt automatically once the target Drand round's randomness is published, with no manual
reveal step required from the validator who created them
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

## Reader Boundary

Commit Reveal should not be read as a replacement for Yuma Consensus, a guarantee against all weight
copying, or proof that independent evaluation occurred. It names delayed visibility for validator
weight signals before those signals become readable to observers
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#commit-reveal)).
