# Tempo

A tempo is a block-based timing interval used by Bittensor subnets. Rewards accumulate during a
tempo and are distributed at the end of that interval through [[yuma_consensus|Yuma Consensus]]. As
a subnet hyperparameter, `tempo` specifies the number of blocks between epoch transitions.

References: [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)

## What a tempo controls

Tempo gives subnet processes a shared cadence. It does not define what useful work means on a
subnet; that comes from the subnet's incentive mechanism. Instead, tempo helps determine when
periodic subnet work is grouped, processed, and paid out. This is why tempo sits near both
[[subnet_creation_mechanisms|subnet mechanism design]] and [[dynamic_tao|emission allocation]].

References:
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)

## Emission timing

Bittensor emission has a two-stage flow: rewards are injected into subnet pools over blocks, and
then distribution happens at the end of each tempo. Yuma Consensus is the aggregation step that uses
validator-submitted evaluations to determine how those rewards are allocated inside the subnet.

References: [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Hyperparameter context

Tempo is also a subnet hyperparameter, so articles should not treat one timing value as a universal
property of every subnet. Exact timing claims belong next to the source that supports them, and
operational decisions should use the relevant subnet's parameter value rather than a generic
description.

Reference: [Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)

## Commit Reveal timing

Commit Reveal uses tempo-based timing as part of delayed weight visibility. Its
`commit_reveal_period` value is measured in tempos, and converting the reveal interval to blocks
requires multiplying that period by the subnet tempo. This is one practical reason tempo matters
beyond ordinary reward distribution.

References: [Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)

## How to read tempo claims

A tempo claim should make clear whether it is describing the concept, a hyperparameter, or a process
that depends on tempo. The stable concept is the block-based cadence. Claims about exact length,
reward timing, or reveal timing need the specific protocol documentation or subnet parameter source
that supports them.

References: [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)

## Relationship to Epoch

Tempo and epoch refer to related but different parts of subnet timing. The
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo) describes tempo as the
subnet-specific cadence for epochs, and the
[Glossary: Epoch](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#epoch) defines an epoch as the
period during which a subnet executes its consensus mechanism.

For readers, tempo names the block-based cadence that determines when epochs run, while epoch names
the consensus period that cadence produces. A tempo should not be read as the epoch itself, only as
the interval that marks epoch transitions.

References: [Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo),
[Glossary: Epoch](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#epoch)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Tempo is usually discussed alongside one incentive mechanism at a time, but subnets can also run
multiple mechanisms in parallel. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix) notes that each
mechanism has its own weight matrix for independent consensus calculations.

For readers, tempo still names the subnet's block-based epoch cadence. Multiple mechanisms add
separate weight and consensus paths within that shared timing, rather than separate tempo schedules
per mechanism.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Weight Matrix](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix)

## Relationship to Netuid

A tempo and a netuid are related but different parts of Bittensor subnet vocabulary. A netuid
selects which subnet context is in scope, while a tempo names the block-based cadence that governs
epoch timing inside that subnet. The
[Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid) places netuid at the
subnet level, and the [Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)
describes tempo as the subnet-specific cadence for epochs.

For readers, netuid answers which subnet market is being discussed, while tempo answers which
block-based timing cadence applies inside that subnet. Tempo language should stay paired with the
netuid that selects the subnet context.

References: [Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)

## Relationship to Block

A tempo and a block are related through subnet timing measurement. Tempo is expressed in blocks
rather than clock time alone, and each new block advances the cadence that subnet processes use for
epoch transitions. The [Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block)
describes a block as a unit of chain data on Subtensor, and the
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo) describes tempo as a
block-based interval for subnet timing.

For readers, blocks mark chain progression that tempo counts toward epoch boundaries. Tempo should
be read as a block-count cadence rather than as a fixed wall-clock duration unless network block
timing is also in view.

References: [Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)

## Relationship to Subnet Hyperparameters

A tempo and subnet hyperparameters are related but different parts of subnet timing vocabulary.
Tempo names the block-based cadence itself, while subnet hyperparameters include the configurable
setting that defines that cadence for a selected subnet. The
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters) reference
lists tempo among subnet-specific settings.

For readers, subnet hyperparameters are the configurable inputs, while tempo is the cadence concept
those settings express. Different netuids can use different tempo values and therefore different
epoch timing.

References: [Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)

## Relationship to Yuma Consensus

A tempo and Yuma Consensus address related but different parts of subnet reward timing. Tempo names
the block-based interval that groups subnet periodic work, while
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes how validator
evaluation signals are aggregated into subnet outcomes at the end of that interval. The
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes rewards
accumulating during a tempo and being distributed through consensus at the interval boundary.

For readers, tempo names the timing cadence, while Yuma Consensus names the aggregation process that
uses validator signals when that cadence completes. Tempo language should not be read as a
substitute for the full Yuma documentation.

References: [Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)
