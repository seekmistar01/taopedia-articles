# Phishing

Phishing is a scam in which an attacker poses as something trustworthy to trick a person into
revealing a secret. In Bittensor the usual prize is a wallet's recovery material, most often the
seed phrase, which the documentation treats as sensitive wallet access material that should be kept
private.

References:
[Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase),
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)

## What It Targets

The center of a Bittensor phishing attempt is the recovery phrase that backs a wallet. The
documentation describes a seed phrase as material that can restore a wallet's keys, so an attacker
who obtains it can rebuild the wallet elsewhere and move its funds. A phishing attempt is, at heart,
an effort to get that phrase out of the owner's sole control by making a fake request look genuine.

Reference:
[Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)

## Development Stage Context

Phishing targets recovery material because a disclosed seed phrase can restore the keys that control
a wallet. Official guidance treats the phrase as key-equivalent and warns against entering it
through untrusted surfaces
([Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)).

The practical risk therefore follows the assets and permissions those keys can move, not the label
on the prompt. A fake message that harvests recovery words is dangerous whenever the compromised
material can reach live [TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau) or
[stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake) the
[coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey) controls
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

Readers should treat any recovery-phrase request as a custody threat and judge phishing examples by
whether disclosure would hand an attacker wallet control, regardless of how trustworthy the prompt
appears.

## Distinction from Seed Phrase

Phishing and seed phrase are related but different parts of Bittensor wallet-security vocabulary.
Phishing names the deceptive request that tries to obtain a secret, while seed phrase names the
recovery-word material that documentation treats as key-equivalent wallet access
([Glossary: Seed Phrase](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#seed-phrase),
[Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)).

Attack-surface vocabulary and secret-material vocabulary answer different questions. Phishing
describes the social prompt; the seed phrase is what that prompt usually tries to obtain.

- **Phishing** — social deception aimed at obtaining wallet secrets.
- **Seed phrase** — recovery material that can rebuild the wallet's keys.

This is why the phrase is the prize: because it can regenerate the keys it belongs to, disclosing it
is close to handing over the wallet itself, which is why official guidance frames disclosure as a
wallet-safety risk rather than a minor mistake
([Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)).

## Distinction from Address Poisoning Scams

Phishing and address poisoning are both wallet-safety scams, but they exploit different steps.
Phishing is social deception that tries to get a user to reveal a secret such as the seed phrase,
while address poisoning plants a lookalike destination address so a value-bearing action is sent to
the wrong recipient
([Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase),
[Address Poisoning Scams](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/address-poisoning-scams)).

Secret-disclosure vocabulary and destination-selection vocabulary answer different questions.
Phishing targets recovery material; address poisoning targets where a signed transfer is sent.

- **Phishing** — deception to obtain secrets such as a seed phrase.
- **Address poisoning** — lookalike addresses that misdirect signed transfers.

## Distinction from Public Key

Phishing and a public key name different sides of wallet cryptography. Phishing is social deception
that tries to extract a secret a user must keep private, while a public key is a cryptographic key
that is publicly available and used for verifying signatures, so sharing it is not itself a
compromise ([Glossary: Public Key](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#public-key)).

- **Phishing** — deception aimed at extracting secret material the owner must never disclose.
- **Public key** — a publicly shareable key used to verify signatures, not a secret.

## Distinction from Private Key

Phishing and a private key describe a request and the asset it ultimately endangers. Phishing names
the deceptive prompt that tries to obtain wallet secrets, while a private key is the private
component of the cryptographic key pair that is crucial for securing transactions
([Glossary: Private Key](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#private-key)).

- **Phishing** — the social request that seeks to compromise wallet secrets.
- **Private key** — the secret key-pair component whose disclosure surrenders control of
  transactions.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Phishing is social deception aimed at tricking a user into revealing wallet recovery material such
as a seed phrase. It names wallet-security threat vocabulary, not the on-chain step that turns
validator weight submissions into emission shares
([Wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Glossary: Seed Phrase](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#seed-phrase)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Phishing targets offline secrets; Yuma Consensus settles scoring
outcomes on a subnet ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep social-engineering threat vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Phishing
answers how attackers try to obtain recovery material; Yuma Consensus answers how validator weights
become emission shares in a subnet ([Wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Phishing** — deception aimed at obtaining wallet recovery secrets.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level. It does not catalog specific scams, sites, or
messages, and the forms phishing takes change constantly. The durable point is the target: a
wallet's recovery phrase is what a Bittensor phishing attempt is ultimately after, which is why that
phrase belongs nowhere but the owner's own secure keeping.

## Coldkey Material Is What Phishing Usually Targets

Official [Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation
separates the [coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey) that holds
[TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau) and
[stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake) from the
[hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey) used for subnet operations.
Phishing that asks for a recovery phrase is therefore aimed at coldkey control, not merely at a
mining or validation hotkey label.

That distinction matters because a stolen hotkey can disrupt subnet participation, but a stolen
coldkey seed phrase can move the underlying funds and stake the wallet controls.

## Leaked Phrases Should Trigger Coldkey Rotation

[Handle your Seed Phrase Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)
documentation treats a leaked secret as a case where the owner can and should rotate access through
a coldkey swap rather than continuing to treat the exposed phrase as safe. Leak vocabulary therefore
points toward replacement, not toward reuse of the same recovery material.

Phishing succeeds when a user treats a fake prompt like a legitimate wallet step. Once disclosure
happens, the durable response in official guidance is rotation of coldkey control, not merely
changing a display password on the same compromised seed.

## Official Guidance Rejects Untrusted Entry Surfaces

The same seed-phrase security page warns against entering recovery material into messaging apps,
email, or online word processors. Those surfaces are untrusted channels where a convincing fake
support message can harvest the phrase.

Phishing depends on that mistake: the attacker imitates a trusted party, but the entry surface
itself is what official wallet guidance marks as unsafe for seed-phrase handling.
