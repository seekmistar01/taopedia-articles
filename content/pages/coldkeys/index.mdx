# Coldkeys (Bittensor)

A coldkey is the long-term, security-sensitive key in Bittensor. It controls TAO and stake and is
used to sign financial operations
([Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Working with Keys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/working-with-keys)).

## Responsibilities

- Hold and secure TAO balances and delegated stake.
- Authorize staking, unstaking, moving stake, and transfers.
- Own one or more hotkeys used for operational roles.

## Security practices

- Keep the coldkey and its seed phrase offline when possible.
- Never place the coldkey on internet-exposed nodes used for mining/validating.
- Verify documented binaries and wallet guidance before performing key operations.

## Operational relationship

- Coldkey provides ownership and control; hotkeys derive authority for operational actions.
- If a hotkey is compromised, the coldkey can rotate or revoke without exposing funds (assuming best
  practices).

## Tooling

- `btcli` and the Wallet SDK support creating, importing, and managing coldkeys.
- Use minimal on-chain interactions from environments you fully trust.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For coldkey
operations on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret evidence
about ownership, stake, and key state.

In localnet, coldkey-based transaction mechanics can be tested in an isolated environment. Local
wallet and stake state reflect local chain configuration rather than production TAO custody.

On testnet, coldkey operations can be observed in a shared non-production network. Testnet stake and
registration context on a selected netuid is separate from mainnet on-chain identity
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

On mainnet, coldkey context concerns actual TAO and stake in live on-chain state. Production coldkey
operations authorize real financial movement and subnet ownership on the connected Bittensor network
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A coldkey operation observed in one environment should not
be read as identical evidence about ownership or key state in another.

## Hotkeys Pair Under Coldkey Ownership

Coldkeys and hotkeys occupy opposite ends of the Bittensor key model. The
[Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey) describes a coldkey
as the security-sensitive key that holds funds and stake, while
[Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey) entries describe
operational keys used for day-to-day network actions such as registering and submitting weights.

For readers, a coldkey should not be confused with a hotkey despite both being part of the same
wallet. The coldkey signs financial operations and owns one or more hotkeys, but it is not present
on live network nodes. A hotkey, by contrast, is designed to run on online infrastructure without
exposing funds ([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

Coldkeys authorize staking, unstaking, transfers, and subnet ownership actions. Hotkeys handle
operational roles such as registration and weight submission without holding TAO balances
([Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer),
[Coldkey and hotkey workstation security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).

## Seed Phrase Custody Protects Coldkey Access

A coldkey is the high-authority key that owns a wallet's balances, stake, and registrations, while a
seed phrase is the offline recovery words that can restore that coldkey's key access if it is lost
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Glossary: Seed Phrase](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#seed-phrase)).

For readers, the coldkey is the authority used to sign sensitive actions, while the seed phrase is
backup-and-recovery material that must stay secret, because anyone holding the seed phrase can
restore the coldkey. The two are protected together but name different things
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

## Coldkey Owns Subnet Registrations

The [Wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation places subnet ownership
and registration authority on the coldkey side of the wallet model. A coldkey can register subnets
and control the hotkeys tied to those registrations
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey)).

That ownership role is separate from day-to-day miner or validator operation. Operational nodes use
hotkeys, but the coldkey remains the account layer that authorizes subnet ownership changes and
other high-consequence wallet actions.

For readers, subnet registration ownership belongs with coldkey vocabulary even when the visible
subnet work happens through hotkeys on live machines.

## Coldkey Material Belongs Off Live Nodes

The
[Coldkey and hotkey workstation security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)
guide treats coldkey material as highly sensitive and warns against placing it on internet-exposed
validator or miner machines. The coldkey is meant for custody and financial signing, not for routine
subnet traffic ([Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey)).

Keeping the coldkey off operational infrastructure limits how often high-value signing material is
online. Hotkeys can run on nodes because they handle operational roles without holding TAO balances.

The workstation-security guide is the reference for that separation. It is aimed at reducing
exposure of coldkey material during mining and validation work.

## Financial Signing Stays on the Coldkey

Staking, unstaking, moving stake, and transfers are coldkey-side actions in Bittensor wallet
vocabulary. The [Glossary: Stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake) and
[Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer) describe those
operations as part of token and delegation movement that requires the coldkey's authority.

Readers should not read a hotkey registration action as permission to move TAO. Registration and
weight submission belong to hotkey operational vocabulary; balance and stake changes belong to
coldkey financial vocabulary.

That split is why wallet documentation treats coldkeys as the custody anchor even when many visible
subnet actions are signed by hotkeys.

## Coldkey Swap Migrates Wallet Identity

A coldkey is the persistent cryptographic key that holds TAO balances and controls the hotkeys and
subnet registrations associated with a wallet. A coldkey swap is the staged migration procedure that
replaces a compromised or aging coldkey with a new one while preserving everything the old coldkey
controlled ([Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

For readers, the coldkey is the identity being protected, while coldkey swap is the lifecycle
operation that re-homes that identity when key rotation is necessary. The swap does not change the
hotkeys owned by the old coldkey or the subnet registrations associated with them; the new coldkey
inherits all of that state. This inheritance is what distinguishes a coldkey swap from an ordinary
transfer: a transfer moves funds, while a swap migrates the identity and ownership itself
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

## Hotkey Swap Rotates Keys Under the Same Coldkey

A coldkey is the security-sensitive key that holds TAO balances and controls the hotkeys associated
with a wallet, while hotkey swap is the operational-key-rotation operation that moves subnet
participation from a compromised or aging hotkey to a fresh replacement hotkey owned by the same
coldkey
([Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security),
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap)).

For readers, a coldkey is the identity that owns hotkeys, while hotkey swap is the operation that
rotates the operational key under that same coldkey without involving the coldkey itself. A coldkey
swap migrates the coldkey identity; a hotkey swap migrates the subnet role from one hotkey to
another under the unchanged coldkey. Each operation addresses its own key level: hotkey swap
addresses a compromised operational key, while coldkey swap addresses a compromised controlling key
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

## Coldkey Is the Wallet Name and Can Stand Alone

The [Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation
notes that in `btcli` the coldkey is equivalent to the wallet name, because the coldkey holds
permissions and ownership over the hotkeys beneath it. The same documentation states that holding a
balance, delegating stake, or creating a subnet can rely on a coldkey alone, while mining or
validating requires a paired hotkey.

A coldkey balance is also subject to the documented existential deposit: if a wallet balance falls
below the minimum required TAO, the account is deactivated and the remaining balance is destroyed
([Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)). That keeps custody
and account-liveness concerns on the coldkey side rather than the operational hotkey side.

## Distinction from Proxy Wallets

A coldkey is the ownership key that controls funds, stake, and paired hotkeys. A proxy wallet grants
a separate account scoped authority to submit specific calls without changing which coldkey owns the
wallet ([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Proxies overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/proxies)).

## Distinction from Wallet Address

A coldkey and a wallet address name a signing key and its public identifier. The coldkey is the
security-critical key that controls TAO and stake, while the wallet address is the coldkey's
public-key-derived identifier used to receive TAO
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Glossary: Wallet Address](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#wallet-address)).

- **Coldkey** — the security-critical signing key controlling TAO and stake.
- **Wallet address** — the coldkey's public identifier used to receive TAO.

## Distinction from Existential Deposit

A coldkey and the existential deposit name a key and a balance rule that applies to it. The coldkey
controls the wallet's funds, while the existential deposit is the minimum TAO a coldkey balance must
keep to stay active; falling below it deactivates the account
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

- **Coldkey** — the key that controls the wallet's TAO and stake.
- **Existential deposit** — the minimum balance that keeps the coldkey account active.

## Distinction from Regenerating a Key

A coldkey is the custody key that controls TAO and hotkeys. Regenerating a key can restore that
coldkey from its mnemonic when local files are lost
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Glossary: Regenerating a Key](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#regenerating-a-key)).

## Distinction from Seed Phrase Security

Coldkey names the custody key whose recovery words carry the highest-consequence authority. Seed
phrase security names how those words are stored and protected
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Handle your Seed Phrase/Mnemonic Securely](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/handle-seed-phrase)).

## Distinction from Bittensor CLI: Permissions Guide

A coldkey is the security-sensitive key that controls TAO and stake, while BTCLI permissions name
which command-line actions require coldkey authority versus hotkey authority or permissionless reads
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Bittensor CLI: Permissions Guide](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/btcli/btcli-permissions)).

Custody-key vocabulary and CLI-authority vocabulary answer different questions. Coldkey names the
protected ownership key; BTCLI permissions name when that key must authorize a CLI action.

- **Coldkey** — security-sensitive key that controls TAO and stake.
- **BTCLI permissions** — which CLI actions require coldkey authority.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

A coldkey is the custody key that holds TAO balances and authorizes staking and wallet operations in
Bittensor. It names key custody vocabulary, not the on-chain step that turns validator weight
submissions into emission shares
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Coldkeys sign extrinsics that move stake, but Yuma Consensus
settles scoring outcomes on a subnet rather than defining custody roles
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep custody vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Coldkeys answer which key
holds and authorizes balances; Yuma Consensus answers how validator weights become emission shares
in a subnet ([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Coldkey** — custody key that holds TAO and authorizes wallet actions.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

A coldkey is the custody and financial-signing key in Bittensor wallet vocabulary. It is not a
hotkey, a seed phrase by itself, or permission to run routine subnet traffic on live nodes
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey)).

Coldkeys authorize staking, unstaking, transfers, and subnet ownership actions. Hotkeys handle
operational roles such as registration and weight submission without holding TAO balances
([Glossary: Transfer](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#transfer),
[Coldkey and hotkey workstation security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).
