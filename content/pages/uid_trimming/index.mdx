# UID Trimming

UID trimming is a subnet-owner operation that reduces the number of registered neuron UIDs on a
subnet down to a chosen maximum and compacts the remaining UIDs into consecutive indices. The
[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming) guide presents it as a way for
a subnet owner to manage the active participant base while keeping clean, sequential UID numbering.

## How Trimming Selects UIDs

Trimming removes the weakest participants first. The process starts from the lowest emitters and
works upward, ranking UIDs by emission scores and removing low performers until the remaining count
is at or below the chosen maximum. It is driven by emission rather than by a separate pruning score
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

## Compression of Remaining UIDs

After the low performers are removed, the survivors are compressed to the left so their indices stay
consecutive. If UIDs 5, 7, and 9 survive, they become 0, 1, and 2. This keeps the subnet's UID
numbering contiguous rather than leaving gaps where trimmed slots used to be
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

## Protected UIDs

Not every UID can be trimmed. Temporally immune UIDs, those still within the
[immunity period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period) after a recent
registration, and owner-owned UIDs are protected from trimming. The number of immune UIDs must not
exceed 80% of the maximum UID count
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

## Owner Control and Limits

Trimming is a [sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo) operation that only
the subnet owner, the wallet that created the subnet, can perform; it is triggered manually rather
than automatically. The guide lists a rate limit of 216,000 blocks, about 30 days, between trims,
and states that the minimum count an owner can trim a subnet down to is currently 64. These are
documented protocol limits, and the values in force should be read for the subnet in question.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For UID trimming
on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret owner trim
examples, immune-UID limits, and compression outcomes.

In localnet, UID trimming can be tested in an isolated environment. Local trim results reflect local
chain configuration and locally configured UID limits rather than production subnet participant
state.

On testnet, trimming behavior can be observed in a shared, non-production network. Testnet trim
outcomes and immune-UID counts are separate from mainnet subnet metagraph state
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

On mainnet, UID trimming is a live owner sudo action on the production Bittensor network that can
remove low emitters and compress surviving UIDs into consecutive indices.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A trimming example from one environment should not be read
as representing production UID layout or trim eligibility on another network.

## Distinction from Deregistration

UID trimming and deregistration are both ways a neuron can leave a subnet, but they answer different
operational questions. Deregistration recycles one incumbent slot when a full subnet admits a new
registration, while UID trimming is a subnet-owner sudo action that lowers the live UID count toward
a chosen maximum and removes many low emitters in one pass
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)).

For readers on a subnet such as netuid 1, deregistration is competition-driven displacement tied to
registration events, while trimming is a bulk resize that compresses survivors into consecutive
indices. Trimming also deletes listed on-chain neuron data — weights, bonds, axon information, and
last-update records — rather than freeing a single slot for one incoming registrant
([The Subnet Metagraph](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/metagraph),
[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

- **UID trimming** — owner-initiated bulk reduction to a target count with survivor compression.
- **Deregistration** — one incumbent removed when a full subnet accepts a new registration.

## Distinction from Pruning Score

Pruning score ranks miners when a full subnet receives a new registration and one slot must open.
UID trimming is a subnet-owner bulk resize that removes many low emitters down to a chosen maximum
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming),
[Glossary: Pruning Score](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#pruning-score),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)).

For netuid 1 readers, pruning score belongs to automatic slot replacement at registration time.
Trimming belongs to an owner sudo action that can remove multiple UIDs and compress survivors into
consecutive indices in one pass ([Mining in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners)).

- **Pruning score** — ranking input when one slot opens for a new registrant.
- **UID trimming** — owner-initiated bulk reduction to a target count.

## Distinction from Immunity Period

Immunity period protects a newly registered UID from displacement for a grace window. UID trimming
skips immune UIDs but names the owner resize operation, not the protection rule
([Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period),
[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

- **UID trimming** — owner-initiated bulk reduction to a target count.
- **Immunity period** — newcomer protection window before displacement.

Protection vocabulary names grace; trimming vocabulary names the owner shrink action
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

## Distinction from Max Allowed UIDs

Max allowed UIDs is the subnet capacity ceiling. UID trimming lowers the live count toward a smaller
owner-chosen maximum and compresses survivors into contiguous indices
([Glossary: Max Allowed UIDs](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#maxalloweduids),
[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

- **Max allowed UIDs** — configured capacity ceiling for registrations.
- **UID trimming** — owner action removing low emitters to a target count.

Ceiling vocabulary and resize vocabulary answer different capacity questions
([Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)).

## Distinction from Register

UID trimming and register sit on opposite sides of the same participant lifecycle. Register is the
subnet-entry path that assigns a UID after a participant pays the registration burn, while trimming
removes low emitters and forces trimmed participants to enter again through that same path if they
want to return ([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register),
[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming),
[Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration)).

For readers, a trimmed UID does not keep its old slot or historical scores. Documentation states
that the participant must register again, receives a new UID number, and starts with default scores
after compression renumbers the survivors. Register therefore names re-entry after trimming, not the
owner resize action itself
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Glossary: Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission)).

- **UID trimming** — owner-initiated removal and compression of low emitters.
- **Register** — subnet entry that assigns a UID, including re-entry after a trim.

## Distinction from Sudo

UID trimming and sudo name different layers of owner authority. Sudo is the privileged control mode
that lets the subnet owner perform owner-only chain actions, while UID trimming is one specific sudo
operation that resizes the participant set down to a chosen maximum
([Glossary: Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo),
[UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).

For readers, not every sudo action is a trim. Trimming is documented as a manual owner resize with
its own emission ranking rules, immune-UID protections, minimum target count, and rate limit between
passes. Sudo vocabulary names who may act; trimming vocabulary names this particular capacity
reduction on netuid 1
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period)).

- **UID trimming** — the owner sudo action that removes low emitters and compresses survivors.
- **Sudo** — the privileged owner control mode that authorizes trimming and other owner-only
  actions.

## Distinction from Mechanism Count

Mechanism count and UID trimming both touch subnet capacity, but they name different operations.
Mechanism count is the hyperparameter that sets how many incentive mechanisms run, while UID
trimming is the owner action that removes excess UIDs when live registration exceeds the configured
ceiling ([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

For readers, trimming reduces an oversized UID set to fit max allowed UIDs. Mechanism count changes
can require that ceiling to move first because the product of mechanism count and max allowed UIDs
must not exceed 256. Trimming addresses live over-capacity; mechanism count addresses how many
evaluation paths share whatever capacity remains
([Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets)).

- **Mechanism count** — the configured number of incentive mechanisms on a subnet.
- **UID trimming** — the action that brings live UID count back under the configured ceiling.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

UID trimming names the process of reducing active participant slots when subnet hyperparameters
lower the allowed UID count. That capacity-management lifecycle is separate from the tempo mechanism
that converts validator weight submissions into emission shares
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming),
[Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

Trimming can remove slots when limits fall, often using pruning score and immunity-period rules.
Yuma still aggregates submitted weights and credits outcomes for participants who remain on the
netuid at settlement time ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Sudo operations can invoke trimming on a subnet. Consensus settlement is a recurring tempo pass, not
the administrative action that reduces slot capacity
([Glossary: Sudo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#sudo)).

Readers should not treat a trim event as an emission calculation. Credited incentives still follow
the documented weight-aggregation path after included weights are recorded
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **UID trimming** — reducing active slots when UID limits fall.
- **Yuma Consensus** — mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level. It does not report which UIDs exist on any particular
subnet, which would be trimmed, or whether an owner has trimmed recently. Those are live chain state
and should be checked for the relevant netuid at the time of observation. The numbers here, such as
the 64 minimum and the roughly 30-day rate limit, are documented protocol values
([UID Trimming](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/uid-trimming)).
