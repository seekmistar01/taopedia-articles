# Deregistration

Deregistration is the process of removing a Bittensor subnet participant from its UID slot. The
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration) entry
defines it as removing a subnet miner or subnet validator from a subnet due to poor performance.

## UID Slot Context

Bittensor subnets have limited UID slots. The official
[Mining](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration) documentation explains that
deregistration matters when a subnet is full and a new registration arrives. In that case, the
lowest-ranked eligible participant can lose its slot so the new registrant can take that UID.

This makes deregistration a capacity and performance concept. It is not the same as voluntarily
leaving a subnet, changing a wallet, or simply receiving lower rewards.

References:
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)

## Performance Boundary

The mining documentation describes deregistration in terms of low emissions and low weights from
validators. A poor-performing participant is at risk after the subnet's protection rules no longer
apply. The important reader-facing point is that deregistration is tied to subnet ranking and
eligibility, not to a manual editorial judgment about a participant.

The same documentation notes that both miners and validators can be deregistered if their emissions
are low, because both roles occupy UIDs inside a subnet.

Reference:
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)

## Immunity Period

New subnet participants are not immediately exposed to ordinary low-performance removal. The
[Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period)
entry defines an immunity period as a protection window for newly registered participants. The
[Mining](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#immunity-period) page explains that the period
starts when a miner or validator registers into a subnet.

For a Taopedia reader, this sets the boundary: deregistration risk becomes relevant after the
participant is no longer protected by the subnet's immunity period.

References:
[Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period),
[Mining: Immunity period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#immunity-period)

## Pruning Score Ranking

When all UID slots in a subnet are occupied, a new registration can displace an existing
participant. The
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)
documentation identifies the participant with the lowest pruning score as the displacement
candidate. The full selection criteria — including immunity-period exceptions, tie handling, and
eligibility rules — are specified in that reference. The
[Subnet Metagraph](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/metagraph) exposes pruning score as a
per-neuron field readers can connect with that displacement rule.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. For
deregistration, that sequence changes how readers should interpret examples of participant removal.

In local testing, deregistration can show that a subnet's capacity and replacement logic is being
represented inside an isolated environment. That is useful for checking how a UID slot can be
vacated, but it does not show that any live public subnet participant has been removed.

On testchain, deregistration behavior can be observed in a shared testing network. This gives
stronger evidence about how removal interacts with registrations, pruning scores, immunity-period
boundaries, and other subnet participants than a private local run, while still keeping the result
separate from production mainchain activity.

On mainchain, deregistration belongs to live subnet state. The
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)
reference describes the full-subnet replacement context, so production readings should keep the
selected subnet, UID slot, block timing, and eligibility context attached.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A deregistration example in one environment should not be
read as identical evidence about participant removal in another environment.

This distinction matters because deregistration is an outcome of several surrounding conditions, not
a standalone label. Capacity, performance, immunity-period status, and network environment all
affect what a removal example proves.

For readers, this keeps deregistration examples from sounding stronger than their environment
supports. Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all contain deregistration language, but they
do not carry the same interpretive weight.

## Relationship to Register

Deregistration and register refer to related but different parts of subnet participation. The
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
describes deregistration as removing a subnet miner or subnet validator from a subnet due to poor
performance. The [Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)
describes register as the subnet-entry process that purchases a UID slot by paying a dynamic
registration burn in TAO.

For readers, register names the entry process that adds a participant to a UID slot, while
deregistration names the removal of a low-performing participant from a slot when subnet capacity
and replacement rules require it. The two are opposite sides of subnet UID capacity rather than a
single action.

References:
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)

## Relationship to Miner Deregistration

Deregistration and miner deregistration address related but different scopes in Bittensor subnet
vocabulary. The
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
describes removing a subnet miner or subnet validator from a subnet due to poor performance, while
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)
describes that removal process applied to a low-performing miner when a full subnet accepts a new
registration.

For readers, deregistration is the general removal term, while miner deregistration names the
miner-specific case of that process.

References:
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Subnets can run more than one incentive mechanism in parallel. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms) notes
that miner performance in one mechanism does not affect their rating in another.

Deregistration still describes removal of a low-performing subnet participant from a UID slot. When
a subnet uses multiple mechanisms, performance for one mechanism does not by itself describe
standing in another.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)

## Relationship to UID Slot

Deregistration and a UID slot are related but different parts of the subnet participation lifecycle.
The [Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
describes removing a subnet participant from a subnet, while the
[Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot) describes the
participant position inside a subnet that removal can vacate.

For readers, a UID slot names the occupied position, while deregistration names the process that can
remove a participant from that position when subnet capacity and replacement rules require it.

References:
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot)

## Relationship to Netuid

Deregistration and a netuid are related but different parts of Bittensor subnet vocabulary. A netuid
selects which subnet context is in scope, while deregistration describes participant removal inside
that selected subnet when eligibility rules are met. The
[Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid) places netuid at the
subnet level, and the
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
describes removal from a subnet due to poor performance.

For readers, netuid answers which subnet market is being discussed, while deregistration answers
what can happen to a low-performing slot holder inside that subnet when a full subnet accepts a new
registration.

References: [Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)

## Relationship to Immunity Period

Deregistration and an immunity period address related but different parts of subnet removal timing.
The [Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period)
describes temporary protection after registration, while the
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
describes removing a low-performing participant from a subnet when replacement rules apply.

For readers, immunity period names a protection window that can delay ordinary low-performance
removal, while deregistration names the removal process that becomes relevant once that protection
no longer applies or when other eligibility rules are satisfied.

References:
[Glossary: Immunity Period](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#immunity-period),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)

## Relationship to Block

Deregistration and a block are related through subnet timing and displacement context. Subnet
removal rules are evaluated as chain state advances block by block, and immunity-period boundaries
are also measured in blocks. The
[Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block) describes a block as a
unit of chain data containing transactions on Subtensor.

For readers, a block marks chain progression during which subnet participation rules continue to
apply. Deregistration should be read as a subnet outcome tied to capacity, performance, and timing
boundaries rather than as an instantaneous editorial decision.

References: [Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block),
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration)

## Relationship to Subnet Deregistration

Deregistration and subnet deregistration share the same base term but operate at different levels in
Bittensor. Deregistration removes a low-performing miner or validator from their UID slot inside a
subnet when that subnet is full and a new registration arrives, while subnet deregistration removes
an entire non-immune subnet from the network when the subnet limit is reached and a new subnet
registration attempts to enter the network, selecting the subnet with the lowest EMA of alpha price
for removal. The
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration)
defines the participant-level removal, and the
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
describes the network-level subnet removal.

When participant deregistration occurs, one UID slot is vacated and the subnet continues operating
with its remaining participants. When subnet deregistration occurs, every participant registered in
that subnet loses their slot simultaneously because the entire subnet context is removed from the
network. Deregistration ends a participant's position inside a continuing subnet; subnet
deregistration ends the subnet that all those positions existed in.

References:
[Glossary: Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#deregistration),
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)

## Reader Boundary

Deregistration is not a universal punishment rule and should not be read as a live status report for
any miner or validator. It is a subnet registration mechanism that depends on UID capacity,
participant performance, immunity-period state, and a new registration event. Exact current status
or pruning details need current subnet data and official operational references.

References:
[Mining: Miner deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-deregistration),
[Subnet Configuration: ImmunityPeriod](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters#immunityperiod)
