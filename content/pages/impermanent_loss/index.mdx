# Impermanent Loss

Impermanent loss is the loss a liquidity provider can end up with when the price of the pooled
assets moves, measured against simply having held those assets instead. In Bittensor it applies
because each subnet runs as an automated market maker holding TAO and that subnet's alpha, and a
provider's position sits inside that pool while the alpha price moves around
([Understand Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Why Providing Liquidity Carries the Risk

An automated market maker rebalances its pool as the price changes, which steadily leaves a provider
holding more of whichever asset is falling and less of whichever is rising. The documentation notes
that liquidity can be lost to organic price volatility, and this rebalancing is the mechanism behind
that: the provider's mix shifts against them precisely as the market moves
([Understand Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Impermanent Until It Is Realized

The name points to the catch. While a provider stays in the pool the shortfall is only on paper, and
if the alpha price drifts back toward where it started, the gap can shrink or close. It turns into a
real loss when the provider withdraws while prices are still divergent, locking in the less
favorable mix. So the loss is potential rather than certain until exit
([Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## How It Differs from Slippage

Impermanent loss is a provider's exposure to ongoing price drift, which sets it apart from slippage.
Slippage is the execution cost a trader pays on a single swap as their order moves the price,
whereas impermanent loss accrues to whoever is supplying the pool over time. One is a moment's cost
to a trader; the other is a drift risk carried by a provider
([Understand Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## Providers Collect a Share of Swap Fees

The [Glossary: Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions) states that
liquidity providers earn trading fees from swaps routed through the pool. Those fees can offset some
of the drift risk described earlier in this article, because pool activity returns income even while
the provider's token mix changes with price movement.

Fee income and impermanent loss answer different questions. Drift risk comes from how reserves
rebalance over time; fee income comes from traders paying to move the pool through swaps
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For impermanent
loss on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how liquidity-provider drift examples
should be read.

In localnet, liquidity positions on netuid 1 can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet pool
price moves do not represent production provider outcomes.

On testnet, liquidity provision on netuid 1 can be exercised in a shared non-production network
where reserve ratios are separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, impermanent loss on netuid 1 concerns live production subnet pools where providers
supply TAO and alpha. Observed drift depends on that subnet's reserve history and trading activity
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An impermanent-loss example from one environment should
not be read as representing production provider outcomes on another network.

## Impermanent Loss Is Relative to Holding, Not an Absolute Fee

Documentation frames impermanent loss as divergence from simply holding the two assets outside the
pool. A liquidity provider deposits TAO and alpha together, and loss appears when the pool's
changing ratio leaves them with less value than they would have had by holding
([Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

For readers, this is why pool trading activity matters. As swaps move the reserve ratio, the
provider's share of the pool can drift away from the outside-market value of the same tokens
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity Pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools)).

Arbitrage and fee income sit on the other side of that drift. External traders and fee flows can
partially offset impermanent loss, but the term itself names the hold-versus-pool comparison rather
than a fixed charge
([Understanding Subnets: Alpha token price](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#pricerate-of-alpha-tokens)).

## Distinction from Price Protection

Impermanent loss and price protection both concern pool price movement, but from opposite roles.
Impermanent loss is the drift risk a liquidity provider carries while TAO and alpha sit in the pool
and the ratio rebalances against simply holding. Price protection is the slippage tolerance a trader
sets on a single swap; it can bound execution cost for that trade but does not offset a provider's
ongoing pool drift
([Understand Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Impermanent loss** — a provider's ongoing hold-versus-pool drift risk.
- **Price protection** — a trader's per-swap tolerance bounding execution cost.

## Distinction from Liquidity Provider Rewards

Impermanent loss and liquidity provider rewards are the two sides of providing liquidity.
Impermanent loss is the opportunity cost a provider bears when pool prices drift away from simply
holding, while liquidity provider rewards are the fee income earned from trades crossing the
provided range. A provider's net outcome depends on whether the rewards earned outweigh the
impermanent loss incurred
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Impermanent loss** — the hold-versus-pool opportunity cost from price drift.
- **Liquidity provider rewards** — the fee income that can offset that cost.

## Distinction from Alpha Price

Alpha price is the TAO-per-alpha quote read from a subnet pool's reserves. Impermanent loss is the
value drift a liquidity provider can bear compared with simply holding as that quote moves away from
where the provider entered
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Glossary: Alpha Price](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-price)).

- **Alpha price** — the TAO-per-alpha quote read from a subnet pool's reserves.
- **Impermanent loss** — the drift cost a liquidity provider can bear as that quote moves.

[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) explains that
each subnet runs its own pool holding
[TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau) and that subnet's
[alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha). A liquidity provider
therefore takes exposure to price drift inside one subnet market, not to a single shared alpha price
across the whole network.

That per-subnet structure is why impermanent loss is tied to a specific pool. The provider's mix
shifts as that subnet's alpha moves against TAO inside its own reserves, independent of how other
subnet tokens are priced elsewhere
([Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Distinction from Arbitrage

Arbitrage closes a price gap through trading. Impermanent loss names the drift exposure a liquidity
provider bears while capital sits in the pool
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

On a subnet such as netuid 1, a liquidity provider supplies TAO and alpha inside one paired subnet
pool while
[reserve ratio](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools) and
alpha price move through trading, staking, and emission activity
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Impermanent loss names the provider's mix drift relative to simply holding those assets.
[Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) names a trader's one-time execution cost,
swap fees can partially offset provider drift, and
[arbitrage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#pricerate-of-alpha-tokens)
can narrow external price gaps without removing the provider's exposure while they remain in the
pool.

Liquidity documentation notes that deeper pools create opportunities for
[arbitrage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#pricerate-of-alpha-tokens)
when a subnet's on-chain alpha price drifts from broader market pricing. Arbitrageurs buy the
cheaper side and sell the dearer side, which tends to pull the pool price back toward the wider
market ([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

That market-pressure context sits beside impermanent loss rather than replacing it. Arbitrage can
narrow price gaps over time, but a provider still faces mix drift while remaining in the pool during
those moves
([Understanding Subnets: Alpha token price](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#pricerate-of-alpha-tokens)).

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Impermanent loss describes how a liquidity provider's pool share can differ from simply holding the
underlying TAO and alpha tokens when subnet pool prices move. It names LP outcome vocabulary, not
the on-chain step that turns validator weight submissions into emission shares
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Liquidity positions interact with subnet pools, but Yuma
Consensus settles validator weight outcomes rather than LP accounting alone
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep LP outcome vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Impermanent loss
answers how pool price moves affect a provider's share; Yuma Consensus answers how validator weights
become emission shares in a subnet
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Impermanent loss** — LP share outcome gap versus holding tokens outside the pool.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level and is not financial advice. Whether any position is
in loss, and by how much, depends on live prices and on when a provider entered and exits, all of
which change over time. The durable point is the shape of the risk: supplying a pool trades exposure
to price drift for a share of pool activity
([Understand Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).
