# Alpha Price

Alpha price is the TAO-denominated price of a subnet's alpha token in Bittensor's Dynamic TAO pool
model. The official
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
defines a subnet alpha token's ideal price as the subnet's TAO reserve divided by its alpha reserve,
and the official [Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation uses the
same reserve relationship when explaining subnet token price.

## Pool-Derived Price

Each Bittensor subnet has its own liquidity pool context with a TAO reserve and a subnet-specific
alpha reserve. Alpha price is read from those reserves as TAO per alpha. In reader terms, the price
answers: how much TAO does one unit of this subnet's alpha represent in the current reserve
relationship?

That makes alpha price a pool-derived concept. It is not a wallet balance, a fixed protocol
constant, or a price copied from an external exchange. Exact reserve values are current chain state;
the article-level concept is the reserve relationship used to read the price.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Per-Subnet Context

Alpha price is subnet-specific because alpha tokens are subnet-specific. Two subnets can have
different TAO and alpha reserves, so they can also have different alpha prices at the same time.
This is part of the Dynamic TAO model: each subnet market has its own pool-side tokenomics context
rather than a single shared alpha price for the whole network.

For readers, this boundary matters when comparing subnets. A statement about one subnet's alpha
price does not automatically describe another subnet's price, reserves, or staking outcome.

Reference: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## Reserve Ratio Context

Alpha price is closely related to the reserve ratio, but it is the directional price result rather
than just the relationship between two reserves. The reserve relationship compares the subnet's TAO
side and alpha side; alpha price reads that relationship as TAO per unit of alpha.

This distinction helps keep claims precise. A statement about reserves describes the pool state. A
statement about alpha price describes the TAO-denominated quote implied by that pool state.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)

## Staking and Slippage Context

Alpha price is an ideal reserve-based price for the current pool state, not a guarantee of the exact
amount a staking or unstaking action will receive. Staking and unstaking interact with the pool, and
the official [Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) documentation
explains that received amounts can differ from static expectations when converting through subnet
reserves.

For readers, alpha price names the current pool quote, while slippage describes why a real
conversion through the pool can produce a different received amount.

Reference: [Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)

## Emission Injection Context

Emission documentation uses alpha price when describing how subnet reserves grow. When TAO is
injected into a subnet's TAO reserve, alpha is also injected into the alpha reserve in proportion to
the current price so the injection itself can grow the pool without changing that price at the
moment of injection.

This keeps alpha price connected to reserve growth without making it the same thing as participant
rewards. Pool injections, miner rewards, validator dividends, staking outcomes, and subnet-owner
allocations are related tokenomics topics, but alpha price itself is the reserve-derived price of
the subnet alpha token.

Reference:
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)

## Relationship to Alpha Outstanding

Alpha price and alpha outstanding are both part of the subnet alpha token picture, but they name
different quantities. Alpha price is the TAO-denominated quote implied by a subnet's current TAO
reserve and alpha reserve, while alpha outstanding names the total amount of alpha that exists
across all positions in that subnet. The
[Glossary: Alpha Outstanding](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-outstanding)
describes the total alpha in supply for a given subnet.

For readers, a change in alpha outstanding changes the context in which alpha price is evaluated,
but the two numbers answer different questions. Alpha price says what one alpha is worth in TAO
terms given current pool reserves; alpha outstanding says how much alpha exists in total. Knowing
both helps readers distinguish a price claim from a supply claim when reading subnet tokenomics.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Glossary: Alpha Outstanding](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-outstanding)

## Relationship to Alpha Reserve

Alpha price and alpha reserve are related but different parts of subnet pool vocabulary. Alpha
reserve is the pool-side balance of subnet alpha tokens held in the liquidity pool, while alpha
price is the TAO-denominated quote derived from dividing the subnet's TAO reserve by its alpha
reserve. The [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)
documentation defines alpha price as TAO reserve over alpha reserve, positioning the alpha reserve
as the denominator in that calculation.

For readers, the alpha reserve names one of the two pool balances, while alpha price names the
derived price result that comes from comparing TAO reserve to alpha reserve. Changes in the alpha
reserve (through emission injection, staking, or unstaking) affect the alpha price by changing the
reserve relationship.

Reference: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## Relationship to TAO Reserve

Alpha price and TAO reserve are related but different parts of subnet pool vocabulary. TAO reserve
is the pool-side balance of TAO held in a subnet's liquidity pool, while alpha price is the
TAO-denominated quote derived from dividing that TAO reserve by the subnet's alpha reserve. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
defines alpha price as TAO reserve over alpha reserve, positioning the TAO reserve as the numerator
in that calculation.

For readers, the TAO reserve names one of the two pool balances, while alpha price names the derived
price result that comes from comparing TAO reserve to alpha reserve. Changes in the TAO reserve
(through emission injection or pool interactions) affect the alpha price by changing the reserve
relationship.

Reference: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## Relationship to Subnet Deregistration

Alpha price and subnet deregistration are connected through the selection mechanism that removes
eligible subnets from the network when capacity is needed. The
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
explains that when the network's 128-subnet slot limit is reached and a new subnet attempts to
register, the non-immune subnet with the lowest exponential moving average of its alpha price is
deregistered to make room. Not every subnet is immediately eligible: newly registered subnets
receive an immunity period of approximately four months before they can be selected for removal.
This makes alpha price a direct input into the deregistration selection among subnets that have
passed their immunity window.

For readers, alpha price is not just a market indicator in this context—it functions as a selection
signal inside the deregistration mechanism once a subnet's immunity period has elapsed. A subnet's
alpha price EMA integrates the reserve-derived price over time, smoothing momentary fluctuations
while capturing sustained pool state. The deregistration rule then uses that smoothed price to
identify which eligible subnet market is weakest at the moment a new registration attempt triggers
the mechanism. Subnet operators and stakers monitoring deregistration risk should track alpha price
trends rather than point-in-time values, keeping in mind that immunity status also determines
whether a subnet can be selected at all.

References: [Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## Reader Boundary

Alpha price should not be read as a live price feed, a subnet ranking, an execution promise, or a
recommendation about a subnet. It is the concept term for the TAO-denominated price implied by a
subnet's TAO and alpha reserves. Specific values should be checked against current official tools or
reproducible chain data.
