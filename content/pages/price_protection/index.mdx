# Price Protection

Price protection is Bittensor's guardrail for adverse price movement during staking-related
activity. It is most relevant around staking and unstaking through subnet pools
([Glossary: Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#price-protection),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The concept sits between pool mechanics and transaction safety. Slippage explains why a received
amount can differ from an expectation; price protection defines how much adverse movement a
staking-related action is allowed to tolerate.

## Price-Movement Context

Price protection belongs to pool-price movement during staking-related activity. It does not cover
every possible wallet, fee, or transaction risk
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

That scope keeps the term close to slippage. Slippage describes the received-amount difference,
while price protection describes the response when movement crosses a selected boundary.

## Protection Modes

Price protection uses strict, partial, and unsafe modes. Strict protection rejects the action when
movement is outside the allowed tolerance. Partial protection limits execution to the portion that
stays inside tolerance. Unsafe mode proceeds without the guardrail
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

The modes are outcome boundaries rather than interface instructions. The important distinction is
what happens when the selected tolerance is exceeded.

## Tolerance and Outcome Boundary

Price protection uses a selected tolerance to decide whether staking-related price movement is
acceptable; it does not promise a fixed received amount. The price-protection documentation frames
the tolerance as the guardrail, while the slippage documentation explains why pool conversions can
still produce a different received amount
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

That boundary keeps price protection from being read as a quote guarantee. It is a rule for handling
movement beyond the chosen limit, not evidence that the pool price, final amount, or network
conditions stayed unchanged.

## Submission and Execution Boundary

Price protection compares movement from a submission reference to execution. Pool conditions can
change while staking-related activity is pending or being processed, so the final result can differ
from the initial expectation
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

That boundary matters because price protection is not a promise that pool conditions stay fixed. It
defines the response after movement is measured against the selected tolerance.

## Slippage Boundary

Slippage is the difference between expected and received amounts during Bittensor pool conversions.
Price protection is a guardrail around that kind of movement
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

The two concepts are adjacent but not interchangeable. Slippage identifies the conversion effect;
price protection controls whether and how the action continues when the effect exceeds tolerance.

## MEV Shield Boundary

Price protection and MEV Shield address different parts of the same risk area.
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield/) hides sensitive pending details
before block inclusion, while price protection controls how staking-related activity handles adverse
price movement.

One protection does not replace the other. A source-backed claim should say whether it concerns
pending visibility, price movement, fee behavior, or final inclusion.

## Network Context

Bittensor separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet environments. Price-protection examples belong to
the network where the pool movement is observed
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).

Local examples can explain mechanics in isolation. Testnet examples belong to shared non-production
state. Mainnet examples belong to production chain history. Those environments do not share one
pool-state record.

## Evidence Boundary

Price protection is a concept reference, not a calculator or operating guide. It supports claims
about guardrail behavior, but it does not guarantee a desired result, remove pool movement, provide
a fee quote, or replace sources about staking strategy
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

Concrete examples need network, staking direction, pool context, and a source for any numeric
amount. Without that context, a price-protection claim can be too broad for the pool-specific
behavior being described.
