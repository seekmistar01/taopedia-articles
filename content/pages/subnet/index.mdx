# Subnet

A subnet is a specialized market inside Bittensor. The official
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet) defines a Bittensor subnet as
an incentive-based competition market that produces a specific kind of digital commodity. The same
definition places miners and validators inside the subnet: miners produce the commodity, and
validators measure the work.

## Market Scope

A subnet narrows Bittensor's work into a particular market instead of treating all useful work as
one undifferentiated task. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes each subnet as having its own goal and standards, while the
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) places subnets
beside the blockchain and TAO incentive layer as a core platform component.

This makes subnet a root concept for reading Bittensor: the subnet names where a specific kind of
work is requested and evaluated, while the broader network supplies shared protocol and incentive
context.

## Miner and Validator Roles

The miner and validator roles give a subnet its work-and-evaluation structure. Miners produce the
requested digital commodity, and validators measure whether the work meets the subnet's standards.
The official [Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet) and
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) sources both
use this producer-and-evaluator framing.

For a reader, this means a subnet is not only a label for a topic area. It is the setting where
participants compete to produce useful work and where evaluation determines how that work is
interpreted within Bittensor.

## Relationship to the Network

Subnets are part of Bittensor, not separate blockchains. The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction) describes the
platform as including a pool of subnets, the Bittensor blockchain, TAO, and open-source tools. Those
pieces are related but distinct: subnets organize work markets, while the blockchain records network
activity and supports incentive flow.

Keeping that distinction clear helps avoid treating subnet as a synonym for the whole Bittensor
network. A subnet is one market context inside the network.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. That
sequence matters because the same subnet idea can appear in different evidence contexts before it
becomes a production market.

In local testing, a subnet can be treated as a design environment for checking whether its roles,
task, and evaluation logic fit together. This is still useful conceptually, but it does not show
public network participation or live market behavior.

On testchain, a subnet can be exercised in a shared testing network. This gives readers stronger
evidence about how miners, validators, and incentive logic interact than a private local run, while
still keeping the result separate from mainchain production activity.

On mainchain, a subnet is part of the live Bittensor market structure. Its miner production,
validator evaluation, and incentive outcomes are interpreted in the production network context
rather than as isolated development evidence.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, so the environment should stay attached when comparing
subnet behavior. A claim about a subnet on one network is not automatically the same kind of claim
about that subnet on another network.

Because [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)
describes each subnet as having its own goal and standards, the development stage does not replace
the subnet definition. It changes what kind of observation the reader is seeing.

For readers, this keeps subnet descriptions from mixing design-stage observations with production
network claims. Local, testchain, and mainchain contexts can all describe a subnet, but they do not
carry the same interpretive weight.

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

A subnet is usually discussed with one incentive mechanism at a time, but Bittensor also allows more
than one mechanism inside the same subnet. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism) notes that each
subnet has one or more incentive mechanisms.

For readers, a subnet still names one specialized work market, but that market can include more than
one evaluation path. Rules and standards for one mechanism should be read separately rather than
assumed to apply across the whole subnet.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Incentive Mechanism](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentive-mechanism)

## Relationship to Yuma Consensus

A subnet and Yuma Consensus are related but different parts of Bittensor vocabulary. A subnet names
the specialized work market where miners and validators operate, while Yuma Consensus is the
aggregation step that turns the validator weight signals produced inside that subnet into miner
incentives and validator dividends. The
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets) documentation
describes the subnet as the market and role boundary, and
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) describes the clipping,
bonding, and emission calculation applied to the weights gathered within it.

For readers, a subnet names where evaluation happens and who participates, while Yuma Consensus
names the on-chain process that resolves that subnet's validator weights into emission outcomes.

References: [Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)

## Relationship to Crowdloans

A subnet and crowdloans address different aspects of subnet economics in Bittensor. A subnet is the
specialized work market where miners produce and validators evaluate a specific digital commodity,
while a crowdloan is a collective funding mechanism through which TAO holders contribute to help
register or sustain a subnet and receive a share of emissions in return. The
[Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans) documentation describes crowdloans
as enabling community-level participation in subnet funding: contributor shares are recorded on
chain, and emissions are distributed to contributors pro-rata—with the subnet's alpha emissions
swapped to TAO before distribution, so contributors receive TAO rather than alpha tokens directly.

For readers, a subnet names the market context itself—its goal, roles, and incentive structure—while
a crowdloan names a mechanism that allows TAO holders outside the subnet's operator role to fund
that market and participate in its emission outcomes through a TAO-denominated return. A subnet can
exist without active crowdloans, and a crowdloan is specific to a particular subnet rather than the
network as a whole. This distinction helps readers separate a subnet's operational design—its miner
and validator roles—from the community funding paths that may exist around it.

References: [Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)

## Relationship to Subnet Registration

A subnet and subnet registration are related but distinct parts of Bittensor network vocabulary. A
subnet is the operational market that miners and validators participate in once it exists, while
subnet registration is the on-chain creation step that brings a new subnet into existence. The
[Create a Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet) documentation describes
subnet registration as the step where a participant pays the registration burn to assign a new
netuid and open an incentive market, and the
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration) documentation
treats registration and deregistration as the two lifecycle events that move a subnet in and out of
the 128-subnet limit.

For readers, a subnet names the running market — its goal, its participants, its incentive mechanism
— while subnet registration names the act of bringing that market into existence on the chain.
Subnet registration produces the subnet; the subnet then operates until deregistration. A reader who
understands subnet as a work market can use subnet registration to identify the act that created
that market, and vice versa.

References: [Create a Subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet),
[Subnet Deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration)

## Reader Boundary

Subnet should be read as the general concept for a Bittensor work market. It is not a live status
report, a guarantee about any particular market's quality, or a complete description of a subnet's
rules. Specific market behavior depends on the subnet's own goal, standards, and official
documentation.
