# Issuance

Issuance is the circulating amount of TAO in the Bittensor network. It includes directly held TAO
and TAO held in subnet liquidity pools
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The term is useful because it names a supply state, not a reward category. Issuance tells readers
what TAO amount is counted as circulating before more specific emission, halving, recycling, or
burning framing is applied.

That makes issuance a supply-accounting term. It sits beside emissions, halving, recycling, and
burning, but it does not replace any of those more specific token-flow concepts
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

## Supply Record Versus Flow Event

Issuance names a counted supply state. Emission, recycling, burning, and halving describe events or
rules that can change how that supply state is interpreted over time
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

This distinction is useful because the same TAO amount can be discussed from more than one angle.
Issuance is the supply record; the neighboring tokenomics terms explain movement, availability, or
rate changes around that record.

The record-versus-flow split is the main reading aid. Issuance names what is counted as circulating
TAO; emission, recycling, burning, and halving explain how tokenomics processes interact with that
count ([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Circulating TAO

Issuance is a supply measure for TAO. It is broader than only direct holdings, only subnet pool
reserves, or only newly emitted tokens
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The broader circulating-TAO picture matters because Bittensor has both direct TAO holdings and
subnet pool reserves. Issuance names the counted circulating amount, while other tokenomics terms
explain where token movement comes from and where it goes.

This keeps issuance separate from an account-level balance. Issuance is network-level tokenomics
vocabulary, not a statement about one holder, one subnet position, or one transfer.

## Emission and Issuance

Emissions describe token creation and allocation over time. Issuance is the circulating TAO amount
that those processes affect ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

This keeps a statement about circulating TAO separate from a statement about who receives newly
created token amounts. Emission flow changes the supply picture; issuance is the resulting TAO
circulation measure.

Issuance answers how supply is accounted for, while emission answers how protocol rewards are
distributed. The terms point to different parts of tokenomics
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The distinction also keeps issuance from becoming a reward-allocation shortcut. Issuance can frame
how much TAO is circulating, but emissions explain how new rewards enter and move through the
system.

## Halving Trigger

TAO halving rules use issuance milestones. A network-level TAO halving reduces TAO emission after
total TAO issuance reaches the relevant supply milestone
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

That means issuance is part of the halving trigger. It is not a date, countdown, or short-term
timing signal.

The distinction keeps halving and issuance separate. Issuance names the counted supply measure;
halving mechanisms name the emission-rate rules that use supply milestones
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Alpha halving is related but separate. It concerns subnet alpha-token emission rate, while issuance
is the TAO circulating-supply measure used by network-level TAO halving
([Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

For readers, halving uses issuance as an input to an emission-rate rule. Issuance itself is still
the supply-counting term.

## Pool and Reserve Supply

The issuance glossary includes TAO held in subnet liquidity pools. That matters because subnet pool
TAO is still part of the circulating-supply picture, even though it is not the same as a direct
wallet balance ([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

This keeps pool vocabulary and supply vocabulary connected without merging them. A TAO reserve
statement describes pool structure; an issuance statement describes counted TAO supply.

That means pool-held TAO can matter for issuance without making issuance a liquidity-position term.
Pool structure and circulating-supply accounting remain separate readings of related TAO.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For issuance, that
sequence changes how readers should interpret circulating-TAO and halving-threshold examples.

In localnet, issuance accounting can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet supply totals do
not represent production TAO circulation.

On testnet, emission and supply metrics can be exercised in a shared non-production network. Testnet
issuance figures are separate from mainnet chain state.

On mainnet, issuance describes live production circulating TAO across the network. Observed totals
depend on current emission, recycling, and halving history
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. An issuance example from one environment should not be
read as representing production supply totals in another environment.

## Pool-Held TAO Does Not Replace Issuance Totals

Pool and reserve vocabulary describe TAO held in subnet liquidity structures, while issuance names
the network circulating-supply accounting measure
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

That keeps pool structure and circulating-supply accounting as separate readings of related TAO.

## Environment Evidence

Bittensor separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet environments. Issuance examples from one
environment belong to that environment because supply state and testing conditions can differ
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).

Localnet examples are isolated development examples. Testnet examples are shared non-production
examples. Mainnet issuance interpretation concerns production Bittensor tokenomics behavior.

Specific totals need the network, source, and timing that produced them. The issuance concept
travels across environments; measured supply belongs to one environment.

## Both Direct and Pool TAO Count Toward Issuance

The [Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance) entry defines
issuance as the circulating amount of TAO, including both directly held TAO and TAO held in subnet
liquidity pools. That scope is why issuance is broader than a sum of wallet balances alone.

For readers, this means a TAO amount sitting inside a subnet pool reserve is still part of the
circulating-supply measure even though it is not in anyone's spendable wallet. Issuance accounting
spans direct holdings and pool reserves, while emission and recycling explain how token amounts
enter and move between those forms ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Distinction from Emission

Emission names token creation and allocation flow. Issuance names the circulating TAO supply measure
that those flows affect ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

Emission explains how new TAO enters the system; issuance explains how much TAO counts as
circulating at a moment.

## Distinction from Halving

Halving names supply-triggered emission-rate cuts as cumulative TAO issuance crosses documented
thresholds ([Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Issuance is the live circulating measure; halving is the threshold event that lowers future mint
rates as that measure climbs.

## Distinction from Maximum Supply

Maximum supply names the network-wide 21 million TAO ceiling. Issuance names circulating TAO counted
at a moment ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

Readers should not treat current issuance as the maximum supply cap.

## Distinction from Block Reward

Issuance names the circulating amount of TAO counted in the network, including direct holdings and
pool reserves, while block reward names the per-block network-wide TAO mint amount that adds new TAO
toward that cumulative total
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Circulating-supply vocabulary and per-block mint vocabulary answer different questions. Block reward
is the per-block mint path; issuance is the broader accounting measure it feeds.

- **Block reward** — the per-block network-wide TAO mint amount.
- **Issuance** — total circulating TAO including direct holdings and pool reserves.

## Distinction from Recycling and Burning

Recycling returns deducted TAO toward future emission accounting. Burning removes spendable
circulation without the same return path
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)).

Issuance is the supply record those adjustments interact with, not the adjustment rule itself.

Recycling adjusts issuance accounting by allowing deducted amounts to return to future emission.
Recycled tokens are subtracted from the issuance record so the same quantity can be emitted again
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Issuance names the supply record, while recycling describes a supply-flow adjustment that can return
amounts to future emission.

Burning removes tokens from spendable circulation without making that amount available for future
emission under the recycling rule
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Together, recycling and burning show why issuance is not the same as ordinary balance movement.
Recycling can make a deducted amount available again, while burning removes spendable circulation
without the same return mechanism.

This makes issuance the accounting backdrop for both terms. Recycling and burning describe how a
removed amount relates to future emission, while issuance names the supply record those adjustments
affect.

## Distinction from Alpha Halving

Alpha halving is a subnet-scoped alpha emission-rate milestone. Issuance is the network TAO
circulating-supply measure used by TAO halving thresholds
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

Subnet alpha halving progress belongs to a named netuid; issuance belongs to network TAO accounting.

Alpha halving concerns subnet alpha-token emission rate, while issuance names the TAO
circulating-supply measure used by network-level TAO halving
([Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

For readers, halving uses issuance as an input to TAO emission-rate rules. Alpha halving progress on
a subnet tracks that subnet's alpha issuance total instead.

## Distinction from Root Claims

Root claims and issuance describe a dividend-handling mechanism and a supply measure. Root claims
route the alpha dividends earned through Root Subnet staking into either alpha or TAO, while
issuance is the circulating TAO supply counted across the network, so one decides the form a reward
takes and the other counts total tokens in circulation
([Root Claims](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/root-claims),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

- **Root claims** — the rule that routes earned alpha dividends to alpha or TAO.
- **Issuance** — the circulating TAO supply measured network-wide.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Issuance is the circulating amount of TAO in the Bittensor network, including directly held TAO and
TAO in subnet liquidity pools. It names a supply record, not the on-chain step that turns validator
weight submissions into emission shares
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. New emissions add to issuance over time, but Yuma Consensus names
subnet weight settlement rather than the cumulative supply total itself
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep supply-record vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Issuance answers how
much TAO is counted as circulating; Yuma Consensus answers how validator weights become emission
shares in a subnet
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Issuance** — circulating TAO supply across wallets and subnet pools.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

Issuance is high-level Bittensor tokenomics terminology. It is not a reward rule, subnet scoring
concept, or report of exact network totals
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Issuance fits circulating TAO supply. Emission fits creation and allocation flow, halving fits
emission-rate rules, recycling fits return-to-emission accounting, and burning fits removal from
spendable circulation.
