# Liquidity Positions

Liquidity positions are range-based liquidity commitments for Bittensor subnet pools. They supply
liquidity for a selected subnet across a selected range
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

The concept belongs to subnet tokenomics. It is about supplying depth to a TAO-and-alpha pool, not
about mining, validation, or subnet work evaluation.

In plain language, liquidity positions answers who supplies range-based depth to a subnet pool and
under what range condition. It is not a staking position with a validator, a mining role, or a
subnet governance action
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

The distinction from staking matters. Staking attaches TAO or alpha support through a subnet's
delegation model; a liquidity position supplies depth to the subnet pool for conversion use. Both
involve subnet assets, but the role is different.

## Pool Role

Bittensor subnets use liquidity pools with TAO and subnet alpha sides
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools)).

A liquidity position is a contribution inside that pool setting. It adds range-based liquidity to a
subnet pool rather than replacing the pool's reserve structure.

That means liquidity-position vocabulary starts from the same TAO-and-alpha pool described by the
subnet reserve model. TAO reserve and alpha reserve describe the pool sides; liquidity position
describes supplied depth around that pool
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools)).

This makes liquidity positions pool-depth terms rather than reward-role terms. The position belongs
to the pool structure, while mining, validation, and staking describe other subnet roles
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools)).

That pool role also separates liquidity positions from simply holding TAO or alpha. Holding an asset
describes ownership; a liquidity position describes range-based depth supplied to the subnet pool
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Range-Based Supply

Liquidity positions use concentrated liquidity. The liquidity-position reference explains that a
position is supplied over a selected range
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

That makes the term range-sensitive. A liquidity position is not just a generic balance in a pool;
it is liquidity supplied under a range condition.

The range also makes the term different from simply holding TAO or alpha. A position describes
liquidity committed to the pool under the selected range model
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

The selected range is part of the position's meaning. Without the pool and range, the phrase does
not say enough about where the liquidity sits or when it contributes depth.

Range wording therefore belongs with the position itself. A claim about a liquidity position is
clearer when it keeps the subnet pool, supplied depth, and selected range together
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Understanding Subnets: Liquidity pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools)).

## Staking Contrast

Liquidity positions and staking answer different questions. The liquidity-position reference
separates liquidity provision from staking
([Liquidity Positions vs. Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions#liquidity-positions-vs-staking)).

Staking supports validator-related subnet participation. A liquidity position supports pool depth
for TAO-and-alpha subnet liquidity. Both can involve TAO and alpha, but they are different concepts.

This contrast prevents every TAO-and-alpha action from being treated as staking. Staking belongs to
validator support, while liquidity positions belong to pool-depth support inside the subnet
liquidity model
([Liquidity Positions vs. Staking](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions#liquidity-positions-vs-staking)).

## Slippage and Fees

Liquidity positions are related to slippage because both concern available pool depth. Slippage
describes how pool depth affects swap outcomes
([Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

The liquidity-position reference also discusses fees for subnet pool activity inside the selected
range ([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

Slippage and fee behavior depend on pool depth, range, and activity. The concept does not promise an
outcome for any specific subnet, range, or amount of supplied liquidity.

Slippage and fees stay attached to pool depth and range conditions. They do not turn a liquidity
position into a staking position or a subnet quality claim
([Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

## The Feature Is Opt-In at the Subnet Level

Liquidity positions are not a universal feature of every Bittensor subnet. Documentation describes
the feature as something a subnet creator chooses to make available, rather than a mechanism every
subnet pool automatically supports
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

That availability question is therefore a separate reading step from the range, fee, and composition
mechanics described above: those mechanics apply only on a subnet where the feature has been made
available in the first place.

## Coldkey and Hotkey Each Supply a Side

Creating a liquidity position draws from two wallet roles rather than one balance.
Liquidity-position documentation states that TAO are taken from the user's
[coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey), while
[alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) tokens are taken from the
[hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey) on which the position was
created, and both sides are locked in the position
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

That split keeps fund custody and subnet operational stake on separate keys. A holder may supply TAO
from coldkey custody while the paired alpha side comes from hotkey stake already associated with the
[subnet](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet) where the position lives.

The same rule applies when modifying a position. Adding or removing liquidity uses the same token
composition formula as creation, so both keys can be involved again depending on the current subnet
price and selected range
([User Liquidity Positions: Modifying a Position](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions#modifying-a-position)).

## Subnet Price Sets the Token Mix

A liquidity position can hold TAO, alpha, or both at once, and the mix depends on where the subnet
token price sits relative to the position's low and high bounds
([User Liquidity Positions: Dynamic token composition](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions#dynamic-token-composition)).

When price falls below the range, the position becomes entirely alpha. When price rises above the
range, it becomes entirely TAO. When price sits inside the range, the position keeps a mixed
composition of both tokens.

That behavior is not cosmetic. The same price-versus-range relationship decides which tokens are
required when a position is created, which tokens return when liquidity is removed, and how fee
accrual interacts with the position at different life-cycle stages
([Managing User Liquidity Positions Tutorial](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions/managing-liquidity-positions)).

## Fees Require an Active Withdrawal Step

Liquidity-position fees are not paid out on the same
[tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo) cadence as
[emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#emission) rewards. Guidance states
that fees accumulate when others [stake](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#stake)
or unstake within the position's price range, but they are tracked separately from the position's
supplied liquidity and reach the wallet only when the creator modifies or removes the position
([User Liquidity Positions: Fee Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions#fee-distribution)).

That collection rule differs from passive holding. A position can be earning fees while those fees
remain locked until the creator performs one of those withdrawal actions.

The same documentation also notes that a position does not accumulate fees from staking operations
performed by the coldkey that owns it. Fee vocabulary therefore names activity by other participants
inside the defined range, not every stake action tied to the same wallet pair
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Pool Depth, Emissions, and Yuma on netuid 1

On a subnet such as netuid 1, [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
allocates miner and validator [emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) shares
through the incentive mechanism
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Liquidity positions sit on a separate pool-depth path. They supply range-based TAO-and-alpha depth
to the subnet pool for conversion and fee activity rather than replacing validator weights, task
evaluation, or emission allocation
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)).

Pool reserves and liquidity positions therefore belong to subnet tokenomics vocabulary, while Yuma
and emission vocabulary belong to incentive outcomes on the same netuid.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For liquidity
positions on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how observed pool data should be read.

In localnet, liquidity positions on netuid 1 can test range-based supply in isolation. Local pool
examples do not represent production subnet pool behavior.

On testnet, liquidity positions on netuid 1 apply in a shared non-production network where pool
state is separate from mainnet.

On mainnet, liquidity-position interpretation on netuid 1 concerns production subnet pools on the
active network ([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A liquidity-position example from one environment should
not be read as representing production pool behavior in another environment.

## Pool Liquidity and Validator Staking Use Different Entry Paths

Documentation separates adding range liquidity inside a subnet pool from staking TAO behind a
validator hotkey. Staking converts TAO through the pool into an alpha-denominated delegated
position, while liquidity positions commit both TAO and alpha inventory inside chosen price ranges
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Understanding Subnets: Liquidity Pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools)).

For readers, liquidity-position vocabulary names pool-depth commitments and fee exposure, not
validator ranking or weight submission. Staking vocabulary names support behind a hotkey and
dividend participation
([Glossary: Protocol Alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha),
[Glossary: Subnet Validator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-validator)).

Keeping those paths distinct prevents pool-range examples from being read as delegation advice
([Impermanent Loss](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

## Out-of-Range Positions Stop Fee Accrual Until Rebalanced

Liquidity position documentation describes fee accumulation only while subnet price remains inside
the provider's chosen bounds. When price moves outside the range, the position can hold inventory
but stop collecting new fees until liquidity is rebalanced or the range is widened
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Impermanent Loss](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

For readers, that range boundary is part of the reward model. A quiet subnet or an out-of-range band
can show low fee income even while emissions continue through consensus on the same netuid
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Impermanent Loss Sits Opposite Fee Income

Provider documentation pairs trading-fee upside with impermanent-loss exposure while TAO and alpha
inventory remain inside the pool range. Fees reward supported activity; principal composition still
drifts with subnet price until withdrawal or rebalance
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity Pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

For readers, liquidity-position vocabulary should be read as a risk-reward pair rather than as
guaranteed yield. Fee accrual and inventory drift are separate outcomes tied to the same price path
([User Liquidity Positions: Fee Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions#fee-distribution)).

## Distinction from Staking

Staking converts TAO through the pool into a delegated position behind a validator hotkey. Liquidity
positions commit TAO and alpha inventory inside chosen price ranges for pool depth and fees
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

Staking names validator support; liquidity positions name range-based pool supply.

## Distinction from Reserve Ratio

Reserve ratio names the TAO-to-alpha relationship inside a subnet pool. Liquidity positions name
range-based commitments that add depth around that pool
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

Reserve ratio describes paired reserves; liquidity positions describe how a provider supplies depth
within a band.

## Distinction from Slippage

Slippage names the received-amount gap when a conversion moves pool reserves. Liquidity positions
name fee-earning depth commitments inside a chosen range
([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

Slippage is an outcome on a move; a liquidity position is a standing pool commitment.

## Distinction from Impermanent Loss

Impermanent loss names inventory drift as subnet price moves away from a provider's entry point.
Liquidity positions name the range-based commitment that can earn fees while that drift occurs
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

Fee income and impermanent loss are separate outcomes tied to the same price path.

## Distinction from Emission

Emission names subnet reward and injection flow. Liquidity positions name pool-depth supply for
conversions and fee activity ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

Emission distribution and Yuma outcomes are separate from range-based pool supply.

## Distinction from Alpha Reserve

Alpha reserve names pool-held alpha on the paired reserve side. Liquidity positions draw from that
pool context without renaming total subnet alpha supply
([Emission: Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection),
[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

Alpha reserve is pool inventory; a liquidity position is a provider commitment inside that pool.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

User liquidity positions name how participants provide alpha liquidity in subnet pools. That market
vocabulary is separate from the tempo mechanism that converts validator weight submissions into
emission shares ([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

Yuma Consensus credits miner incentives and validator dividends from evaluation signals submitted as
weights. Pool swaps and liquidity fees follow subnet market rules rather than replacing that
documented settlement path ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Staking and liquidity provision can both involve alpha, but they answer different participation
questions. Yuma still settles from validator weights, not from how much alpha moved through a pool
during the interval
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Provider-fee rules also keep self-routed activity from substituting for independent demand. That
design is market-side; Yuma remains the on-chain aggregation step for miner and validator credits
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

- **Liquidity positions** — alpha liquidity provision in subnet pools.
- **Yuma Consensus** — mechanism that turns validator weights into emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

Liquidity positions are range-based liquidity commitments inside subnet pools
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)). They are not an
earnings promise, staking guide, subnet quality signal, or operator procedure.

When the focus is validator support, staking is the more precise concept. When the focus is pool
depth and range-based liquidity, liquidity position is the more precise term.
