# Chain Reads

Chain reads are a Bittensor information-access concept. They describe looking at public state on
Subtensor without asking the chain to record a new action. In plain terms, a chain read is
observation rather than authorization.

The official [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) page says
that reading chain state is free. That statement is the key boundary for Taopedia readers: looking
at public chain information is different from submitting a state-changing action that the chain must
process.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Why the Boundary Matters

The read-versus-submit boundary helps separate ordinary public inspection from actions that need
wallet authority. A person can look at many public facts about the chain without moving funds,
changing stake, registering anything, or changing a subnet participant's on-chain position.

This does not make chain data private or risk-free to interpret. It only means the read itself is
not the same as giving permission for the chain to do something new.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Chain Inspection Context

The official
[Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain)
page frames chain inspection as a way to look at Bittensor chain state, metadata, blocks, and other
public records. It also separates inspection from signing flows.

Chain reads fit inside that inspection side. They are useful when a reader wants to understand what
the chain currently records before deciding whether any separate action is needed.

References:
[Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Fee Context

Because the official fee page treats reading chain state as free, chain reads should not be
described as fee-bearing transactions. Fees belong to the action side of the network when a
submitted operation asks Subtensor to process a change.

This distinction is especially helpful in articles about wallet safety, staking, registration,
transfers, and validator activity. Those topics often involve both observation and action, and the
two should not be collapsed into one category.

Reference: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Network Context

A chain read belongs to the Bittensor network being inspected. The
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) page separates
mainnet, testnet, and localnet contexts. A fact observed on one network should not automatically be
treated as the same fact on another network.

For readers, this means a chain read should be interpreted with its network context in mind. A
mainnet observation, a testnet observation, and a localnet observation can all be read-only, but
they are not interchangeable chain records.

Reference: [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from local testing to testchain and then mainchain. Chain
reads can appear at each stage, but the environment determines what the read can prove.

In local testing, a chain read can show state inside an isolated local blockchain. That is useful
for checking whether a development environment records expected values, but it should not be read as
evidence about public Bittensor network state.

On testchain, a chain read can inspect shared testing-network records. This gives stronger evidence
about public-network behavior than a private local run, while still keeping the result separate from
production mainchain activity.

On mainchain, a chain read belongs to production Bittensor chain context. The inspected network,
query type, account or subnet key, and block context matter because public state can change as new
blocks are produced and new actions are included.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, and the
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) page separates free
reads from submitted state-changing actions. Together, those boundaries keep chain reads from being
overstated.

This distinction matters because a chain read is observation, not authorization. A local, testnet,
or mainnet read can show what is visible in that environment, but it does not sign an extrinsic, pay
a fee, or explain every cause behind the observed value.

For readers, this keeps read-only evidence tied to the stage where it was collected. Local testing,
testchain, and mainchain contexts can all include chain reads, but they do not carry the same
interpretive weight.

## Interpreting Read Results

A chain-read result is evidence about what a selected Bittensor network exposes through public chain
state. It is strongest when the claim being made is also chain-visible: a storage value, account
state, subnet record, block record, or other public datum. The official chain-inspection guide
frames Polkadot.js as a way to inspect chain state, metadata, blocks, and records; that is the
evidence boundary a chain read supports.

The same read does not prove every surrounding claim. It does not prove why a value changed, whether
a later transaction will succeed, or whether a wallet should take a specific action. Those claims
need their own sources. A chain read can answer "what is visible here," but it should not be treated
as a complete explanation of intent, cause, or future outcome.

References:
[Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain),
[Subtensor Storage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/storage)

## Point-in-Time and Network Scope

Chain reads are scoped to the network and point in chain history being inspected. The
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) documentation
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, so a read from one environment should not be generalized
to another. A localnet or testnet observation can be useful for development context, but it is not a
mainnet fact.

The point-in-time boundary matters for the same reason. Public state can change as new blocks are
produced and as submitted actions are included. A read of current state should therefore be read as
current for the inspected context, not as a permanent guarantee. When precision matters, the claim
should identify the network and the relevant block or inspection context rather than speaking as if
every Bittensor environment has the same state.

References: [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain)

## Reference Definitions Versus Queried Values

Official Subtensor references describe what can be inspected; a chain read returns or displays a
particular value from the selected chain context. The distinction is important. A storage reference
can define a storage entry, while a query shows the value for a chosen key, subnet, account, or
network context. A constants reference can define runtime constants, while inspection shows how the
current runtime exposes those definitions.

Events and storage should also be read separately. Events are records emitted around chain activity,
while storage entries describe state that can be queried. A chain read may use both kinds of
evidence, but the reader should not collapse them into the same thing. A claim based on an emitted
event is different from a claim based on a current storage value, even when both are visible through
chain inspection.

References: [Subtensor Storage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/storage),
[Subtensor Events](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/events),
[Subtensor Constants](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/constants)

## Read Evidence Versus Submitted Action

The official fee documentation says reading chain state is free, while state-changing activity
belongs to the transaction side of the network. That distinction keeps chain reads from being
confused with permission to act. Observing a balance, subnet value, event, or metadata entry does
not sign an extrinsic, pay a fee, register a participant, move stake, or change chain state.

This boundary is useful for safety as well as terminology. A reader can inspect public data before
acting, but the later action remains separate and may require signing authority, fees, role
eligibility, or other conditions. Chain reads can inform that later decision; they do not replace
the authority and outcome rules for submitted actions.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Subtensor Extrinsics](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subtensor-api/extrinsics),
[Bittensor CLI: Permissions Guide](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/btcli/btcli-permissions)

## Reader Boundary

Chain reads explain how public observation differs from submitted action. They do not name a
specific wallet app, procedure, or live chain value. Exact values can change as blocks are produced,
as runtime references change, and as the inspected network changes.

## Relationship to Subtensor

Chain reads and Subtensor are related but different parts of Bittensor chain vocabulary. Subtensor
names Bittensor's layer 1 blockchain and system of record, while chain reads name observation of
public state on that chain without asking it to record a new action. The
[Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor) describes the
blockchain layer, and the
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) documentation states
that reading chain state is free.

For readers, Subtensor names the chain layer, while chain reads name passive observation of public
state on that layer.

References: [Glossary: Subtensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subtensor),
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)

## Relationship to Block

Chain reads and a block are related but different parts of Bittensor chain vocabulary. A block names
one unit of chain progression on Subtensor, while chain reads name looking at public chain state
such as blocks and other records without submitting a new action. The
[Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block) describes a block as a
unit of chain data, and the
[Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain)
documentation frames chain inspection as a way to look at blocks and other public records.

For readers, a block is one kind of public chain record, while chain reads name the observation
activity that can inspect such records without changing state.

References: [Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block),
[Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain)

## Relationship to Batch Transactions

Chain reads and batch transactions are related but different parts of Bittensor chain action
vocabulary. Chain reads name observation of public chain state without changing it, while batch
transactions name grouped submission of multiple state-changing actions inside one on-chain
transaction. The [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)
documentation contrasts free chain reads with state-changing actions, and the
[Batch Transactions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/batch-transactions) documentation
describes grouped submission of multiple calls.

For readers, chain reads name passive observation, while batch transactions name grouped submission
of actions that can change chain state.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Batch Transactions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/batch-transactions)

## Relationship to Transaction Fees

Chain reads and transaction fees are related but different parts of Bittensor fee vocabulary.
Transaction fees name the TAO cost of state-changing chain actions, while chain reads name
observation that the official fee documentation treats as free. The
[Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees) documentation states
that reading chain state is free and explains fees for state-changing actions that the runtime must
process.

For readers, transaction fees belong to submitted actions that change state, while chain reads name
observation that does not incur that fee category.

References: [Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Inspecting the Chain with Polkadot.js](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/inspecting-the-chain)
