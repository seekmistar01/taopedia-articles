# Drand Time-Lock Encryption

Drand time-lock encryption helps Bittensor Commit Reveal keep newly submitted validator weights
concealed until a later reveal round. In the commit-then-reveal flow, validators commit weight
information before it becomes readable, reducing the usefulness of copied public weights
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

The term belongs to timing and visibility. It explains when committed validator-weight information
can be read, while validator weights and Yuma Consensus explain how revealed evaluations are used
after the delay
([Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Delayed Visibility

Delayed visibility matters because validator weights are valuable consensus inputs. A validator that
can simply reuse public weight signals adds less independent evaluation to a subnet
([The Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

Commit Reveal reduces that copying window by separating submission from later readability. The
validator commits information first, and the readable weight information appears later in the reveal
part of the process ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

That delay is useful because the valuable signal is the fresh validator evaluation, not a public
copy of another validator's recent weights. Drand time-lock encryption is the timing tool that helps
preserve that delay.

## Drand Round Timing

Time-lock encryption binds access to a later round instead of immediate decryption. Encrypted data
becomes decryptable only after a future Drand round
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

The Drand round supplies the timing reference. Before that round, the committed weight information
stays concealed; after the relevant round, the reveal process can make the information readable.

This makes the phrase more specific than generic encryption. The important feature is delayed
readability tied to a future Drand round, not merely that the data was encrypted.

## No Single Party Controls the Timing Source

Drand is not a service Bittensor or any single organization operates. The glossary describes Drand
as a distributed randomness beacon network run by the League of Entropy, a consortium of independent
organizations that each operate Drand nodes
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

That distributed operation means no single operator decides when a given round's data becomes
decryptable. The reveal timing is tied to a beacon produced by an independent consortium rather than
a clock any one party controls.

## The Encrypted Payload Cannot Be Selectively Revealed Early

Time-lock encryption applies the same way regardless of who created the ciphertext. The glossary
states that data encrypted for a future Drand round cannot be decrypted before that round arrives,
even by the person who encrypted it
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

That property is scoped to the encrypted artifact itself. It means the on-chain ciphertext a
validator commits cannot be decrypted or selectively revealed by anyone, including that validator,
before the target round — a separate question from what the validator privately knows about their
own submission before encrypting it.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Drand
time-lock encryption, that sequence changes how readers should interpret concealed commit payloads
and reveal-round timing examples.

In localnet, time-lock concealment can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet Drand round
references do not represent production commit-reveal behavior.

On testnet, concealed weight submission can be exercised in a shared non-production network. Testnet
reveal timing is separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, Drand time-lock encryption applies as a live timing layer on production subnets using
commit reveal. Observed concealment intervals depend on the selected subnet's reveal settings and
the Drand round schedule ([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A time-lock example from one environment should not be
read as representing production weight concealment in another environment.

## Drand Rounds Supply the Delay Reference

Time-lock encryption binds decryption to a future Drand round rather than immediate access. Before
that round, committed weight information stays concealed; after reveal, the weights can enter the
consensus flow for the relevant epoch
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption),
[Glossary: Tempo](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tempo)).

## Why a Distributed Beacon Rather Than a Local Timer

The concealment guarantee depends on the timing source being outside any single participant's
control. Because Drand is operated by an independent consortium rather than the chain or a
validator, no party can advance the reveal round to decrypt a committed payload early
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

For readers, that is what separates this approach from a self-imposed delay. A validator could
ignore a local timer, but the time-lock ciphertext stays unreadable until the external Drand beacon
reaches the target round, so the concealment that protects fresh
[weight vectors](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector) from copying does
not rely on trusting the committer
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

## Distinction from Weight Copying

Drand time-lock encryption and weight copying name a defense and the problem it addresses. Weight
copying is the problem of a validator reusing public weight signals instead of evaluating
independently, while drand time-lock encryption is the timing tool that conceals committed weights
until a later round to reduce that copying window
([The Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

- **Weight copying** — the problem of reusing public weight signals.
- **Drand time-lock encryption** — the timing tool that conceals weights to reduce it.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Drand time-lock encryption and Yuma Consensus act at different stages. Time-lock encryption controls
when committed weights become readable, while Yuma Consensus aggregates the revealed weights into
miner incentives and validator dividends after the concealment interval ends
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Drand time-lock encryption** — controls when committed weights can be read.
- **Yuma Consensus** — aggregates the revealed weights into emissions.

Committed weights remain encrypted on chain during the concealment interval and therefore do not
enter Yuma Consensus until reveal completes
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

That timing boundary is different from merely hiding scores from observers. Concealed commitments
are excluded from aggregation until they become readable validator-weight inputs.

Drand time-lock encryption controls when concealed weight information becomes readable; it does not
score miners or allocate emissions. After reveal, validator evaluations enter the Yuma Consensus
aggregation process for miner incentives and validator dividends
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

The delayed-visibility layer protects inputs before that aggregation step. Once the weights are
revealed, nearby terms such as validator weights, rank, incentives, and dividends describe the
consensus and emission path.

## Distinction from Commit Reveal

Commit Reveal is the commit-then-reveal weight submission process: validators submit concealed
weights first and reveal readable weight information later
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)). Drand time-lock
encryption is the timing layer inside that process — committed weight ciphertext stays unreadable
until the public Drand beacon reaches the target round, even for the validator who created it
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption),
[Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector)).

- **Commit Reveal** — the two-step submission pattern that separates commit from later reveal.
- **Drand time-lock encryption** — the beacon-timed lock that keeps committed weights concealed
  until reveal.

Put another way, Commit Reveal is the broader commit-then-reveal process, while Drand time-lock
encryption is the delayed-readability tool used inside that process
([Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption)).

## Distinction from Time Lock Encryption

Time-lock encryption names the general idea that ciphertext unlocks only after a scheduled future
time or round. Drand time-lock encryption is Bittensor's form for Commit Reveal: decryption is bound
to a specific future Drand round from the League of Entropy beacon rather than an arbitrary unlock
schedule
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

- **Time-lock encryption** — generic delayed-decryption pattern for any chosen unlock time or round.
- **Drand time-lock encryption** — Bittensor's Drand-beacon form used to conceal validator weights
  until reveal.

## Distinction from Block

A block is the chain's own time unit, a bundle of transactions processed roughly every twelve
seconds, whereas drand time-lock encryption ties readability to an external Drand round rather than
to the chain's block production
([Glossary: Block](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#block)).

- **Drand time-lock encryption** — keys reveal timing to an outside beacon round no chain
  participant advances.
- **Block** — the chain's internal transaction-and-hash unit produced on its own roughly
  twelve-second cadence.

## Distinction from Validator-Miner Bonds

Drand time-lock encryption governs when a validator's committed weights become readable, while
validator-miner bonds record the investment a validator has made in evaluating a specific miner, a
quantity that only forms from weights after they are revealed
([Glossary: Validator-Miner Bonds](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-miner-bonds)).

- **Drand time-lock encryption** — sets when committed weights can first be read.
- **Validator-miner bonds** — track a validator's standing investment in a miner, built from
  already-revealed weights.

## Reader Boundary

Drand time-lock encryption should not be read as a separate consensus algorithm, a miner scoring
method, or an emission allocator. It names a timing and visibility tool that delays readability of
committed validator-weight information until a future Drand round
([Glossary: Drand/time-lock encryption](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#drandtime-lock-encryption),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal)).

The term belongs beside Commit Reveal and weight-copying protection. Validator weights and Yuma
Consensus explain how revealed evaluations are used after the concealment interval ends
([Glossary: Weight Vector](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).
