# Hotkeys (Bittensor)

A hotkey is the operational key in the Bittensor wallet model. It is used by participants to perform
routine on-chain actions for roles like miners and validators
([Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey)).

References: [Wallets (learnbittensor.org)](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets),
[Working with Keys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/working-with-keys),
[BTCLI Reference](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/btcli)

## Responsibilities

- Identify and authorize operational actions by network participants (e.g., register a
  miner/validator, submit evaluation weights).
- Interact with subnet services according to published mechanisms.

## Security model

- Hotkeys are designed to be used on online infrastructure (e.g., validator and miner nodes).
- They should not control funds; if compromised, the coldkey can rotate or revoke authority.

## Operational practices

- Use separate hotkeys for distinct roles or nodes to bound blast radius.
- Monitor activity and rotate keys if suspicious behavior is detected.
- Keep node environments patched and restrict access to hotkey material.

## Tooling

- btcli and the Wallet SDK support generating and managing hotkeys under a wallet.
- Registration and day-to-day actions are performed with the hotkey.

## Hotkey Role Inside a Wallet

A Bittensor wallet pairs one coldkey with one or more hotkeys. The
[Wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation describes the coldkey as the
key that controls TAO and stake, while hotkeys handle operational roles such as mining and
validation.

In the intended wallet model, TAO and stake belong under coldkey authority. The hotkey is part of
the wallet identity, but it is not the key meant for fund custody.

References: [Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey),
[Wallets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)

## Hotkeys Sign Routine Network Actions

The [Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey) describes a hotkey
as the operational key used for day-to-day network actions. Those actions include registering on
subnets and submitting weights as part of miner or validator participation.

Subnet registration and weight submission belong to hotkey vocabulary. They concern subnet
participation rather than coldkey fund movement.

References: [Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey),
[Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)

## Hotkeys Should Not Hold TAO

The wallet model is designed so TAO and stake stay under coldkey authority. The
[Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey) holds funds and
signs financial operations, while the hotkey performs operational roles.

Official wallet guidance treats sending TAO to a hotkey as contrary to the intended design and risky
even when technically possible. Hotkey material is meant for operational signing, not for fund
custody.

The [Glossary: Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-vector) shows a
hotkey-side validator action: submitting evaluation weights that influence subnet incentives. Those
signals use hotkey signing authority without replacing coldkey custody of TAO and stake.

If a hotkey is compromised, the coldkey can rotate it with `btcli wallet swap-hotkey` when custody
practices keep the coldkey protected
([Coldkey and hotkey workstation security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).

That rotation path is different from coldkey compromise, which affects the wallet's fund and stake
authority directly.

References: [Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Coldkey and hotkey workstation security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)

## Relationship to Encrypting the Hotkey

Hotkey and encrypting the hotkey are related but different terms. A hotkey is the operational key
used on online infrastructure to authorize routine work such as registration and weight submission,
while encrypting the hotkey is a password-protection step that secures the hotkey's keyfile at rest
([Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey),
[Glossary: Encrypting the Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#encrypting-the-hotkey)).

For readers, the hotkey names the key and its operational role, while encryption names how that
key's stored file is protected so a copied keyfile is not immediately usable without the unlock
secret. Encryption does not change what the hotkey can authorize once it is unlocked
([Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).

## Relationship to Coldkeys

Hotkeys and coldkeys form a two-tier key model in Bittensor. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey) describes a hotkey as the
operational key for network roles, while
[Coldkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey) hold TAO and stake and sign
financial operations such as staking, unstaking, and transfers.

For readers, a hotkey should not be read as a substitute for a coldkey. A hotkey is safe to run on
internet-connected nodes, but it does not hold funds. The coldkey owns the hotkey and provides the
authority for fund movements; a hotkey compromise does not expose the underlying TAO balance as long
as the coldkey is kept offline.

References: [Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey),
[Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey),
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)

## Relationship to Wallets

Hotkeys and wallets are related but different scopes in Bittensor wallet vocabulary. A wallet is the
overall cryptographic identity formed by coldkey and hotkey pairs used to access TAO and sign
transactions, while a hotkey is the specific operational key within that wallet used for day-to-day
network activities like registering on subnets and submitting weights. The
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation
describes the wallet as containing a coldkey and one or more hotkeys, where the coldkey controls
financial operations and the hotkeys handle operational roles.

For readers, the wallet names the complete identity structure and custody model, while the hotkey
names the operational-level key designed for routine network participation without exposing the
coldkey or TAO balances.

Reference: [Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)

## Relationship to Hotkey Swap

Hotkeys and hotkey swap are related but different parts of Bittensor operational-key vocabulary. A
hotkey is the operational key used by miners, validators, and subnet actors for day-to-day network
activities, while hotkey swap is the rotation procedure that moves subnet participation from a
compromised or aging hotkey to a fresh replacement hotkey owned by the same coldkey. The
[Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)
documentation describes hotkey swap as the response when a hotkey may have leaked, and the
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation
describes hotkeys as the operational keys that a coldkey controls.

For readers, the hotkey is the key material itself used for operational roles, while hotkey swap is
the lifecycle operation that replaces that key when rotation is necessary without changing the
controlling coldkey or requiring the participant to re-register from scratch on subnets.

References:
[Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security),
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)

## Relationship to Multiple Mechanisms

Hotkeys authorize operational actions such as weight submission for miners and validators. The
[Glossary](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix) notes that each
incentive mechanism has its own weight matrix for independent consensus calculations.

For readers, a hotkey still names the operational key for subnet actions. When a subnet runs more
than one incentive mechanism, weight-related actions should be read with that mechanism's matrix
context in mind.

References:
[Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Weight Matrix](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#weight-matrix)

## Relationship to Coldkey Swap

A hotkey's ownership and operational authority derive from the coldkey that controls it. When a
coldkey undergoes a swap, the hotkeys it owns transfer to the new coldkey along with balances and
subnet registrations. The
[Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap) documentation
describes the swap as moving on-chain identity—including any hotkeys the old coldkey controlled—to a
new coldkey key pair. From a hotkey perspective, a coldkey swap changes which coldkey exercises
ownership authority over it without interrupting the hotkey's registered roles or operational state.

For readers, the hotkey is the operational layer, while coldkey swap is a lifecycle operation at the
ownership layer. The swap does not reset or re-register hotkeys; it re-homes their ownership to a
new coldkey while leaving their registered positions intact. This distinction matters when planning
key rotation: rotating a hotkey and swapping a coldkey are separate operations with different
scopes. A hotkey rotation removes and replaces the operational key for a role; a coldkey swap
migrates the controlling identity for all hotkeys and assets that wallet owns. The
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets) documentation covers
how this ownership relationship is structured across the two key types.

References: [Rotate or Swap your Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-swap),
[Wallets, Coldkeys and Hotkeys](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/wallets)

## Relationship to Yuma Consensus

Hotkeys (Bittensor) and Yuma Consensus describe related parts of Bittensor's incentive system. Yuma
Consensus is the on-chain process that aggregates validator weight signals within a subnet into
miner incentives and validator dividends, applying consensus clipping, bonding, and emission
calculation ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

For readers, hotkeys (bittensor) names a specific part of that incentive picture, while Yuma
Consensus names the consensus process that turns validator weights into the resulting incentives and
dividends.

## Reader Boundary

Hotkeys should not be read as fund-custody keys, wallet creation tutorials, or validator
recommendations. The term names operational signing authority under a coldkey for subnet roles and
routine network actions
([Glossary: Hotkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#hotkey)).

## Registration Attaches Subnet Identity to a Hotkey

The [Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register) describes
subnet entry as purchasing a UID slot assigned to a hotkey when it registers as a miner or
validator. Hotkey vocabulary therefore includes the operational identity that occupies a subnet
slot, while coldkey vocabulary remains the ownership anchor for the wallet
([Glossary: UID Slot](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#uid-slot)).

Readers citing a registered miner or validator should name the hotkey that holds the UID slot unless
the prose is explicitly about coldkey fund authority.

## Weight Submission Uses Hotkey Signing

Official validator material describes subnet validators evaluating miners and setting weights that
feed Yuma Consensus ([Validators](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/validators),
[Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights)).
Those weight actions belong to hotkey operational signing rather than to coldkey balance movement.

Hotkey compromise can therefore affect operational subnet behavior even when coldkey-held funds
remain protected under the intended wallet model
([Coldkey and Hotkey Workstation Security](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/keys/coldkey-hotkey-security)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Hotkeys, this
sequence gives readers a boundary for interpreting hotkey examples and operational-role notes.

Localnet examples are isolated and reflect local chain state, so they are useful for controlled
experiments rather than evidence of live Bittensor behavior. Testnet examples add shared
non-production conditions, which can reveal integration behavior without touching mainnet state.

On mainnet, Hotkeys examples should be read as live production hotkey behavior on the production
Bittensor network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, so outcomes from one environment should not be treated as
proof of behavior in another.
