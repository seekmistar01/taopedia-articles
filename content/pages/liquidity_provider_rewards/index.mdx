# Liquidity Provider Rewards

Liquidity provider rewards are the trading fees a provider earns for supplying liquidity to a
subnet's pool. Bittensor's liquidity positions are based on a concentrated-liquidity model, and the
documentation describes positions accumulating fees from the staking and unstaking activity that
flows through them
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Earning Fees from Activity

The reward is a share of the trading a provider's liquidity makes possible. A position accumulates
fees as users stake and unstake within the price range the position covers, and the provider can
later withdraw those accumulated fees into their wallet. Supplying the pool turns other people's
trading into income for the provider.

## The Price Range Matters

These are concentrated positions, so the range a provider chooses shapes what it earns. A position
collects fees from activity that falls inside its range, and a position whose range the current
price has left simply holds its assets without taking in new fees. Choosing where to place liquidity
is therefore part of what determines the reward, not just how much is supplied.

## Not From Your Own Trades

A documented limitation shapes who the reward comes from. A position does not accumulate fees for
staking operations performed by the coldkey that owns it. A provider therefore earns from other
users' activity, not by trading against its own liquidity, which closes an obvious loophole where
someone might try to pay fees to themselves.

## Reward Set Against Risk

Fees are the upside of providing liquidity, and they sit opposite the downside described as
impermanent loss, the exposure to price drift a provider carries while in the pool. The decision to
provide liquidity weighs expected fee income against that drift risk, so rewards are best understood
next to the risk rather than on their own
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For liquidity
provider rewards on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret
fee income and pool-activity examples.

In localnet, liquidity positions can be tested in an isolated environment. Localnet swap fees do not
represent production provider returns.

On testnet, liquidity provision can be exercised in a shared non-production network. Testnet fee
accumulation is separate from mainnet subnet state.

On mainnet, liquidity provider rewards concern live production subnet pools. Observed fees depend on
the selected subnet's trading activity and the provider's price range
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A liquidity-reward example from one environment should not
be read as representing production provider returns in another environment.

## Staking Conversions Drive Much of the Fee Flow

[Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage) describes staking and
unstaking as conversions that pass through subnet pool reserves. Positions accumulate fees when that
activity falls inside the provider's range, so reward size depends on how much delegation traffic
moves through the supplied liquidity.

Swaps are not the only source of pool movement. Staking and unstaking use the same reserve pair, and
concentrated positions collect fees only from activity that crosses their chosen bounds.

## Reserve Ratio Shapes the Pool Context Fees Move Through

[Reserve ratio](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#reserve-ratio) vocabulary
describes how TAO and alpha reserves relate inside the subnet pool where liquidity positions sit.
Provider rewards come from activity passing through that paired reserve structure, not from the
reserve ratio itself
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Liquidity Fees Are Separate From Emission Payouts

Subnet [incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives) flow to miners
and validators through [Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)
ranking and tempo distribution. Liquidity provider rewards instead come from trading fees collected
inside a supplied price range. Fee income and emission shares follow different documented paths
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

A provider can earn from pool activity without receiving miner or validator emission allocations.

## Accumulated Fees Stay in the Position Until Withdrawn

[User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions) describes fees
building up inside a position as activity passes through it. A provider later withdraws those
accumulated fees to their wallet rather than receiving them as automatic emission credits.

The reward is a balance accrued inside the position from supported trading and staking activity,
distinct from consensus dividends paid through subnet emission
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).

## Range Width Changes Fee Exposure Without Changing Emission Eligibility

Liquidity position documentation describes fees as accruing only when activity crosses the
provider's chosen price bounds. A narrower range can concentrate fee capture when price stays inside
the band, but it also increases the chance that price moves outside the range and stops fee accrual
until rebalanced ([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions),
[Impermanent Loss](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

For readers, liquidity provider rewards therefore depend on live pool activity and range placement,
not on validator weights or miner rank. Emission vocabulary remains on the consensus path even when
the same subnet pool also supports staking conversions
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Fee Income Sits Opposite Impermanent-Loss Exposure

Provider documentation pairs trading-fee upside with impermanent-loss downside while inventory
remains inside the pool. Fees accumulate from supported activity; principal TAO and alpha in the
range still move with pool price until withdrawn or rebalanced
([Understanding Subnets: Liquidity Pools](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets#liquidity-pools),
[Impermanent Loss](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

For readers, that keeps reward vocabulary distinct from guarantee language. A quiet pool or an
out-of- range position can show low fee accrual even when subnet emissions continue through
consensus ([Glossary: Incentives](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#incentives),
[Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

Accumulated fees stay inside the position until an explicit withdrawal step rather than arriving as
automatic tempo credits
([User Liquidity Positions: Fee Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions#fee-distribution)).

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level and is not financial advice. What a position actually
earns depends on live trading activity, the chosen range, and prices at the time of observation. The
durable point is the shape of the reward: a provider is paid in fees out of the activity its
liquidity supports
([User Liquidity Positions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/liquidity-positions)).
