# Weight Copying

Weight copying is a consensus-quality problem where a validator relies on another validator's weight
information instead of independently evaluating miners. The
[Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)
describes this as free-riding on validator work, while
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) explains why validator
weights matter for miner incentives and validator dividends.

The term is about the source of a weight signal. Agreement between validators is not the issue by
itself; the concern is dependence on copied information rather than independent subnet evaluation.

## Independent Evaluation

Independent validator evaluation gives a subnet multiple assessments of miner performance. When a
validator copies another validator's weights, the copied signal can look like another assessment
without adding a new evaluation of the miners
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

That weakens the evaluation layer that downstream consensus receives. The article-level boundary is
therefore evaluation independence, not whether two validators happen to produce similar rankings.

## Consensus Impact

Weight copying matters because validator weights are inputs to consensus. In
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus), validator rankings of miner
performance are aggregated into incentive and dividend outcomes. Copied weights can make the set of
inputs less independent than it appears.

This places weight copying in consensus vocabulary rather than only validator-behavior vocabulary.
The risk is not just that a validator avoids work; it is that copied signals can reduce the quality
of the information available to consensus.

## Delayed Visibility

Delayed visibility is one defense against copied weights. The
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal) mechanism separates the
commitment to weights from later visibility, which reduces the value of copying recently visible
weight information. The weight-copying reference places commit reveal in that mitigation context
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)).

The useful idea is timing. If a validator cannot immediately see and reuse another validator's
weight information, copied weights become less useful as a substitute for independent scoring.

## Consensus-Based Weights

Consensus-based weights, also described through liquid alpha context, are another related defense.
The [Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights)
reference describes bond adjustment around consensus alignment, while the weight-copying reference
places this mechanism beside delayed visibility as a response to copied or stale weights.

Delayed visibility and consensus-based weights address different parts of the same problem. One
limits the usefulness of copying fresh weight information; the other changes how aligned or stale
signals affect validator bond outcomes.

## Evidence Boundary

Similar weight patterns do not automatically prove copying. The weight-copying problem concerns
dependence on copied or derived weights, while Yuma Consensus still allows honest validators to
converge when they evaluate miner performance similarly
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

That boundary keeps interpretation precise. Weight copying is a claim about lack of independent
evaluation, not a general label for every similar-looking validator score.

## Weight-Signal Boundary

Weight copying should also stay separate from the weight signal itself. Validator weights are the
signals submitted for consensus, while weight copying names a failure mode in how those signals may
be produced
([Glossary: Validator Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-weights)).

This distinction helps readers separate a normal consensus input from a risk affecting the quality
of that input.

## Reader Boundary

Weight copying is the right term when the focus is validator dependence on copied weight information
([Weight Copying Problem](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/weight-copying-in-bittensor)). If
the focus is aggregation, Yuma Consensus is more precise. If the focus is delayed visibility, Commit
Reveal is more precise. If the focus is bond adjustment around consensus alignment, Consensus-based
Weights is more precise ([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Commit Reveal](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/commit-reveal),
[Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights)).

For Taopedia readers, the stable concept is that weight copying weakens independent evaluation by
turning copied validator weight information into a substitute for subnet-specific miner scoring.
