InfiniteHash is Bittensor Subnet 89. Its on-chain description is "BTC Pool + Lightning Network." The
[InfiniteHash README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/backend-developers-ltd/InfiniteHash/master/README.md)
describes a subnet that combines Bitcoin mining-pool participation with Lightning Network
infrastructure.

## What InfiniteHash Provides

InfiniteHash runs a shared Bitcoin mining pool on Bittensor. Participants dedicate Bitcoin mining
hardware to the pool and receive subnet rewards in proportion to the hashrate they contribute. The
subnet pairs this mining layer with Lightning Network participation: miners can also operate
Lightning nodes and earn additional rewards based on the quality of that service, building a payment
layer alongside the mining operation.

## Auction Context

The
[auction incentive documentation](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/backend-developers-ltd/InfiniteHash/master/docs/subnet_auction_incentive_system.md)
describes a repeated auction mechanism for allocating subnet emissions to hashrate delivery. Miners
publish commitments that represent offered hashrate and price. Validators calculate how much
hashrate the current subnet budget can purchase, select winning commitments, and later publish
weights from the delivered results.

This gives the subnet a clearer economic loop than a simple participation list. A miner is not
rewarded merely for being registered; the useful work is priced as Bitcoin mining capacity and then
checked against what was actually delivered. The auction frame also lets the available budget adjust
with subnet emissions and market price inputs, so reward allocation can respond to changing mining
conditions.

## Delivery Verification Context

InfiniteHash's source describes delivery verification as a separate step after auction selection.
For each winning hotkey, validators compare the committed hashrate with observed pool snapshots for
the relevant window. Delivered winners can be recorded for weight publication, while miners that
fail delivery can be excluded from later auctions until the ban condition clears.

That distinction matters for readers because Bitcoin mining has an external measurable output:
hashrate delivered to the pool. The subnet's scoring problem is therefore not only "who bid for
hashrate," but "who delivered the committed mining capacity after the auction." Pairing bids with
delivery checks helps keep subnet rewards tied to real mining contribution.

## Pool Work and Auction Settlement Boundary

The
[InfiniteHash README](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/backend-developers-ltd/InfiniteHash/master/README.md)
frames the subnet around Bitcoin mining-pool participation and Lightning Network infrastructure.
That is the broad work surface: miners contribute hashrate to the pool, and the subnet can also
consider Lightning service quality. The auction documentation narrows one part of that surface by
describing how hashrate commitments become reward evidence.

The
[auction incentive documentation](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/backend-developers-ltd/InfiniteHash/master/docs/subnet_auction_incentive_system.md)
describes a repeated auction flow where miners offer hashrate capacity and validators later compare
that offer with observed delivery. A winning commitment is therefore provisional. It becomes
rewardable only when the delivered mining capacity matches the work the miner offered.

This boundary is useful because Bitcoin mining has an observable output outside the article prose:
hashrate delivered to the pool. Validators do not have to treat a miner's description as enough on
its own. They can compare the promised capacity with pool-side evidence of what was actually
delivered.

The same boundary keeps the mining-pool role separate from the Lightning-service role. Both belong
to the broad InfiniteHash subnet description, but hashrate settlement is the part where auction
commitments and pool delivery are paired. Lightning service quality is adjacent infrastructure, not
the same evidence path as delivered mining capacity.

For readers, Subnet 89 is best read as a mining-capacity market with delivery checks. The pool and
Lightning framing describe what the subnet supports, while the auction-and-delivery flow describes
how hashrate contribution becomes rewardable evidence.

## Miner and Validator Roles

Miners contribute by pointing Bitcoin mining hardware at the subnet's pool and linking their
Bittensor hotkey to their pool worker. Miners who also operate Lightning nodes can earn on top of
their hashrate contribution.

Validators track the hashrate each miner delivers to the pool and assess Lightning service quality
where applicable, then translate those measurements into weights. Reward distribution follows
[[Yuma Consensus]].
