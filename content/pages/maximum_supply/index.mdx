# Maximum Supply

Maximum supply is the fixed ceiling on how much TAO can ever exist.
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving) documentation states that TAO
has a hard cap of 21 million, similar to Bitcoin's maximum supply. Issuance can approach this
ceiling over time but cannot exceed it.

## The 21 Million Cap

The cap is a protocol-level maximum: across the network's whole lifetime, the total amount of TAO is
bounded at 21 million. This is the total ceiling, which is different from how much TAO is in
circulation at any given moment. The circulating amount changes continuously, while the cap stays
fixed ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

## Halving by Supply, Not Blocks

Bittensor approaches the cap through halvings, but the documentation notes a key difference from
Bitcoin: rather than halving on block numbers, Bittensor halves the emission rate when total
issuance reaches supply thresholds. Each time a threshold is crossed, the rate at which new TAO is
created is cut in half, so issuance slows as the total climbs toward 21 million
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Why the Cap Matters

A fixed ceiling makes TAO's long-run total predictable even though the pace of issuance is dynamic.
Readers can treat the 21 million figure as the upper bound on TAO that will ever exist, while
treating the timing of how quickly issuance approaches it as variable and dependent on network
activity rather than a fixed calendar
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

## Block Reward Minting Feeds the Supply Schedule

[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes the block reward
as the amount of new [TAO](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#tao-tau) created each
block for the whole network. [Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)
tie reductions in that per-block rate to total issuance crossing documented supply thresholds on the
way toward the 21 million ceiling.

Maximum supply therefore names the lifetime cap, while block reward and halving vocabulary name the
per-block mint path that approaches it. Readers should not treat the cap as if it were the same
label as the current per-block issuance rate.

## Transaction Fees Also Reduce Tracked Issuance

Registration recycling is not the only flow that pushes back the next halving threshold. Fee
documentation states that paid base transaction fees are deducted from `TotalIssuance`, the same
issuance accounting that halving thresholds measure against
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees)).

That means two distinct activities slow the measured approach to the cap: registration burns recycle
TAO out of circulation, and ordinary fee-bearing transactions deduct their paid fees from the same
tracked total. Both reduce the issuance figure that supply thresholds are measured against, separate
from how quickly new TAO is minted.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. Maximum supply
names the documented 21 million TAO ceiling for the whole network, not a per-subnet alpha limit
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

In localnet or testnet, issuance examples can illustrate how halving thresholds work without
reporting live mainnet supply.

On mainnet, circulating supply, recycling, and the next threshold crossing are live values read from
current chain state ([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates environments. A supply example from one network should not be read as mainnet issuance
history.

## Distinction from Circulating Supply

Maximum supply and circulating supply both count TAO but answer different questions. Maximum supply
is the fixed 21 million lifetime ceiling that issuance can approach but never exceed, while
circulating supply is the amount of TAO actually in circulation at a given moment, a figure that
rises with emission and falls as registration burns and transaction fees recycle TAO out of the
tracked total, so the cap stays constant while circulating supply moves continuously
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Maximum supply** — the fixed 21 million lifetime ceiling on total TAO.
- **Circulating supply** — the live amount in circulation, changing with issuance and recycling.

## Distinction from Halving

Maximum supply names the protocol's lifetime ceiling on total TAO issuance, while halving names the
scheduled emission-rate reduction that triggers when cumulative issuance crosses supply thresholds
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving)).

Lifetime-cap vocabulary and rate-cut vocabulary answer different questions. Each halving reduces
future issuance rates rather than erasing balances already held
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Maximum supply** — the fixed 21 million lifetime ceiling on total TAO.
- **Halving** — the rate-cut event when cumulative issuance crosses supply thresholds.

## Distinction from Block Reward

Maximum supply names the protocol's lifetime ceiling on total TAO issuance, while block reward names
the amount of new TAO minted each block for the whole network as issuance moves toward that ceiling
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Lifetime-cap vocabulary and per-block mint vocabulary answer different questions. Halvings cut the
block reward at supply thresholds as cumulative issuance climbs toward the documented TAO cap
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

- **Maximum supply** — the total TAO that can ever exist.
- **Block reward** — the per-block mint rate that approaches that ceiling.

## Distinction from Recycling

Maximum supply names the fixed 21 million TAO lifetime ceiling. Recycling returns deducted TAO
toward the emission pool, which can delay when cumulative issuance crosses the supply thresholds
that trigger halvings without changing the cap itself
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Maximum supply** — fixed lifetime ceiling on total TAO issuance.
- **Recycling** — reuse path that can slow the approach toward that ceiling.

## Distinction from Alpha Tokens

[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation describes a two-level
model in which network-level TAO flow supports reserve injection while each subnet also emits its
own [alpha](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#protocol-alpha) for role-facing
rewards ([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

The 21 million TAO cap bounds network-wide TAO issuance rather than every subnet alpha balance a
participant may receive inside subnet economics.

- **Maximum supply** — fixed ceiling on total network TAO.
- **Alpha tokens** — per-subnet emission track separate from the TAO cap.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Maximum supply is the documented lifetime ceiling on total TAO that can ever exist in Bittensor. It
names a supply cap, not the on-chain step that turns validator weight submissions into emission
shares
([Glossary: Maximum Supply](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#maximum-supply),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Yuma Consensus is the on-chain process that converts submitted validator weights into emission
shares after evaluation completes. Emissions move issuance toward the maximum supply over time, but
Yuma Consensus performs weight aggregation and settlement rather than setting the cap itself
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Readers should keep lifetime-cap vocabulary separate from the settlement step. Maximum supply
answers the TAO ceiling issuance approaches; Yuma Consensus answers how validator weights become
emission shares in a subnet
([Glossary: Maximum Supply](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#maximum-supply),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Maximum supply** — lifetime TAO ceiling for cumulative issuance.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain weight aggregation that settles emissions.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level. It does not report the current circulating supply,
the date of the next halving, or current recycling rates, which are live chain state and change over
time. The 21 million figure is the fixed protocol maximum; live figures should be read from current
Bittensor sources ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).
