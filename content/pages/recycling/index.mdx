# Recycling

Recycling describes token reuse or re-emission in Bittensor supply accounting. It applies to token
flows that can return to future protocol distribution instead of being permanently removed from
spendable circulation.

## Tokenomics Role

Recycling is a supply-flow term, not ordinary payment language. It describes what happens after a
token amount enters protocol accounting. The key question is whether that amount can remain
available for later distribution.

That makes recycling different from spending, receiving, or transferring. A transfer changes the
holder of tokens. Recycling describes the supply-accounting status of a deducted amount after it
enters a protocol flow.

Burn cost is a narrower, related label rather than a synonym: it names the specific TAO amount
recycled when creating a subnet, not the general recycling concept this page describes
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

## Fee Flow

Transaction fees can sit near recycling language because fees are token flows attached to
state-changing actions. The fee names the cost side of the action. Recycling describes how a
deducted amount can remain available to future protocol accounting after collection.

This separation keeps fee prose precise. A fee-bearing action explains why an amount was paid;
recycling explains the supply-accounting treatment of the collected amount.

## Emission Return

Emission is the creation and allocation side of Bittensor tokenomics. Recycling connects to emission
because a deducted amount can be made available to the future emission process instead of
disappearing from the emission picture.

The term still has a narrow role. Recycling does not identify a future recipient or explain the
reward calculation. It says that the amount remains available to protocol distribution instead of
being treated as permanently removed.

## Development Stage Context

Bittensor separates localnet, testnet, and mainnet environments. Recycling examples from one
environment belong to that environment because supply state and testing conditions can differ
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks),
[Introduction to Bittensor: Subnet development](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)).

Localnet examples are isolated development examples. Testnet examples are shared non-production
examples. Mainnet examples concern production tokenomics behavior.

Specific totals need the network, source, and timing that produced them. The concept of recycling
travels across environments; measured supply state belongs to one environment.

## Registration Burn Recycles Through Dynamic Bounds

A neuron registration pays a burn in TAO to claim a UID slot, and that deducted amount is the kind
of flow recycling describes: it leaves the registrant and returns toward issuance for future
emission rather than moving to another holder. The
[Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register) entry describes
that registration burn as dynamic, bounded by MinBurn and MaxBurn and decaying over time under
BurnHalfLife.

That makes the recycled amount a moving figure rather than a fixed fee. Recycling names the
supply-accounting treatment of the burn, while the register bounds explain why the size of each
recycled registration cost shifts with demand and time
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

## Recycling and Burning Also Apply to Subnet Alpha

Recycling vocabulary is not limited to TAO. The
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)
entry states that both TAO and subnet-specific alpha can be removed from circulation, meaning the
tokens sit in neither a wallet nor a liquidity pool and cannot be transacted.

For readers, that extends the same supply-flow question to the subnet layer. A removed alpha amount
is recycling when protocol accounting can reissue it and burning when it cannot, the same outcome
split that applies to TAO
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning)).

## Issuance Deduction Precedes Future Emission Return

Recycled tokens are deducted from Bittensor's issuance record so the same quantity can be emitted
again later. Issuance names the broader supply record, while recycling names the return path for a
deducted amount
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

For readers, recycling keeps deducted TAO inside protocol accounting rather than treating the amount
as permanently removed from circulation.

## Burning and Recycling Name Different Outcomes

Recycling and burning are paired because both describe token flows after a deduction. Recycling
points to reuse or later re-emission, while burning points to removal from spendable circulation
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

The split is about the supply outcome, not the wording of an event: the same deducted amount is
recycling when issuance can emit it again and burning when it cannot
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Recycling Affects Halving Timing, Not the Cap

Because halvings are triggered when cumulative issuance crosses supply thresholds rather than on a
fixed schedule, recycling changes the timing of those crossings. Recycled TAO is removed from
circulation and returned toward the emission pool, which pushes back the next supply threshold and
therefore extends the time until the next halving
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

For readers, this keeps recycling distinct from the supply ceiling itself. More recycling slows how
quickly issuance approaches documented thresholds, but it does not raise or lower the total TAO that
can ever exist; it only makes the approach dynamic rather than calendar-based.

## Distinction from Burning

Burning permanently removes tokens from spendable circulation. Recycling returns a deducted amount
toward future protocol emission instead
([Glossary: Burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burning),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

- **Burning** — no return path to spendable circulation.
- **Recycling** — deducted amount can be emitted again later.

## Distinction from Issuance

Issuance tracks cumulative TAO created over time. Recycling adjusts that ledger when a deducted
amount is made available to be emitted again rather than treated as permanently gone
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Issuance names the supply record. Recycling names the reuse path for one deducted flow
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Distinction from Transaction Fees

A transaction fee is the cost of submitting an extrinsic. Recycling describes what happens to the
collected fee amount in protocol supply accounting after deduction
([Transaction Fees in Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/fees),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

Fee prose answers why an amount was paid. Recycling prose answers whether that deducted amount can
return toward future emission.

## Distinction from Registration Allowed

Registration allowed is the per-subnet switch that decides whether new neurons may register.
Recycling describes the supply treatment of TAO deducted when a registration burn does occur
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

When registration allowed is off for a subnet, no registration burn can occur on that subnet.
Recycling vocabulary still belongs when explaining what happens to a burn that did occur while
registration was open
([Glossary: Register](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#register)).

## Distinction from Halving

Halving cuts the per-block mint rate at documented supply thresholds. Recycling changes how quickly
issuance reaches those thresholds by returning deducted TAO toward the emission pool
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

More recycling can delay the next halving crossing. Halving vocabulary names the rate cut at the
crossing itself, not the reuse path for one deducted amount
([Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving)).

## Distinction from Burn Cost

Burn cost is the TAO a participant pays to register a neuron and claim a UID. Recycling is the
supply treatment that can return that registration payment toward future emission rather than
destroying it ([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling)).

Burn-cost vocabulary names the payment size at registration time. Recycling vocabulary names what
happens to that payment in protocol accounting after the burn is recorded
([Subnet Hyperparameters](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-hyperparameters)).

## Distinction from Miner Registration

Recycling and miner registration describe the cost mechanism and the subnet-entry result. Recycling
is the mechanism by which the TAO paid to register is burned out of circulation, while miner
registration is the process that gives a miner a UID slot to take part in a subnet
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Mining: Miner registration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/miners#miner-registration)).

## Distinction from Block Reward

Recycling and block reward describe returned supply and new issuance. Recycling describes TAO
returned to the protocol's supply path, while block reward is the amount of new TAO the network
mints each block before any split across subnets
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

Recycled TAO re-entering issuance can change how quickly the network reaches the next halving
threshold that steps the block reward down, so recycling names a return-to-supply movement while
block reward names the per-block mint rate that adds new TAO toward cumulative issuance
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

## Distinction from Maximum Supply

Recycling and maximum supply are related but different tokenomics terms. Recycling names the
protocol reuse of deducted TAO, such as registration cost returned toward the emission pool, while
maximum supply names the protocol's 21 million TAO lifetime ceiling
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Glossary: Maximum Supply](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#maximum-supply),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

- **Maximum supply** — the fixed lifetime TAO ceiling issuance moves toward.
- **Recycling** — a supply-flow adjustment that changes how quickly issuance approaches that
  ceiling.

Recycling does not raise or lower the maximum-supply cap. It changes how quickly issuance approaches
the ceiling, while maximum supply still bounds the total TAO that will ever exist
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Reader Boundary

Recycling is tokenomics vocabulary, not market advice, transaction guidance, or a report of exact
network totals. Its stable meaning is that a deducted token amount remains available for future
protocol accounting rather than being treated as permanently removed
([Glossary: Recycling](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That reading keeps recycling separate from broader supply statements. It can explain a protocol-side
outcome, but it does not predict emissions, set valuation expectations, or report a live supply
number.
