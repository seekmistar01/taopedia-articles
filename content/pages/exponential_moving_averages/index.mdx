# Exponential Moving Averages

An exponential moving average, or EMA, blends a new measurement with a running value from earlier
steps. In Bittensor, EMAs help smooth signals before they affect emissions, validator-miner bonds,
and consensus incentives ([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)).

The concept matters because Bittensor signals can move abruptly. EMA smoothing lets a signal react
over time without treating every short movement as final
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Smoothing Method

EMA smoothing gives newer measurements more influence while keeping part of the earlier running
value. A larger smoothing factor reacts faster, while a smaller factor changes more slowly
([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema),
[Glossary: Exponential Moving Average](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#exponential-moving-average-ema)).

This makes EMAs useful when a Bittensor process should respond to sustained movement rather than
brief noise.

## Subnet Flow Context

Bittensor uses EMA-smoothed net TAO flow when comparing subnet-level emission allocation. Smoothing
keeps a short staking or unstaking burst from dominating the flow signal
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Glossary: Subnet Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-weights)).

In this context, EMA names the smoothing method. The emission documentation supplies the allocation
meaning.

## Bond Context

EMAs also appear in validator-miner bond smoothing. A bond should evolve from repeated evaluation
signals rather than jump entirely from one measurement
([Glossary: Validator-Miner Bonds](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#validator-miner-bonds),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

That places EMA vocabulary inside the bond path. Validator weights are evaluation inputs, while the
EMA helps smooth the bond value that develops from those inputs.

## Liquid Alpha Context

Liquid alpha changes the EMA smoothing factor for validator-miner bonds according to consensus
alignment. It is a specific Bittensor use of EMA behavior, not a separate smoothing method
([Consensus-based Weights](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/consensus-based-weights),
[Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema)).

This distinction keeps the vocabulary precise. EMA names the general smoothing technique; liquid
alpha names a Bittensor rule for varying that smoothing in the bond path.

## Multiple-Mechanism Context

When a subnet uses multiple incentive mechanisms, EMA statements should stay tied to the mechanism
being discussed. A smoothed signal in one mechanism is separate from a smoothed signal in another
([Multiple Incentive Mechanisms Within Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-multiple-mech-subnets),
[Glossary: Multiple Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#multiple-incentive-mechanisms)).

The smoothing method may be the same, but the signal and mechanism still determine what the EMA
means.

## Reader Boundary

An EMA claim should identify what is being smoothed. In Bittensor, the phrase can refer to subnet
flow, validator-miner bonds, or another reward-related signal
([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The stable article-level point is the smoothing technique itself: an EMA blends new measurements
with prior history so Bittensor processes can respond to sustained changes.
