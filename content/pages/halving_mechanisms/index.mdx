# Halving Mechanisms

A halving reduces a token emission rate by 50%. In Bittensor, halving mechanisms apply to both
network-level TAO emissions and subnet-level alpha-token emissions
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

The important distinction is asset scope. TAO halvings apply to the network token, while alpha
halvings apply to a specific subnet's alpha token.

That distinction keeps halving from becoming a generic reward label. A halving changes an emission
rate; it does not by itself decide miner scores, validator dividends, or distribution logic inside a
subnet ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

## What Halving Changes

Halving changes the rate at which new tokens are emitted. It does not by itself define how miner,
validator, or staker outcomes are compared inside a subnet
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

For readers, halving is supply-rate terminology. Reward distribution and consensus describe later
allocation questions.

This places halving near issuance and emissions. Issuance explains the supply measure, emissions
explain token creation and distribution, and consensus explains how validator signals shape
allocation after emissions exist
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## TAO and Alpha Scopes

A TAO halving reduces network-level TAO emission after total TAO issuance reaches a supply-based
threshold ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance)).

This makes TAO halving an issuance-threshold concept rather than a simple calendar event. The
trigger depends on issued supply.

Bittensor halvings are supply-threshold events rather than fixed calendar or block-count events.
That makes the relevant question how much supply has been issued, not what date appears on a
schedule ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Alpha halving works at subnet scope. It reduces a subnet alpha token's emission rate after that
subnet's alpha issuance reaches the relevant threshold
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

This is different from a TAO halving. TAO halving is network-level; alpha halving is tied to a
specific subnet alpha token.

The asset label matters. TAO halvings and alpha halvings both reduce emission rates, but they do so
at different scopes and for different assets
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

## Pool Injection

TAO halvings also affect subnet pool injection. When TAO injection into subnet pools is cut in half,
the matching alpha injected alongside TAO is reduced so the pool relationship can be maintained
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

That does not mean all alpha rewards halve because of a TAO halving alone. The halving mechanism
separates alpha pool injections from alpha rewards.

Pool-injection language belongs to reserve movement, while reward-allocation language belongs to
incentive and consensus outcomes. A pool relationship can change when TAO injection changes, while
miner and validator outcomes still depend on the subnet's incentive and consensus design
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

This separation matters because alpha can appear in several tokenomics locations. Alpha pool
injection, subnet alpha emission, and alpha-token rewards are related vocabulary, but they do not
all name the same movement
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

## Dynamic TAO

Dynamic TAO gives subnets alpha-token reserves and outstanding alpha, so halving statements need to
say which asset and scope they describe
([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

For readers, a TAO halving, an alpha halving, and an alpha pool-injection change are related but not
identical tokenomics statements.

The subnet-specific part is alpha. A TAO halving belongs to network-level TAO issuance; an alpha
halving belongs to a subnet's alpha-token emission schedule
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Supply Triggers

Issuance is the supply measure that TAO halving thresholds use. Halving mechanisms are the rules
that change emission rates when the relevant supply thresholds are reached
([Glossary: Issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

This keeps issuance and halving separate. Issuance names supply; halving mechanisms name the
rate-change rules that activate when supply reaches the relevant threshold.

Halving mechanisms are supply-based emission triggers, not block-count triggers or fixed-date
schedules. The halving source explains that the actual date of each halving is not fixed because it
depends on supply movement ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

For readers, the relevant distinction is what the halving measures and when it activates. TAO
halvings activate at network-level supply thresholds, while subnet alpha halvings use subnet-level
alpha issuance thresholds.

## Issuance Rate Steps Down at Supply Thresholds

Supply-based halving cuts the network issuance rate when cumulative
[issuance](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#issuance) crosses documented
thresholds. The [Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving)
describes each event as cutting Bittensor's daily token emission rate in half, reducing how quickly
new TAO enters circulation.

That makes the issuance rate a moving figure along a supply-triggered schedule rather than a fixed
calendar. Emission material documents the first such cut lowering the per-block network reward from
1 TAO to 0.5 TAO, and later thresholds continue the same halving pattern as issuance climbs
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Alpha halving follows the same rate-cut logic at subnet scope. After a subnet's alpha issuance
reaches its threshold, that subnet's alpha emission rate drops while network TAO halving progress
remains a separate schedule
([Alpha Halvings](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving#alpha-halvings)).

## Maximum Supply Bounds the Halving Path

Halving mechanisms slow how quickly new tokens approach the documented TAO ceiling.
[Maximum supply](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#maximum-supply) names the
protocol's 21 million TAO lifetime bound, while halving names the supply-triggered rate cuts that
reduce per-block minting as issuance climbs toward that cap
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

Each threshold crossing lowers the issuance rate without changing the ceiling itself. Recycling can
return deducted TAO toward future emission, which pushes back when the next threshold is reached and
extends the time until the next halving
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

Readers should not treat maximum supply as a halving event, or read a halving as changing the cap.
Halving slows new issuance; maximum supply bounds total TAO that will ever exist.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For halving
mechanisms on a subnet such as netuid 1, that sequence changes how readers should interpret supply
totals, recycling paths, and subnet alpha issuance.

In localnet, halving thresholds and emission rates can be tested in an isolated environment. Local
issuance totals and alpha halving examples reflect local chain configuration rather than production
supply schedules.

On testnet, TAO and alpha halving behavior can be observed in a shared non-production network.
Testnet issuance and subnet alpha totals on a selected netuid are separate from mainnet chain state
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving)).

On mainnet, halving mechanisms describe live production issuance rules on the connected Bittensor
network. Observed timing or supply values depend on current emission, recycling, and halving history
on the active network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. A halving-mechanism example from one environment should
not be read as representing production issuance behavior on another network.

## Recycling Shifts Halving Timing

The [Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving) ties halving
timing to daily TAO recycling activity. When more TAO is recycled, the measured path toward an
issuance threshold can slow.

Recycling vocabulary describes tokens returned to the protocol supply path and subtracted from
issuance accounting. Registration fees, transaction fees, and other recycled flows can change how
quickly measured issuance approaches a halving threshold
([Glossary: Recycling and burning](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#recycling-and-burning)).

## Coinbase Applies Halving Rules Each Block

Halving mechanisms name the supply thresholds and emission-rate reductions, while coinbase names the
per-block process that distributes emissions and updates issuance totals. The
[Coinbase Implementation](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/navigating-subtensor/emissions-coinbase)
documentation covers block emission calculation and block reward amounts that follow a halving
schedule.

Each block, coinbase runs emission accumulation that advances the TAO and subnet alpha issuance
totals halving rules monitor, injects liquidity into subnet pools, and accumulates pending emissions
before epoch execution
([Glossary: Coinbase](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coinbase)).

Halving documentation explains when rates drop. Coinbase documentation explains the recurring block
process that applies those rates through subnet emission paths.

## Alpha Halving Reduces Participant Payouts

[Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving) documentation states that
during an alpha halving, the amount of
[Glossary: Alpha Tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-tokens)
distributed to miners, validators, and subnet owners each block is reduced by half. That reduction
applies uniformly to all participants within the affected subnet
([Alpha Halvings](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving#alpha-halvings)).

That outcome differs from what a TAO halving does to the same participant-facing alpha stream. The
halving source explains that alpha rewards to miners, validators, and subnet owners do not halve
during a TAO halving and remain constant, while only the reserve-side alpha injection tied to TAO
pool movement is cut
([Effect on Alpha Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving#effect-on-alpha-emissions)).

An alpha halving therefore names a subnet-level slowdown in future alpha paid out to roles, not a
reserve injection adjustment alone. Readers comparing halving headlines should ask whether the
statement refers to network TAO issuance, matched pool injection, or this uniform participant payout
cut on one subnet.

## Matched Alpha Injection Scales With TAO

When a TAO halving reduces subnet pool injection, the alpha injected alongside that TAO drops as
well. Halving documentation describes that matched alpha as directly proportional to the subnet's
TAO injections so the current price ratio can be maintained
([Effect on Alpha Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving#effect-on-alpha-emissions)).

[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions) documentation states the same
reserve-side rule in plain terms: alpha is injected into a subnet's alpha reserve in proportion to
TAO injection so pool growth does not by itself move alpha token price
([Alpha reserve injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#alpha-reserve-injection)).

That proportional link explains why TAO halving language can appear in reserve discussions without
renaming participant alpha rewards. The halving cuts how much matched alpha enters the pool with
each TAO injection step; it does not, by itself, halve the separate alpha outstanding path paid to
miners, validators, and subnet owners until an alpha halving applies on that subnet.

## Halving Indices Shape ADR

The [Glossary: ADR](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#adr-alpha-distribution-ratio)
entry defines Alpha Distribution Ratio as a comparison of alpha distributed outside the pool with
alpha injected into the pool. Under the current protocol, ADR tracks two raised to the power of (k
minus n), where k is the global TAO halving index and n is the subnet's alpha halving index.

That formula connects two halving scopes into one readable ratio on a subnet. Network TAO halving
progress and a subnet's alpha halving progress can diverge because each mechanism watches its own
issuance total, and ADR gives vocabulary for how far those schedules have moved relative to each
other ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Halving](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#halving)).

ADR therefore sits beside halving mechanisms rather than replacing them. Halving mechanisms name
when emission rates drop after supply thresholds; ADR names how the outside-pool versus
pool-injection split responds to the gap between those indexes on a subnet using Dynamic TAO alpha
reserves.

## Reader Boundary

Halving mechanisms should not be read as reward tables, exact timing reports, or advice about token
movement. They name supply-based rules that reduce TAO and subnet alpha emission rates when relevant
thresholds are reached ([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

TAO halving vocabulary belongs to the network token supply path. Alpha halving vocabulary belongs to
subnet alpha issuance inside a specific subnet. Pool-injection effects during TAO halving belong to
the reserve side of Dynamic TAO rather than to participant alpha payout lines
([Halving Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/halving),
[Glossary: Alpha Tokens](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#alpha-tokens)).
