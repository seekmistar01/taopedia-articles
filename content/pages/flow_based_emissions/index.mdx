# Flow-Based Emissions

Bittensor emissions include TAO and subnet-specific alpha tokens. Flow-based emissions are the
subnet-level allocation model that compares net TAO movement before TAO reserve injection is
assigned across subnets
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

The concept sits before within-subnet reward allocation. It helps decide the subnet-level injection
context; Yuma Consensus and later reward logic handle the distribution inside a subnet
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

## Injection Context

Bittensor emissions can be read at two levels. Flow-based emissions belong to the subnet comparison
level, where net TAO movement informs TAO reserve injection
([Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection),
[Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

That makes the term narrower than emissions as a whole. It does not rank one miner or calculate one
validator dividend; it sets a subnet-level allocation context.

## Net Flow Signal

The signal compares TAO entering a subnet through staking with TAO leaving through unstaking. The
net direction of that movement is the demand signal used when subnets are compared
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

For readers, the important idea is the direction of flow. Net inflow supports stronger injection
share context, while net outflow weakens it.

## Smoothing Context

Flow signals can be noisy, so smoothing helps prevent brief bursts from dominating subnet
comparison. Bittensor uses exponential moving averages in several contexts for smoothing changing
signals ([Exponential Moving Averages](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/ema),
[Emission: TAO Reserve Injection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#tao-reserve-injection)).

In this article, smoothing belongs to the comparison stage. It is not the final reward table inside
a subnet.

## Negative Flow Context

When smoothed net flow is negative, the emissions documentation connects that condition with a
zero-injection outcome for the affected subnet
([Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

Zero injection should be read as an injection-stage result. It is not a separate penalty rule for an
individual miner, validator, or staker.

## Distribution Handoff

After subnet-level injection is resolved, reward allocation still depends on within-subnet logic.
Yuma Consensus explains how validator evaluations feed miner incentives and validator-side outcomes
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission: Distribution](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution)).

Flow-based emissions therefore compare subnets. They do not replace consensus, weights, or
within-subnet reward allocation.

## Reader Boundary

Flow-based emissions should be read as a tokenomics concept for subnet comparison. It is not a
staking tutorial, miner-ranking formula, or complete explanation of all Bittensor emissions
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Emission: Distribution Across Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions#distribution-across-subnets)).

The stable article-level point is that net TAO movement helps determine subnet-level injection
before rewards are allocated inside the selected subnet.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Flow-Based
Emissions, this sequence gives readers a boundary for interpreting emission-flow examples, subnet
comparisons, and reward-distribution notes.

Localnet examples are isolated and reflect local chain state, so they are useful for controlled
experiments rather than evidence of live Bittensor behavior. Testnet examples add shared
non-production conditions, which can reveal integration behavior without touching mainnet state.

On mainnet, Flow-Based Emissions examples should be read as live production emission behavior on the
production Bittensor network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, so outcomes from one environment should not be treated as
proof of behavior in another.

## Distribution Scope

Flow-based emissions should be read as a mechanism for describing how emissions move through the
system, not as a guarantee about any participant's future reward. Emission and incentive-mechanism
documentation explain distribution and evaluation separately
([Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions),
[Understanding Incentive Mechanisms](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/anatomy-of-incentive-mechanism)).

That boundary keeps the concept tied to protocol accounting rather than validator rankings or return
forecasts.
