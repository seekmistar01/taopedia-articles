# Back-Running

Back-running is observing a pending transaction and submitting your own transaction so that it
executes immediately after it, to profit from the state change the first transaction causes. In
Bittensor it appears around subnet pool conversions visible in the mempool before block inclusion
([MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value),
[Glossary: Mempool](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mempool)).

## What It Represents

A pending stake, unstake, or swap in the mempool can signal an upcoming pool price move. A
back-runner submits a trade to execute immediately after the target and act on the dislocation the
target just caused ([Understanding Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

Price protection caps acceptable execution movement for the caller's own order. MEV Shield encrypts
sensitive pending contents so observers cannot read intent before inclusion
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection),
[MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

Back-running examples from one network environment should not be read as representing production
mempool behavior on another
([Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

## Distinction from MEV

MEV names the broader value-extraction category built on transaction ordering and pre-block
visibility. Back-running is the specific tactic that submits immediately after a visible pending
trade
([Glossary: MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **Back-running** — ordering immediately after a visible pending trade.
- **MEV** — broader category covering front-running, sandwich attacks, and back-running.

## Distinction from Front-Running

Front-running orders ahead of the target to profit from the move it is about to cause. Back-running
orders immediately after to act on the move it just caused
([MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **Front-running** — ordering before the target swap.
- **Back-running** — ordering immediately after the target swap.

## Distinction from Sandwich Attack

A sandwich attack combines a front-run and back-run around one victim trade. Back-running alone is
often plain arbitrage on the post-move dislocation rather than the full exploit
([Glossary: Sandwich Attack](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).

- **Back-running** — the single trade placed after a target.
- **Sandwich attack** — front-run plus back-run wrapping one victim trade.

## Distinction from Arbitrage

Arbitrage profits from genuine price differences between markets. Back-running names transaction
ordering immediately after an observed pending swap
([Slippage](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/slippage)).

- **Back-running** — ordering a trade right after an observed pending swap.
- **Arbitrage** — trading to close a price difference between markets.

## Distinction from Price Protection

Price protection is the trader-set tolerance that bounds adverse execution movement. Back-running is
the adversarial ordering tactic that exploits visible pending trades
([Price Protection](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/price-protection)).

- **Back-running** — ordering immediately after a visible pending swap.
- **Price protection** — tolerance that caps loss from adverse execution.

## Distinction from MEV Shield

MEV Shield encrypts pending transaction data so it stays unreadable until finalization. Back-running
depends on reading visible pending details in the mempool
([MEV Shield](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/mev-shield)).

- **Back-running** — ordering immediately after a visible pending swap.
- **MEV Shield** — encryption that hides pending data before inclusion.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Back-running names mempool ordering risk around pool conversions. Yuma Consensus converts submitted
validator weights into emission shares after evaluation completes
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

- **Back-running** — trading immediately after a visible pending swap.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement from validator weights to emission shares.

## Reader Boundary

This page defines the concept at a high level and is not trading advice. Back-running can be
harmless arbitrage or the closing slice of a sandwich; live pool depth and protections determine
which applies
([MEV](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#mev-maximal-extractable-value)).
