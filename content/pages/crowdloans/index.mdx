# Crowdloans

A crowdloan is a Bittensor funding structure for pooling TAO toward a specified action. The
documented action can be an extrinsic execution or a balance transfer, with subnet creation as the
primary example in Bittensor's crowdloan documentation
([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Crowdloan Lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans#crowdloan-lifecycle)).

The concept is narrower than general fundraising. In Taopedia prose, crowdloan names the campaign
structure that gathers contributions and applies them to the selected action; it does not by itself
prove subnet launch, future emissions, or any particular contributor outcome
([Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost),
[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).

## Funded Action Model

Crowdloans collect TAO toward a campaign-defined target. The target gives the campaign its meaning:
contributors are not simply placing TAO into a generic pool, they are funding a named action under
the crowdloan rules ([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Crowdloan Lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans#crowdloan-lifecycle)).

A crowdloan for subnet creation should be read differently from a crowdloan whose funded action is a
transfer, even though both use the same collective-funding vocabulary.

Crowdloan describes how TAO is gathered and conditionally applied; the selected action describes
what the campaign is trying to fund.

## Subnet Creation Example

Subnet creation is the main example in the Bittensor crowdloan documentation. That example connects
collective funding to the burn-cost context for bringing a subnet into existence
([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

Burn cost and crowdloan are related but not interchangeable. Burn cost names the TAO cost attached
to subnet creation; crowdloan names one way multiple contributors can fund an action that may
include that creation cost
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost)).

The crowdloan concept is collective funding, while subnet creation is the documented use case that
explains why the funding structure matters in Bittensor.

## Development Stage Context

The
[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For crowdloans,
that sequence gives readers a boundary for interpreting campaign examples, refund paths, and
subnet-creation funding notes.

In localnet, crowdloan examples are isolated and reflect local chain state, useful for controlled
experiments rather than evidence of live Bittensor behavior.

On testnet, crowdloans can be observed in a shared non-production network whose campaign outcomes
stay separate from mainnet state.

On mainnet, crowdloan examples should be read as live production funding campaigns on the production
Bittensor network.

The [Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet, so outcomes from one environment should not be treated as
proof of behavior in another environment.

## Campaign Outcome

A campaign can succeed or fail to finalize. If it succeeds, the funded action is performed. If it
does not, the documentation describes a refund path instead of treating the attempted campaign as a
completed subnet or transfer ([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans)).

A crowdloan is conditional while the campaign is open or unfinalized; only the finalized result
tells the reader whether the funded action occurred or whether contributors remain in the refund
path.

## Unfinalized Campaigns Keep the Refund Path Open

Crowdloan documentation describes campaigns that can succeed and execute the funded action or fail
and follow a refund path instead. Until finalization, the outcome remains conditional rather than
completed
([Crowdloan Lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans#crowdloan-lifecycle)).

An open campaign is not the same lifecycle evidence as a launched subnet or completed transfer.

## Refunds Are Permissionless; Dissolving the Campaign Is Not

The refund path does not depend on the campaign creator taking action. Crowdloan documentation
states that once an end block passes without finalization, anyone can call refunds for the
campaign's contributors, while dissolving the crowdloan afterward is reserved for the creator and
can only happen once every contributor has been refunded
([Crowdloan Lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans#crowdloan-lifecycle)).

That split keeps the two cleanup steps separate. Refunding returns contributor funds and can be
triggered by anyone; dissolving removes the campaign itself and is gated to the creator, with full
contributor repayment as a precondition.

## A Crowdloan's Funded Action Cannot Be Changed Mid-Campaign

A crowdloan also locks in its purpose at creation. The documentation states that exactly one
finalization action, either a call or a target address, must be specified when the campaign is
created, and that choice is immutable for the life of the campaign
([Crowdloan Lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans#crowdloan-lifecycle)).

That immutability prevents a campaign from being repurposed after contributors have already
committed funds toward a specific stated action. Contributors funding a subnet-registration
crowdloan can rely on that same action being what finalization executes, not a substituted one.

## Burn Cost Names Creation Price, Not the Crowdloan Mechanism

The [Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost) and subnet
creation documentation describe the TAO cost attached to bringing a subnet into existence. Crowdloan
names one collective-funding structure that can help fund an action such as subnet creation.

Burn-cost and crowdloan vocabulary should stay separate. One names the creation price context; the
other names how contributors pool TAO toward a campaign-defined action.

## Crowdloans Fund Actions, Not Delegation Stakes

Crowdloan vocabulary describes collective funding for a named extrinsic or transfer, commonly subnet
creation. Staking and delegation vocabulary describes committing TAO to support validators inside
subnet markets
([Staking and delegation overview](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/staking-and-delegation/delegation)).

A crowdloan can gather TAO for a burn-cost or transfer action without that action being a delegation
stake. Campaign funding should be kept separate from validator stake moves.

## Distinction from Burn Cost

Burn cost names the TAO amount required for subnet creation, while a crowdloan names one collective
funding path that can help contributors pool TAO toward that creation action
([Glossary: Burn Cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#burn-cost),
[Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Create a Subnet: Burn cost](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/create-a-subnet#burn-cost)).

Burn-cost and crowdloan vocabulary should stay separate. One names the creation price context; the
other names how contributors pool TAO toward a campaign-defined action.

- **Burn cost** — the dynamic TAO amount required to create a new subnet market.
- **Crowdloan** — collective funding toward a campaign-defined creation action.

## Distinction from Extrinsics

A crowdloan and an extrinsic relate as a funding campaign and the action it can execute. Crowdloan
documentation describes a campaign that, on finalization, runs exactly one specified action — a call
(an extrinsic) or a target-address transfer — chosen when the campaign is created
([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Crowdloan Lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans#crowdloan-lifecycle)).

- **Crowdloan** — the pooled-funding campaign that triggers one finalization action.
- **Extrinsic** — the on-chain call a campaign can execute when it finalizes.

## Distinction from Subnet Creator

A crowdloan is a funding structure that pools TAO toward a specified action, such as paying for
subnet creation, while a subnet creator is the entity that registers and owns the resulting subnet
([Subnets: Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Glossary: Subnet Creator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#subnet-creator)).

Funding-mechanism vocabulary names how TAO is pooled; creator vocabulary names who owns the subnet
it can help fund.

When registration happens through the crowdloan path that ends in a
`subtensor::register_leased_network` call, the registration can produce both a new subnet and a
[subnet lease](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans) — split profit ownership where
the crowdloan creator becomes the lease beneficiary and contributors become lease shareholders.

Not every crowdloan produces a lease. Direct subnet creation funding opens the subnet without that
layered ownership arrangement.

Crowdloans sit near subnet creation because subnet creation is the primary documented example. They
remain distinct from later lifecycle events such as
[subnet deregistration](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/subnet-deregistration), which
removes an entire subnet when the cap is full rather than refunding an unfinalized campaign.

A failed crowdloan does not create the same lifecycle evidence as a launched subnet, and a launched
subnet can later face lifecycle events that are no longer part of the original funding campaign.

## Distinction from Transfer

A crowdloan can also finalize into a transfer rather than a call. The crowdloan is the funding
campaign, while a transfer is a balance movement between accounts and is one of the two
finalization-action types the documentation allows
([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Crowdloan Lifecycle](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans#crowdloan-lifecycle)).

- **Crowdloan** — the pooled-funding campaign that triggers one finalization action.
- **Transfer** — a balance movement between accounts, used as a finalization action.

## Distinction from Yuma Consensus

Crowdloans name the collective funding structure that pools TAO toward a specified extrinsic or
transfer, with subnet creation as the primary documented example. Yuma Consensus is the separate
mechanism that converts validator weight submissions into emission shares each tempo
([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

- **Crowdloans** — collective funding toward a specified on-chain action.
- **Yuma Consensus** — on-chain settlement that turns validator weights into emission shares.
  Crowdloans name collective funding toward on-chain actions; Yuma processes validator weights into
  rewards
  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).

## Distinction from Nominator

A crowdloan and a nominator describe two different ways TAO leaves a contributor's control. The
glossary defines a nominator as an account that stakes TAO on a validator's hotkey, an ongoing
position tied to that validator; a crowdloan instead pools contributions toward one campaign-defined
finalization action such as subnet creation
([Glossary: Nominator](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#nominator)).

- **Crowdloans** — pool TAO toward a single campaign action that finalizes or refunds.
- **Nominator** — an account that stakes TAO on a validator's hotkey.

## Distinction from Coldkey

A crowdloan is the campaign that gathers and conditionally applies TAO, while a coldkey is part of
the wallet a contributor uses to authorize that movement. The glossary defines a coldkey as the
wallet component responsible for securely storing funds and performing high-risk operations such as
transfers and staking
([Glossary: Coldkey](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#coldkey)).

- **Crowdloans** — the pooled-funding campaign that finalizes one action or refunds.
- **Coldkey** — the wallet component that secures funds and performs transfers and staking.

## Distinction from Emission

When the documented subnet-creation crowdloan succeeds, resulting emissions are shared according to
contribution share. That pro-rata relationship belongs to the subnet-creation case, where
contributors funded the path that brought the subnet into existence
([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).

This does not make a crowdloan an emission guarantee. Emissions still depend on subnet behavior,
consensus results, and the broader reward-distribution context
([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).

The safe reading is contribution-linked sharing, not performance prediction.

## Reader Boundary

Crowdloan examples should keep their campaign and network context attached. A cap, end block,
contribution set, refund path, or emission-sharing result belongs to the campaign where it was
observed, not to every possible Bittensor crowdloan
([Crowdloans](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/crowdloans),
[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks)).

This article defines the concept, not a participation workflow. It should not be read as staking
advice, a yield estimate, a campaign recommendation, or a step-by-step funding guide.
